Joe Biden

The senator who spent 36 years in Congress, lost a wife and daughter to a car crash and a son to cancer, won the White House at 77, passed the most significant climate legislation in American history — and then stepped aside to save his party from himself.

Origins & Rise

Joe Biden was born in Scranton, Pennsylvania, in November 1942, into a family that had slipped from middle-class comfort into financial strain. His father, Joseph Biden Sr., worked through a series of jobs — used cars, heating oil, janitorial supplies — before the family moved to Claymont, Delaware, when Joe was ten. The Scranton working-class identity never left him: he invoked it so consistently throughout his career that it became a political brand. Whether that brand was authentic or performance was an argument his opponents never tired of making, and one that he never fully escaped.

He graduated from the University of Delaware in 1965 and from Syracuse University College of Law in 1968, finishing in the lower third of his class. He was admitted to the Delaware bar and practiced briefly before entering politics. His student deferments during the Vietnam War — later described by his office as the result of a childhood asthma condition — would become a recurring point of attack, though he was never charged with wrongdoing. At 27, he ran for a Senate seat from Delaware against a two-term Republican incumbent, J. Caleb Boggs. Almost nobody expected him to win. He won.

Biden was 29 years old on Election Day 1972 — just old enough to meet the constitutional minimum of 30 by the time he was to be sworn in. He campaigned on opposition to the Vietnam War and an instinctive appeal to working-class Catholic voters. The victory was significant enough that it made national news. Then, three weeks after the election, on December 18, 1972, his wife Neilia and their one-year-old daughter Naomi were killed when a tractor-trailer struck their station wagon near Wilmington. Their sons Beau and Hunter survived with serious injuries. Biden was sworn in at his sons' hospital bedsides.

Biden considered resigning from the Senate after the accident but was persuaded to stay by Senate Majority Leader Mike Mansfield. He began commuting daily from Wilmington to Washington by Amtrak — a habit that lasted his entire Senate career and that became one of his most recognisable political symbols, along with his aviator sunglasses and his unpolished, sometimes garbled speechmaking. He remarried in 1977, to Jill Jacobs, an English teacher who later earned a doctoral degree in educational leadership and was known throughout his presidency as Dr. Jill Biden.

His Senate career spanned three and a half decades, the Cold War, Watergate, the Reagan revolution, the Gulf War, the Clinton years, September 11, and the Iraq War. He chaired the Senate Foreign Relations Committee and the Senate Judiciary Committee, and his two most consequential senatorial moments were arguably his worst: his handling of the 1991 Anita Hill hearings, in which he allowed Republican members to savage Hill while questioning Clarence Thomas's fitness for the Supreme Court only gently, and his 1994 Crime Bill, which contributed to a mass incarceration explosion that fell disproportionately on Black communities and that became a major liability during his 2020 campaign. He ran for president in 1988 and withdrew after it emerged he had plagiarised a speech by a British Labour politician, Neil Kinnock. He ran again in 2008, withdrew early in the primary, and was selected as Barack Obama's running mate.

Context

The Anita Hill hearings: a wound that never fully healed

In October 1991, Biden chaired the Senate Judiciary Committee hearings on Clarence Thomas's nomination to the Supreme Court. Anita Hill, a law professor who had worked under Thomas, testified that he had sexually harassed her. Biden did not call three other women who had reported similar conduct with Thomas. His questioning of Hill was widely seen as insufficient, and his management of the hearings was viewed by critics as enabling Thomas's confirmation. Biden later apologised — in 2019, during his presidential campaign — but Hill herself said the apology did not go far enough. The episode was a genuine failure of leadership that Biden never fully escaped.

As vice president from 2009 to 2017, Biden was given the Iraq portfolio — overseeing the withdrawal of combat troops — and became a reliable, sometimes loose-lipped spokesperson for the Obama administration. He was deeply involved in the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act, the president's foreign policy deliberations, and the push for same-sex marriage rights. He announced his own support for same-sex marriage in May 2012, slightly ahead of Obama — a semi-accidental move that forced the president's hand and accelerated the administration's public shift on the issue.

Biden's son Beau, the Attorney General of Delaware and a figure widely seen as a future political star, died of glioblastoma — an aggressive brain cancer — on May 30, 2015. Biden has said that the grief from Beau's death made him decide not to run in 2016, though he has also been candid that he was not certain he would have won the primary against Hillary Clinton. He announced his third presidential campaign in April 2019, framing it explicitly as a fight for "the soul of America" against Donald Trump. He won the 2020 Democratic primary after a series of early setbacks — a fourth-place finish in Iowa, a fifth-place finish in New Hampshire — and a decisive victory in the South Carolina primary, which proved to be the turning point. He was inaugurated on January 20, 2021, at 78 years old, the oldest person ever to assume the American presidency.

Worldview: The Institutionalist

Biden is not, and never pretended to be, an ideologue. His political formation was the Senate — an institution that rewards coalition-building, compromise, and the cultivation of personal relationships across party lines. He was shaped by senators like Mike Mansfield, Hubert Humphrey, and Daniel Patrick Moynihan; by the foreign policy establishment of the Cold War era; and by a Catholic social justice tradition that emphasised care for the working class without demanding systemic transformation. He read the politics of his coalition carefully and moved his positions accordingly — on crime, on busing, on same-sex marriage — which his supporters called realism and his critics called opportunism.

The deepest consistency in Biden's worldview is not ideological but institutional: a belief that democracies depend on norms and alliances and that both must be actively maintained. His first foreign policy act as president was to re-enter the Paris Climate Agreement; his second was to cancel American withdrawal from the World Health Organisation. Both took effect on Day One. His approach to Russia's invasion of Ukraine — assembling and sustaining a large coalition of allies, calibrating weapons deliveries to avoid escalation, insisting on Ukrainian agency — was methodical institutionalism in action. His critics argued it was too slow and cautious; his defenders argued it held NATO together under the most serious threat to the alliance since its founding.

Alliance Multilateralism

Biden believed the post-1945 order of alliances, international institutions, and shared rules was not just a diplomatic convenience but a structural guarantee of stability. His self-described foreign policy goal — "America is back" — was a direct repudiation of Trump's transactional unilateralism. He spent heavily in early months rebuilding relationships with NATO, the EU, South Korea, and Japan that had frayed under his predecessor.

Empathy as Political Method

Biden's most singular political trait was his ability to project sincere personal grief. His losses — Neilia, Naomi, Beau — gave him a credibility on suffering that few American politicians have possessed. He used it deliberately, meeting regularly with Gold Star families, veterans, and cancer patients. Critics accused him of weaponising his grief; supporters saw in it a genuine form of democratic leadership.

Middle-Class Economics

Biden's domestic economic philosophy was shaped by a consistent belief that working families had been left behind by both the Reagan revolution and the globalisation of the 1990s. His legislative agenda — the Infrastructure Act, the CHIPS Act, the Inflation Reduction Act — represented the most significant investment in American industrial and climate policy since the New Deal. He described this as "building from the middle out, not the top down."

Limits of American Power

By the time he reached the presidency, Biden had developed a sceptical view of what American military force could achieve. His decision to withdraw from Afghanistan drew explicitly on his own experience watching the war drag on without producing the conditions that would justify an indefinite presence. As Laurel Miller (2021) observed, Biden asked "can we" rather than "should we" — and answered candidly that America had reached the limits of what it could plausibly accomplish.

"Don't tell me what you value. Show me your budget, and I'll tell you what you value."
— Joe Biden, a phrase he used repeatedly throughout his career, including in his 2022 State of the Union address

Biden's relationship with the progressive wing of his party was always tense and transactional. He was not a transformationalist; he did not believe the system was broken, only that it had been temporarily hijacked. His agenda was ambitious by the standards of recent Democratic presidents but stopped well short of what his party's left flank demanded. He rejected Medicare for All, declined to abolish the Senate filibuster, and opposed the "defund the police" movement even as it defined the summer of 2020. His willingness to use executive action — student debt relief, climate regulations — was significant but always constrained by a lawyer's awareness of what the courts would tolerate.

The Presidency: The Legislative Record

Biden entered office on January 20, 2021, with the United States recording approximately 3,000 COVID deaths per day, the Capitol still processing the aftermath of the January 6 insurrection two weeks earlier, and a Senate split 50-50 between parties. The constraints were severe. What his administration accomplished within them was more substantial than his presidency is often given credit for.

COVID and the American Rescue Plan

The American Rescue Plan, signed on March 11, 2021, was a $1.9 trillion COVID relief package — the largest single economic intervention in American history up to that point. It funded vaccine distribution, extended enhanced unemployment benefits, sent $1,400 stimulus checks to most Americans, dramatically expanded the child tax credit (temporarily reducing child poverty by around 30%), and provided fiscal relief to state and local governments that had been running deficits through the pandemic. It passed with zero Republican votes, through the budget reconciliation process. Biden oversaw the distribution of 200 million COVID vaccine doses in his first 100 days — double the goal he set on inauguration day.

The inflation that followed was the central domestic political problem of his presidency. Consumer prices rose at their fastest rate in 40 years through 2022, peaking at around 9% annual inflation in June 2022. Whether this was primarily caused by the American Rescue Plan's spending, by global supply chain disruptions, by Russia's invasion of Ukraine driving up energy prices, or by some combination of all three remains a genuine economic argument. Biden and his advisors initially described the inflation as "transitory" — a prediction that proved embarrassingly wrong. The political cost was severe: by late 2021, his approval rating had dropped below 45% and never recovered.

The legislative hat trick

What makes Biden's domestic legislative record historically significant is that he passed three major infrastructure, industrial, and climate bills in a 12-month window. The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (November 2021, $1.2 trillion), the CHIPS and Science Act (August 2022, $280 billion), and the Inflation Reduction Act (August 2022, $369 billion) collectively represented the largest American investment in physical infrastructure since Eisenhower's interstate highway system, the largest investment in domestic semiconductor manufacturing in American history, and the largest climate legislation ever signed. The Inflation Reduction Act alone was projected to reduce American greenhouse gas emissions by approximately 40% below 2005 levels by 2030 — still short of the Paris Agreement targets but far beyond any previous legislative commitment.

Historical context

Why the "hat trick" matters

Presidents generally pass major legislation in their first two years, before midterm losses reduce their congressional leverage. Biden passed all three bills in that window — the Infrastructure Act with bipartisan votes (19 Republican senators, 13 Republican House members), the CHIPS Act with significant bipartisan support, and the IRA through reconciliation by the slimmest possible margin. Comparable bursts of legislative productivity in modern American history are rare: Johnson's Great Society (1964-65), Reagan's tax cuts (1981), and Obama's ACA + stimulus (2009-10).

The limits of success

Biden failed on several major priorities. Build Back Better — a $3.5 trillion social spending package covering universal pre-K, paid family leave, expanded Medicare benefits, and housing — was negotiated down by Senator Joe Manchin of West Virginia and ultimately died. Voting rights legislation was blocked by the filibuster. Immigration reform produced no major legislation. His administration's handling of the southern border became one of his most consistent political vulnerabilities, with illegal crossings reaching record levels through 2023 before executive action in mid-2024 reduced them significantly. His June 2023 executive order on asylum — restricting claims in ways that advocacy groups said violated domestic and international law — illustrated the tension between his institutionalist instincts and the political necessity of toughening enforcement.

Controversy

Hunter Biden and the family financial questions

Biden's son Hunter Biden pleaded guilty in September 2023 to federal tax charges and was convicted in June 2024 on federal gun charges — becoming the first child of a sitting president to be convicted of a crime. Biden initially said he would not pardon Hunter, then on December 1, 2024 — weeks before leaving office — granted his son a sweeping preemptive pardon covering any crimes he may have committed between January 2014 and December 2024. Biden also pardoned his brother James Biden for any federal crimes over the same period. Critics called the pardons a betrayal of his stated commitment to the rule of law; supporters argued the prosecutions were politically motivated. The episode damaged his legacy on institutional integrity.

Foreign Policy

Biden was arguably the most experienced foreign policy president in modern American history. He had served on the Senate Foreign Relations Committee for most of his career, chaired it twice, and had visited virtually every major U.S. ally and adversary multiple times. His foreign policy team — Secretary of State Antony Blinken, National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan, Deputy National Security Advisor Jon Finer — were experienced institutionalists who shared his worldview. The foreign policy record of his four years was defined by three events: the withdrawal from Afghanistan, the Russian invasion of Ukraine, and the Gaza war.

April–August 2021
Afghanistan: The End of a Twenty-Year War
▼

On April 14, 2021, Biden announced that all U.S. troops would leave Afghanistan by September 11 — the twentieth anniversary of the attacks that had started the war. The deadline was later moved to August 31. The decision was Biden's own, taken against the advice of most of his military commanders, who recommended keeping a small residual force of 2,500 troops. Biden's reasoning, as Laurel Miller (2021) has analysed in detail, was a kind of strategic realism: he concluded that the conditions for U.S. success — a stable Afghan government capable of defending itself — were not achievable within any reasonable period at any affordable cost. He framed the central question as "can we" rather than "should we." His conclusion was that the United States had reached the limits of what it could plausibly accomplish. Miller (2021)

The decision was also partly constrained by inheritance. Trump's administration had signed a deal with the Taliban in Doha in February 2020, promising a full withdrawal by May 1, 2021, in exchange for Taliban counter-terrorism commitments. The deal had been agreed without the Afghan government's participation. Biden inherited an agreement that had already, as Miller put it, "exhausted the leverage argument" — the idea that keeping troops as a bargaining chip could produce better conditions. The leverage had already been traded away. Miller (2021)

What Biden did not anticipate, and what his administration consistently underestimated, was the speed of the Afghan government's collapse. Kabul fell on August 15, 2021 — two weeks before the withdrawal deadline — as Afghan security forces, demoralised by years of corruption, inadequate support, and the knowledge that American air cover was ending, dissolved almost overnight. The images from Kabul's Hamid Karzai International Airport — Afghans crowding the tarmac, clinging to C-17 fuselages as they departed — were among the most damaging of his presidency. On August 26, an ISIS-K suicide bomber detonated at Abbey Gate, killing 13 American service members and approximately 170 Afghan civilians. Biden stuck to the August 31 deadline despite calls from allies for an extension. He accepted, as Miller described it, that a Taliban return to power was an "undesirable but tolerable outcome" for the United States. Miller (2021)

The political cost was immediate and lasting. Biden's approval rating dropped roughly 10 points through August and September 2021 and never fully recovered. The military, intelligence, and foreign policy establishment were sharply critical — not so much of the decision to withdraw, which many agreed was correct, but of the execution and particularly of the failure to accelerate the evacuation of Afghan allies before the deadline. Approximately 120,000 people were evacuated in the final two weeks; tens of thousands of Afghans who had worked with U.S. forces were left behind.

February 2022 – January 2025
Ukraine: Assembling the Coalition
▼

On February 24, 2022, Russia launched a full-scale invasion of Ukraine. The Biden administration had spent the preceding weeks publicly warning allies — and the Ukrainian government, which was sceptical — that an invasion was coming, sharing intelligence assessments with unusual transparency in an effort to pre-empt Russian denial and strengthen the coalition's resolve. The response to the invasion was the most significant accomplishment of Biden's foreign policy: within days the U.S. had coordinated sweeping financial sanctions on Russia, the exclusion of major Russian banks from SWIFT, the freezing of approximately $300 billion in Russian central bank assets held in Western accounts, and the beginning of a sustained military assistance programme to Ukraine.

The Biden approach to Ukraine was deliberate and calibrated. Each major weapons system — Stingers, Javelins, HIMARS multiple-rocket systems, Abrams tanks, F-16s — was debated carefully before authorisation, with the White House consistently weighing Ukraine's military needs against the risk of NATO escalation with a nuclear power. Critics, including many Ukrainian officials, argued this caution cost lives and prolonged the war unnecessarily; Biden's team argued it maintained Western unity and avoided the risk of direct NATO-Russia conflict. The coalition he assembled — pulling in European governments that had been reluctant to confront Russia, securing Swedish and Finnish membership in NATO, coordinating weapons deliveries across dozens of countries — was a genuine organisational achievement.

Biden secured approximately $175 billion in U.S. assistance to Ukraine through January 2025, though the final package required a months-long congressional battle in late 2023 and early 2024. The war was still ongoing when he left office, with Russian forces having made incremental gains in eastern Ukraine but failing to achieve any of their major operational objectives.

October 2023 – January 2025
Gaza: The War That Defined His Final Year
▼

On October 7, 2023, Hamas launched a surprise attack from Gaza into southern Israel, killing approximately 1,200 Israelis — the worst single-day killing of Jews since the Holocaust — and taking roughly 250 hostages. Biden's immediate response was categorical support for Israel's right to self-defence: he flew to Tel Aviv within days and embraced Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu publicly. The gesture was politically and symbolically important in the immediate aftermath; it would become a significant liability over the following year.

Israel's military campaign in Gaza — which the Israeli government described as aimed at destroying Hamas's military and governing capacity — produced a humanitarian catastrophe on a scale that made it increasingly difficult for Biden to sustain uncritical support. By early 2024, the Palestinian death toll had exceeded 25,000 and was rising; UN agencies warned of famine conditions in northern Gaza; and footage of civilian casualties was producing mass protests across American university campuses and in cities around the world. Biden's calls for civilian protection and "humanitarian pauses" were publicly ignored by Netanyahu. His administration paused a single weapons shipment — of 1,800-pound bombs — in May 2024, after months of internal pressure from Democratic officials and administration staff. The pause was limited and temporary.

The Gaza war drove a significant portion of Arab-American and young Democratic voters away from Biden — a factor that became directly relevant to his 2024 electoral calculations. Michigan, with a large Arab-American population, produced a significant "uncommitted" primary vote in February 2024 as a direct protest. Biden's handling of Gaza became a central argument in the wider debate about whether he could win a general election in November 2024 — and the debate about whether he should step aside.

The Departure

The question of Biden's age — he was 78 when he took office and would have been 86 at the end of a second term — had been present from the beginning of his presidency but became politically acute through 2023 and early 2024. A Department of Justice special counsel's report in February 2024, investigating Biden's handling of classified documents, characterised him as a "well-meaning, elderly man with a poor memory" — a description that was widely criticised as inappropriate for a prosecutorial document but that captured a widespread private anxiety within the Democratic Party.

June 27, 2024
The CNN Debate: A Pivotal 90 Minutes
▼

The debate against Donald Trump on June 27, 2024 — the earliest general election presidential debate in American history — was a political catastrophe. Biden appeared confused at several points, lost his train of thought repeatedly, spoke in a near-whisper at times, and failed to land any of the attacks on Trump's record that his campaign had prepared. Trump, who had made dozens of false statements during the debate, emerged looking stronger by contrast simply because he was more alert.

The reaction from the Democratic Party was unlike anything in modern American political history. Within 48 hours, major donors were calling privately for Biden to step aside. By July, sitting Democratic senators and House members — initially one or two, then a steady stream — began publicly urging him to withdraw. The White House pushed back aggressively, insisting the debate was a "bad night" and pointing to Biden's full legislative record. Biden himself told donors and allied leaders that he was not dropping out. He held a press conference on July 11 that was intended to demonstrate his command of his brief; it was credible in substance but did not arrest the political bleeding.

July 21, 2024
The Withdrawal: "I Believe It Is in the Best Interest of My Party"
▼

On July 21, 2024 — 107 days before the election — Biden announced via a letter posted on social media that he was withdrawing from the 2024 presidential race. He endorsed Vice President Kamala Harris. The decision, he wrote, was in "the best interest of my party and the country." It was the first time in modern American history that an incumbent president had stepped aside under pressure rather than defeat. Biden had spoken by phone with former President Barack Obama and former Secretary of State Hillary Clinton the night before; both had reportedly indicated, in carefully calibrated language, that they supported his assessment of the political situation.

The debate about whether Biden made the right decision, and whether he should have made it earlier, was immediate and will continue for years. Supporters argued it was a selfless act of democratic patriotism — putting the country ahead of personal ambition — and pointed out that the very willingness to step aside distinguished him from Trump in a fundamental way. Critics argued he had waited too long, that the delay after the debate had damaged Harris's campaign by consuming precious weeks, and that his earlier insistence that he was not stepping down had left the party with a chaotic, compressed timeline for selecting and building support for his successor. Harris ultimately lost the 2024 election to Donald Trump.

Legacy & Debate

Any assessment of Joe Biden's presidency written in 2024 or early 2025 must acknowledge that the lens is not yet fixed. He was a one-term president who lost to the man he had defeated four years earlier, and whose successor began dismantling his legislative achievements within hours of taking office — withdrawing from the Paris Agreement again, reversing his executive orders, pausing his student debt relief, and beginning to unwind the IRA's implementation. Whether the structural investments of the Infrastructure Act and the CHIPS Act prove durable or reversible will be a significant factor in long-term historical assessment.

The case for a consequential presidency
  • Passed more significant domestic legislation in one term than any president since Lyndon Johnson
  • Led the most effective Western response to Russian aggression since NATO was founded
  • Signed the most significant climate legislation in American history
  • Oversaw the fastest job creation of any presidency since records began
  • Restored American standing with allies after four years of transactional disruption
  • Made the selfless decision to step aside rather than risk handing the election to Trump through ego
  • Oversaw vaccine rollout that ended the acute COVID emergency
The case against: failures and costs
  • The Afghanistan withdrawal's execution was a genuine failure that left allies and partners behind
  • Inflation — whatever its causes — was the dominant political reality of his term and he never recovered from it
  • The Gaza war put him on the wrong side of a major humanitarian crisis and cost his party with key constituencies
  • His age diminished his presidency before its end and his insistence on running until July 2024 may have cost Harris the election
  • Failed to pass major voting rights, immigration, or social spending legislation
  • Hunter Biden pardons undermined his claimed commitment to rule of law
  • Failed to build durable political support for his legislative achievements — most Americans couldn't name them
Genuinely contested
  • The Afghanistan withdrawal: was the decision correct even if the execution was flawed? Miller (2021) offers the most sustained analysis — concluding the leverage argument was already exhausted before Biden took office
  • The inflation question: how much of the 2022 inflation surge was Biden's spending and how much was global supply chain disruption? Economists genuinely disagree
  • The Gaza question: could more pressure earlier have produced different outcomes, or was Israeli decision-making genuinely beyond American influence?
  • The stepping-aside question: did it help or hurt? The counterfactual — Biden staying in — is unknowable

What distinguishes Biden from most of the other figures in this series is that his administration was not authoritarian — he left office voluntarily, transferred power peacefully, and accepted the result of a democratic election that went against his party. In an era when this has become a genuinely contested norm rather than an assumption, that matters more than it might otherwise seem. The historians who assess Biden a generation from now will likely argue about whether his legislative record was progressive or insufficient, whether his foreign policy was careful or timid — but the basic fact of democratic continuity may, in time, appear more significant than any individual policy.

"I've always believed you can define a man by what makes him angry. And Biden was always most angry about injustice — he just wasn't always sure exactly what to do about it."
— A former senior White House official, quoted anonymously in press accounts during the 2024 transition period
