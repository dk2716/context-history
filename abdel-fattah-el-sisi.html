Abdel Fattah el-Sisi

عبد الفتاح السيسي

The general who overthrew Egypt's first democratically elected president, then ordered the bloodiest crackdown in modern Egyptian history — and has ruled ever since by building a security state that scholars describe as more repressive than anything Mubarak attempted.

Origins & Rise

Abdel Fattah el-Sisi was born in November 1954 in Cairo's Gamaleya district — a working-class neighborhood in Islamic Cairo, close to Al-Azhar mosque. His family background was conservative and religious by Egyptian standards but not Islamist; his great-aunt was a member of the Muslim Brotherhood's women's wing in the 1940s, a biographical detail he has carefully avoided discussing. He entered the Egyptian Military Academy in the mid-1970s — the standard path for ambitious Egyptian men of his class — and graduated in 1977. Nothing in his early career suggested a future that would make him one of the most consequential figures in post-independence Egyptian history.

His career was steady rather than spectacular, moving through the standard progression of Egyptian military ranks. The critical inflection point came in 2006, when he attended the United States Army War College in Carlisle, Pennsylvania. His classified thesis, obtained and reported on years later, was titled "Democracy in the Middle East." It argued that democracy was compatible with Islam but that it needed to be developed organically within Arab societies, not imposed from outside — a position that reads less like a brief for liberal politics than like an argument for patient, state-managed political development. His American classmates described him as professional and courteous; none apparently identified him as someone building toward anything unusual.

In 2010, Mubarak appointed him head of military intelligence — a position with significant visibility and influence over Egypt's domestic security picture. When the Arab Spring toppled Mubarak in February 2011 and the Supreme Council of the Armed Forces took over, Sisi was part of the inner military establishment navigating the transition. After Mohamed Morsi and the Muslim Brotherhood won Egypt's first competitive presidential election in June 2012, Morsi appointed Sisi as Defense Minister in August of the same year — a move Morsi apparently calculated would bring the military establishment into alliance with his presidency. He miscalculated badly.

Worldview & Ideology

Stability Above All

Sisi's worldview begins and ends with order. He represents a strand of Egyptian military thinking that views political instability — of any kind, from any direction — as Egypt's existential threat. The Muslim Brotherhood's year in power was, in this framework, not merely bad governance but an existential threat to the Egyptian state itself. His repeated use of the phrase "the state" as something precious and fragile that must be defended frames all political opposition as inherently dangerous, whether Islamist, liberal, or leftist.

Secular Nationalism — Egyptian, Not Arab

Unlike Nasser, Sisi has little interest in pan-Arab ideology or regional leadership ambitions beyond what serves Egyptian security interests. He is Egyptian-first in a way that Nasser, whose identity was bound up with the Arab world's transformation, never quite was. He has maintained security cooperation with Israel over Gaza, normalized relations with Gulf monarchies that are ideologically far removed from Egyptian military nationalism, and has shown no interest in ideological projects beyond Egyptian state power.

The Military as the Nation

In Sisi's Egypt, the military is not merely a guarantor of security — it is the most legitimate institution in the state, the carrier of Egyptian national identity, and the proper arbiter of political questions that civilians cannot be trusted to resolve. This is a worldview common across the Egyptian officer corps, and it explains why Sisi has expanded military involvement in the civilian economy: construction, food production, consumer goods, infrastructure. Scholars estimate the military controls 25-40% of the Egyptian economy, though exact figures are deliberately opaque.

Counterterrorism as Governance

After Rabaa, Sisi framed almost all political opposition as either terrorism or foreign interference. The Muslim Brotherhood was designated a terrorist organisation in December 2013. Journalists, human rights lawyers, civil society activists, and political opponents have been prosecuted under anti-terrorism statutes. This securitization of politics — treating dissent as a national security threat — is the defining feature of his governance and has been documented extensively by Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch.

"You can't be naive about Egypt. We either confront terrorism or we don't. There is no middle."
— Abdel Fattah el-Sisi, 2014 — a formulation that has been applied with consistent expansiveness, classifying an ever-wider range of opponents as "terrorists."

How He Rules

Sisi's Egypt is what political scientists call a fully consolidated authoritarian state — one where the formal institutions of democracy (elections, parliament, courts) continue to exist but have been systematically hollowed out. What changed after 2013 was not only the removal of the Brotherhood but the targeting of every organised political force in Egyptian society, including the secular liberals who had cheered the coup against Morsi.

Mass Imprisonment

The scale of political detention in Egypt under Sisi is without precedent in the country's modern history. Human rights organisations estimate between 60,000 and 100,000 political prisoners, including journalists, bloggers, lawyers, academics, and politicians across the political spectrum. The conditions of pre-trial detention have been documented to include prolonged solitary confinement, torture, and denial of medical care. Josua and Edel's 2021 research in Mediterranean Politics explicitly describes Egypt as "under the grip of a military dictatorship more repressive than ever under Mubarak" — a strong claim given Mubarak's own record. The Political Terror Scale data they cite shows Egypt's repression level jumped sharply from 2013 and remained at elevated levels through 2018.

Documented Atrocity

The Rabaa al-Adawiya Massacre — August 14, 2013

Egyptian security forces dispersed two sit-in protests by supporters of ousted President Mohamed Morsi — at Rabaa al-Adawiya Square and al-Nahda Square in Cairo. Human Rights Watch's detailed investigation, "All According to Plan" (2014), documented at least 817 deaths at Rabaa alone over approximately ten hours; the true figure is likely higher. Security forces used armoured vehicles, snipers, and tear gas; medical personnel were prevented from reaching the wounded. HRW described it as "one of the world's largest killings of demonstrators in a single day in recent history." Sisi has never expressed regret for the operation. Egypt's government designated the deaths as a necessary counterterrorism operation.

Control of Media and Civil Society

Egyptian authorities have systematically dismantled independent media. Dozens of news websites have been blocked; journalists from Al Jazeera and independent Egyptian outlets were imprisoned on terrorism charges; social media monitoring has been expanded. The 2019 constitutional amendments gave Sisi control over senior judicial appointments, eliminating one of the last institutional checks on executive power. Universities, as the Scholars at Risk network has documented, have been subjected to quasi-military oversight — academics self-censor or emigrate.

Economic Programme: Megaprojects and IMF Deals

Sisi has pursued an ambitious programme of large-scale infrastructure projects — most prominently, a new administrative capital being built from scratch in the desert east of Cairo, projected to cost $58 billion and designed to house government ministries and the presidential palace. A second Suez Canal lane was inaugurated with enormous fanfare in August 2015. These projects, combined with an IMF deal in 2016 worth $12 billion that required devaluation of the Egyptian pound and subsidy cuts, generated real hardship for ordinary Egyptians while creating business for military-owned construction firms. Economic growth has improved on paper, but the gains have not been widely distributed and subsidy cuts have been severe.

The Coup and Rabaa

JUNE–JUNE 2012
Morsi Elected — The Brotherhood in Power
▼

Mohamed Morsi of the Muslim Brotherhood won Egypt's first genuinely competitive presidential election in June 2012 with 51.7% of the vote — a margin that reflected deep ambivalence rather than enthusiasm, since many Egyptians voted for him primarily to prevent the election of Ahmed Shafik, Mubarak's last prime minister. Morsi's presidency was troubled from the start: he issued a constitutional declaration in November 2012 placing presidential decisions above judicial review, triggering massive protests; the economy continued to deteriorate; and he proved unable to manage either the military establishment or the fractured political opposition.

Morsi's appointment of Sisi as Defense Minister in August 2012 — alongside the retirement of Field Marshal Tantawi, who had led the post-Mubarak military council — was intended to demonstrate civilian control of the military. It proved to be the opposite: Sisi used his position to plan the removal of the president who had appointed him.

JUNE–JULY 2013
Tamarod, Mass Protests, and the Coup
▼

In spring 2013, a petition campaign called Tamarod ("Rebellion") emerged, claiming to gather signatures demanding early presidential elections. The campaign was later revealed to have had significant funding and organisational support from Egyptian security services — though it also reflected genuine popular discontent. By late June 2013, large protests against Morsi were taking place across Egypt; some estimates claimed millions in the streets, though the figures were disputed. On July 3, 2013, Sisi appeared on television flanked by the heads of Al-Azhar, the Coptic Church, and key political leaders to announce Morsi's removal and suspension of the constitution. It was a coup, though Egyptian authorities and their international supporters avoided using the word — because the US foreign aid law required cutting military assistance to governments that came to power through a military coup against a democratically elected president.

The Obama administration's response was notably ambivalent. The US did not immediately cut aid; Secretary of State Kerry spoke of the military "restoring democracy." Kirkpatrick's account from inside the US Embassy in Cairo describes a deliberate choice by American officials to avoid confronting the reality of what had happened. Gulf states — particularly Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Kuwait — were far less equivocal: they immediately pledged $12 billion in aid to the new Egyptian government, replacing US economic leverage overnight.

AUGUST 14, 2013
Rabaa — The Massacre That Defined His Rule
▼

After the coup, Brotherhood supporters organised a sit-in at Rabaa al-Adawiya Square in the Nasr City district of Cairo, insisting on Morsi's reinstatement. A smaller sit-in occupied al-Nahda Square near Cairo University. For six weeks the two camps faced off, with the interim government issuing ultimatums and the protesters refusing to leave. On the morning of August 14, 2013, security forces moved in with armoured bulldozers, live ammunition, and snipers on surrounding rooftops. The operation lasted approximately ten hours.

Human Rights Watch's detailed forensic investigation, drawing on hundreds of witness accounts, satellite imagery, and video evidence, documented at least 817 deaths at Rabaa — making it one of the largest mass killings of protesters recorded in a single day in recent history, comparable in scale to Tiananmen Square and the 1988 Halabja chemical attack. Bodies were found with gunshot wounds to the head and chest. A makeshift field hospital was specifically targeted. The surrounding streets were set alight after the main square was cleared. An estimated 43 people were killed at al-Nahda Square simultaneously. HRW concluded that "Egyptian security forces and military committed serious violations of international human rights law, some of which may amount to crimes against humanity."

The massacre was presented to Egyptian audiences — and to significant parts of the Egyptian public — as a necessary security operation against armed terrorists. This framing succeeded domestically to a degree that shocked outside observers. Edel and Josua's research specifically identified the Rabaa aftermath as a case study in how autocrats frame mass killings through securitization — "Autocrats often frame dissenters as enemies of the state, as was the case in Egypt when Muslim Brotherhood supporters were massacred after the 2013 coup."

MAY 2014 & 2019
Elections, Reelection, and Constitutional Power Grab
▼

In the presidential elections of May 2014, Sisi received 96.9% of the vote on a turnout of 47.5% — low enough that authorities had to extend voting by a day and reportedly urge Egyptians to come out in an embarrassing public reminder that even managed elections require some minimal participation. His only opponent, Hamdeen Sabahi, received 3.1%. In 2018, Sisi was reelected with 97.1%; his main potential rival, former general Sami Anan, was arrested after announcing his candidacy.

The 2019 constitutional amendments were the most consequential consolidation of Sisi's power. They extended the presidential term from four to six years, reset his term counter (allowing him to remain president until at least 2030), gave the president the power to appoint senior judges, and formally codified the military's role as "guardian" of Egyptian democracy — a phrase that would be comic if its implications weren't serious. The amendments passed a referendum with 88.8% approval, a result that reflected the absence of any meaningful opposition campaign rather than genuine consensus.

Egypt Under Sisi

2014–PRESENT
The Sinai Insurgency — A Real Security Problem Alongside Political Repression
▼

Sisi's most legitimate security challenge has been the Sinai Peninsula, where an ISIS affiliate — Wilayat Sinai, formerly Ansar Beit al-Maqdis — has conducted sustained guerrilla operations against Egyptian security forces and, periodically, civilians. The group was responsible for the bombing of Metrojet Flight 9268 in October 2015, killing all 224 people aboard, and the November 2017 attack on the Al-Rawda mosque in North Sinai that killed 311 worshippers. The Egyptian military's counterinsurgency operations in Sinai have themselves been accused of serious abuses — forced displacement of North Sinai villages, destruction of homes, and documented torture of detainees — creating a cycle in which heavy-handed tactics have compounded rather than resolved the insurgency.

Human Rights Watch (2019)
FEBRUARY 2016
The Giulio Regeni Case — A Signal to the World
▼

Giulio Regeni was an Italian PhD candidate at Cambridge University researching independent trade unions in Egypt. He disappeared on January 25, 2016 — the fifth anniversary of the Egyptian Revolution. His body was found on February 3 in a ditch along the Cairo-Alexandria highway, bearing signs of systematic torture over multiple days. Egyptian authorities initially attributed his death to a car accident, then to criminal gangs. Italian investigators, working with Italian prosecutors over the following years, produced evidence pointing to Egyptian security services as responsible. The case produced a significant diplomatic crisis between Egypt and Italy and served, as Josua and Edel noted in their research, as a chilling signal to researchers worldwide: a Western PhD student with a Cambridge affiliation studying something as relatively benign as trade unions could be kidnapped and killed with impunity.

Josua & Edel (2021)
JUNE 2019
Morsi Dies in Custody
▼

Mohamed Morsi, Egypt's first elected president, died during a court session on June 17, 2019, after collapsing in the defendant's dock. He had spent six years in pre-trial detention, much of it in conditions that UN human rights experts described as "enforced disappearance" in a secret prison. He had been denied adequate medical care for diabetes and liver disease despite documented deterioration. A UN statement called for an independent investigation into what it termed a "cruel, inhuman and degrading treatment" case. The Egyptian government described his death as a heart attack and held no independent investigation. He was buried in a prison cemetery without his family present at the burial.

UN Human Rights Office (2019)

Legacy & Debate

Sisi is a deeply polarising figure for reasons that go beyond ordinary political disagreement. The core contested question is whether what he represents is a necessary corrective to a catastrophic democratic experiment, or whether it is a comprehensive destruction of the political space that Egyptians created in 2011 — one that has left Egypt with higher repression, greater military dominance of the economy, and no path toward political development.

Arguments Made in his Favour
  • Prevented Egypt from experiencing the kind of state collapse that followed the Arab Spring in Libya, Syria, and Yemen
  • Maintained basic security and economic functioning in a country of 105 million people under severe external pressures
  • Built real infrastructure — the New Administrative Capital, Suez Canal expansion, road network — that represents genuine development investment
  • Managed the Sinai insurgency without complete loss of territorial control, unlike what happened to the Libyan and Yemeni states
  • Maintained the Egypt-Israel peace and the Camp David framework, stabilising the most important bilateral relationship in Arab-Israeli relations
Arguments Against his Record
  • The Rabaa massacre — at least 817 killed in a single day — is one of the largest mass killings of protesters in recent history and has never been investigated
  • An estimated 60,000–100,000 political prisoners, including journalists, academics, lawyers, and secular democrats
  • Egypt's repression score on the Political Terror Scale reached levels higher than any point under Mubarak's 30-year rule
  • The military's economic dominance has deepened inequality and produced projects (the New Capital) that serve military-class interests more than ordinary Egyptians
  • Systematic destruction of civil society, independent media, and political opposition has left Egypt with no viable path to peaceful political change
  • The Giulio Regeni murder and the death of Morsi in custody represent a complete absence of accountability even to diplomatic partners
The Genuinely Contested Question
  • Was Morsi's Muslim Brotherhood government actually a threat to Egyptian democracy — or was the coup a military intervention against a government it found politically inconvenient?
  • Comparative cases suggest that electoral Islamist governments can be peacefully alternated in power (Tunisia 2014, Turkey) — but Egypt was also not Tunisia or Turkey
  • The Gulf states' immediate $12 billion pledge to Sisi suggests his coup was partly a regional counterrevolution against the Brotherhood model, not purely an Egyptian security calculation

Their framework of "counterrevolutionary upscaling of repression" is the most precise academic characterisation of what happened in Egypt after 2013. Their data from the Political Terror Scale shows Egypt's repression reaching levels without precedent in the post-WWII period. They document how Egypt became the leading case study in how authoritarian governments learn from, and coordinate with, each other to suppress protest movements — including the Saudi-UAE funding that made Sisi's repression financially viable. Open access.
Neil Ketchley
Oxford · Egypt in a Time of Revolution (2017)
Ketchley's account focuses on the contentious politics of the revolutionary period itself — the strategic choices made by protesters, the military's calculations, and the spatial dynamics of how Tahrir Square functioned as a political arena. He is careful to show how protests evolved and adapted under pressure, and his account of how the military maintained its institutional coherence through both Mubarak's fall and the Morsi period illuminates why Sisi was able to consolidate power so quickly. Available in academic libraries.
