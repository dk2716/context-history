Iraq & Syria · 2004–present

ISIS / The Islamic State

A al-Qaeda splinter group that seized a third of Iraq and Syria, declared itself a caliphate, and built the deadliest jihadist movement in history — before losing every inch of that territory in five years.

Overview

The Islamic State — known variously as ISIS, ISIL, Daesh, or simply the Islamic State — is the most territorially successful jihadist organisation in modern history. At its peak in late 2014 and early 2015, it controlled roughly 88,000 square kilometres of Iraq and Syria, an area larger than the United Kingdom's mainland, ruling over an estimated 8 million people. On June 29, 2014, its leader Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi declared himself caliph — the first time a jihadist group had claimed to formally reconstitute the caliphate, the seventh-century form of Islamic governance abolished by Atatürk in 1924. No other jihadist group, including al-Qaeda, had ever held and governed territory of that scale.

The group's origins trace to Abu Musab al-Zarqawi's al-Qaeda in Iraq, formed in the chaos following the 2003 U.S. invasion. It survived the near-destruction of its leadership in 2006–2010, was rebuilt by Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi from 2010, and exploited the vacuum created by the Syrian civil war to re-emerge in 2013 as a transnational force with resources, veteran fighters, and captured American weapons. Its 2014 blitz through northern Iraq — including the fall of Mosul, Iraq's second city, to a few thousand fighters facing a nominally 30,000-strong garrison — triggered a U.S.-led international coalition, a five-year war, and one of the largest foreign fighter mobilisations in history: an estimated 40,000 people from over 100 countries travelled to join it.

The Islamic State's territorial caliphate was destroyed by March 2019, when U.S.-backed Syrian Democratic Forces overran its last redoubt at Baghouz. Baghdadi was killed in a U.S. raid in northwestern Syria that October. But the organisation did not disappear — it reverted to the insurgency it had always been capable of being, and its provincial affiliates in Afghanistan, West Africa, and the Sahel have in some years surpassed the Iraq-Syria core in lethality. Tens of thousands of its fighters' family members remain held in Syrian camps, an unresolved crisis that scholars and intelligence officials describe as a live incubator for the movement's next generation.

Origins & Causes

The Islamic State cannot be understood apart from the 2003 U.S. invasion of Iraq and the specific policy choices that followed it. The Coalition Provisional Authority's decision to dissolve the Iraqi army and purge Ba'ath Party members from public life — de-Ba'athification — put hundreds of thousands of trained, armed, and suddenly unemployed Sunni men, many with intelligence and military expertise, outside any legitimate political structure. Jordanian jihadist Abu Musab al-Zarqawi arrived in this vacuum, founding Jama'at al-Tawhid wal-Jihad in 2000 and relocating it to Iraq after the invasion. In October 2004 he pledged allegiance to Osama bin Laden, rebranding as al-Qaeda in Iraq (AQI).

Zarqawi's strategy differed sharply from bin Laden's. Where al-Qaeda's leadership prioritised attacking the "far enemy" (the United States and the West), Zarqawi deliberately targeted Iraq's Shia majority — bombing the al-Askari shrine in Samarra in February 2006 was the starkest example — in an explicit bid to trigger a sectarian civil war he believed would radicalise Sunnis and destroy any chance of a stable, Shia-led Iraqi state. It worked, at horrific cost: Iraq descended into a civil war that killed tens of thousands and displaced millions between 2006 and 2008.

AQI was nearly destroyed twice. First by the Anbar Awakening (Sahwa) — Sunni tribal militias, armed and paid by the United States from 2006, that turned against AQI's brutality and helped drive it underground. Second by a sustained U.S.-Iraqi decapitation campaign that killed its top two leaders, Abu Ayyub al-Masri and Abu Omar al-Baghdadi, in a joint raid near Tikrit in April 2010. By that point the group — renamed the Islamic State of Iraq (ISI) in 2006 — controlled no territory and appeared to be a spent force.

Two developments reversed that trajectory. First, the Iraqi government under Prime Minister Nouri al-Maliki systematically marginalised the Sunni Awakening fighters after the U.S. troop drawdown, cutting their pay, refusing to integrate them into the security forces, and pursuing Sunni politicians on terrorism charges — recreating the exact grievances ISI needed to rebuild a support base. Second, the outbreak of the Syrian civil war in 2011 gave the reorganising group, now under Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi, a lawless border region in which to rebuild, recruit, and eventually seize territory and oil infrastructure that funded its transformation into something far more dangerous than an insurgency.

Incubator

Camp Bucca — the U.S. detention facility that networked the future Islamic State

Camp Bucca, the large American-run detention facility in southern Iraq that held tens of thousands of detainees between 2003 and 2009, became an unintended organising space for the group's future leadership. Former Ba'athist military and intelligence officers — secular men with no prior interest in jihadism — were held alongside religious extremists, and the two currents fused. Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi himself was detained there in 2004; numerous future ISIS commanders, including the Iraqi ex-officers who built the group's shadow governing and intelligence apparatus, either met there or passed through its population. Former U.S. commanders later described Bucca as "a pressure cooker for extremism" and, more bluntly, as a jihadist university.

Phases of the Conflict

2004–2006
Al-Qaeda in Iraq: Zarqawi's Sectarian War
▼

Zarqawi's al-Qaeda in Iraq waged a deliberate campaign of anti-Shia violence — mass-casualty car bombings, the beheading of Western hostages broadcast on video, and, in February 2006, the bombing of the al-Askari shrine in Samarra, one of Shia Islam's holiest sites. The strategy was explicit: provoke a civil war that would radicalise Iraq's Sunni population and make coexistence with a Shia-led state impossible. Al-Qaeda's central leadership, including Ayman al-Zawahiri, privately warned Zarqawi that the brutality risked alienating the Muslim public — a tension that foreshadowed the eventual AQI/al-Qaeda split. Zarqawi was killed in a U.S. airstrike near Baqubah on June 7, 2006.

Hashim (2014)Weiss & Hassan (2015)
2006–2010
Islamic State of Iraq: Near-Destruction
▼

Zarqawi's successors rebranded the group as the Islamic State of Iraq (ISI) in October 2006, under the nominal leadership of Abu Omar al-Baghdadi, and continued a bloody insurgency against U.S. forces, the Iraqi government, and Sunni rivals. The Anbar Awakening — Sunni tribes turning against the group's brutal governance and forced marriages — combined with the U.S. troop surge to devastate ISI's networks between 2007 and 2009. The killing of both Abu Omar al-Baghdadi and military commander Abu Ayyub al-Masri in an April 2010 raid near Tikrit appeared to be a fatal blow: by mid-2010, roughly 80 percent of the group's leadership had been killed or captured, and it controlled no territory at all.

Hashim (2014)The Rise of Islamic State (RAND)
2010–2013
Baghdadi's Rebuild and the Move into Syria
▼

Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi, an Iraqi religious scholar who had been detained at Camp Bucca in 2004, took command in May 2010. With Haji Bakr, a former Iraqi intelligence colonel, he rebuilt ISI's structure around former Ba'athist officers who brought military discipline and surveillance tradecraft rather than religious credentials. When the Syrian uprising against Bashar al-Assad turned into civil war in 2011, Baghdadi dispatched operatives to found Jabhat al-Nusra as a Syrian franchise. In April 2013, Baghdadi unilaterally announced a merger of the Iraqi and Syrian operations under the name "Islamic State of Iraq and Syria" (ISIS); al-Nusra's leadership rejected the merger and appealed to al-Qaeda, triggering an open, violent split between ISIS and al-Qaeda that was formalised in February 2014, when al-Qaeda publicly disowned the group.

Hashim (2014)Gerges (2016)
June 2014 – 2015
The Fall of Mosul and the Caliphate Declaration
▼

On June 10, 2014, a few thousand ISIS fighters routed a nominally 30,000-strong Iraqi garrison and seized Mosul, Iraq's second-largest city, in a matter of days — capturing enormous quantities of U.S.-supplied military equipment abandoned by fleeing Iraqi troops. The group swept across northern and western Iraq and eastern Syria over the following weeks. On June 29, 2014, spokesman Abu Muhammad al-Adnani announced the restoration of the caliphate, with Baghdadi as "Caliph Ibrahim" and the group renamed simply the Islamic State. In August 2014, IS fighters carried out a genocidal campaign against the Yazidi religious minority around Sinjar — killing thousands of men and enslaving thousands of women and girls — prompting the first U.S. airstrikes and the formation of a 80-plus-member international coalition against the group.

Hashim (2014)UN Human Rights Council (2016)
2015–2019
Coalition War and Territorial Collapse
▼

From 2015, a U.S.-led coalition combined airstrikes with local ground forces — Iraqi security forces (rebuilt with U.S. and Iranian-backed militia support) and the Kurdish-led Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF) — to methodically retake IS territory. Ramadi fell in December 2015, Fallujah in June 2016. The battle for Mosul (October 2016 – July 2017) was the largest urban battle since World War II, killing thousands of civilians and levelling much of the Old City. Raqqa, the group's de facto capital, fell to the SDF in October 2017 after a four-month siege. The last remnant of Islamic State territory, the village of Baghouz on the Euphrates, fell to the SDF on March 23, 2019, formally ending the territorial caliphate. Baghdadi survived in hiding until October 26, 2019, when he detonated a suicide vest during a U.S. special operations raid on his compound in Barisha, Idlib province.

Turning Points

The timeline above traces what happened. The moments below are the analytical hinges — decisions and structural shifts, some barely visible at the time, that determined whether the group would remain a marginal insurgency or become a transnational catastrophe.

Hinge 1
De-Ba'athification created the manpower the group would later need

The 2003 decision to dissolve Iraq's army and purge Ba'ath Party members produced hundreds of thousands of trained, unemployed, and humiliated men with military and intelligence skills. It was this pool — not religious zealots — that Haji Bakr and other former officers drew on to build ISIS's disciplined command structure a decade later.

Hinge 2
Maliki's post-Awakening betrayal reopened the door

ISI was operationally defeated by 2010. It was the Iraqi government's decision to marginalise, underpay, and prosecute the Sunni tribal fighters who had defeated it that regenerated the group's support base — a self-inflicted wound that historians treat as more decisive than any single military failure.

Hinge 3
The Syrian civil war supplied the territory a rebuilt insurgency needed to become a state

Without the ungoverned space, oil infrastructure, and battle-hardened recruits the Syrian war made available after 2011, Baghdadi's reorganised network would likely have remained a contained Iraqi insurgency rather than a cross-border proto-state.

Hinge 4
Mosul's fall converted a insurgency into a conventional army — and a global brand

The scale and speed of the June 2014 victory, and the captured American weaponry it yielded, transformed the group's credibility overnight. The caliphate declaration three weeks later was only possible because Mosul had already proven the group could hold and administer a major city.

Governing the Caliphate

What distinguished the Islamic State from every prior jihadist group was not just territory but governance. Under the framework designed by Haji Bakr and refined after 2014, IS built a functioning bureaucratic state across the areas it controlled — a fact that unsettled counterterrorism analysts more than the group's violence alone, because it suggested durability rather than mere insurgency.

The Diwan System

The Islamic State organised its territory through diwans — ministries — covering everything from education and health to agriculture, "public services," and a religious police force (the hisbah) that enforced dress codes and prayer attendance. Civil servants, doctors, and teachers who remained in IS-held cities were often required to keep working under the new administration, receiving IS-issued identification and paying IS-collected taxes.

Financing a State

At its peak, the Islamic State was the best-funded terrorist organisation in history, with an estimated annual revenue in the hundreds of millions of dollars drawn from smuggled oil sold on the black market (including, at times, to the Assad regime it was nominally fighting), extortion and taxation of the population under its control, looted antiquities, kidnapping ransoms, and bank robberies — including an estimated $400 million seized from Mosul's central bank branch in 2014.

Hudud, Slavery, and Genocide

The group applied its interpretation of hudud (fixed Quranic punishments) publicly and often broadcast the results as propaganda — amputations, floggings, and executions by beheading or immolation. Its August 2014 campaign against the Yazidi minority at Sinjar, which the UN and multiple national governments have formally recognised as genocide, included the mass killing of men and the systematic enslavement of thousands of Yazidi women and girls, sold and traded within an organised slave market the group openly codified in religious rulings.

Propaganda as Statecraft

The Islamic State's media operation — its Amaq news agency, the glossy multilingual magazine Dabiq (later Rumiyah), and a coordinated social media apparatus — was unprecedented in jihadist history for its production values and reach. It served two audiences simultaneously: recruiting foreign fighters and migrants with images of state-building and utopian brotherhood, and terrorising enemies with graphic violence, most infamously the filmed beheadings of Western hostages in 2014.

Foreign Fighters

The Islamic State's caliphate declaration triggered the largest foreign fighter mobilisation in the history of modern jihadism. Estimates compiled by intelligence agencies and researchers place the total number of foreign fighters who travelled to join IS and allied groups in Syria and Iraq between 2013 and 2018 at approximately 40,000, drawn from more than 100 countries — a scale that dwarfed the foreign fighter flows to Afghanistan in the 1980s or to Iraq during the 2003–2011 insurgency.

The largest contingents came, on a per-capita basis, from Tunisia (an estimated 6,000–7,000 fighters, the largest national contingent of any country), and in absolute Western European terms from France, the United Kingdom, Germany, and Belgium — with Belgium producing the highest number of foreign fighters per capita of any Western European state. Recruitment drew on a combination of factors that researchers studying the phenomenon have found operate differently across contexts: economic marginalisation and discrimination faced by Muslim minorities in Europe; state failure and post-Arab Spring political vacuums in North Africa and the Levant; the pull of an apparently successful "utopian" state-building project amplified through slick social media propaganda; and, for a significant subset, genuine ideological and religious conviction rather than purely socioeconomic grievance.

Unresolved crisis

Al-Hol and the repatriation stalemate

Tens of thousands of people connected to the Islamic State — overwhelmingly women and children — remain held in SDF-run camps in northeastern Syria, principally al-Hol, which UN officials and researchers have repeatedly described as a humanitarian catastrophe and an active radicalisation environment. Most fighters' countries of origin have been reluctant to repatriate their citizens, citing security and legal difficulties in prosecution, leaving the camps in a prolonged legal and humanitarian limbo that researchers studying the group's foreign fighter phenomenon identify as one of its most consequential unresolved legacies.

Why It Matters Today

The Islamic State lost its last territory in 2019 and its founding caliph months later, but treating the group as a closed chapter of history is a mistake intelligence agencies and researchers warn against consistently. The organisation's structure, ideology, and global affiliate network continue to shape conflict across multiple continents.

The Insurgency Never Ended in Iraq and Syria

The Islamic State reverted to the decentralised insurgency it had always retained the capacity to be, conducting periodic attacks in Iraq and, following the December 2024 collapse of the Assad regime, exploiting instability in Syria's transition. Analysts warn that the security vacuum left by Assad's fall and the reduced U.S. troop presence in Syria creates renewed opportunity for the group to regenerate.

  • A January 2022 IS assault on Hasakah prison, freeing hundreds of detained fighters, demonstrated the group's continued capacity for complex operations
  • The U.S. maintains a residual troop presence in Syria specifically to prevent an IS resurgence and to guard detention facilities holding thousands of its fighters
  • The SDF, IS's principal ground opponent, faces its own political uncertainty amid Syria's post-Assad transition, raising questions about who secures the detention camps long-term

Global Affiliates Have Eclipsed the Core

Islamic State Khorasan Province (ISIS-K), based in Afghanistan, has carried out some of the group's deadliest recent attacks worldwide, including the March 2024 assault on Moscow's Crocus City Hall that killed over 140 people and a January 2024 bombing in Kerman, Iran, that killed nearly 100. In West Africa, ISWAP and allied groups have destabilised the Lake Chad Basin for a decade.

  • ISIS-K has claimed responsibility for attacks across Afghanistan, Iran, Russia, and repeatedly targeted the Taliban government it considers insufficiently Islamic
  • ISWAP and Boko Haram-linked factions have displaced millions of people across Nigeria, Chad, Niger, and Cameroon
  • The Pentagon and independent researchers have separately assessed IS affiliate activity in the Sahel and West Africa as growing faster than the group's historical core in Iraq and Syria

The Sahel Has Become the New Epicentre

Islamic State Sahel Province, operating across the tri-border area of Mali, Niger, and Burkina Faso, has expanded rapidly amid the collapse of French counterterrorism operations, a series of military coups, and the entry of Russian Africa Corps (Wagner successor) forces backing juntas whose own human rights record has fuelled further recruitment into jihadist ranks.

  • The Sahel has recorded a rising share of the world's terrorism-related deaths in recent years according to the Global Terrorism Index, driven substantially by IS and al-Qaeda-affiliated groups
  • Military juntas in Mali, Niger, and Burkina Faso have expelled French and UN forces since 2022–2023, removing established counterterrorism infrastructure
  • Russian mercenary deployments backing these juntas have been linked to civilian massacres that researchers say function as recruitment propaganda for IS-affiliated groups

A Template for Counterterrorism Doctrine

The IS campaign reshaped how the U.S. and its allies think about defeating a governing insurgency: the "by, with, and through" model of arming and advising local ground forces (Iraqi forces, the SDF) rather than deploying large conventional armies became the dominant post-2014 U.S. counterterrorism approach, with consequences for how Washington now approaches conflicts from the Sahel to Somalia.

  • The 80-plus member Global Coalition to Defeat ISIS, formed in 2014, remains active and is frequently cited as a model for future multinational counterterrorism coalitions
  • U.S. reliance on the Kurdish-led SDF created a lasting rift with NATO ally Turkey, which considers the SDF's core component, the YPG, a terrorist organisation linked to the PKK
  • Debates over the origins of the 2003 invasion and its role in creating the conditions for ISIS remain central to arguments about the costs of regime-change interventions

Legacy & Debate

Few historical debates are as contested, or as politically charged, as the question of what caused the Islamic State — because the answer implicates the decisions of multiple governments, not just the ideology of the group itself. Historians broadly agree on the sequence of events; they disagree sharply on where to place explanatory weight.

Fawaz A. Gerges
London School of Economics, author of ISIS: A History

Gerges argues that ISIS is best understood as a product of state collapse and civil war — in Iraq after 2003 and in Syria after 2011 — rather than primarily as an ideological phenomenon. He is critical of accounts that treat the group as a purely religious movement, insisting that its rise depended on specific, contingent political failures that a different set of policy choices could plausibly have avoided.

Graeme Wood
Journalist, author of The Way of the Strangers; "What ISIS Really Wants," The Atlantic (2015)

Wood's widely debated argument is that mainstream Western commentary badly underestimated the sincerity of the group's theology, particularly its apocalyptic beliefs about an imminent end-times battle at Dabiq in northern Syria. He contends that treating the group as "un-Islamic" or purely opportunistic, as many Western officials did, obscured a genuine — if minority — theological current that shaped its strategy and appeal.

Ahmed S. Hashim
Military historian, "From al-Qaeda Affiliate to Caliphate," Middle East Policy (2014)

Hashim's analysis, written as the caliphate was being declared, emphasises organisational continuity — tracing a direct institutional line from Zarqawi's AQI through the Islamic State of Iraq to the 2014 caliphate — while stressing that the specific manpower and tradecraft that made 2014 possible came from former Iraqi Ba'athist officers whose radicalisation was a direct consequence of de-Ba'athification, not pre-existing jihadist commitment.

What is not seriously disputed among historians is that multiple, compounding failures were necessary conditions for the Islamic State's rise: the 2003 invasion and de-Ba'athification created the manpower; sectarian Iraqi governance under Maliki regenerated the grievance base after 2010; the Syrian civil war supplied territory and resources; and a genuinely apocalyptic and totalitarian ideology, once in possession of a state's administrative and military capacity, proved able to attract and organise tens of thousands of people from around the world. Reducing the story to any single cause — "the Iraq War caused ISIS," or "ISIS is simply what literalist Islam produces" — is a simplification that the weight of the scholarship does not support.

"The Islamic State is a product of the modern Middle East and its multiple, overlapping state failures — not a throwback to the seventh century, whatever its own propaganda claims."
Fawaz A. Gerges, ISIS: A History (2016)
