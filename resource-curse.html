The Resource Curse

The paradox at the heart of petrostates: countries with vast oil and mineral wealth often end up poorer, more authoritarian, and more prone to civil war than countries with nothing — and economists have spent thirty years arguing about whether the curse is real, why it happens, and how to escape it.

What Is the Resource Curse?

In 1993 the British economist Richard Auty noticed something counterintuitive in the development data: the countries that had discovered large oil and mineral deposits in the 1970s should have been using that windfall to industrialise and develop. Instead, they had stagnated. Countries like Nigeria, Venezuela, and Zambia had grown more slowly than comparable resource-poor countries like South Korea and Taiwan. Auty named this pattern the "resource curse" — the idea that natural resource wealth, far from being an advantage, tends to undermine economic development and political institutions.

The term stuck because it captured something that felt intuitively right to development economists who had watched the 1970s oil booms curdle into the debt crises and authoritarian entrenchment of the 1980s and 1990s. But "the resource curse" is actually a shorthand for several distinct arguments that need to be separated. The economic version — that oil wealth creates distorted, undiversified economies — is sometimes called "Dutch Disease." The political version — that oil revenue allows rulers to buy off citizens and avoid accountability — is the "rentier state" thesis. The conflict version — that valuable resources create incentives for armed groups to capture them — is Paul Collier's "conflict trap." These three arguments are related but not identical, and the evidence for each is different.

What all three share is a single underlying insight: how a country's economy works shapes what kind of politics it produces. In resource-rich states, the dominant economic relationship is between the government (which controls the resource) and foreign oil companies (which extract it) — not between a government and its taxpaying, working citizens. When governments don't need to tax their populations, the theory goes, they don't need their populations' consent. The social contract dissolves, along with accountability.

Key Distinction

The Resource Curse ≠ Being Naturally Wealthy

The "curse" doesn't apply to all natural resources. It tends to be strongest for point-source resources — oil, gas, diamonds, copper — that can be extracted from a small number of locations by a small number of companies and whose revenues flow directly to the state. Diffuse resources like agricultural land, which require millions of people to work and generate income distributed across society, tend not to produce the same political pathologies. Norway has vast oil wealth and is a flourishing democracy. Botswana has diamonds and is relatively well-governed. The curse is probabilistic, not deterministic — it describes a tendency, not a fate.

Origins & Development

The intellectual roots of the resource curse go back to the 1977 observation by Dutch economists about what happened to the Netherlands after the discovery of North Sea natural gas in 1959. As gas revenues flooded in, the Dutch guilder strengthened, making Dutch manufactured exports more expensive and less competitive. Manufacturing declined as energy boomed. Economists named this "Dutch Disease" — the pattern where a commodity boom crowds out other productive sectors of the economy by pushing up the exchange rate and wages, leaving the country more dependent on the commodity when the boom ends.

For two decades Dutch Disease was considered primarily an economic curiosity — a problem of macroeconomic adjustment, not a political or developmental catastrophe. What changed in the 1990s was the availability of cross-country data that allowed economists to run systematic comparisons. Jeffrey Sachs and Andrew Warner's landmark 1995 paper — "Natural Resource Abundance and Economic Growth" — provided the first rigorous statistical demonstration that resource-abundant countries had grown more slowly than resource-poor countries between 1970 and 1990, controlling for other variables. The paper was enormously influential: it transformed the resource curse from an anecdote about the Netherlands into a global empirical claim.

The political dimension was added most influentially by Hossein Mahdavy, who in 1970 coined the term "rentier state" to describe Iran under the Shah — a state whose revenues came from oil rents paid by foreign companies rather than from taxing its own citizens. Hazem Beblawi and Giacomo Luciani later elaborated the theory in the 1980s: in rentier states, governments distribute oil rents as subsidies, patronage, and public employment, creating a population that is dependent on the state rather than the state being dependent on the population. Without taxation, citizens have less reason to organise and demand accountability. The "no taxation, no representation" logic of Western democratic development runs in reverse: no taxation, no representation.

The Four Mechanisms

Dutch Disease — The Economic Mechanism

When a country discovers oil, two things happen to its exchange rate and wages: they rise. This makes everything else the country produces — textiles, manufactured goods, food exports — more expensive for foreign buyers. Sectors that might have been competitive withers. When the oil eventually runs out (or the price drops), the country is left with an economy that has lost the industries it would otherwise have built, no diversified tax base, and a population whose skills and infrastructure are oriented around a single extractive sector that is shrinking. Venezuela's oil curse followed almost exactly this script between 1970 and 2010.

The Rentier State — The Political Mechanism

In a normal state, the government taxes its citizens to fund itself, and citizens (eventually) demand accountability in exchange for those taxes. In a rentier state, the relationship runs backward: oil revenues let the government fund itself from abroad, then distribute that money as subsidies, public jobs, and patronage to citizens who are thereby dependent on the government. The incentive for citizens to organise, pay attention to public finances, or hold rulers accountable is reduced — there's less at stake from the tax side, and less capacity on the civil society side. Michael Ross's systematic analysis in The Oil Curse (2012) showed that oil-rich states are measurably less democratic and more autocratic than otherwise-comparable oil-poor states.

The Conflict Trap — The Violence Mechanism

Paul Collier and Anke Hoeffler's research found that primary commodity exports dramatically increase the probability of civil war — a 1999 paper estimated that a country where primary commodity exports are 26% of GDP has a 23% chance of experiencing civil war in any five-year period, compared with 0.5% for a country with no primary commodity exports. The intuition is that "lootable" resources — oil wells, diamond mines, drug crops that can be taxed or seized — give armed groups something worth fighting for. Sierra Leone's diamond wars, Angola's oil-funded MPLA vs. UNITA conflict, South Sudan's oil-driven civil war: all follow this pattern.

Volatility — The Macroeconomic Mechanism

Commodity prices are more volatile than the prices of manufactured goods. An oil-dependent state's government revenues swing dramatically with global price cycles — flush during booms, suddenly squeezed during busts. This volatility is toxic for long-term investment, infrastructure planning, and institutional development. Governments tend to expand spending during booms (as political pressure to distribute the windfall is intense) and then struggle to cut it during busts (as those who benefited from expanded spending resist cuts). The result is a procyclical fiscal policy that amplifies boom-bust cycles rather than smoothing them, leaving countries more exposed to international commodity price shocks than diversified economies.

Common Misunderstanding

The Curse Isn't About Lack of Money — It's About What the Money Does to Institutions

The resource curse is not simply about corruption or mismanagement of funds. Even when oil revenues are not stolen, they can weaken institutions: by reducing the need for taxation (which drives state capacity-building), by allowing governments to repress or buy off political opposition without needing popular legitimacy, and by creating enclave economies where oil companies operate alongside populations that have little connection to the resource wealth. Norway avoided the curse not primarily because its oil fund is managed honestly, but because Norway had strong pre-existing institutions before oil was discovered — a point that matters enormously for what "solutions" to the resource curse are actually possible.

The Resource Curse in Practice

NIGERIA — 1960s TO PRESENT
Africa's Oil Giant: Wealth Without Development
▼

Nigeria is the textbook resource curse case. At independence in 1960 it had a diversified agricultural economy — palm oil, cocoa, groundnuts, and cotton were its major exports. The oil boom of the 1970s changed everything: oil revenues flooded in and the Nigerian naira appreciated, destroying the competitiveness of agricultural exports in almost exactly the Dutch Disease pattern. By 1980, oil accounted for over 90% of export revenues and 80% of government revenues. Agriculture, which had employed most Nigerians and generated much of the pre-oil GDP, had been crowded out.

The political consequences were equally severe. Oil revenues financed military governments and, after the return to civilian rule in 1999, a system of patronage politics in which control of the federal government — and thus control of oil revenue distribution — was the prize that drove political competition. The Niger Delta, where the oil is actually extracted, has seen some of the worst outcomes: Shell and other companies' operations have caused extensive environmental damage (oil spills, gas flaring), while local communities have seen negligible economic benefits. Armed groups in the Delta — the Movement for the Emancipation of the Niger Delta, among others — have periodically disrupted production through sabotage, embodying both the conflict-trap and the inequality dynamics the resource curse predicts. Nigeria has earned over $1 trillion in oil revenues since the 1970s; its GDP per capita today is lower in real terms than in the 1970s.

VENEZUELA — 1970s TO PRESENT
The Petrostate That Destroyed Itself
▼

Venezuela sits atop the world's largest proven oil reserves (overtaking Saudi Arabia in 2011), yet it has experienced arguably the most spectacular socioeconomic collapse of any country not at war in the twenty-first century. The pattern was set decades before Chávez: the 1970s oil boom produced massive government spending, industrial policy driven by oil rents, and an exchange rate so strong that Venezuelan agriculture and manufacturing became uncompetitive. When oil prices collapsed in the 1980s, Venezuela entered a decade of economic contraction and political crisis that culminated in the near-revolution of the 1989 Caracazo, when price riots killed hundreds.

Hugo Chávez came to power in 1999 on the promise of using oil revenues to directly benefit Venezuela's poor — and he did, through health, education, and food subsidy programmes ("misiones") that reduced poverty dramatically during the 2000s oil boom. But these programmes were financed entirely by oil revenues, not by the diversification that might have built a sustainable economic base. When oil prices collapsed after 2014, the revenue to fund them vanished, and the economy contracted by over 75% between 2013 and 2021 — one of the worst economic contractions in modern history outside of wartime. Over seven million Venezuelans have fled the country since 2015, constituting one of the largest displacement crises in the Western Hemisphere. The resource curse's trajectory — boom, political dysfunction, bust, collapse — could not be more clearly illustrated.

Ross (2012)Sachs & Warner (1995)
NORWAY — 1969 TO PRESENT
The Exception That Proves the Rule
▼

Norway discovered oil in the North Sea in 1969 and is consistently cited as proof that the resource curse can be escaped. The Norwegian state oil company Equinor (formerly Statoil) is majority state-owned. The Government Pension Fund Global — established in 1990 to invest oil revenues for future generations — is the world's largest sovereign wealth fund, currently holding assets of over $1.7 trillion. Norway has maintained its strong democratic institutions, generous welfare state, and diversified economy throughout the oil era. It is, by most measures, one of the best-governed countries in the world.

The problem with using Norway as a template is that the explanation for Norway's success is circular for countries trying to avoid the curse: Norway avoided the resource curse largely because it had strong institutions, social trust, and democratic governance before it discovered oil. The question for countries that discover oil without those pre-existing institutions is: how do you build them in an environment where oil revenues are undermining the conditions under which they would normally develop? Scholars like Michael Ross argue that Norway-style success is possible when oil wealth arrives after democratic consolidation but not before. For countries like Angola or South Sudan, the sequencing was reversed — institutions were weak when oil was discovered, and the revenues made them weaker. Norway isn't so much a model as a lucky timing case.

Ross (2012)Haber & Menaldo (2011)
BOTSWANA — 1966 TO PRESENT
Diamonds Without the Curse — A Contested Success
▼

Botswana is the other frequently cited "exception" to the resource curse: one of the world's poorest countries at independence in 1966, it discovered massive diamond deposits shortly afterward and has since achieved some of the highest sustained economic growth rates in the world. The state diamond company Debswana — a 50/50 joint venture between the government and De Beers — has generated revenues that funded health, education, and infrastructure. Botswana is relatively democratic by regional standards and has avoided the violent conflict that afflicts so many resource-rich African states.

The Botswana case is contested, however. Critics note that Botswana's success has been almost entirely dependent on diamond revenue — the diversification that would normally indicate genuine economic development has not occurred — and that HIV/AIDS, which killed a disproportionate share of the working-age population, leaves the long-term sustainability of the model uncertain. Others point out that Botswana's success depended on decisions made at independence to invest diamond revenues in public goods rather than patronage — decisions that reflected both the particular character of the Tswana political tradition and the choices of individual leaders, neither of which is easily replicable. Botswana is perhaps best understood not as a template for escaping the resource curse but as evidence that the curse is not deterministic — that it can be avoided under specific institutional and political conditions.

Collier (2007)Ross (2012)
IRAQ & LIBYA — 1960s TO PRESENT
Rentier States, Authoritarianism, and the Arab Petrostate
▼

The Arab petrostates are the rentier state thesis's core empirical territory. Both Iraq under Saddam Hussein and Libya under Gaddafi illustrate the political mechanism most clearly: oil revenues funded massive state patronage networks, subsidised food and fuel, and military and security apparatus expansion, while simultaneously allowing rulers to repress political opposition without needing to negotiate with a tax-paying citizenry. In both countries, the political economy that oil created made governments simultaneously less accountable to their populations and more capable of violent repression.

Gaddafi's Libya is particularly instructive. Oil revenues made possible both the Jamahiriya's patronage distribution (free housing, subsidised education, generous public employment) and its foreign adventurism (funding insurgencies across Africa and the Middle East). The complete absence of non-oil economic development meant that when Gaddafi fell in 2011, there were no functioning institutions capable of managing a democratic transition. Libya's post-Gaddafi collapse into civil war followed almost exactly the pattern that resource curse theory predicts: oil revenues had sustained a repressive but economically functional state, and removing the repression without building the non-oil institutions first produced not democracy but chaos. The oil fields then became the prize that drove the civil war's violence, adding the conflict-trap dynamic to the institutional-collapse dynamic.

ANGOLA & SOUTH SUDAN — 1970s TO PRESENT
Oil and Civil War: The Conflict Trap in Action
▼

Angola's civil war (1975–2002) is the archetypal resource-fuelled conflict: the MPLA government controlled the offshore oil revenues that funded its war effort, while UNITA rebel forces controlled diamond revenues in the east. Both sides had self-sustaining financial models for continued warfare, which is one reason the conflict lasted 27 years after independence and killed over 500,000 people. The resources didn't cause the conflict — the underlying political dispute between the MPLA and UNITA predated oil and diamond dependency — but they made it dramatically more durable by giving both sides the means to continue fighting independent of popular support or territorial control.

South Sudan's story is more recent and more tragic: it achieved independence in 2011 with 75% of Sudan's oil revenues but almost no other functioning state institutions, and descended into civil war in 2013 as rival political and ethnic factions fought for control of the oil revenues. Collier's prediction — that countries where primary commodity exports are a high share of GDP face dramatically elevated civil war risk — was borne out almost exactly. South Sudan now ranks consistently among the worst humanitarian situations in the world, its oil revenues having funded militias and government forces rather than the hospitals and schools a country with no civil service, no roads, and no educational infrastructure desperately needed.

Why It Matters Today

The Energy Transition Will Create New Resource Curse Risks

The shift away from fossil fuels and toward electric vehicles and renewable energy is driving massive demand for lithium, cobalt, nickel, copper, and rare earth elements — the materials in batteries, solar panels, and wind turbines. Many of the countries with the largest reserves of these "green minerals" — the Democratic Republic of Congo (cobalt), Chile and Bolivia (lithium), Indonesia (nickel) — are the same countries where resource curse dynamics have historically been most severe. Whether the twenty-first century green energy transition reproduces the oil curse in new materials, or whether improved international governance frameworks can prevent it, is one of the defining development questions of the coming decades.

  • The DRC supplies roughly 70% of global cobalt — extracted partly by child labour in artisanal mines, with revenues flowing disproportionately to armed groups
  • Bolivia, Chile, and Argentina — the "Lithium Triangle" — hold over 50% of known global lithium reserves; all three have experienced political instability partly driven by competition over lithium policy
  • Transparency initiatives like the Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative (EITI) exist but rely on voluntary participation and have limited enforcement

Gulf State Diversification — A Test Case for Escape

Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030, the UAE's economic diversification, and Qatar's investment-based development model represent the most ambitious state-level attempts to escape the resource curse while still holding the oil revenues that would otherwise drive it. These projects are building non-oil industries (tourism, tech, manufacturing), investing oil revenues in global assets, and attempting to build institutions before the oil runs out. Whether they succeed will provide the most important real-world test of whether authoritarian petrostates can execute the institutional transformation the resource curse theory says is necessary.

  • Saudi Arabia: non-oil GDP grew 4.6% in 2023, but the economy remains 40%+ dependent on hydrocarbons and the state on oil revenues
  • The UAE's diversification — Dubai especially — is the most advanced, with tourism, logistics, and finance driving growth; Abu Dhabi remains heavily oil-dependent
  • Qatar's sovereign wealth fund (Qatar Investment Authority) holds approximately $450 billion in global assets — an attempt to convert oil into permanent wealth
  • None of these transitions has yet demonstrated a path to democratic accountability — all are authoritarian systems using oil revenues to delay rather than enable political opening

The Transparency and Revenue Management Debate

A generation of international development policy has been dedicated to the proposition that the resource curse can be cured through transparency, better revenue management, and improved governance frameworks. The Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative, the Natural Resource Charter, IMF technical assistance programmes, and international anti-corruption law all rest on this assumption. The evidence is mixed: transparency has improved in some cases, and a few countries have improved their management of resource revenues. But the structural dynamics of the rentier state — that governments with non-tax revenues have less incentive to be accountable — are not easily resolved by disclosure requirements.

  • The EITI now has 57 implementing countries; academic evidence on its effectiveness is mixed — it improves information but doesn't necessarily change political incentives
  • Chad's oil pipeline deal with the World Bank in 2003 — a pioneering revenue management experiment — collapsed by 2006 when the government diverted oil revenues away from agreed poverty reduction spending
  • The UK Unexplained Wealth Orders and the US Foreign Corrupt Practices Act target the supply side (corrupt payments) but have limited ability to address the demand side (resource rents creating incentives for corruption)

Geopolitical Competition Over Resource-Rich Failed States

Libya, South Sudan, the Sahel's mineral belt, and the DRC's mining regions are all simultaneously suffering from resource curse dynamics and serving as the terrain for great-power competition — between the US, China, France, Russia, and Gulf states — that is partly driven by resource interests. China's resource-for-infrastructure deals across Africa, Russia's Wagner Group presence in gold- and uranium-producing Sahel states, and French reluctance to lose access to uranium in Niger: these are all expressions of how the resource curse is not merely a domestic problem for resource-rich states but a structural driver of international competition and intervention.

  • China holds over $150 billion in mineral extraction deals across Africa, secured primarily through infrastructure financing rather than cash payments — a new form of resource extraction that bypasses some traditional corruption channels while creating others
  • Russia's Wagner Group (now Africa Corps) has been paid partly in mining concessions in Mali, the Central African Republic, and Sudan
  • Niger's 2023 military coup was partly driven by grievances over uranium revenue distribution — France extracted uranium from Niger for decades at below-market prices under Areva contracts

Legacy & Debate

The resource curse became one of the dominant frameworks in development economics and political science during the 1990s and 2000s, generating hundreds of papers, dozens of books, and substantial policy influence. Then, in the 2010s, a serious empirical challenge emerged. Stephen Haber and Victor Menaldo's 2011 paper — "Do Natural Resources Fuel Authoritarianism?" — argued that the statistical relationship between resource wealth and authoritarianism was an artefact of the data and methods used. When you look at countries' trajectories over time rather than across countries at a single moment, they argued, you find that oil wealth doesn't make countries more authoritarian — countries that were already authoritarian tended to discover oil, and countries that were already democratic tended to stay democratic regardless of what happened to their oil reserves.

This methodological debate — cross-sectional correlation vs. within-country longitudinal analysis — is technical, but the stakes are high. If Haber and Menaldo are right, the resource curse is largely a statistical illusion generated by the fact that poor, badly-governed countries were more likely to be explored for oil in the 1960s and 1970s. If Ross, Sachs, Warner, and Collier are right, the curse is real and the policy implications (sovereign wealth funds, transparency requirements, international governance frameworks) are urgent.

Jeffrey Sachs & Andrew Warner
Harvard / "Natural Resource Abundance and Economic Growth" (1995)
The foundational statistical paper establishing the resource curse as an empirical fact. Sachs and Warner showed that between 1970 and 1990, countries with high ratios of natural resource exports to GDP grew more slowly than resource-poor countries, controlling for initial income, investment rates, trade policy, and other variables. Their results were robust across multiple specifications and initiated the modern literature on the resource curse. The paper has been cited thousands of times and remains the empirical starting point for the debate. Freely available at NBER (nber.org/papers/w5398).
Stephen Haber & Victor Menaldo
Stanford / "Do Natural Resources Fuel Authoritarianism?" (2011, APSR)
The most important challenge to the resource curse thesis. Haber and Menaldo re-ran the analysis using within-country time-series variation rather than cross-country comparisons and found no evidence that increases in resource dependence caused declines in democracy. Their argument: the cross-sectional correlation between oil and authoritarianism reflects historical sorting (authoritarian states happened to get oil wealth) rather than causation. The paper generated extensive responses from Ross and others and remains the methodological crux of the debate. Widely available through JSTOR and academic libraries.
Michael L. Ross
UCLA / The Oil Curse: How Petroleum Wealth Shapes the Development of Nations (2012, Princeton)
The most comprehensive book-length treatment of the resource curse, specifically focused on oil. Ross responds directly to Haber and Menaldo's challenge, defending the causal argument with improved data and methods. He distinguishes between different types of oil wealth (onshore vs. offshore, nationalised vs. foreign-owned) and shows that oil's effects on democracy are conditional on institutional context. His most controversial finding: oil wealth specifically disadvantages women's political and economic empowerment by suppressing labour market participation. Essential reading; widely available.
Paul Collier
Oxford / The Bottom Billion (2007) and Plundered Planet (2010)
Collier's "conflict trap" argument focuses on the relationship between primary commodity dependence and civil war risk, rather than authoritarianism. His empirical work (with Anke Hoeffler) established that countries where primary commodity exports are a large share of GDP have dramatically elevated civil war risks — and that this effect works through the "feasibility" of rebellion (valuable resources give armed groups something to fight for and a way to finance the fight) rather than grievance. His policy prescriptions — international commodity revenue certification, anti-money-laundering laws targeting stolen resource revenues — are more market-friendly than other resource curse scholars. Widely available.

The Central Contested Question: Does Oil Cause Authoritarianism, or Do Authoritarian States Get Oil?

The methodological debate between Ross and Haber/Menaldo reduces to a single question: is the observed correlation between oil wealth and authoritarianism a causal relationship (oil makes countries less democratic) or a selection effect (countries that were already badly governed happened to have oil deposits discovered)? This matters enormously for policy: if the curse is causal, the case for sovereign wealth funds, transparency requirements, and international governance frameworks is strong. If it's a selection effect, those interventions are treating a symptom that won't respond to the treatment.

The current state of the scholarly debate, as of the mid-2020s, is that most specialists believe some version of the resource curse is real — particularly the political-economy version — but that its effects are conditional on prior institutional context. Oil doesn't automatically make countries autocratic, but it does give autocratic governments tools and incentives that make them harder to dislodge. Ross's conditional argument — that the curse operates through specific mechanisms that can in principle be interrupted — has more policy traction than either Sachs and Warner's unconditional curse claim or Haber and Menaldo's no-curse claim.

The resource curse is a real causal relationship
  • Cross-country statistical evidence consistently shows resource-rich countries growing more slowly and being less democratic — Sachs & Warner (1995), Ross (2012)
  • The mechanisms are theoretically coherent: Dutch Disease, rentier state dynamics, and conflict-trap incentives are all independently plausible — Collier (2007), Mahdavy (1970)
  • Case studies like Nigeria, Venezuela, and Libya fit the predicted trajectory extremely closely — Ross (2012)
  • The oil-and-women finding — that oil suppresses women's political and economic participation — holds up across multiple specifications — Ross (2008, 2012)
The statistical evidence is weaker than claimed
  • Within-country time-series analysis shows no consistent evidence that oil revenues cause democratic decline — Haber & Menaldo (2011)
  • Countries like Norway, Botswana, and the UAE complicate any deterministic version of the curse — multiple scholars
  • The Sachs-Warner measure (primary commodity exports as share of GDP) conflates different types of resource wealth with very different effects — Brunnschweiler & Bulte (2008)
  • Post-2000 evidence suggests some resource-rich countries have improved governance — the "curse" may be weakening as international transparency standards improve — Haber & Menaldo (2011)
"Oil wealth tends to make authoritarian governments more durable and democratic governments more fragile... Yet oil is not destiny. Countries can escape the oil curse through good policies and good luck — but mostly through the difficult work of building institutions that are capable of managing resource revenues in the public interest."
— Michael L. Ross, The Oil Curse: How Petroleum Wealth Shapes the Development of Nations (2012), Princeton University Press — synthesising three decades of research into a conditional but real relationship between oil wealth and political dysfunction.
