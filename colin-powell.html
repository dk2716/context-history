Colin Powell

The son of Jamaican immigrants who became America's first Black National Security Advisor, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs, and Secretary of State — and who delivered a 76-minute UN speech in February 2003 that he would spend the rest of his life calling "a blot on my record."

Origins & Rise

Colin Powell was born in Harlem to Jamaican parents who had come to New York in the wave of Caribbean immigration of the 1920s and 1930s. His father Luther worked in the garment district; his mother Maud as a seamstress. They settled in the South Bronx, a working-class neighbourhood that Powell later described as ethnically mixed — Jews, Poles, Italians, Puerto Ricans, blacks — and deeply community-oriented. He was, by his own account, an indifferent student with no clear direction, until he discovered the Reserve Officers' Training Corps at City College of New York. The military gave him what school had not: structure, purpose, and a clear pathway to achievement.

He commissioned as a second lieutenant in the US Army in 1958 and was sent to West Germany, then to Vietnam — twice. His first tour, in 1962–63, was as a military adviser to South Vietnamese forces in the A Shau Valley, where he was wounded stepping on a punji stick. His second tour, in 1968–69, was as a major serving as an assistant to the commanding general of the Americal Division — the same division responsible for the My Lai massacre of March 1968. Powell was tasked with investigating a soldier's letter alleging atrocities. His report, which found no evidence of wrongdoing, was later found to have been inadequate and to have failed to follow the evidence. Powell acknowledged in his memoir that he was not proud of this episode.

After Vietnam, Powell moved through a series of staff and policy positions that brought him steadily closer to the political centre of American power. He served as a White House Fellow under Nixon, as a senior aide in the Carter administration's Defence Department, and increasingly in roles that put him at the interface between military planning and political decision-making. Under Reagan, he served as Military Assistant to the Deputy Secretary of Defense and then as National Security Advisor — the first Black official to hold that role. It was in this position, from 1987 to 1989, that he managed the later stages of the Iran-Contra affair and built the bipartisan relationships that would define his reputation as a trusted, non-partisan military professional.

The Powell Doctrine

Powell's most durable intellectual contribution emerged from his experience in Vietnam and his reading of why the US had failed there. A military man who had seen what happened when political leaders committed forces to an open-ended conflict with vague objectives and insufficient strength, he developed a set of principles for the use of American military power that became known as the Powell Doctrine. It was not, strictly speaking, a doctrine he ever formally articulated — it was assembled from his writings, speeches, and the practice of the 1991 Gulf War into a coherent framework.

Overwhelming Force

Military action should only be taken with enough force to achieve the objective quickly and decisively. Gradual escalation, as in Vietnam, allows adversaries to adapt and creates endless entanglement. If you go in, go in with everything you need to win fast.

Clear Political Objective

Before committing forces, political leaders must define precisely what success looks like — and what the military is being asked to achieve. Vague objectives like "restoring stability" guarantee open-ended commitments. Powell regarded the 1993 Somalia intervention, which expanded without clear objectives, as a cautionary tale.

Exit Strategy

There must be a defined endpoint. How will you know when the mission is accomplished? How will you withdraw? Without an exit strategy, military commitments expand to fill political vacuums indefinitely. The Gulf War ended when the specific objective — expelling Iraq from Kuwait — was achieved.

Last Resort

Military force should be used only when all other options — diplomacy, economic pressure, international coalitions — have been genuinely exhausted. Powell was famously the voice for exhausting diplomacy first, which put him in structural tension with Cheney and the neoconservative officials who dominated the Bush White House's foreign policy.

The Gulf War of 1991, which Powell helped plan and execute as Chairman of the Joint Chiefs, is the paradigmatic example of the Doctrine working. George H.W. Bush set a clear objective (expel Iraqi forces from Kuwait), assembled an overwhelming coalition, authorised decisive military action with 500,000 troops, and then stopped — controversially to some — when that objective was achieved, rather than marching on Baghdad. Powell supported the decision to stop. He would later watch his successor's war in Iraq — the one that did march on Baghdad — as a partial refutation of his own framework, and as a demonstration of everything he had warned against.

"Once you've got Baghdad, it's not clear what you do with it. It's not clear what kind of government you would put in place of the one that's currently there now. Is it going to be a Shia regime, a Sunni regime or a Kurdish regime? Or one that tilts toward the Baathists, or one that tilts toward the Islamic fundamentalists? How much credibility is that government going to have if it's set up by the United States military when it's there?"
— Colin Powell, 1994 interview, explaining why the Gulf War did not march on Baghdad

Secretary of State: The Reluctant Warrior

When George W. Bush appointed Powell Secretary of State in January 2001, many observers expected him to be the dominant foreign policy voice in the administration. He was the most prominent Republican on the international stage, the most experienced military figure in the Cabinet, and broadly popular in the country. Instead, he found himself consistently outmanoeuvred by Dick Cheney and the network of neoconservative officials — Paul Wolfowitz, Douglas Feith, Scooter Libby — who had the Vice President's ear and who had spent years arguing for the removal of Saddam Hussein by force.

The internal debates of 2001 and 2002 are well documented. Powell argued for engaging the UN, for building a broad international coalition before any military action, for allowing the weapons inspectors to complete their work. Cheney argued that going to the UN was a waste of time and that inspections would not work. Bush ultimately sided with a hybrid approach: go to the UN, but treat it as a step toward war rather than an alternative to it. Powell was sent to New York to deliver the resolution.

A critical warning Powell gave Bush — reportedly in a private dinner meeting in August 2002 — became known as the "Pottery Barn Rule." Powell told Bush: "If you break it, you own it." Meaning: if the United States invaded Iraq and shattered the existing state structure, it would be responsible for everything that followed — the reconstruction, the governance, the security, the enormous human cost. The warning was prescient. It was not heeded. The post-invasion planning, led by the Pentagon and the Coalition Provisional Authority rather than the State Department, proceeded without the kind of serious civilian reconstruction planning that Powell's warning implied was necessary.

Internal Divisions

State vs. Pentagon

The Bush administration's foreign policy was marked by an unusual degree of open conflict between State (Powell) and Defense (Rumsfeld/Cheney). State Department officials were largely excluded from post-war planning for Iraq. Powell's Future of Iraq Project — which had gathered extensive analysis from Iraqi experts on post-invasion governance — was sidelined by the Pentagon. The results of that sidelining became visible within weeks of the fall of Baghdad.

The UN Speech: February 5, 2003

By late 2002, Powell had concluded — reluctantly, and based on the intelligence he was shown — that Saddam Hussein did possess weapons of mass destruction and was actively concealing them from UN inspectors. He was given the task of presenting the case to the UN Security Council in a speech that would make the public argument for the intelligence the administration was using to justify war. CIA Director George Tenet sat directly behind him at the Security Council table — a visual signal that the intelligence community stood behind every claim Powell was about to make.

The presentation on February 5, 2003 lasted 76 minutes. It included audio recordings of intercepted Iraqi communications (which Powell claimed showed Iraqi officers ordering the concealment of weapons), satellite imagery of what were described as chemical weapons production facilities, and assertions about mobile biological weapons laboratories. Powell stated: "Every statement I make today is backed up by sources, solid sources. These are not assertions. What we're giving you are facts and conclusions based on solid intelligence." He argued that Iraq posed an "immediate and existing threat" and that Saddam had "what is assessed to be mobile biological weapons production" capacity.

Almost none of the specific claims held up. The audio intercepts were ambiguous. The satellite images showed facilities that could have had multiple uses. The mobile biological weapons laboratories — the most dramatic element of the presentation — were described by post-invasion investigators as food-testing trucks. The intelligence Powell presented had been assembled under intense pressure from the Office of the Vice President, which had pushed the CIA to find stronger links between Saddam and al-Qaeda and to harden assessments about WMD capacity. Powell himself spent four days at CIA headquarters in the run-up to the speech scrubbing claims he found unconvincing — but the underlying intelligence assessments he was working from were themselves flawed. AJIL (2004)

When the Iraq Survey Group reported in September 2004 that Saddam had abandoned his WMD programmes in 1991 and had not resumed them, Powell gave a television interview in which he said the speech was "a blot on my record" and that it was "painful" to him. He was the senior US official who most clearly and publicly acknowledged the error.

Powell's presentation was watched live by an estimated 200 million people worldwide. It was designed to replicate Adlai Stevenson's famous 1962 moment during the Cuban Missile Crisis, when the US ambassador confronted the Soviet Union with U-2 photographs of missile installations. But where Stevenson's evidence was real and verified, Powell's was, in crucial respects, fabricated or misrepresented.

The claim about mobile biological weapons factories — described by Powell as "one of the most worrisome things that emerges from the thick intelligence file" — was based in large part on the testimony of a single Iraqi defector, codenamed "Curveball," whose information the German intelligence service had told US counterparts was likely fabricated. The Defense Intelligence Agency had flagged Curveball as unreliable before the speech. Powell was not told this, or, accounts differ, was told but proceeded anyway.

After the Iraq Survey Group's definitive report that no WMDs existed, Powell sat down with NBC's Tim Russert and stated: "I'm the one who presented it on behalf of the United States to the world, and it will always be a part of my record. It was painful. It's painful now." He acknowledged that the information he had been given was wrong and that he had presented it as fact.

Powell resigned as Secretary of State in November 2004, shortly after Bush won re-election. He was replaced by Condoleezza Rice. His departure was widely interpreted as the triumph of the Cheney-Rumsfeld axis over the more cautious approach Powell had represented — though Powell's caution had not, in the end, prevented the war.

Abu Ghraib & the Interrogation Debates

The Abu Ghraib scandal — photographs of Iraqi detainees being abused, sexually humiliated, and tortured by members of the 372nd Military Police Company, published in April 2004 — produced a political and legal crisis that cut across the entire Bush administration. The abuse had occurred between October and December 2003. An internal army investigation ordered by General Richard Sanchez in January 2004, led by General Antonio Taguba, found "systemic and illegal abuse" and concluded that military intelligence officers and CIA personnel had "actively requested" that the MP guards help set "physical and mental conditions for favorable interrogation." AJIL (2004)

Powell's relationship to Abu Ghraib was complicated. The State Department, which he led, was not directly involved in detention policy or interrogation; those were handled by the Department of Defense under Rumsfeld and ultimately overseen by Cheney's office. But the broader policy framework that created Abu Ghraib — the decision to apply "enhanced interrogation" techniques beyond the Army Field Manual, the legal arguments that the Geneva Conventions did not apply to "unlawful combatants" — had been contested internally, and Powell had been among the most prominent internal dissenters. AJIL (2004)

In January 2002, Powell had written a memorandum to White House Counsel Alberto Gonzales arguing strongly that the US should apply the Geneva Conventions to all detainees in Afghanistan. His argument was both legal and pragmatic: violating Geneva would "reverse over a century of U.S. policy and practice in supporting the Geneva Conventions" and could expose US military personnel to legal jeopardy in future conflicts. His memo was rejected. The administration's position — developed in a series of legal opinions by John Yoo and Jay Bybee at the Office of Legal Counsel — was that the Conventions did not apply to Taliban or al-Qaeda detainees, and that the President's authority as Commander-in-Chief could override statutory prohibitions on torture. AJIL (2004)

Key Finding

The April 2003 DoD Working Group Report

On April 14, 2003, a DoD working group delivered a report to Secretary Rumsfeld on interrogation techniques for use at Guantanamo. The report concluded that while the Convention Against Torture applied, narrow exceptions existed; it also asserted that the President's Commander-in-Chief authority could override criminal statutes prohibiting torture. Rumsfeld approved 24 interrogation techniques on April 16, 2003. The Taguba Report found that these authorizations, which extended beyond what the Army Field Manual permitted, contributed to the conditions that produced Abu Ghraib. Powell's State Department was not part of this process. AJIL (2004)

After leaving government, Powell became a critic of the interrogation policies he had been unable to stop from within. He opposed Bush's 2006 Military Commissions Act, which sought to redefine the Geneva Conventions' "cruel treatment" standard. In a public letter, he wrote that the world "is beginning to doubt the moral basis of our fight against terrorism." In 2008, he endorsed Barack Obama for president — a break with the Republican Party that marked the final rupture between his views and the direction the party had taken under Bush and Cheney.

Legacy & Debate

Colin Powell's legacy is defined by two competing images. One is the decorated soldier and statesman who broke racial barriers, built the Gulf War coalition with disciplined professionalism, and repeatedly counselled caution against the wars he feared would end badly. The other is the man at the UN Security Council podium, presenting intelligence he did not fully believe as established fact, lending his enormous credibility to a case for war that he knew was contested. Both images are accurate. The question is how to weigh them.

Achievements
  • First Black National Security Advisor, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs, and Secretary of State — breaking significant barriers in each role
  • Gulf War (1991): helped design and execute one of the most professionally managed US military operations in history
  • Powell Doctrine: remains an influential framework for thinking about when and how to use military force
  • Opposed applying "enhanced interrogation" to detainees — warned it violated Geneva Conventions and would damage US standing
  • Publicly acknowledged the UN speech was wrong — one of the few senior Bush officials to do so clearly
  • Endorsed Obama in 2008, citing concerns about Republican foreign policy direction — an act of political courage
Failures & Criticisms
  • Delivered UN speech based on flawed intelligence presented as certainty — despite personal doubts about some of its content
  • Failed to stop the Iraq War despite being the Cabinet's most credible military voice against it
  • Did not resign over Iraq — stayed in administration after the war began, lending continued credibility to Bush's foreign policy
  • Vietnam: 1969 investigation report into Americal Division atrocities found "no wrongdoing" — later found to be inadequate
  • His "Pottery Barn Rule" warning was ignored; critics argue he should have insisted harder or resigned when it was

Powell was consistently the voice of caution in a Cabinet that had decided on war. He was deceived by the intelligence he was given and by colleagues who withheld exculpatory information. His decision to stay in the administration rather than resign reflects a military culture in which loyalty and institutional service outweigh personal disagreement.

Wilkerson, who helped prepare the UN speech, became a sharp public critic of both the Bush administration's foreign policy and of Powell's failure to resign. "I participated in a hoax on the American people, the international community and the United Nations Security Council," Wilkerson said in 2005. He believed Powell should have spoken out earlier and louder.
