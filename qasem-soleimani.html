Qasem Soleimani

قاسم سلیمانی

The peasant's son who became commander of Iran's Quds Force, built a regional network of Shia militias spanning four countries, and was killed by a U.S. drone strike that Washington called self-defense and much of the world called an assassination.

Origins & Rise

Qasem Soleimani was born in 1957 in the mountainous, impoverished Rabor district of Kerman province in southeastern Iran, to a family of farmers. As a teenager he travelled to the city of Kerman to work construction jobs and pay off an agricultural debt his father owed the state — an origin story far removed from the clerical and mercantile backgrounds of many of Iran's post-revolutionary elite, and one that Iranian state media would later mythologise as evidence of his authenticity as a man of the people. He found work with the Kerman water department, a job he held when the 1979 Islamic Revolution overthrew the Shah.

At 22, Soleimani joined the newly formed Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC), the parallel military force built to protect the revolution and its clerical leadership. He received only a few weeks of formal training but was quickly identified as a capable officer and set to work training recruits. The formative experience of his early career was the Iran-Iraq War (1980–1988), in which he served as a field commander noted for reconnaissance skill, a willingness to take personal risks, and close bonds with the soldiers under his command — qualities that built the personal loyalty networks he would rely on for the rest of his career.

In 1998 Soleimani was appointed commander of the Quds Force, the IRGC's expeditionary and clandestine-operations wing, tasked with extending Iranian influence beyond its borders — primarily by cultivating and arming Shia militias and allied movements across the Middle East. He held the post for more than two decades, transforming a relatively obscure unit into what the IISS has called Iran's "main instrument of influence in the region," compensating for the conventional weakness of Iran's military with a patchwork of loyal, ideologically aligned proxy forces.

Building the Axis of Resistance

Soleimani's defining achievement was organisational: he built, personally and deliberately, the hierarchical regional network that came to be known as the "axis of resistance" — a coalition of Iran-aligned state and non-state actors spanning Lebanese Hizbullah, Iraqi Shia militias organised under the Popular Mobilisation Units (PMU), Yemen's Houthi movement, Palestinian Hamas and Islamic Jihad, and the Assad government in Syria. According to Chatham House's 2025 study of the network, Soleimani "established a hierarchical regional network with himself and the Quds Force at the apex," directing the axis's politics, operations and logistics through field commanders who served as his personal deputies across each country.

Personal Relationships as Strategy

Soleimani maintained close personal ties to the region's senior Shia commanders — Hassan Nasrallah of Hizbullah and Abu Mahdi al-Muhandis of Iraq's Kataib Hizbullah among them — using those relationships to shape government formation and navigate crises in both Lebanon and Iraq. This was not simply patronage: it was a command structure built on trust and shared ideology rather than formal chains of authority, which made the network both resilient to formal sanctions and, later, vulnerable to the loss of the one man who held it together.

From Shadow Operative to Public Statesman

Until roughly 2010, most of Soleimani's work happened out of public view. He then reinvented himself as a visible statesman and diplomat — negotiating the terms of Iran's 2015 wartime partnership with Russia directly with President Vladimir Putin, and cultivating an Instagram following built on photographs of himself alongside militia fighters in Iraq and Syria. The publicity made him one of the most popular figures in Iran even as it made him one of the most reviled in the Gulf Arab states, where he was blamed for sectarian violence, threats against Gulf governments, and an alleged role in the 2005 assassination of former Lebanese prime minister Rafik Hariri.

Securing Assad in Syria

When the Syrian uprising turned into civil war after 2011, Soleimani organised Bashar al-Assad's defence against domestic insurgents, coordinating Shia militias — including Hizbullah fighters redirected from Lebanon — into a unified pro-Assad force. His 2015 trip to Moscow, where he presented Russian officials with the case for direct military intervention, is widely credited with helping secure the Russian air campaign that reversed the war's momentum in Assad's favour.

Top-Down by Design

Unlike a loose alliance of convenience, the axis under Soleimani operated as what Chatham House researchers describe as a top-down organisation, with constituent members taking direction from Tehran through Soleimani personally. This gave Iran unusual coordination and reach across multiple theatres simultaneously — but it also concentrated an enormous amount of institutional knowledge and personal authority in a single, targetable individual.

Key Campaigns

2003–2011
Arming the Iraqi Insurgency Against U.S. Forces
▼

Following the 2003 U.S. invasion of Iraq, Soleimani directed the Quds Force's support for the Shia insurgency against the American occupation, most notoriously by funnelling explosively formed penetrators (EFPs) — armour-piercing roadside bombs — to allied militias through Iraq. The United States held him personally responsible for killing hundreds and wounding thousands of American soldiers as a result. Discussion of targeting Soleimani directly reportedly arose during both the George W. Bush and Barack Obama administrations but was dismissed each time as strategically counterproductive.

2006
Guiding Hizbullah's War With Israel
▼

Soleimani was instrumental in arming Lebanese Hizbullah in the years before its 2006 war with Israel, and reportedly guided aspects of the group's operations during the conflict itself. The war cemented Hizbullah's role as the most militarily capable member of Iran's regional network and deepened Soleimani's personal relationship with Hassan Nasrallah, a partnership that would remain central to the axis of resistance for the rest of his life.

IISS Strategic Comments (2020)
2014–2017
The Campaign Against ISIS
▼

When the Islamic State seized Mosul in June 2014, Soleimani arrived in Baghdad within hours to coordinate Iraq's Shia militia response, and spent the following three years overseeing IRGC-backed operations to retake territory from the group — including the battles for Tikrit, Fallujah and, in coordination with Iraqi security forces, the broader anti-ISIS campaign across Anbar province. His visible, often photographed presence at these front lines — visiting Kurdish Peshmerga commanders, Iraqi Shia clerics and militia leaders alike — became central to his domestic popularity in Iran and his public image as a hands-on battlefield commander rather than a distant intelligence official.

IISS Strategic Comments (2020)
2019
Escalation After the U.S. Withdrawal From the Nuclear Deal
▼

After the Trump administration withdrew from the 2015 nuclear agreement in May 2018 and imposed a "maximum pressure" sanctions campaign, Soleimani played a central role in shaping Iran's calibrated retaliation: Iranian-backed militias in Iraq harassed U.S. installations, Iran shot down a U.S. surveillance drone over the Persian Gulf in June 2019, and Iranian cruise missiles and drones struck Saudi Aramco oil facilities in September 2019. On December 27, 2019, a rocket attack blamed on the Iran-aligned militia Kataib Hizbullah killed a U.S. contractor near Kirkuk, triggering U.S. airstrikes on militia bases and, in turn, a mass protest at the U.S. embassy in Baghdad — the immediate backdrop against which the decision to kill Soleimani was made.

Instruments of Influence

Ruthlessness and Adaptability

Soleimani's reputation combined ideological conviction with tactical pragmatism. He was, in the IISS's description, "a skilled strategist animated by ideological zeal" who achieved "extraordinary standing at home and abroad" through a combination of personal popularity, Shia religious commitment, and a consistent record of tactical success. That record made him a trusted adviser to Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei — achieving, according to the same account, greater practical influence than many officials who formally outranked him within Iran's governing hierarchy.

Plausible Deniability Through Proxies

The Quds Force model Soleimani built and refined relied on operating through local partners rather than direct Iranian action — a structure that let Tehran exercise power across Iraq, Syria, Lebanon, Yemen and the Palestinian territories while retaining the ability to deny direct responsibility for any specific attack. Iran's ballistic missile programme was reserved for the rare moments when this indirection was set aside: researcher Mohammad Eslami's study of Iran's missile doctrine notes that in the four decades since the Iran-Iraq War, Iran directly employed its ballistic missile arsenal only four times — including its January 2020 strike on U.S. forces at Ain al-Asad and Al-Taji airbases in Iraq, launched in retaliation for Soleimani's killing, which Ayatollah Khamenei described publicly as merely "a slap" rather than the full revenge Iran had promised.

Vilification and Popularity, Simultaneously

Few figures in the modern Middle East attracted such starkly divided reputations. In Iran, Soleimani was a national hero whose funeral drew what state media described as millions of mourners. Across much of the Sunni Arab world and the Gulf states, he was viewed as an architect of sectarian violence and a direct threat to regional governments, blamed for interventions that fuelled ethnic cleansing and for frequent public threats against Gulf capitals. Both reputations were, in essence, accurate reflections of the same activity viewed from opposing sides of a regional sectarian and geopolitical divide.

The Killing & Succession

January 3, 2020
The Baghdad Airstrike
▼

In the early hours of January 3, 2020, a U.S. MQ-9 Reaper drone fired on a two-car convoy departing Baghdad International Airport, killing Soleimani, PMU deputy commander Abu Mahdi al-Muhandis, and eight others. President Trump claimed responsibility the same day, and administration officials initially argued the strike was defensive, citing unspecified intelligence that Soleimani was "actively developing plans to attack American diplomats and service members." Within two weeks, that justification had substantially unravelled: Secretary of Defense Mark Esper publicly acknowledged seeing no evidence of the specific embassy plots the administration had cited, and no supporting intelligence was formally transmitted to the UN Security Council to substantiate the Article 51 self-defense claim the U.S. relied on.

January 8, 2020
Iran's Retaliation at Ain al-Asad
▼

Five days after the strike, Iran fired between 10 and 20 conventionally armed ballistic missiles at Iraq's Ayn al-Asad airbase, housing U.S. forces engaged in anti-ISIS operations. The attack damaged airbase infrastructure and gave scores of U.S. soldiers traumatic brain injuries, but killed no one — a calibrated response widely read as satisfying domestic Iranian demand for retaliation while stopping short of triggering a full-scale war. Iran's foreign ministry characterised the strike as an act of self-defense and declared the round of reprisals over, even as IRGC commanders and Khamenei himself warned it was only the opening move in a longer campaign.

2020–present
Qaani's Succession and the Axis's Restructuring
▼

Soleimani's successor, Brigadier-General Esmail Qaani, a Quds Force deputy since 1997 who had built the Afghan Shia militia Liwa Fatimiyoun, inherited an organisation his predecessor's death had exposed as dangerously personalised. According to Chatham House's 2025 research, Qaani "struggled to replicate Soleimani's charismatic leadership and deep-rooted personal connections with axis leaders," and responded by deliberately restructuring the network — shifting it from Soleimani's top-down, Tehran-directed model toward one granting constituent groups significantly greater autonomy, with Hassan Nasrallah's Hizbullah emerging as an increasingly important broker in its own right, even providing strategic guidance to Qaani himself. Iran's post-2023 reliance on this more decentralised network — after Hezbollah's own leadership losses and the fall of the Assad regime — is, per Chatham House, direct evidence that the restructuring Soleimani's death forced has made the axis more resilient to the loss of any single leader, even as it has made it harder for Tehran to direct with the precision Soleimani once provided.

Why It Matters Today

Soleimani has been dead for more than six years, but the network he built and the precedent his killing set both remain central to how the Middle East functions. Understanding him is inseparable from understanding both the ongoing Iran-Israel confrontation and the unresolved legal debate over how far states may go in targeting individuals outside declared wars.

The Legal Precedent of Targeted Killing

The strike on Soleimani remains one of the most-cited case studies in international law on the limits of self-defense doctrine. Legal analysis of the killing has concluded it failed to meet the "imminent threat" standard required to justify preemptive self-defense under the UN Charter, violated Iraqi sovereignty under Article 2(4), and breached international humanitarian law's requirements of distinction and proportionality. The UN Special Rapporteur on extrajudicial executions reached a similar conclusion in a formal 2020 report.

  • The U.S. never demonstrated Soleimani posed an "instant, overwhelming" threat under the customary Caroline standard for preemptive self-defense
  • A 2008 U.S.-Iraq security agreement explicitly barred the U.S. from using Iraqi territory for offensive operations, a restriction the strike arguably violated regardless of self-defense claims
  • The precedent — a state killing a senior foreign military official outside a declared war, based on undisclosed intelligence — has been invoked in subsequent debates over the legality of targeted strikes elsewhere

Iran-U.S. Relations Hardened, Not Reset

Far from "restoring deterrence," as some Trump administration officials predicted, the killing united Iran's usually fractious political elite against the United States and strengthened domestic hardliners at the direct expense of officials open to nuclear diplomacy — a hardening whose effects outlasted the immediate crisis and shaped Iran's negotiating posture for years afterward.

  • Large crowds of mourners at Soleimani's funeral and a nationalist surge in Iranian public sentiment strengthened hardliners ahead of February 2020 legislative elections
  • The killing shifted Iraq's internal debate toward reducing the U.S.-led coalition's presence in the country, since it was seen domestically as a violation of the narrow counter-ISIS mandate under which U.S. forces remained

A More Resilient, More Diffuse Axis of Resistance

The network Soleimani spent two decades personally building did not collapse after his death, but it did change shape. Its post-Soleimani decentralisation — designed to survive the loss of any single leader — helped the axis absorb even heavier blows after October 2023, including the deaths of most of Hezbollah's senior leadership and the fall of the Assad regime in Syria, without Iran's regional strategy collapsing outright.

  • Iran has extended relationships beyond the axis itself, deepening ties with China and Russia partly to compensate for the axis's post-2024 setbacks
  • The axis's more horizontal, autonomous structure under Qaani gives Iran continued plausible deniability even as it complicates Tehran's ability to direct events with Soleimani-era precision

Legacy & Debate

Soleimani's legacy is inseparable from the sharply divided reputations he cultivated deliberately during his lifetime. He built, arguably, the single most consequential non-state military network in the modern Middle East — and did so through methods that, in the territories where his proxies operated, were frequently indistinguishable from sectarian violence and coercion.

What his admirers argue
  • He built an effective counterweight to the Islamic State when Iraqi state forces had collapsed in 2014, personally coordinating the militia response that helped prevent Baghdad's fall
  • His network gave Iran, a country with a conventionally weak military relative to its adversaries, an asymmetric capacity to project power and deter attack across the region
  • He was, by most accounts, personally austere and battlefield-present rather than a distant bureaucrat — a trait that built genuine loyalty among the fighters under his influence
  • His 2015 diplomacy with Russia is widely credited with saving the Assad government from near-certain collapse, which his supporters frame as preventing a jihadist takeover of Syria
What the evidence shows
  • The U.S. held him directly responsible for the deaths of hundreds of American soldiers in Iraq through EFP-armed militias — a documented, not merely alleged, campaign
  • His network's actions across Lebanon, Iraq and Syria are widely tied to sectarian violence and, in the Syrian case, to defending a government that killed hundreds of thousands of its own civilians
  • He was implicated, though not conclusively proven responsible, in the 2005 assassination of Lebanese prime minister Rafik Hariri
  • His personal, highly centralised command structure left the entire axis of resistance dangerously exposed to a single point of failure — his own death exposed this immediately in the ensuing succession crisis
What remains genuinely contested
  • Was the U.S. strike a legitimate act of self-defense against an active, ongoing threat, or an extrajudicial killing that violated Iraqi sovereignty and international law — as most international law scholars examining the case have concluded?
  • Did Soleimani's death meaningfully weaken Iran's regional strategy, or did it force a restructuring that ultimately made the axis of resistance more durable?
  • Is Soleimani best understood as a strategic mastermind whose personal relationships were irreplaceable, or as one skilled operator within a system whose durability was always more institutional than personal?
