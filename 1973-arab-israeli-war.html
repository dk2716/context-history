Middle East · October 6–25, 1973

The 1973 Arab-Israeli War

Sadat's surprise attack crossed a canal, shook an army, and — by not quite winning — made peace possible.

Overview

On October 6, 1973 — Yom Kippur, the holiest day of the Jewish year, and simultaneously the 10th day of Ramadan — Egyptian and Syrian forces launched a coordinated surprise attack on Israel. Egypt crossed the Suez Canal into the Israeli-held Sinai Peninsula with 70,000 soldiers and 1,500 tanks; Syria attacked the Israeli-held Golan Heights with 1,400 tanks against an Israeli force of fewer than 200. In the first 48 hours, Israeli positions along the Bar-Lev Line crumbled, Israeli tanks burned on the Golan, and Israel's defence minister told the prime minister that what he was seeing was "the destruction of the Third Temple." Israel had been on the verge of losing.

Nineteen days later, Israeli forces had not only repelled both attacks but had crossed the Suez Canal themselves, encircled Egypt's entire Third Army, and were within striking distance of both Cairo and Damascus. A Soviet threat to intervene prompted the United States to raise its nuclear alert to DEFCON 3 — the highest level since the Cuban Missile Crisis. A ceasefire brokered by Henry Kissinger's shuttle diplomacy ended the fighting on October 25.

The war was inconclusive militarily. Neither side achieved its stated war aims completely. But politically, it was transformative. Egypt's initial military success — the crossing of the Bar-Lev Line, which had been considered impregnable — restored Egyptian and Arab honour after the humiliation of 1967. That restoration was what Anwar Sadat had actually wanted. He did not need to destroy Israel; he needed to break the diplomatic stalemate that had frozen Israeli-occupied Sinai as permanent. He succeeded. The war created the conditions for the 1974 disengagement agreements, the 1975 Sinai II accord, Sadat's 1977 visit to Jerusalem, and ultimately the 1979 Camp David peace treaty that returned Sinai to Egypt.

Origins: Why Sadat Chose War

Anwar Sadat inherited the presidency of Egypt in September 1970 following Nasser's death. He also inherited Nasser's defining failure: the catastrophic loss of the Sinai Peninsula to Israel in the Six-Day War of June 1967, when Israel destroyed the Egyptian Air Force in three hours and then seized Sinai, the Golan Heights, the West Bank, and Gaza in six days. The Sinai was not an abstraction — it was Egyptian territory, it contained oil fields, and its loss had humiliated an entire generation of Arab nationalists who had believed that Nasser would lead them to dignity and strength.

Sadat's problem was diplomatic. UN Security Council Resolution 242, passed in November 1967, called for Israeli withdrawal from occupied territories in exchange for peace. Israel had no intention of withdrawing without a full peace treaty; Arab states had no intention of making peace without withdrawal. The stalemate had persisted for six years. American shuttle diplomacy under Nixon and Kissinger had produced nothing. Sadat had offered a full peace treaty to Israel in February 1971 — a remarkable step — and received no response. He had expelled Soviet military advisers in July 1972 as a signal to Washington that Egypt was open to realignment. Nothing moved.

Sadat concluded, correctly, that the only way to break the stalemate was to make the cost of inaction higher than the cost of negotiation. He did not need to win a war; he needed to show that the status quo was unsustainable. "Everything in this world can change," he told his military commanders. "Let us start a new chapter." What followed was the most sophisticated use of limited war as a diplomatic instrument in the 20th century — a war whose military objective was not victory but negotiating leverage.

Strategic Logic

Sadat's war aim was not to destroy Israel — it was to make peace possible

This distinction is crucial and is often missed. Sadat told his commanders that Egypt needed to cross the canal, hold a strip of Sinai, and create a new military-political situation that would force a diplomatic process. He explicitly did not plan to drive Israel into the sea or recapture all of Sinai militarily. He needed enough of a military success to restore Egyptian credibility, break American complacency about the frozen status quo, and give himself political room at home to negotiate. This explains decisions during the war that otherwise appear inexplicable — including his refusal to let his generals pursue the initial advantage.

The Crossing: October 6–9

October 6, 1973 — 14:05 local time
The Bar-Lev Line Falls: Egypt Crosses the Suez Canal
▼

The Bar-Lev Line — a chain of Israeli fortifications along the eastern bank of the Suez Canal, built after 1967 at a cost of $300 million — was one of the most formidable defensive structures in the modern Middle East. Israeli military doctrine held it as impregnable. On October 6, at exactly 2:05 pm, 8,000 Egyptian guns opened fire simultaneously along a 100-mile front. Two thousand pieces of artillery delivered 175 rounds per gun in the first minute. Under this cover, Egyptian engineers used high-pressure water cannon to blast gaps through the sand embankments on the Israeli side, and then — in a feat of military engineering — threw 10 bridges across the canal within hours.

Egyptian infantry crossed in rubber boats, established bridgeheads, and secured the opposite bank while the bridges were completed. By nightfall, 90,000 Egyptian soldiers and 850 tanks had crossed. The Bar-Lev Line's strongpoints were overwhelmed or bypassed; isolated Israeli garrisons fought until they ran out of ammunition. Egyptian soldiers — who had trained for the crossing for two years under conditions of total secrecy — performed with a discipline and cohesion that astonished Israeli commanders. The Israeli soldiers watching from across the canal transmitted one word back to headquarters: "War."

October 6–8, 1973 — Golan Heights
The Syrian Assault: 1,400 Tanks on the Golan
▼

Simultaneously with Egypt's canal crossing, Syria launched a massive armoured assault on the Golan Heights with approximately 1,400 tanks against an Israeli force of 177. The Golan is a volcanic plateau that overlooks northern Israel — if Syria retook it, Israeli towns in the Galilee would be in direct artillery range. For 48 hours, the Israeli 7th Armoured Brigade fought what has been called one of the greatest tank battles in history, destroying hundreds of Syrian tanks in a valley now known as the "Valley of Tears." Israeli tank commanders fought until they had fewer than ten operational vehicles and were on the verge of ordering retreat.

The Israeli Air Force, which had expected to dominate as it had in 1967, flew into a wall of Soviet-supplied surface-to-air missiles that it had not encountered before. In the first three days, Israel lost 50 aircraft. The SAM batteries changed the entire air-ground battle equation and would force a fundamental rethinking of how the Israeli Air Force operated. The Golan was ultimately held, but only barely — and the closeness of the call became one of the defining psychological facts of Israeli military history.

October 7–9, 1973
Israel's Darkest Hours: The Nuclear Option Considered
▼

On October 7, Israeli Defence Minister Moshe Dayan — who had been a symbol of Israeli military confidence since 1967 — told Prime Minister Golda Meir that they might be witnessing the "destruction of the Third Temple." Israel, he believed, might lose the war. He reportedly recommended alerting Israel's nuclear arsenal — the thirteen nuclear warheads widely believed to be Israel's undisclosed deterrent. Meir did not authorise a launch, but she reportedly authorised preparing the weapons for use if necessary.

This moment — never officially confirmed by Israel, reconstructed from accounts of U.S. intelligence and Israeli participants — is believed to have been the primary reason Nixon authorised the emergency U.S. airlift at speed. Kissinger and Nixon concluded that if Israel appeared to be losing, the risk of nuclear escalation was real. Getting Israel conventional weapons quickly was the alternative to a scenario too dangerous to contemplate. The airlift began on October 13.

The Reversal: October 10–22

October 13–14, 1973
Operation Nickel Grass: The American Airlift
▼

Nixon authorized a massive American airlift to Israel — Operation Nickel Grass — on October 13, after European allies refused to allow U.S. planes to refuel on their territory (Germany, Britain, France, and Italy all refused, fearing Arab oil retaliation). Only Portugal allowed its Azores bases to be used. Over the next 32 days, American C-141 Starlifters and C-5 Galaxies flew 567 sorties delivering 22,325 tons of military equipment: tanks, artillery ammunition, aircraft engines, electronic warfare systems, and anti-tank missiles that had proved critical against Arab armour.

The airlift was a turning point. It replaced Israeli equipment losses — which had been severe in the first days — and demonstrated unambiguously that the United States would not allow Israel to be defeated. It also had a political dimension: Nixon, mired in Watergate, saw in the crisis an opportunity to demonstrate presidential decisiveness. The Saturday Night Massacre — in which Nixon fired the Watergate special prosecutor — occurred on October 20, during the war. For the final two weeks of the conflict, the American president was simultaneously managing a potential superpower confrontation and the worst domestic political crisis since Reconstruction.

Kissinger (1982)Quandt (2005)
October 15–17, 1973
Sharon Crosses the Canal: The War Turns
▼

Israeli General Ariel Sharon — later Prime Minister, at this point a divisional commander — identified a gap between two Egyptian armies and proposed crossing the Suez Canal in the opposite direction: from east to west, into Egypt. Over the objections of his superiors (who wanted to continue attacking Egyptian forces in Sinai), Sharon pushed a small force through the gap on the night of October 15–16 and established a bridgehead on the Egyptian side of the canal. Israeli engineers then threw pontoon bridges across and began moving armoured forces into Egypt.

The strategic effect was decisive. With Israeli armour now west of the canal and driving south, Egypt's entire Third Army — 45,000 soldiers and 250 tanks in Sinai — was being encircled. Their supply lines were cut; their retreat route across the canal was threatened. Egyptian generals demanded Sadat order a counterattack; Sadat, understanding that the military situation had reversed and that he needed a ceasefire before his Third Army was destroyed, began urging the Soviets and Americans to broker one.

October 20–25, 1973
Kissinger's Shuttle and the Superpower Confrontation
▼

On October 20 — the same day as the Saturday Night Massacre in Washington — Kissinger flew to Moscow to negotiate a ceasefire with Soviet General Secretary Brezhnev. The two agreed on UN Security Council Resolution 338, calling for an immediate ceasefire and negotiations. The ceasefire came into force on October 22, but Israel continued advancing, tightening the encirclement of the Egyptian Third Army. On October 24, Brezhnev sent Nixon a message threatening to send Soviet forces to enforce the ceasefire unilaterally if the U.S. did not act. The Soviet airborne divisions were reportedly placed on alert.

Kissinger, with Nixon incapacitated by the Watergate crisis, convened a meeting of the National Security Council's deputies and recommended — and they approved — raising the U.S. military readiness to DEFCON 3 worldwide, including nuclear forces. It was the most dangerous moment in superpower relations since the Cuban Missile Crisis eleven years earlier. The message was received: the Soviets did not send forces, and a final ceasefire took effect on October 25. The encircled Egyptian Third Army was supplied through Israeli-controlled corridors — a humiliation for Egypt but also the beginning of a working relationship between Egyptian and Israeli military commanders that would eventually lead to peace.

The Superpower Dimension

The 1973 war was the most direct superpower confrontation since Cuba in 1962. Both the United States and the Soviet Union airlifted weapons to their respective allies simultaneously; both threatened direct intervention at various points; and the DEFCON 3 alert of October 24–25 put American nuclear forces on a raised readiness posture without any public announcement. Understanding the superpower dimension is essential for understanding both why the war ended when it did and why Kissinger's subsequent shuttle diplomacy succeeded.

The Soviet Airlift

The Soviet Union began airlifting supplies to Egypt and Syria almost immediately after the war began, matching the American airlift within days. Soviet-supplied surface-to-air missiles (SAM-6 batteries) had changed the air battle fundamentally — Israeli aircraft that had dominated the 1967 war flew into a completely different electronic and missile environment in 1973. Soviet advisers were present with Arab forces, though they did not participate directly in combat operations. The Soviet threat to intervene on October 24 was the most direct Soviet military threat toward the United States since the Berlin crises.

Nixon and Kissinger: Divided Authority

The key decisions — the airlift, the DEFCON alert — were made by Kissinger with Nixon either unavailable or politically impaired by Watergate. The Saturday Night Massacre occurred on October 20; Nixon's public and congressional standing was collapsing simultaneously with the military crisis. Multiple accounts suggest that the DEFCON 3 decision was made by Kissinger and Defence Secretary Schlesinger without explicit presidential authorisation — one of the most extraordinary moments of informal power transfer in modern American history, never fully acknowledged by any participant.

Détente Under Stress

Kissinger's strategy was explicitly to manage the war in ways that would preserve the U.S.-Soviet détente he had spent years building. He delayed the airlift initially — hoping to avoid a direct U.S.-Soviet confrontation — and pushed for a ceasefire before Israel could completely destroy the Egyptian Third Army. Allowing Egypt's total military humiliation would have destabilised Sadat (who Kissinger correctly saw as the key to a subsequent peace process) and poisoned U.S.-Arab relations for years. The managed outcome — Israel wins but Egypt doesn't lose everything — was precisely the leverage Kissinger needed for the shuttle diplomacy that followed.

Europe's Refusal

Almost every major U.S. European ally refused to allow American planes to use their territory or airspace for the Israeli airlift, fearing Arab oil retaliation. Germany, Britain, France, Italy, Greece, Turkey, and Spain all declined — only Portugal allowed access to the Azores. This was a significant fracture in the Atlantic alliance, and it influenced Kissinger's subsequent approach to European allies. The episode demonstrated that European governments would not support U.S. Middle East policy when their own energy security was at stake — a pattern that has persisted through subsequent decades.

The Oil Embargo: Energy as a Weapon

On October 17, 1973 — eleven days into the war — the Organization of Arab Petroleum Exporting Countries (OAPEC) announced an oil embargo against the United States, the Netherlands, Portugal, South Africa, and Rhodesia in retaliation for their support of Israel. OPEC simultaneously raised the posted price of oil by 70%, from $3.01 to $5.11 per barrel. By December, oil was trading at $11.65 — a 300% increase in two months. It was the first major oil shock of the 20th century and it restructured the global economy.

Economic Shockwave

The oil embargo that changed the world economy

The impact of the 1973 oil embargo extended far beyond the immediate price shock. In the United States, petrol rationing was introduced; speed limits were lowered to 55 mph; gasoline stations closed on Sundays; daylight saving time was extended year-round. In Europe, the Netherlands banned Sunday driving. In Japan, emergency conservation measures were imposed across industry. The embargo triggered a global recession in 1974–75, accelerated inflation that would plague Western economies for a decade, and fundamentally altered the relationship between oil-producing and oil-consuming nations. It also demonstrated that Arab states had an economic weapon they had never previously wielded — and that the West was far more vulnerable to it than had been understood.

The embargo lasted until March 1974, when Arab states lifted it after the Sinai I disengagement agreement between Egypt and Israel — brokered by Kissinger — was signed. But the structural changes it had set in motion were permanent. The oil shock accelerated the development of North Sea oil, Alaskan pipeline oil, and nuclear power in France and other countries as Western governments sought to reduce dependence on Middle Eastern supplies. OPEC's demonstration that it could dictate prices rather than simply supply at Western-determined prices changed the terms of the entire global energy economy.

The embargo also had a direct political effect in the United States: it contributed to the end of the Bretton Woods dollar-oil relationship and accelerated the negotiation of the petrodollar system under which OPEC countries would price oil in dollars and recycle oil revenues through U.S. financial institutions — an arrangement that has structured the global financial system ever since.

Why It Matters Today

The 1973 war's significance is not primarily military — it was short, geographically limited, and produced no permanent territorial change. Its significance is political, economic, and strategic: it broke a diplomatic freeze that had persisted since 1967, produced the conditions for the only comprehensive Arab-Israeli peace agreement in history, demonstrated the vulnerability of Western economies to oil disruption, and set patterns of U.S. Middle East engagement that persist today.

The Peace It Made Possible

The 1973 war is the direct predecessor of the 1979 Egypt-Israel peace treaty — the only comprehensive land-for-peace agreement in the Arab-Israeli conflict. Sadat could not have made peace without fighting. The war restored Egyptian military honour sufficiently that Sadat could negotiate without appearing to capitulate; it gave him the political standing at home to take the risk of flying to Jerusalem in 1977. The Camp David Accords — and the entire subsequent architecture of Middle East peace diplomacy — begin in October 1973.

  • Kissinger's shuttle diplomacy (January 1974, May 1974, September 1975) produced three disengagement agreements before Camp David — all made possible because the war had given both sides something to negotiate about
  • The model of land-for-peace — territorial withdrawal in exchange for diplomatic recognition — was established by the Sinai I and II agreements that followed the war, before being formalised at Camp David
  • The Jordan-Israel peace treaty (1994) and the Abraham Accords (2020) are downstream of the diplomatic infrastructure the 1973 war made possible

Oil as a Geopolitical Weapon — the Template Still Used

The 1973 embargo was the first successful use of oil as a geopolitical weapon by producing states against consuming states. It demonstrated that energy dependence created strategic vulnerability in a way that purely economic arguments about trade had not conveyed. Every subsequent energy geopolitics debate — the EU's dependence on Russian gas (2022), China's rare earth export restrictions, the "weaponisation" of trade — is a variation on the model OAPEC established in 1973.

  • Russia's use of natural gas as leverage over European countries — cutting supply to Ukraine, Poland, and Germany at various points between 2006 and 2022 — follows the exact logic the Arab oil embargo established
  • The strategic petroleum reserve, created in 1975 as a direct response to the embargo, remains the primary U.S. tool for managing oil supply disruptions — it was drawn down in 2022 in response to Russian invasion of Ukraine
  • The petrodollar system — OPEC pricing in dollars, recycling revenues through U.S. financial markets — is a direct institutional consequence of the 1973 crisis and remains foundational to the dollar's global reserve currency status

The Limits of Intelligence — a Warning Still Relevant

The 1973 war was an intelligence failure of the first order. Israel's Mossad, Military Intelligence, and the CIA all had multiple warning indicators in the weeks before October 6 — Egyptian military exercises on the canal that never returned to base, Syrian deployments on the Golan — and collectively failed to assess them as preparation for an attack. The failure was conceptual: Israeli intelligence was operating under a "concept" (the Hebrew: HaKontzeptsia) that Egypt would not attack without an air force capable of striking Israeli airfields, and that Syria would not attack without Egypt. When Egypt moved without that capability, the warning signs were reinterpreted to fit the pre-existing concept.

  • The concept failure in 1973 is the most frequently cited historical precedent in intelligence reform debates — it shaped how U.S. intelligence tried to reorganise after the 9/11 failure and the Iraq WMD failure
  • The specific failure mode — multiple warning indicators discounted because they didn't fit the existing analytical framework — has been replicated in virtually every major intelligence surprise from Pearl Harbor to 9/11 to the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine
  • Israel's 1973 intelligence failure led directly to the creation of a "Devil's Advocate" unit in Israeli Military Intelligence — an office specifically tasked with arguing against the prevailing assessment

Kissinger's Legacy: Managed Outcomes and Their Costs

Kissinger's decision to delay the American airlift initially, to prevent Israel from destroying the Egyptian Third Army entirely, and to manage the ceasefire timing — all aimed at preserving Sadat's political position and the possibility of a diplomatic process — is still debated as either the most sophisticated example of coercive diplomacy in modern history or as a cynical manipulation that left Israel's security subordinated to American strategic interests. The 1973 crisis is Kissinger's strongest evidence that realpolitik can produce better outcomes than idealism — and his critics' strongest evidence that the costs of his management are always paid by others.

  • Israel was not informed of the Kissinger-Brezhnev ceasefire agreement in advance — it was presented as a fait accompli, which damaged U.S.-Israel trust for years
  • The decision to supply the Egyptian Third Army through Israeli-controlled corridors — rather than allowing its destruction — is credited by some historians with saving the peace process; by others with rewarding Sadat for starting the war
  • Kissinger's "step-by-step" diplomacy (producing small agreements rather than comprehensive settlement) avoided the hardest issues (Palestinian statehood, Jerusalem, full peace) — those issues remain unresolved 50 years later

Legacy & Debate

The 1973 war has at least three distinct historical arguments attached to it, which tend to be conducted in parallel without intersecting. The first is a military debate about intelligence failure and command decisions. The second is a diplomatic debate about Kissinger's shuttle diplomacy and whether step-by-step produced better or worse outcomes than a comprehensive approach. The third is a political debate about whether Sadat's "war for peace" strategy was the most ethical and effective path to Israeli-Egyptian reconciliation — or whether it set a precedent that normalised war as a diplomatic tool.

Itamar Rabinovich
Israeli historian, former ambassador to the United States

Rabinovich's account in The Yom Kippur War is the most comprehensive Israeli-perspective history, drawing on declassified Israeli sources. He assesses the intelligence failure as the central tragedy — Israel had the capacity to prevent the attack but failed to act on the warnings. His analysis of the subsequent diplomacy credits Kissinger's management as genuinely skilled, while noting the costs to Israeli autonomy.

William B. Quandt
NSC official in 1973, later Brookings Institution

Quandt's Peace Process offers the most detailed account of the American side — he was in the NSC during the crisis. He argues that Kissinger's managed outcome was indispensable: a comprehensive Israeli military victory would have destroyed the conditions for peace, and a comprehensive Arab victory would have produced a different and worse political settlement. The "frustrating draw" was the only outcome that made subsequent diplomacy possible.

Mohamed Heikal
Egyptian journalist, confidant of Nasser and early Sadat

Heikal, who had been Nasser's closest journalistic ally before breaking with Sadat, argues in The Sphinx and the Commissar and later works that Sadat's war-for-peace strategy was a betrayal of Arab solidarity — that using Egypt's military for a bilateral Egyptian advantage abandoned the Palestinians and set the precedent that Arab states could make separate peace with Israel. Heikal represents the Arab nationalist critique that Sadat's strategy was brilliant for Egypt and catastrophic for the broader Arab cause.

The weight of historical evidence supports Sadat's calculation that war was the only way to break the diplomatic stalemate — the six years between 1967 and 1973, during which every other approach was tried and failed, are the evidence. Whether the result was worth the cost (approximately 15,000–20,000 dead across all sides) depends on whether you credit the 1979 peace treaty as a genuine long-term achievement or regard it as a cold peace that legitimised Israeli occupation of other Arab territories by removing Egypt from the equation.

"There was no alternative to war. The situation was deadlocked; it had to be unlocked."
Anwar Sadat, In Search of Identity: An Autobiography (1978)
