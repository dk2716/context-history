Afghanistan · 2001–2021

The War in Afghanistan (2001–2021)

Twenty years, a war whose true long-term bill Harvard researchers estimate at $4 to $6 trillion once combined with Iraq, and 2,443 American lives — how the U.S. and its allies toppled the Taliban in weeks, then spent two decades failing to build a state that could survive without them.

Overview

The War in Afghanistan began on October 7, 2001, when U.S. and allied forces launched airstrikes against Taliban and al-Qaeda targets in response to the September 11 attacks. It ended, in the sense that matters most, on August 15, 2021, when the Taliban walked back into Kabul without a fight — nearly twenty years, a war whose full long-term financial legacy runs far beyond its wartime appropriations, and 2,443 American service members' lives after it began. In between lay the longest war in U.S. history: a campaign that destroyed the Taliban's government in weeks, then spent two decades trying to build a replacement state that collapsed in roughly eleven days once U.S. support was withdrawn.

The war is often narrated as two separate failures — a successful initial intervention followed by a doomed nation-building project — but the scholarship gathered here suggests something more specific: the United States never resolved a basic contradiction between a counterterrorism mission (destroy al-Qaeda, deny it safe haven) and a state-building mission (construct a legitimate, functioning Afghan government) that required fundamentally different tools, timelines, and theories of success. Both missions were pursued simultaneously, underfunded relative to their ambitions in the crucial early years, and ultimately undermined by an insurgency that had sanctuary across the border in Pakistan and by an Afghan political settlement that never developed durable local legitimacy.

Origins & the 2001 Intervention

The United States invaded Afghanistan to destroy al-Qaeda and remove the Taliban regime that had sheltered it since the mid-1990s. The campaign that followed the October 7, 2001, airstrikes was, in narrow military terms, a rapid success: combining U.S. air power and Special Forces with the Afghan opposition's Northern Alliance ground forces, the coalition captured Kabul by mid-November and had scattered the Taliban and al-Qaeda leadership — including Osama bin Laden, who escaped into Pakistan's tribal areas at Tora Bora in December — within roughly two months. Political scientist Anthony King's blunt summary captures the whiplash of what followed: "By early December, the Taliban and al-Qaeda have been defeated and had fled. However, a war that began so successfully in 2001, eventually ended ignominiously on August 30, 2021" — the date the last U.S. military aircraft left Kabul.

With the Taliban's government gone, the international community moved immediately to construct a replacement. The Bonn Agreement, negotiated in Germany and endorsed by UN Security Council Resolution 1383 on December 6, 2001, created an Afghan Interim Authority under Hamid Karzai and laid out a tight multi-year timetable toward a permanent constitutional government. Richard Ponzio's account of the Bonn process describes UN Special Representative Lakhdar Brahimi's deliberate "light footprint" approach: rather than deploying a large UN peacekeeping and administrative presence (as in Kosovo or East Timor), the UN and its partners chose to build Afghan institutions with a comparatively small international footprint, betting that Afghan ownership would generate more durable legitimacy than an internationally-administered protectorate. It was, in Ponzio's phrase, "a risky strategy of democratization with a 'light footprint' during a... low-intensity insurgency" — and it depended on international resources that, in the critical early years, were never allocated at the scale the ambition required.

Key Miscalculation

The war that was supposed to be over in 2001 was declared won at least three separate times.

Because the initial campaign succeeded so quickly and so cheaply, U.S. attention and resources shifted to Iraq by 2002–2003, even as the state-building project in Afghanistan was just beginning and the Taliban — assumed dead — was quietly regrouping across the Pakistani border. The war would be "restarted" in practice by the 2009–2010 Obama surge and again by the 2017 Trump-era troop increases, each time treating an ongoing insurgency as a problem that had already been solved once and merely needed reinforcement to finish.

Phases of the War

October–December 2001
Operation Enduring Freedom — the Taliban's Rapid Collapse
▼

U.S. airstrikes, CIA paramilitary teams, and Special Forces embedded with the Afghan Northern Alliance combined to collapse Taliban control of Afghanistan's major cities within weeks. Kabul fell on November 13, 2001; Kandahar, the Taliban's spiritual home, fell in early December. Osama bin Laden and much of al-Qaeda's leadership escaped across the border into Pakistan during the Battle of Tora Bora in December 2001, a strategic failure that would shape the war's next two decades — al-Qaeda's core leadership, and later the Taliban's own leadership council, operated from Pakistani sanctuary for the rest of the war.

King (2023)
December 2001 – 2005
The Bonn Process and the "Light Footprint"
▼

The Bonn Agreement established an Afghan Interim Authority (December 2001–June 2002) and then a Transitional Authority (July 2002–December 2004), moving toward a new constitution (ratified January 2004) and Afghanistan's first presidential election, which Hamid Karzai won in October 2004. The UN's "light footprint" strategy relied on Afghan elites to build durable institutions with limited direct international administration. Richard Ponzio's assessment is that this period saw skillful UN diplomatic engagement but insufficient international resources — troops, funding, and sustained attention — for the scale of the state-building task at hand, a shortfall that widened as U.S. priorities shifted toward Iraq from 2002 onward.

Ponzio (2011)
2005–2009
The Taliban's Resurgence
▼

Having been dismissed as a spent force after 2001, the Taliban rebuilt itself from roughly 2005 onward with backing from Pakistan's Inter-Services Intelligence (ISI), which — per Anthony King's account — reversed its post-2001 wariness once it judged the Karzai government to be moving into India's orbit. Financing, sanctuary, and military training flowed across the border; improvised explosive devices, whose components were sourced with Pakistani and Iranian assistance, became the insurgency's signature weapon and the leading cause of U.S. and allied casualties. By 2008–2009, the security situation had deteriorated enough that the incoming Obama administration ordered a full strategic review.

King (2023)
2009–2010
The Obama Surge — Operation OMID
▼

In June 2009, General Stanley McChrystal took command of NATO's ISAF mission and, that November, launched Operation OMID ("Hope") — a population-centric counterinsurgency campaign built around an additional 30,000 U.S. troops. McChrystal's own leaked strategic assessment framed the mission bluntly: "Our strategy cannot be focused on seizing terrain or destroying insurgents; our objective must be the people... Success will be achieved when [the government] has earned the support of the powerful Afghan people." The plan concentrated forces in 80 key districts, aiming to link Afghan government representatives with local and tribal leaders and displace both the insurgency and the warlord powerbrokers who had captured much of the Karzai government's local authority.

President Obama, however, paired the troop surge with an announced 18-month withdrawal timeline — a decision Anthony King identifies as politically necessary but strategically self-defeating: "it was quite obvious that Barack Obama's surge had to be short... Consequently, while the surge squeezed the Taliban out of many areas, it also could never provide a permanent political solution. The gains were temporary." The surge achieved tactical successes in Helmand and Kandahar provinces but did not fundamentally alter the insurgency's access to Pakistani sanctuary or the Karzai government's underlying legitimacy problems.

King (2023)
2011–2020
Transition, Drawdown, and the Peace Talks
▼

Following the surge, U.S. strategy shifted to "transition" — handing security responsibility to the Afghan National Defense and Security Forces (ANDSF) while gradually drawing down international troop levels. A 2017 SIGAR review of this effort found the U.S. government had been "ill-prepared to conduct [security force assistance] programs of the size and scope required," that initial post-2001 planning had not even included building an Afghan army or police force, and that persistently high ANDSF attrition — driven, per SIGAR's research, less by combat operations than by poor and corrupt leadership — undermined every subsequent effort to build a force capable of standing on its own. Parallel efforts to negotiate directly with the Taliban culminated in the U.S.–Taliban Doha Agreement of February 2020, under the Trump administration, which set the terms for a full U.S. withdrawal — the immediate prelude to the government's collapse the following year, covered in depth on Context's dedicated page, The Withdrawal from Afghanistan (2021).

State-Building & the Rule of Law

Of all the state-building efforts the U.S. and its partners undertook in Afghanistan, few reveal the underlying structural problem as clearly as the two-decade attempt to build the rule of law. Geoffrey Swenson's 2017 study in International Security argues that U.S. rule-of-law assistance was "ad hoc, ineffective, and predicated on widely optimistic assumptions" — and, in certain respects, actively counterproductive. The core failure, in Swenson's analysis, was a refusal to seriously reckon with Afghanistan's legal pluralism: the reality that most Afghans, especially outside major cities, resolved disputes through informal tribal and religious mechanisms that long predated — and continued to operate alongside — the formal state courts the U.S. and its donor partners spent billions of dollars trying to build.

A Justice Vacuum, Filled by the Taliban

Official U.S. policy explicitly framed rule-of-law programs as counterinsurgency: "Justice and rule of law programs will focus on creating predictable and fair dispute resolution mechanisms to eliminate the vacuum that the Taliban have exploited." Yet by treating nonstate justice actors as mere "cogs" in a counterinsurgency strategy rather than legitimate partners, Swenson argues, U.S. programs never displaced the informal justice sector — and the Taliban's own shadow courts, however brutal, filled the gap left by a corrupt and often absent formal state judiciary.

Aid as Rent, Not Reform

Roughly 90% of Afghan justice-sector funding came from foreign donors, chiefly Western states and the UN Development Programme. Swenson's assessment is that this aid, rather than building durable institutions, often became "another source of rent extraction for a rentier state" — propping up courts and ministries that remained fundamentally compromised by corruption and the Karzai government's dependence on warlord powerbrokers who had no genuine interest in the rule of law.

Building an Army Without a State to Serve

The 2017 SIGAR review of the Afghan National Defense and Security Forces found that initial post-2001 U.S. planning "focused solely on U.S. military operations and did not include the development of an Afghan army, police, or supporting ministerial-level institutions" — meaning the security forces meant to eventually replace international troops were an afterthought bolted onto the counterterrorism campaign, not a foundation planned from the start.

Militias That Undermined the State They Were Meant to Serve

SIGAR also found that early U.S. partnerships with independent Afghan militias — formed to advance immediate counterterrorism objectives against al-Qaeda remnants — "ultimately undermined the creation and role of" the national army and police, entrenching local warlord power at exactly the moment the state was trying to establish a monopoly on legitimate force.

Swenson's conclusion is not that state-building in a legally pluralist society is impossible, but that the U.S. approach never took the underlying strategic vision seriously enough: "the United States never truly sought to engage with nonstate justice as a means to promote a more just legal order in Afghanistan, but rather saw nonstate actors as mere cogs in a deeply flawed counterinsurgency approach."

Why the Taliban Won

Anthony King's 2023 analysis in Armed Forces & Society, written just months after the Taliban's return to Kabul, resists the temptation to reduce twenty years of war to a single cause. Instead, King identifies three "decisive conditions" that together explain the U.S. defeat: the physical environment, Afghan politics, and the resilience of the Taliban itself as a fighting organization.

The Environment

"Afghanistan is a hard country," King writes bluntly — a landlocked, high-altitude mountainous terrain with no ports or navigable rivers, where major passes are snowbound for roughly half the year and summer temperatures in the south regularly reach 40–50°C. This geography made large-scale conventional military operations logistically punishing under the best of circumstances. Layered onto this terrain is a fractured human geography — a Pashtun plurality alongside large Hazara, Uzbek, and Tajik populations, with further tribal subdivisions especially pronounced in the Pashtun south — that made any uniform, centrally imposed political or military solution extraordinarily difficult to sustain.

Afghan Politics

King argues the West never developed a workable political theory of the Afghan state it was trying to build. A leaked U.S. government internal review put it starkly, per King's citation of investigative reporting: "We were devoid of a fundamental understanding of Afghanistan — we didn't know what we were doing." Counterinsurgency theorist Robert Thompson's foundational principle — that success requires a clear, achievable political aim — was, in King's judgment, never remotely applied to the Western intervention.

Taliban Resilience

Despite internal divisions after 2010 that left it, in King's words, "a federated front rather [than] a truly single party," the Taliban remained "comparatively advantaged over its opponents" — cohesive enough, relative to Afghanistan's fractured tribal and governmental politics, to sustain a multi-decade insurgency from Pakistani sanctuary, rebuild after near-total defeat in 2001, and outlast a coalition of the world's most powerful militaries.

No Afghan "Anbar Awakening"

King draws a pointed comparison to Iraq, where the 2007 U.S. troop surge succeeded partly because it coincided with the "Anbar Awakening" — Sunni tribal leaders turning against al-Qaeda in Iraq and allying with U.S. forces. "The problem in Afghanistan was that there was no awakening," King writes. Western forces, in his assessment, never successfully built the kind of durable local political alliance that made the Iraq surge's tactical gains stick.

Costs & Lessons Learned

Anthony Cordesman's September 2021 assessment for the Center for Strategic and International Studies opens with a warning rooted in institutional memory: the United States has a documented history of failing to learn systematically from its own wars. Cordesman traces this pattern through Vietnam, the 1991 Gulf War, and the 2003 Iraq invasion, arguing that Afghanistan was at serious risk of repeating it — not least because the war's end coincided with a bitter, partisan domestic political fight over "who lost Afghanistan" that threatened to substitute blame-assignment for genuine institutional learning.

The Special Inspector General for Afghanistan Reconstruction's own final "lessons learned" reports supply the financial and human reckoning Cordesman warns policymakers not to lose. Across two decades, the U.S. government spent approximately $145 billion on reconstruction — funding meant to build Afghan governance, security forces, and civil society — on top of $837 billion in Department of Defense warfighting costs, for a combined total approaching $1 trillion in direct wartime appropriations alone. That figure, large as it is, understates the war's true budgetary legacy: Harvard's Linda Bilmes finds that once long-term veteran medical care, disability compensation, and war-debt interest are included, the Iraq and Afghanistan wars combined will ultimately cost the United States between $4 and $6 trillion, with the bulk of that bill still unpaid decades after the fighting itself ends. More than 775,000 U.S. troops rotated through Afghanistan over the war's two decades. The human cost fell heaviest, of course, on Afghans themselves, but SIGAR's own accounting records 2,443 American service members and 1,144 allied troops killed, and 20,666 American troops wounded.

Documented Findings

SIGAR's own verdict: money and troop numbers were never the constraint

SIGAR's 2021 "What We Need to Learn" report — the agency's capstone lessons-learned document — concluded that the reconstruction effort's core problems were not primarily a matter of insufficient resources. Even after two decades and $145 billion in reconstruction spending alone, on top of nearly $9 billion specifically spent trying to reverse Afghanistan's opium poppy cultivation, the report found conditions on the ground "progressively worsened" across many of the metrics the effort was meant to improve. The report's own language calls this the first explicit U.S. government recognition that its strategy across two decades had failed to bring the security and governance outcomes it was designed to produce.

Cordesman's broader argument is that lessons-learned exercises fail when they focus on tactical or command-level decisions while ignoring the structural mismatch between stated strategic goals and the resources, timelines, and theories of victory actually applied to achieve them. The war's ending, he argues, deserves the same rigorous, non-partisan institutional scrutiny that the U.S. failed to give Vietnam — precisely because, without it, the same structural mistakes are likely to recur in the next state-building intervention Washington undertakes.

Why It Matters Today

The War in Afghanistan formally ended in 2021, but its lessons remain unresolved and its consequences are still unfolding across U.S. foreign policy, Afghan society, and the broader debate over what military power can and cannot accomplish.

The Collapse Was Not a Surprise to Everyone

SIGAR's reporting throughout the 2010s repeatedly flagged ANDSF attrition, corruption, and dependency on U.S. enablers (airpower, logistics, contractor maintenance) as structural vulnerabilities — meaning the government's roughly eleven-day collapse in August 2021, while shocking to much of the public, was consistent with findings its own oversight agency had been documenting for years. This raises hard questions about how intelligence and lessons-learned findings translate — or fail to translate — into policy change.

A Template for the Limits of State-Building

Swenson's rule-of-law findings and SIGAR's ANDSF findings converge on the same broader lesson: external actors cannot substitute for a state's own political legitimacy, and aid delivered primarily for counterinsurgency purposes — rather than genuine institution-building — tends to reproduce the patronage and corruption problems it is meant to solve. This has become a reference case for how the U.S. and other international actors evaluate future stabilization and state-building missions.

The Iraq Comparison Still Shapes U.S. Strategic Thinking

King's observation that Afghanistan had no "Anbar Awakening" — no equivalent moment where a critical mass of local power-holders durably switched sides — remains a central reference point in debates over why nominally similar counterinsurgency doctrine produced very different outcomes in the two wars, and what conditions (if any) that doctrine actually requires to succeed.

Pakistan's Role Remains a Live Diplomatic Fault Line

The sanctuary Pakistan's tribal areas provided to both the Taliban leadership and, initially, al-Qaeda's remnants is treated by King and others as one of the decisive, underappreciated factors in the war's outcome. The unresolved question of Pakistani state complicity continues to shape — and complicate — U.S.-Pakistan relations and regional counterterrorism policy.

Legacy & Debate

The War in Afghanistan presents a historical judgment that is, in one sense, unusually settled and, in another, still very much contested. It is largely uncontroversial among the scholars gathered here that the initial 2001 campaign achieved its narrow objective of removing the Taliban government and scattering al-Qaeda, and equally uncontroversial that the subsequent two-decade state-building effort failed to produce an Afghan government or security force capable of surviving the withdrawal of international support. What remains genuinely debated is why — and which failures were structural versus contingent.

SIGAR's own institutional verdict is that resourcing was never the primary constraint — poor planning, corruption, and a failure to build durable Afghan institutions mattered more than money. Cordesman extends this into a broader warning about the U.S. government's chronic failure to institutionalize lessons learned from its wars.

The case that the war was winnable with different choices
  • Swenson explicitly argues Afghanistan's outcome "reflects policy decisions" and that "different decisions may well have produced more positive results" — the failure was not inevitable
  • The 2009–2010 surge achieved real, if temporary, tactical gains in Helmand and Kandahar, suggesting the underlying approach was not hopeless, only under-resourced and mistimed
  • Early U.S. and Afghan planning gaps — including the initial failure to plan for an Afghan army at all — were correctable errors of sequencing, not proof the entire project was doomed
The case that the mission was structurally unworkable
  • King's "environment" factor argues Afghanistan's geography and fractured human terrain made any externally imposed, centrally administered solution extraordinarily difficult regardless of resourcing choices
  • Pakistani sanctuary for the Taliban leadership meant the insurgency could not be defeated by any strategy confined to Afghan territory — a structural constraint no U.S. administration resolved in twenty years
  • SIGAR's finding that conditions "progressively worsened" even after $145 billion in reconstruction spending suggests the problem was not principally a matter of more resources or better management
