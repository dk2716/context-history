United States · 42nd President

Bill Clinton

The Arkansas governor who charmed his way to the White House, presided over America's longest peacetime economic boom, and became only the second president in history to be impeached — undone not by his enemies but by his own choices.

Origins & Rise

Bill Clinton was born into poverty in Hope, Arkansas, in 1946, three months after his father died in a car crash. His mother, Virginia, was a nurse; his stepfather, Roger Clinton, was an alcoholic who beat his wife. Clinton's childhood in Hot Springs — a small Arkansas city with a complicated relationship with gambling, organized crime, and political patronage — gave him an intuitive understanding of people who struggled, and an equally intuitive capacity to compartmentalize his personal life from his public one. Both would define his career.

At 16, he shook hands with John F. Kennedy at the White House as part of Boys Nation, an image that has been reproduced so often it risks cliché — but the encounter was apparently genuine in its impact. Clinton went to Georgetown, won a Rhodes Scholarship to Oxford, then completed a law degree at Yale, where he met Hillary Rodham. He returned to Arkansas to teach law and immediately ran for Congress in 1974, losing narrowly. He became state Attorney General in 1976, Governor at 32 in 1978 — the youngest governor in the country — then lost re-election in 1980 after a perceived arrogance in office, won it back in 1982 with a famous personal apology campaign, and held it until 1992.

His path to the 1992 Democratic nomination was improbable. He was little-known nationally; his campaign was nearly derailed before it began by allegations of a twelve-year extramarital affair with Gennifer Flowers, and by questions about how he had avoided the Vietnam draft. He and Hillary appeared together on 60 Minutes in January 1992 in what became one of the most studied interviews in American political history — he acknowledged "wrongdoing" in his marriage without admitting anything specific, she pointedly refused to be seen as "some little woman standing by my man like Tammy Wynette." It worked. He was dubbed "the Comeback Kid" after a second-place finish in New Hampshire and never looked back.

Domestic Policy

Clinton came to office promising a "New Democrat" approach — rejecting what he characterised as the failed liberalism of Mondale and Dukakis, embracing free markets and fiscal discipline while preserving a social safety net. The approach, also called the "Third Way" (shared with Tony Blair's New Labour in Britain), was premised on the idea that Democrats had lost working-class voters by appearing hostile to business and soft on crime. Clinton's solution: beat Republicans on their own terrain.

The strategy produced some genuine achievements and some lasting controversies. His 1993 budget — passed without a single Republican vote — raised taxes on high earners and combined with an economic boom to produce four consecutive budget surpluses by the end of his term. The Family and Medical Leave Act, passed in his first weeks, gave Americans the right to unpaid leave for family emergencies. The earned income tax credit was substantially expanded, lifting millions of working-poor families above the poverty line. The Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP), created in 1997, insured millions of children whose families earned too much for Medicaid but too little for private insurance.

Most Contested Legacy

The 1994 Crime Bill

The Violent Crime Control and Law Enforcement Act of 1994 was the largest crime bill in American history. It added 100,000 police officers, funded prison construction, expanded the death penalty, and included a "three strikes" provision mandating life imprisonment for a third violent felony. It also included the Violence Against Women Act. Supporters credit it with contributing to a dramatic crime decline in the late 1990s. Critics — including many in the Congressional Black Caucus who eventually voted for it — argue it fuelled mass incarceration that fell disproportionately on Black and Latino communities. By 2015, Clinton himself was acknowledging that "we overshot the mark."

NAFTA — the North American Free Trade Agreement, signed by George H.W. Bush and ratified by Clinton in 1993 — was the signature trade achievement of his presidency and its most divisive domestic legacy. Clinton argued it would create jobs on both sides of the border, lower prices for consumers, and tie Mexico more tightly to democratic norms. Critics, including labour unions and the left of his own party, warned it would accelerate the movement of manufacturing jobs to lower-wage Mexico. Both were partly right: consumer prices fell, and US exports to Mexico grew substantially. But an estimated 700,000–900,000 American manufacturing jobs moved south. In the Rust Belt communities that later voted for Donald Trump in 2016, NAFTA became shorthand for Clinton-era economic policies that benefited educated professionals at the expense of factory workers.

Welfare reform — the Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act of 1996 — was Clinton's most contested domestic action. He had promised to "end welfare as we know it" and did: the law replaced guaranteed federal welfare payments with block grants to states, imposed work requirements, and set a five-year lifetime limit on benefits. Three senior officials in the Department of Health and Human Services resigned in protest. Clinton's defenders argue the reform reduced poverty and dependency in a period of economic growth. Critics argue it removed a safety net without replacing it, leaving the poorest families — particularly children — exposed when the next recession hit. In 2001, it did.

1993
Healthcare Reform Fails — The First Major Defeat
▼

Clinton's first major legislative initiative was universal healthcare — a plan developed in a task force led by Hillary Clinton that proposed a system of managed competition through regional health alliances. It was complex to the point of incomprehensibility, ran to 1,342 pages, and was immediately attacked by the insurance industry in the famous "Harry and Louise" television campaign, which portrayed it as government takeover of medicine. The bill never reached a floor vote in either chamber. Its failure left Democrats without a major legislative victory in their first two years and contributed to the catastrophic 1994 midterm elections, in which Republicans gained 54 House seats — the largest midterm swing in 40 years — and took control of both chambers.

The healthcare defeat defined Clinton's subsequent governing style: smaller, targeted proposals rather than comprehensive reform. "The era of big government is over," he declared in his 1996 State of the Union address — a line that infuriated the left of his party and was celebrated by the centre as shrewd repositioning. Healthcare reform would wait until Barack Obama's 2010 Affordable Care Act.

1994–2000
The Dot-Com Boom — Prosperity Without Purpose?
▼

The Clinton years produced the longest peacetime economic expansion in American history — 116 consecutive months of GDP growth from March 1991 to March 2001. Unemployment fell from 7.3% to 3.9%. Poverty rates declined. The federal government ran surpluses for four straight years. The median household income rose. These are real numbers representing real improvements in real people's lives.

The harder question is how much of this was Clinton's doing. The technology revolution — the commercialisation of the internet — drove much of the growth, and would likely have occurred regardless of who was president. The Federal Reserve's management of monetary policy under Alan Greenspan played a central role. And the boom contained the seeds of subsequent busts: the deregulation of financial derivatives under the Commodity Futures Modernization Act of 2000 (which Clinton signed) removed safeguards that would have limited the 2008 financial crisis. Clinton presided over prosperity; the extent to which he caused it, rather than managed it, is genuinely contested.

Foreign Policy

Clinton came to office focused on domestic economic renewal — "It's the economy, stupid" was his campaign's internal motto. Foreign policy was largely delegated. The results were mixed: genuine successes in Northern Ireland and the Balkans; a catastrophic failure in Rwanda; and a series of half-measures on terrorism that did not disrupt al-Qaeda before September 11.

1994
Rwanda — The Failure Clinton Most Regrets
▼

In 100 days between April and July 1994, approximately 800,000 Rwandan Tutsis and moderate Hutus were murdered — roughly 8,000 people per day, mostly with machetes, by their neighbours. The Clinton administration knew what was happening within days. Romeo Dallaire, the UN force commander in Rwanda, sent urgent requests for permission to intervene and was told to stand down. The administration deliberately avoided using the word "genocide" in official communications — because the Genocide Convention would have required a response.

Clinton's public explanation was that the administration did not understand the speed and scale of what was happening. Internal documents declassified since suggest the understanding was clearer than admitted. The administration was still reeling from the "Black Hawk Down" disaster in Somalia in October 1993, in which 18 US soldiers were killed, and had no appetite for another African intervention. Clinton himself has said Rwanda was the decision he most regrets, and visited the country in 1998 to apologise publicly. Whether a US intervention could have saved significant numbers of lives — and at what cost — is genuinely disputed by military historians.

1995–1999
Bosnia and Kosovo — Reluctant Intervention, Eventual Success
▼

In Bosnia, Clinton inherited a war that had been burning since 1992 and a European response that had failed to stop systematic ethnic cleansing. His administration intervened effectively — eventually. The 1995 Dayton Accords, brokered by US special envoy Richard Holbrooke after NATO airstrikes forced Serb compliance, ended the Bosnian War and have held for thirty years. Critics argue the US should have intervened three years earlier; defenders say domestic political constraints made that impossible.

Kosovo in 1999 was Clinton's most aggressive foreign policy action. When Serbian forces under Milosevic began systematic ethnic cleansing of Kosovar Albanians, Clinton launched a 78-day NATO air campaign — without UN Security Council authorisation (Russia would have vetoed). Milosevic capitulated; Kosovo was placed under UN administration. The Kosovo intervention became the template for the "humanitarian intervention" doctrine and the forerunner of the Responsibility to Protect. It was controversial in its legal basis and contested in its consequences, but it stopped an ongoing atrocity. Tony Blair was Clinton's closest partner in pushing for the intervention.

1993–2000
Terrorism — The Missed Opportunities
▼

Al-Qaeda attacked the US repeatedly during Clinton's presidency: the World Trade Center bombing in February 1993, the Khobar Towers bombing in Saudi Arabia in 1996, the simultaneous bombings of US embassies in Kenya and Tanzania in 1998 (killing 224 people), and the bombing of the USS Cole in October 2000 (killing 17 sailors). The administration's responses — missile strikes on al-Qaeda training camps in Afghanistan and a pharmaceutical factory in Sudan in 1998 — were dismissed by critics as insufficient and by late-night comedians as "wagging the dog" (distracting from the Lewinsky scandal).

The 9/11 Commission found that both the Clinton and Bush administrations had failed to treat al-Qaeda as a sufficient priority. Clinton's defenders argue he tried harder than is remembered — the 1998 missile strikes, the "Millennium Plot" disruption — and was hampered by Congressional opposition to aggressive counter-terrorism funding. His critics note that bin Laden had been expelled from Sudan in 1996 and could have been killed or captured on multiple occasions, and that the decision not to act reflected a failure of strategic will rather than a lack of opportunity. The question of what could realistically have been done differently remains contested.

The Scandal & Impeachment

The defining event of Clinton's second term was not a policy decision but a personal one. In January 1998, special prosecutor Kenneth Starr — originally appointed to investigate the Whitewater real estate dealings — revealed that he had evidence of a sexual relationship between Clinton and Monica Lewinsky, a 22-year-old White House intern. Clinton had denied the relationship under oath in a civil deposition in the Paula Jones sexual harassment case. The lie — not the relationship — triggered impeachment.

The Specific Charges

What Clinton Was Impeached For

Clinton was impeached on two articles: perjury before a grand jury (denying a sexual relationship with Lewinsky while under oath) and obstruction of justice (encouraging Lewinsky to file a false affidavit and discussing a cover story with his aide). He was not impeached for the sexual relationship itself, which was not illegal. The House vote was largely along party lines. In the Senate trial, the perjury article failed 45-55, and the obstruction article failed 50-50 — well short of the two-thirds required for removal. Clinton remained in office.

The impeachment produced one of the most severe partisan splits in American history and left a legacy that remains raw. Republicans argued — credibly — that perjury and obstruction of justice were serious crimes regardless of the underlying subject matter, and that a president who lied under oath had undermined the rule of law. Democrats argued — also credibly — that the investigation had been a politically motivated fishing expedition that spent $70 million to find what amounted to an extramarital affair, and that Republicans who had themselves committed infidelities were using the process as a weapon. Both arguments contained genuine substance.

Clinton's public handling of the crisis — his August 1998 televised address in which he admitted the relationship but attacked Starr rather than genuinely apologising — was widely regarded as a political mistake that extended the scandal. His approval ratings paradoxically rose during the impeachment, reflecting public distaste for the process, but his moral authority and his ability to govern in his final two years were substantially damaged. The affair and its cover-up also overshadowed genuine foreign policy achievements in Northern Ireland and the Middle East — and handed his enemies a tool that has been wielded against Democrats for a generation.

Legacy & Debate

Bill Clinton's legacy is among the most genuinely contested in modern American political history — contested not just between left and right but within the Democratic Party itself. A generation of progressives who came of age after 2008 have revisited his presidency with considerably less charity than the contemporaries who re-elected him by 8 points in 1996.

  • CHIP insured millions of previously uninsured children

What He Got Wrong
  • Rwanda — deliberate inaction during genocide that killed ~800,000
  • Crime Bill — contributed to mass incarceration that fell on Black communities
  • NAFTA — accelerated deindustrialisation of the American Midwest
  • Welfare reform — removed safety net without adequate replacement

  • Don't Ask Don't Tell — compromise that institutionalised discrimination

Genuinely Contested
  • "Triangulation" — shrewd adaptation to political reality, or abandonment of Democratic Party principles?
  • The impeachment — politically motivated persecution, or legitimate enforcement of rule of law?
  • The economic boom — Clinton's achievement, or technological revolution he happened to preside over?
  • Don't Ask Don't Tell — the best achievable at the time, or an unnecessary compromise that could have been fought?
  • The Oslo Process — genuine path toward Middle East peace, or fatally flawed from the start?
Joe Klein
Time Magazine · The Natural: The Misunderstood Presidency of Bill Clinton (2002)
Klein argues Clinton was a genuinely gifted politician whose presidency produced real results — economic growth, successful foreign interventions, welfare reform that worked — and whose legacy has been unfairly defined by a scandal that reflected a personal failing, not a policy failure. He was, in Klein's phrase, a "natural" politician squandering a natural talent.
Ta-Nehisi Coates and critics from the left
The Atlantic and broader progressive scholarship (2010s)
The post-2010 progressive reassessment argues that Clinton's "Third Way" was a capitulation to Reaganism that locked in financial deregulation, mass incarceration, and punitive welfare policy under a Democratic brand. The economic growth of the 1990s concealed structural damage that became visible in 2008 and after. Clinton's personal charisma masked a governing philosophy indistinguishable from moderate Republicanism.
John Harris
Washington Post · The Survivor: Bill Clinton in the White House (2005)
The most balanced account — documents both the real achievements and the self-inflicted wounds with equal seriousness. Harris's Clinton is a man of extraordinary political intelligence and ordinary self-destructive weaknesses, who frequently sabotaged the very agenda he was trying to advance. The question is not whether Clinton was talented but whether he was disciplined enough to fully convert that talent into lasting policy change.
