Vladimir Putin

Владимир Путин

The KGB lieutenant colonel who watched the Soviet empire collapse from a courtyard in Dresden, rose through patronage to Russia's presidency in a single decade, and has spent the quarter-century since rebuilding a personalized, hybrid state built to outlast reform — and to fight, when he judged it necessary, a war his own scholars call a case study in hubris.

Origins & Path to Power

Vladimir Putin was born on October 7, 1952, in Leningrad, into a family of modest means: his father worked in a factory, his grandfather had once cooked for Stalin, and the family shared a communal flat's kitchen with two other households. He studied law and German at Leningrad State University from 1970 to 1975 and became a city judo champion. Political scientist Peter Rutland, whose 2000 study of Putin's rise remains one of the earliest and most detailed English-language accounts, describes a man whose biography "suggests humble origins and modest ambition: intelligent, competent, and loyal; an organization man and a team player" — the opposite, in other words, of an obvious future autocrat.

Joining the KGB had been Putin's ambition since before he entered university, and he achieved it directly after graduating. He worked in foreign intelligence in Leningrad before being posted to Dresden, East Germany, in 1985, where he rose to the modest rank of lieutenant colonel. It was there, in November 1989, that Putin witnessed the collapse of the German Democratic Republic at first hand — describing decades later how he helped hold off an angry crowd outside the KGB's Dresden office while a call for military backup to the nearest Soviet garrison went unanswered for hours. "Moscow was silent," he later said. Biographers and scholars alike treat this moment — watching the Soviet security apparatus fail to protect its own outpost — as a formative memory behind Putin's later preoccupation with state weakness and humiliation.

Putin returned to Leningrad, briefly continued KGB work at the university, and then in 1990 was recruited as an aide to Anatoly Sobchak — the reformist, newly elected mayor of Leningrad (soon renamed St. Petersburg) and Putin's former law professor. He resigned from the KGB on the first day of the August 1991 coup against Gorbachev. Under Sobchak, Putin handled the city's dealings with foreign investors, reportedly capably, and built a reputation as a discreet, effective fixer. When Sobchak lost re-election in 1996, Putin — out of a job — moved to Moscow, joining the presidential property administration under Pavel Borodin with the backing of liberal reformist patrons Anatoly Chubais and Alexei Kudrin.

Putin's rise through the 1990s followed the classic pattern of Yeltsin-era Kremlin politics: promotion through patronage rather than elections or party competition. In March 1997 he was made deputy presidential chief of staff overseeing relations with Russia's regions, and a year later, first deputy chief of staff. In July 1998, Yeltsin appointed him director of the FSB. Then, in August 1999, in a decision that surprised even seasoned Russia-watchers — Putin's name had not appeared on any serious list of successors as late as spring 1999 — Yeltsin named him Prime Minister. Political scientists at the time were tracking Yevgeny Primakov, Gennady Zyuganov, Yuri Luzhkov, and other established figures; Putin, an outsider with no electoral base, was not among the names discussed.

What followed cemented his position. The Second Chechen War began that same August, triggered by a Chechen-based incursion into Dagestan and a wave of apartment-building bombings in Russian cities that killed nearly 300 people and were blamed on Chechen militants. Putin's blunt, uncompromising rhetoric about the war — he vowed to pursue terrorists "even in the outhouse" — and his association with a decisive military response drove his approval ratings from obscurity to dominance within months. On December 31, 1999, Yeltsin abruptly resigned, making Putin Acting President; his first act was to sign a decree granting Yeltsin and his family immunity from prosecution. Putin won the March 2000 presidential election with just over 50% of the vote — Russia's first peaceful, constitutional transfer of presidential power in its history, and the first electoral test of a leader assembled almost entirely by patronage rather than public political life.

The Hybrid State

Political scientists have struggled for two decades to name the system Putin built. Richard Sakwa's 2010 concept of a Russian "dual state" — a formal constitutional order shadowed by an informal "administrative state" of factional conflict and para-constitutional practice — captured something real but, as Graeme P. Herd argued in a widely cited 2018 analysis, understated how thoroughly the system had by then become personalized around access to Putin himself rather than institutional rank.

Herd's alternative framework — the "hybrid state" — distributes power across three interlocking pillars. The first is the visible, formal "normative state": the Presidential Administration, Cabinet of Ministers, State Duma, courts, and bureaucracy, encompassing roughly 73 senior officials by a 2018 U.S. Treasury count. The second pillar is semi-official: heads of "parastatal" entities, roughly 29 large state-owned corporations in which the state holds a 25% stake or greater — enterprises that by some estimates generate 70% of Russia's GDP and account for a third of its jobs. Rosneft, under Igor Sechin, exemplifies this tier: a state-within-a-state with a quarter-million employees and tens of billions in revenue, run by a figure who, in the words of one Russian defense analyst, "has never occupied high posts in the executive branch" and yet wields more real influence than most cabinet ministers.

The Third Pillar: The Oligarchic Court

Herd identifies roughly 96 individuals — including figures like the Kovalchuk brothers, Gennady Timchenko, and Arkady Rotenberg — who form Putin's informal inner circle, each with a net worth exceeding $1 billion, gained and sustained through demonstrated political loyalty. As one political consultant put it, "Rotenberg is more influential than a lot of federal ministers. But who is Rotenberg?" — the point being that formal office is a poor predictor of real power in Putin's system.

Carrots and Sticks

Putin manages the tensions between these pillars through a mix of incentives: favored access, sanctioned corruption, and property redistribution on one hand; FSB surveillance, kompromat, selective prosecution, and intimidation on the other. As one former economic minister who fell from favor described the machinery bluntly: a person is invited for a walk, asked to hold a briefcase — and then arrested.

Survival Over Reform

Herd's central argument is that Putin's regime is "focused on survival and self-preservation rather than reform, development and expansion" — and that since roughly 2007, destabilizing Russia's neighbors has functioned as a rational strategy for a system unable or unwilling to pursue genuine structural reform at home. Manufactured confrontation with the West offers a domestic political payoff: it justifies "military-patriotic mobilization" precisely when economic reform is off the table.

Deniable Proxies

The hybrid state's defining feature, per Herd, is its deliberate blurring of public and private, domestic and external. This is the logic behind Russia's use of organized crime networks, "active measures" intelligence operations, and above all private military companies — the Wagner Group chief among them — which let the Kremlin project force in Libya, Mali, Syria, and Ukraine while maintaining official deniability.

"Some of those in office are not in power, and some of those in power are not in office."
— Graeme P. Herd, "Russia's Hybrid State and President Putin's Fourth-Term Foreign Policy?" (2018)

Ideology & Derzhavnost

It is tempting, historian and Russian opposition politician Vladimir Ryzhkov argues, to see Putin as "a venal thug — unprincipled, pragmatic, cynical and kleptocratic." Ryzhkov's 2024 analysis pushes back hard on this reading: Putin, he contends, has a genuine — if, in Ryzhkov's judgment, deeply misguided — ideology, and understanding it is a precondition for dealing with him effectively. "He's a true believer," Ryzhkov writes.

That ideology was laid out with striking explicitness in Putin's own programmatic essay, "Russia at the Turn of the Millennium," published on December 30, 1999 — days before he became Acting President. It named four pillars of a "Russian idea": patriotism, understood as pride in the Fatherland's history; derzhavnost, roughly meaning enduring great-power status; gosudarstvennichestvo, or statehood, explicitly opposed to liberal Western models of individual rights; and social solidarity, the priority of collectivism over individualism. "For Russians," Putin wrote, "a strong state is not an anomaly, not something to be fought against, but, on the contrary, the source and guarantor of order, the initiator and the main driving force behind any change."

Ryzhkov traces a radicalization of these ideas across two decades: the 2007 Munich speech, in which Putin publicly broke with the West's post-Cold War order; the 2008 Russo-Georgian war; the 2014 annexation of Crimea; and the full-scale 2022 invasion of Ukraine, each framed as a defense of Russia's rightful sphere of influence against Western encroachment. Domestically, the turning point came in 2012, after mass urban protests against Putin's return to the presidency and allegations of rigged Duma elections. From that year, Ryzhkov argues, Putin's government pursued an intensifying emphasis on "traditional values," dismantling federalism, eliminating the separation of powers, and systematically restricting civil society, religious and sexual minorities, and independent media — culminating, after the 2022 invasion, in comprehensive censorship, mandated ideological history curricula (including a new university course on "the Fundamentals of Russian Statehood"), and the criminalization of dissent as foreign-agent activity.

Ryzhkov's conclusion is stark: Putin's Russia is in the process of "consolidating a totalitarian system" — one that, while unlikely to mirror the Soviet Union's or Nazi Germany's exact institutional form, represents a comparable expansion of state control into spheres of life previously left unregulated. He draws an explicit parallel to George Orwell's observation that permanent war against a purportedly existential enemy is the optimal framework for mobilizing a population and justifying repression. "The Kremlin's main enemy is not Ukraine but the West," Ryzhkov writes. "The weakening, retreat or, better yet, the collapse of the West is its goal."

Explaining the 2022 Invasion

Why did Putin invade Ukraine in February 2022? Realist scholars like John Mearsheimer and Stephen Walt locate the answer in structural power politics: NATO's post-Cold War enlargement, in this reading, produced a threatening shift in the balance of power that Russia had rational strategic reasons to resist by force. Ryuta Ito's 2023 article in International Affairs takes this thesis seriously but argues it cannot, on its own, explain the war Russia actually fought — a war that killed large numbers of Russian soldiers who did not want to be there, devastated the Russian economy, and turned Moscow into an international pariah. As Ito puts it, citing fellow scholar Harald Edinger, "the current invasion is a strategic disaster." A rational, power-maximizing actor does not usually choose strategic disasters.

Ito's answer is a concept he calls "hubris balancing" — a form of what balance-of-power theorists call overbalancing, in which a state misperceives a defensively minded rival as an aggressor and responds with force disproportionate to any real threat. Ito grounds this in the psychology of self-deception: the well-documented human tendency to overestimate one's own power and underestimate risk, described by biologist Robert Trivers as denying "the truth to ourselves" through repression, rationalization, and self-serving cognitive distortion. Applied to Putin, Ito identifies three interlocking pathways.

Overconfidence

Putin, by multiple scholarly accounts, genuinely believed Ukraine would not meaningfully resist and that a swift, decisive campaign would install a compliant government in Kyiv. Angela Stent, in a source that anchors the closing analysis of this article, describes Putin as having been "led to believe that the war would be over after a 72-hour blitzkrieg" — so confident of victory that Russian officers reportedly carried ceremonial uniforms for a planned victory parade. Volodymyr Zelenskyy's approval rating stood near 30% before the war; Putin's advisers, scholars argue, told him what he wanted to hear rather than what was true.

Anger

Ito and the scholars he cites (notably Richard Ned Lebow) describe an emotional dimension inseparable from the strategic one: Putin's enduring fury at the Soviet collapse and at what he frames as Western moral and civilizational aggression — explicit, for instance, in his own remarks tying the war to his rejection of Western LGBTQ+ rights discourse as a threat to "our own future." Putin has cast the war as a "clash of civilizations," not a narrow territorial dispute.

Nationalist Myth-Making

Ito highlights Putin's July 2021 essay "On the Historical Unity of Russians and Ukrainians" as a case study in what he terms nationalist myth-making — the use of a highly selective, thousand-year historical narrative to deny Ukraine's legitimacy as a distinct nation, laying rhetorical groundwork for the invasion well before it began.

The Putin Doctrine

Angela Stent's "Putin Doctrine" framing, published in Foreign Affairs days before the 2022 invasion, ties these threads to three linked strategic ambitions: reversing what Putin has called the "greatest geopolitical catastrophe" of the Soviet Union's collapse, splitting the Western transatlantic alliance, and renegotiating the post-Cold War European security settlement Russia views as imposed under duress in the 1990s.

These frameworks are not mutually exclusive with Ryzhkov's ideological account or Herd's hybrid-state model — if anything, they reinforce one another. A leader steeped in derzhavnost ideology, operating atop a personalized power network insulated from dissenting information by design, is structurally primed for exactly the kind of self-deceiving overconfidence Ito describes. The invasion's catastrophic early failures — the retreat from Kyiv, the atrocities at Bucha, the humiliating exposure of Russian military weakness — did not, notably, produce a course correction. Herd's "hybrid state" framework helps explain why: a system built around loyalty and deniability rather than institutional accountability has few internal mechanisms for admitting error.

"Putin apparently had been led to believe that the war would be over after a 72-hour blitzkrieg, Kyiv would fall, and [Zelenskyy] would surrender or flee and be replaced by a puppet government controlled by Russia."
— Angela Stent, quoted in Ryuta Ito, "Hubris Balancing" (2023)

Why It Matters Today

Putin is not a historical figure. As of 2026 he remains in power, presiding over a full-scale war in Europe now well into its fifth year, a hybrid state that has proven far more durable than many Western analysts expected, and a Russia whose global posture — in Ukraine, Syria, Africa, and beyond — continues to be shaped by the ideological and psychological patterns scholars have spent two decades trying to name.

The War in Ukraine Continues to Define European Security

The invasion Ito describes as a "strategic disaster" has nonetheless proven durable — Russia continues to hold significant Ukrainian territory, and the war has reshaped NATO, European defense spending, and global energy markets. Whether the invasion is ultimately judged a rational, if costly, strategic gambit or the hubris-driven miscalculation Ito describes remains one of the central open questions of contemporary international relations.

The Hybrid State Has Outlasted Predictions of Its Collapse

Sanctions, battlefield losses, and the 2023 Wagner mutiny were each, at various points, predicted to fracture Putin's power network. None has. Herd's argument — that the hybrid state's blend of formal legitimacy and deniable proxy power makes it unusually resilient to external pressure — has held up better than many alternative predictions.

Succession Remains Genuinely Unresolved

Herd's 2018 analysis flagged succession as the central long-term vulnerability of Putin's system: a successor must be loyal enough to protect Putin but not so strong as to challenge him. No such figure has been publicly identified, and the question of what happens to Russia's hybrid state after Putin — whether it fragments along elite factional lines or reproduces itself under a chosen heir — remains entirely open.

The Wagner Model Has Been Exported Globally

The deniable, hybrid-state logic Herd identifies did not stay confined to Russia's near abroad. It was exported, via the Wagner Group and its state-controlled successor Africa Corps, to Libya, Mali, the Central African Republic, and Sudan — making Putin's model of statecraft a template studied and, in places, imitated by other authoritarian governments.

Legacy & Debate

Putin's legacy, like Herd's "hybrid state" itself, resists a single settled verdict — not least because, unlike most subjects of a historical biography, his story has no ending yet. What can be said is that he inherited a Russia widely perceived (accurately or not) as chaotic and humiliated, built a system that has proven durable under extraordinary external pressure, and chose, in 2022, to gamble that system's stability on a war that scholarly consensus increasingly treats as a serious strategic and moral miscalculation.

What his supporters argue
  • He ended the political and economic chaos of the 1990s, restoring a functioning, centralized Russian state after Yeltsin-era near-collapse
  • He restored Russia's standing as a great power capable of shaping outcomes in Syria, Africa, and its immediate neighborhood, after a decade of post-Soviet marginalization
  • His emphasis on "traditional values" and social solidarity reflects a genuine conservative constituency in Russia exhausted by 1990s liberal shock therapy
  • He built a hybrid state resilient enough to withstand the most extensive Western sanctions regime ever assembled against a major economy
What the evidence shows
  • Since 2012 he has dismantled federalism, eliminated meaningful separation of powers, and — per Ryzhkov — moved the country toward what scholars describe as an emerging totalitarian system
  • The 2022 invasion of Ukraine has killed and displaced enormous numbers of civilians and soldiers on both sides, for strategic goals Ito and others argue were built on self-deceiving overconfidence rather than sound assessment
  • His power network, per Herd, is organized around personal loyalty, kompromat, and sanctioned corruption rather than institutional accountability or the rule of law
  • Political opposition, independent media, and civil society have been systematically criminalized, particularly after 2022
What remains genuinely contested
  • Was the 2022 invasion a rational response to NATO enlargement, as realists like Mearsheimer argue, or the ideologically and psychologically driven miscalculation described by Ryzhkov and Ito?
  • Is Putin's "hybrid state" a durable new model of authoritarian governance, or a personalized system that cannot survive his own departure from power?
  • Did Putin's ideology precede and drive his consolidation of power, or did the ideology harden in service of a power-preservation project that began for more pragmatic reasons?
  • Can a system this dependent on one man's judgment and one network's cohesion actually plan for succession — or is a succession crisis, as Herd suggested in 2018, effectively inevitable?

The most defensible reading, drawing on all four scholarly accounts behind this article, is that Putin's rise and rule cannot be reduced to either pure cynicism or pure ideological conviction. He is, per Ryzhkov, a genuine believer in Russian great-power restoration; he is, per Herd, the architect and referee of a power network organized around personal loyalty rather than institutions; and he is, per Ito, a leader whose confidence in his own and his country's power has, at critical junctures, badly outrun reality. None of these three descriptions is complete without the others.

"Putin has had a guiding ideology from his regime's earliest days. He consolidated power not merely to seize the country's wealth, but also to advance an ideal Russia and secure what he considered its rightful place in the world."
— Vladimir Ryzhkov, "Putin's Ideological State" (2024)
