The Northern Mali Conflict

A sixty-year Tuareg independence struggle merged with jihadist expansion, a military coup, and a French intervention — producing a war that never truly ended and a state that barely exists in half its territory.

Unlike many events on this site, the Northern Mali Conflict is ongoing. The Malian government does not control roughly two-thirds of the country's territory. Jihadist groups conduct attacks weekly. A military junta has replaced an elected government and expelled French forces and the UN peacekeeping mission. The situation described in this article continues to develop.

Background

When France drew its West African borders in the late nineteenth century, it cut across the Sahara without much attention to who already lived there. The Tuareg — a Berber-speaking, nomadic people who had traded, raided, and governed the central Sahara for centuries — found themselves divided between what would eventually become Mali, Niger, Algeria, Burkina Faso, and Libya. When those countries gained independence, the Tuareg gained very little. The new governments in Bamako and Niamey were dominated by sedentary, southern populations; the Saharan north was distant, poor, and governed almost entirely by neglect. Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024)

Mali achieved independence from France in September 1960. Within three years, Tuareg in the northern Kidal region had launched the first of what would become four armed uprisings against Bamako. The 1963–64 revolt was crushed militarily. The 1990–96 rebellion ended with a peace agreement (the National Pact) that promised northern autonomy but was poorly implemented. The 2007–09 uprising — the Tuareg Alliance rebellion — was suppressed with Algerian mediation. Each time, the pattern was the same: armed revolt, negotiated peace, failed implementation, accumulated grievance. By 2012, the north's structural marginalization had produced a political culture in which armed rebellion was the only language Bamako reliably heard. Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024)

Into this volatile situation came two forces that transformed a familiar regional conflict into something far more dangerous. The first was AQIM — Al-Qaeda in the Islamic Maghreb — which had been operating across the Sahel since the early 2000s, financing itself primarily through kidnapping European tourists and aid workers (an estimated $100 million in ransom payments between 2003 and 2012). AQIM embedded itself in northern Mali through strategic marriages into Tuareg families, social service provision — food distribution, basic medical care, dispute resolution — and careful cultivation of local networks that exploited the same state absence that fuelled Tuareg grievances. The second transforming force was the collapse of Libya in 2011. When Gaddafi fell, his stockpiles of weapons — including heavy weapons and surface-to-air missiles — flooded across the Sahara, and thousands of Tuareg who had served as mercenaries in his army returned to Mali armed and battle-hardened. Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024)

Key Figures

Iyad Ag Ghaly
Founder, Ansar Dine; Leader, JNIM
The most consequential individual in the Northern Mali conflict — and one of the least understood internationally. Ag Ghaly is himself a Tuareg from the Ifoghas clan of Kidal, who led Tuareg rebellions in the 1990s before radicalising toward Salafist Islam in the 2000s, reportedly influenced by Pakistani Tablighi Jamaat missionaries. His movement, Ansar Dine, provided jihadist legitimacy and AQIM connections to the 2012 rebellion; he later became the founding emir of JNIM, the dominant jihadist coalition, in 2017. He remains at large. Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024)
Moussa Ag Assarid
MNLA Spokesperson
The public face of the secular Tuareg independence movement, the MNLA (Mouvement National pour la Libération de l'Azawad). Ag Assarid, based partly in France, articulated the Tuareg case for an independent Azawad to international audiences, arguing that sixty years of marginalisation under Bamako justified self-determination. He represents the nationalist strand of the conflict that jihadists ultimately overshadowed.
Amadou Sanogo
Coup leader, March 2012
A relatively junior army captain who led the coup that toppled President Amadou Toumani Touré on March 21–22, 2012, claiming the civilian government had mishandled the northern insurgency. The coup backfired spectacularly: instead of strengthening the military's hand, it created a power vacuum in Bamako that accelerated the fall of the north. Sanogo was later charged with complicity in murder and political detentions; his case dragged through Mali's courts for years.
François Hollande
French President, 2012–2017
The French president who authorised Operation Serval in January 2013 after jihadist forces crossed the line between north and south and began advancing toward Bamako. The intervention was broadly welcomed at the time — even by critics of French neo-colonialism in Africa — but the subsequent ten-year mission (Operation Barkhane) became deeply controversial, stretching French resources and ultimately being expelled by the very juntas it had helped protect.
Assimi Goïta
Military junta leader; President since 2021
The colonel who led Mali's 2020 coup (ousting President Ibrahim Boubacar Keïta) and the 2021 coup (ousting the transitional president), making him Mali's dominant political figure. Goïta expelled French forces and the UN peacekeeping mission MINUSMA, invited Russian Wagner Group fighters, and has shown no credible timeline for returning to civilian rule. Under his government, jihadist attacks have increased rather than decreased.
Abdelmalek Droukdel
AQIM emir; killed 2020
The Algerian leader of AQIM who directed the group's Sahel expansion from the early 2000s until he was killed by French special forces in northern Mali in June 2020. Droukdel oversaw the kidnapping-for-ransom strategy that financed AQIM's growth, the alliance with Tuareg movements, and the 2012 advance across northern Mali. His death — though celebrated by counterterrorism officials — did not significantly weaken JNIM's operational capacity. Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024)

Key Events

January–April 2012
The MNLA Uprising and the Fall of the North
▼

On January 17, 2012, the MNLA launched coordinated attacks on Malian army positions in the north, beginning with the garrison at Ménaka. The movement had been formed just months earlier from returning Tuareg fighters from Libya and veterans of the 2007–09 uprising, and was formally committed to a secular, independent state of Azawad — the Tuareg name for northern Mali. Their advance was rapid; the Malian army, poorly paid and poorly equipped, struggled to respond. Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024)

The MNLA fought alongside Ansar Dine, Iyad Ag Ghaly's Islamist movement, and elements of AQIM and MUJAO (Movement for Unity and Jihad in West Africa). The alliance was uneasy from the start — the MNLA wanted a secular Tuareg state; Ansar Dine wanted Islamic law — but both wanted Bamako out of the north. On March 21–22, before the north had fully fallen, army captain Amadou Sanogo staged a coup in Bamako, claiming the civilian government was mismanaging the war. The coup produced exactly the opposite of its stated aims: the capital's political chaos created the vacuum that allowed the north to fall within days.

By April 1, 2012, the MNLA and its jihadist allies controlled Kidal, Gao, and Timbuktu — the three main northern cities. On April 6, the MNLA declared the independence of Azawad. No country recognised the declaration. Within weeks, the alliance fractured, and jihadists began pushing the MNLA out of the towns it had captured. The Islamist tail was now wagging the Tuareg dog. Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024)

Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024)
April–December 2012
Jihadists Impose Sharia Across the North
▼

After pushing the secular MNLA out of Gao and Timbuktu, Ansar Dine and MUJAO established Islamist governance across northern Mali. In Timbuktu — a UNESCO World Heritage site and a city of enormous Islamic scholarly heritage — jihadists imposed strict Sharia law: music and smoking banned, women required to veil, shrines of Muslim saints destroyed as idolatry. Over the following months, armed groups systematically demolished fourteen of Timbuktu's sixteen ancient mausoleums. The International Criminal Court later convicted Ahmad al-Faqi al-Mahdi for these destructions — the first ICC conviction for cultural heritage crimes.

The jihadists consolidated social control through a combination of coercion and co-optation. AQIM and its affiliates had spent years building local networks through strategic marriages into Tuareg and Arab families, provision of food and medicine in areas where the Malian state had provided nothing, and control of smuggling routes carrying cigarettes, drugs, and weapons across the Sahara. When they took formal control of northern cities, they were not purely external invaders — they had cultivated genuine local networks, however coercive. Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024)

In Bamako, political paralysis continued. The coup government was internationally isolated; ECOWAS (the West African regional bloc) imposed sanctions; a transitional civilian government was nominally installed but the military retained real power. The Malian army could not mount an effective counteroffensive. By the end of 2012, the situation was deteriorating: jihadist fighters began probing positions south of the Niger River, threatening a push toward the capital.

Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024) ICC (2016)
January 2013
France Intervenes: Operation Serval
▼

On January 10, 2013, jihadist columns crossed the line at Konna, the last major town before the southern heartland, and began advancing toward Bamako. Mali's transitional government requested French military intervention the same day. French President François Hollande authorised Operation Serval within hours — a decision that would have been unthinkable in most foreign policy contexts but which Hollande framed as stopping "terrorist" seizure of a sovereign UN member state.

French airstrikes and rapid deployment of ground forces halted the jihadist advance almost immediately. French and Malian troops, supported by Chadian forces, then conducted a series of offensives retaking Gao (January 26), Timbuktu (January 28), and Kidal (January 30). Hollande visited Bamako on February 2 to scenes of euphoric public celebration; France was, for a moment, celebrated as a liberator across Mali. Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024)

Operation Serval achieved its primary objective of preventing jihadists from seizing southern Mali, but it could not achieve what would have required: destroying JNIM's dispersed networks across a desert territory the size of France. The jihadists melted back into the Sahara and began a sustained insurgency of ambushes, IEDs, and attacks on French, UN, and Malian targets. The "French intervention" that was supposed to last weeks became Operation Barkhane — a nine-year mission that ended not in victory but in French expulsion by a junta that came to power partly through anti-French sentiment.

Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024)
2013–2020
Fragmentation, MINUSMA, and the Algiers Accord
▼

The United Nations deployed MINUSMA (Mission Multidimensionnelle Intégrée des Nations Unies pour la Stabilisation du Mali) in July 2013 — one of the most dangerous peacekeeping missions in UN history. MINUSMA ultimately lost 306 personnel to hostile action over its ten-year mandate, making it the deadliest UN peacekeeping mission of the modern era. The mission was mandated to support the peace process and protect civilians, but lacked the mandate and resources for the offensive counterterrorism operations that the situation arguably required.

In 2015, under Algerian mediation, the Malian government and Tuareg armed groups signed the Algiers Accord — a peace agreement that promised northern autonomy, Tuareg integration into the national army, and development investment in the north. Implementation was slow, incomplete, and eventually abandoned. JNIM (Jama'at Nusrat al-Islam wal-Muslimin) was formed in 2017, merging AQIM's Sahara branch, Ansar Dine, al-Mourabitoun, and the Macina Liberation Front into a single coordinated jihadist coalition. A rival group, ISGS (the Islamic State in the Greater Sahara, ISIS-affiliated), operated in the Liptako-Gourma tri-border area of Mali, Niger, and Burkina Faso. Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024)

As the security situation deteriorated and French forces sustained casualties without visible progress, anti-French sentiment grew in Mali. Protests accused France of prolonging instability for neo-colonial reasons. Russian social media disinformation campaigns amplified these narratives. Ibrahim Boubacar Keïta (IBK), elected president in 2013 and re-elected in 2018, faced massive street protests in 2020 over corruption and economic mismanagement. On August 18, 2020, the military deposed him.

Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024) UN DPKO (2023)
2020–Present
Juntas, Wagner, and a Fracturing State
▼

Mali's 2020 coup brought Colonel Assimi Goïta to power. A second coup in May 2021 consolidated his control further after he ousted the transitional government. The junta — formally the CNSP (National Committee for the Salvation of the People) — suspended the transition timeline, expelled French Ambassador Joël Meyer, and in 2022 formally ended Operation Barkhane, requiring France to withdraw its forces after nine years. The junta also ended MINUSMA's mandate in June 2023, with the UN mission completing its withdrawal by the end of the year.

In place of French and UN forces, the junta brought in the Wagner Group — Russian private military contractors who had also deployed in Central African Republic, Libya, Sudan, and Mozambique. Wagner fighters have been documented committing serious human rights abuses in Mali, including a massacre of an estimated 500 civilians at Moura in March 2022. The junta denied the abuses and the Russian government denied Wagner involvement. UN investigators and Human Rights Watch documented the killings extensively. Human Rights Watch (2022)

Today, the situation is arguably worse than when France intervened in 2013. JNIM and ISGS continue to expand southward, attacking markets, villages, and UN convoys. The Tuareg factions, who had nominally aligned with the Algiers Accord, declared a rupture with the agreement in 2023 and resumed fighting Malian army positions. Mali under its junta is internationally isolated from its West African neighbours (ECOWAS imposed sanctions; Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger formed the Alliance of Sahel States in defiance of regional institutions) and dependent on Russian military support. The state does not control its own territory. The war that began in 2012 has never ended.

Human Rights Watch (2022) Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024)

Turning Points

Turning Point 1
The March 2012 Coup That Opened the North

Captain Sanogo staged his coup in Bamako just as the north was about to fall, claiming the civilian government had mishandled the war. The coup produced the opposite of its intention: political paralysis in Bamako accelerated the military's collapse in the north, and Kidal, Gao, and Timbuktu fell within days. Had the civilian government remained intact, it might have negotiated a diplomatic solution with the MNLA — which, at that point, still controlled the rebellion — before the jihadists displaced it.

Turning Point 2
Jihadists Displace the MNLA from Northern Cities

After April 2012, MUJAO and Ansar Dine pushed the secular MNLA out of Gao and Timbuktu, taking sole control of northern governance. This was the moment the conflict changed character fundamentally — from a territorial dispute between a national government and an ethnic autonomy movement into a jihadist territorial project. Had the MNLA retained control of the north, a negotiated settlement with Bamako along the lines of the Tuareg National Pact was at least conceivable. With jihadists in control, it was not.

Turning Point 3
France's Decision to Stay After Serval (2013)

Operation Serval succeeded in its original objective within weeks. The decision to transition to the longer-term Operation Barkhane — a sustained counterterrorism presence across five Sahel countries — proved strategically problematic. France became responsible for a security situation it could not resolve, and the multi-year presence generated anti-French sentiment that the junta successfully mobilised for domestic political support. A faster exit after Serval, with more resources directed at Malian army capacity-building, might have produced a different outcome.

Turning Point 4
The Juntas Expel France and the UN (2022–2023)

When the junta expelled French forces in 2022 and MINUSMA in 2023, it removed the two main external constraints on both jihadist expansion and its own behaviour. The Wagner Group that replaced French forces had neither the training, the mandate, nor the political accountability of Western forces. The MINUSMA withdrawal removed the main mechanism for civilian protection and peace process monitoring. Both decisions were made by an unelected military government; neither was obviously reversible.

"The Tuareg rebellions were not simply ethnic conflicts or terrorist enterprises. They were the product of a structural failure of the Malian state to extend effective governance, services, or legitimacy to its northern territories in six decades of independence."
— Paradela-López & Jima-González, Resource Mobilization, Jihadist Networks and the Northern Mali Conflict (2024)

Why It Matters Today

The Northern Mali Conflict is not an isolated regional crisis. It is the most visible example of a broader Sahel collapse that is reshaping West African politics, refugee flows, European migration policy, and global counterterrorism strategy.

The Sahel Has Become a Jihadist Expansion Zone

JNIM and ISGS have expanded well beyond northern Mali into central Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger — countries that have also experienced military coups and are struggling to contain jihadist insurgencies. The broader "Sahel crisis" is now the world's fastest-growing jihadist conflict zone by territorial control and civilian deaths.

  • JNIM's territory has expanded significantly south of its 2012 origin in northern Mali
  • Burkina Faso lost two elected governments to coups (2022) amid jihadist expansion
  • Niger experienced a coup in 2023; its new junta also expelled French forces
  • The Alliance of Sahel States (Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger) is pursuing Russian alignment

The Tuareg Question Has No Answer

Sixty years after independence, the Malian state has not resolved the fundamental question of how northern communities are governed. Each time a peace agreement has been reached — 1992, 2006, 2015 — it has failed to produce genuine autonomy or development. The 2023 breakdown of the Algiers Accord means the conflict between Tuareg movements and the Malian state is again active.

  • Tuareg armed factions declared the Algiers Accord null in 2023 and resumed attacks
  • Kidal, the historic Tuareg stronghold, was retaken by Malian army and Wagner forces in 2023
  • Tuareg refugees have fled to Niger and Algeria
  • No political negotiation process is currently underway

Wagner's Africa Template

Mali became one of the most studied deployments of the Wagner Group before its partial reorganisation following the death of Yevgeny Prigozhin in 2023. Wagner's model — offering security services to African juntas in exchange for mining concessions and political influence — has been replicated across the continent. The Moura massacre and subsequent impunity illustrated the accountability vacuum that comes with Russian private military contracting.

  • Wagner reportedly received gold mining concessions in exchange for security services
  • The Moura massacre (March 2022) killed an estimated 500 civilians; no prosecutions followed
  • Wagner's African operations were partially absorbed into the Russian military after Prigozhin's death
  • France and the EU have designated Wagner's activities in Africa as destabilising

Migration and European Politics

The displacement produced by the Sahel conflict — and the broader instability it fuels — is one of the main drivers of trans-Saharan migration toward Europe. The collapse of Sahel security arrangements has contributed to European political pressure on migration, with direct effects on EU–Africa relations and European domestic politics.

  • An estimated 140,000+ Malians remain refugees in neighbouring countries
  • Sahel instability is a significant driver of migration through Libya to Europe
  • EU military training missions in the Sahel have been suspended or ended
  • Migration from West Africa is a contested issue in multiple European elections

Legacy & Debate

The Northern Mali Conflict challenges almost every framework commonly used to explain African conflicts. It is not simply an ethnic conflict (Tuareg vs. southern Malians), not simply a jihadist insurgency (secular nationalist movements were involved before and alongside Islamist ones), and not simply a failure of French military strategy (it was a failure long before France arrived). The core scholarly debate is about causation: which factor was primary — state failure, jihadist ideology, resource competition, or external intervention?

Paradela-López & Jima-González
— Resource Mobilization, Jihadist Networks and the Northern Mali Conflict (2024)
Apply Resource Mobilization Theory to explain jihadist expansion: groups like AQIM and Ansar Dine succeeded not because of pure ideology but because they mobilised local resources — finances (kidnapping), social capital (marriages, services), and networks (smuggling routes) — in a space where the state had mobilised nothing. Ideology was a tool of recruitment; resource control was the mechanism of power.
Human Rights Watch / UN Experts
— Various reports, 2012–2023
Documentation of systematic abuses by all parties — including Malian army, Wagner contractors, jihadist groups, and Tuareg factions — shows that the conflict produces civilian harm regardless of which actor controls territory. The junta's post-2020 claim that Russian partnership would improve security is contradicted by documented evidence that civilian deaths increased under Wagner's presence.
Bruce Whitehouse
Lehigh University — Research on Malian politics and identity
The anti-French sentiment that drove the juntas' political legitimacy is not manufactured; it reflects genuine resentment of French dominance that long predates the conflict. But the juntas have exploited legitimate grievances to insulate themselves from accountability. Mali's political crisis is as much about the failure of democratic governance as about security.

Was the Tuareg Independence Claim Legitimate?

The most contested political question about the 2012 crisis is whether the MNLA's declaration of Azawad independence had any claim to international legitimacy. The Tuareg case is structurally similar to other secessionist movements that have received varying degrees of international sympathy: a distinct ethnic group with a coherent territorial claim, subjected to sixty years of marginalisation by a centralised post-colonial state that drew borders cutting across their traditional territory. The parallels with the Kurdish case in Iraq, the South Sudanese case, and the Western Saharan case are not accidental. Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024)

The counter-argument — made by the African Union, ECOWAS, and virtually every UN member state — rests on the principle of territorial integrity: African borders, however arbitrarily drawn, cannot be renegotiated through armed force without opening a Pandora's box of secessionism across the continent's 54 states. This principle has been applied consistently in Africa in ways it has not always been applied elsewhere, and African leaders are generally sincere in their concern that recognising one secessionist movement creates precedents they cannot manage.

The practical reality is that neither the jihadist takeover of the MNLA's revolt nor the international community's refusal to engage seriously with Tuareg political claims has produced stability. Every peace agreement has failed. The argument that only greater autonomy — whether within Mali or in some form of confederal arrangement — can address the structural roots of the conflict is made by some scholars and is taken seriously by none of the actors currently in power. Paradela-López & Jima-González (2024)

The Bamako Perspective: Sovereignty and Territorial Integrity
  • Armed rebellion against a democratic government — however imperfect — cannot be the mechanism for resolving political grievances in international law
  • The MNLA's alliance with jihadist groups from the outset undermines its claim to be a purely nationalist movement
  • Azawad independence would set precedents across the Sahel affecting multiple states' territorial integrity
  • The Malian state has limited capacity but has attempted multiple peace processes; the failures are sharedWhitehouse
  • Jihadist actors who drove the conflict have no political legitimacy under any framework, including Tuareg ones

The conflict also reveals a fundamental tension in international counterterrorism strategy. France spent nine years and significant blood and treasure in the Sahel; the result was not stability but the creation of anti-French sentiment that a series of juntas exploited for domestic legitimacy. The lesson — that military counterterrorism without political solutions addressing state legitimacy does not work — was understood by analysts from the beginning and ignored by policy-makers throughout. The Sahel today is more unstable than in 2012.

"The jihadist groups that dominate northern Mali did not appear from outside. They grew in the gap between what the Malian state promised and what it delivered — and in that gap they built schools, hospitals, and courts, while offering a moral framework that spoke to communities the state had ignored."
— Paradela-López & Jima-González, Resource Mobilization, Jihadist Networks and the Northern Mali Conflict (2024)
