Foreign Policy Doctrine · Declared September 2001

The War on Terror

The doctrine that took the United States from Kabul to Baghdad in the name of fighting terrorism — and whose internal contradictions helped produce the very insurgency, and later the caliphate, it was meant to prevent.

What Was the War on Terror?

The "War on Terror" is the name given to the doctrine and the resulting global campaign the United States declared following the September 11, 2001 attacks — a framework that justified the invasion of Afghanistan in October 2001, the invasion of Iraq in March 2003, a global expansion of surveillance and detention powers, and two decades of military, intelligence, and legal action against transnational terrorist organizations. Unlike a conventional war against a state, the War on Terror was declared against a tactic and a category of non-state actor, with no clearly defined end state, no single enemy government to defeat, and — as President Bush's own 2002 National Strategy for Homeland Security quietly acknowledged — no realistic prospect of total victory.

This concept page focuses on the doctrine and ideas that justified the War on Terror — the intellectual and political architecture behind the decisions to invade Afghanistan and Iraq, and how that architecture shaped the later fight against ISIS. Dedicated pages exist elsewhere on this site for the Iraq War itself and, in time, for the Afghanistan war specifically; this page is concerned with the overarching doctrine that connected them, rather than the blow-by-blow military history of either conflict.

Definition

A doctrine, not a single war

Scholars studying the War on Terror have emphasized that it was never a single coherent military campaign but rather a flexible doctrine — "remarkably dynamic" and applied selectively, country by country, according to shifting U.S. strategic priorities rather than any consistent standard. It encompassed conventional invasions (Afghanistan, Iraq), covert drone campaigns (Pakistan, Yemen, Somalia), detention and interrogation programs (Guantanamo Bay, CIA black sites), and a vast expansion of domestic surveillance (the USA PATRIOT Act) — a heterogeneous set of policies unified more by shared rhetorical justification than by strategic coherence.

Origins: The Bush Doctrine

President George W. Bush first used the phrase "war on terror" in a September 20, 2001 address to a joint session of Congress, declaring that the campaign "will not end until every terrorist group of global reach has been found, stopped, and defeated." The doctrine that followed — later termed the "Bush Doctrine" — rested on several linked claims: that the September 11 attacks constituted an act of war rather than a criminal matter, justifying a military rather than purely law-enforcement response; that states harboring or supporting terrorist organizations bore responsibility equivalent to the terrorists themselves (the basis for invading Afghanistan to remove the Taliban, which had hosted al-Qaeda); and that the United States retained the right to act preemptively against emerging threats rather than waiting to be attacked, a claim that would become the central justification for the 2003 invasion of Iraq.

The "with us or against us" framing Bush used in the same September 2001 address became one of the doctrine's most consequential features: it converted counterterrorism cooperation into a binary loyalty test for foreign governments, reshaping alliance structures overnight. Pakistan pivoted from a period of strained relations with Washington to become a nominal frontline coalition partner; India, previously non-aligned, offered bases and intelligence sharing in a strategic pivot toward the U.S. that would culminate years later in deepened defense cooperation agreements.

The Core Justification

The intellectual justification for the War on Terror rested on treating two distinct interventions — Afghanistan and Iraq — as a single continuous campaign against a single category of threat, even though the evidentiary basis for each was very different.

Afghanistan: A Stronger Just-War Case

Evaluated against classical just-war criteria — just cause, last resort, proper authority, right intention, reasonable chance of success, and proportional ends — scholars applying this framework find the 2001 Afghanistan intervention had a genuine, if imperfect, justification: al-Qaeda had conducted a mass-casualty attack from Afghan soil, and the Taliban government had refused to hand over its leadership. Where the Afghanistan case fails the just-war framework is in its later stages — a 20-year occupation that scholars argue failed the "last resort" and "proportional ends" tests well before the 2021 withdrawal.

Iraq: A Justification Built on a Symbol

The 2003 Iraq invasion, by contrast, is assessed by the same just-war framework as failing almost entirely: there was no operational link between Saddam Hussein's government and the September 11 attacks, and the alleged weapons of mass destruction program cited as justification did not exist. Analysts argue the invasion was justified politically by invoking the emotional symbol of 9/11 and the broader War on Terror framing, despite the absence of a direct evidentiary connection — a rhetorical extension of the doctrine beyond what its own logic could support.

Preemption as Doctrine

The 2002 National Security Strategy formally articulated a doctrine of preemptive war — the claim that the United States could act against threats before they fully materialized, rather than only in self-defense against an actual attack. This represented a significant departure from prior international law norms around the use of force, and its invocation to justify Iraq (based on since-discredited WMD intelligence) is widely cited as having damaged the doctrine's credibility for any future preemptive claims.

Democracy Promotion as Justification

As the WMD rationale for Iraq collapsed under scrutiny, the Bush administration increasingly emphasized a secondary justification: that removing authoritarian governments and promoting democracy in the Middle East would, over the long run, drain the ideological and social conditions that produced terrorism. This "freedom agenda" framing extended the War on Terror's logic from counterterrorism into a broader project of regional political transformation — a much larger and more open-ended undertaking than the original post-9/11 mandate implied.

The Just-War Verdict

"Nothing global" about the Global War on Terror

Scholars applying orthodox terrorism theory and just-war criteria to the full two-decade campaign conclude that it was neither truly global nor consistently principled — it was selective and country-specific, pursued vigorously in some contexts (Afghanistan, Iraq, Pakistan's tribal areas) while tolerating or even overlooking terrorism sponsorship in strategically important partner states. The clearest example cited is Pakistan's documented "double game": simultaneously serving as a nominal U.S. coalition partner while state security services provided sanctuary and support to the Taliban and the Haqqani Network — a contradiction that persisted for the entire duration of the Afghanistan war with only sporadic U.S. pushback.

From Afghanistan to Iraq

The doctrinal move that most shaped the subsequent two decades was the decision to treat Iraq as a natural extension of the campaign begun in Afghanistan, rather than as a separate strategic question requiring its own independent justification. Once the War on Terror was established as an open-ended campaign against "terrorist groups of global reach" and the states that supported them, the door was rhetorically open to apply that logic to any government the administration wished to characterize as a sponsor of terrorism or a proliferation risk — regardless of whether a concrete operational link to al-Qaeda existed.

The consequences of this doctrinal overextension are, according to multiple scholarly assessments, severe and largely self-inflicted. Bruce Hoffman, a leading terrorism scholar, has characterized the 2003 Iraq invasion as "the worst failure of the U.S. government in its fight against terrorism" — arguing that it diverted military and intelligence resources away from finishing the campaign against a weakened al-Qaeda in Afghanistan and Pakistan, while simultaneously creating, through the U.S. occupation's mismanagement of post-invasion Iraq, the exact conditions of chaos, unemployment, and Sunni political marginalization that allowed a new and more extreme jihadist movement to take root.

The direct organizational lineage is well documented: al-Qaeda in Iraq, founded by Abu Musab al-Zarqawi during the U.S. occupation, absorbed large numbers of former Iraqi Ba'athist military and security officers left unemployed by the 2003 de-Baathification order. This organization would survive Zarqawi's 2006 death and the U.S. troop surge, rebuild during the Syrian Civil War from 2011 onward, and re-emerge in 2014 as the Islamic State (ISIS) — seizing roughly a third of Iraqi and Syrian territory and declaring a caliphate. Some analysts extend this causal chain further, arguing that the broader "mood and policies" the War on Terror generated across the region — including regime-change interventions in Iraq and Libya — are causally linked to the destabilization that later fed the Arab Spring's most violent trajectories, particularly in Syria and Libya.

The Domestic Politics of Fear

The War on Terror was not simply imposed on the American public from above — it enjoyed broad, sustained public support for policies that would otherwise have been considered dramatic infringements on civil liberties, and understanding why requires looking at public psychology rather than only elite decision-making.

Survey research examining American public opinion during the mid-2000s found a striking and counterintuitive pattern: increased perceived personal threat from terrorism did not primarily push authoritarian-leaning individuals toward greater support for "hawkish," civil-liberties-restricting policies — those individuals already supported such policies regardless of threat level. Instead, it was ordinary, low-authoritarian Americans who shifted sharply toward supporting expansive government power as their perceived threat increased. In one national survey, support for media self-censorship of counterterrorism methods rose from roughly 20% to roughly 80% among low-authoritarian respondents as their perceived personal threat rose from "not worried" to "very worried" — converging with the already-high 80–90% support such policies enjoyed among high-authoritarian respondents regardless of threat perception.

Mainstream, Not Fringe

Broad public support made expansive War on Terror policy politically low-cost

The same body of research found substantial baseline public support in the mid-2000s for policies that would once have been considered exceptional: 71% supported expanded public video surveillance, 65% supported media self-censorship of counterterrorism methods, 48% supported torture of captured terrorism suspects, and 47% supported warrantless wiretapping. This finding matters for understanding the doctrine's political durability: policies like the PATRIOT Act's surveillance expansions, indefinite detention at Guantanamo Bay, and the CIA's post-9/11 interrogation program were not narrow elite impositions overriding public opposition — they reflected, and were reinforced by, a genuinely mainstream threat-driven shift in public opinion.

The Victory Trap

A recurring feature of the War on Terror's domestic politics has been what scholars term the "victory trap": every U.S. president since Bush has been politically compelled to use maximalist "victory" and "defeat" rhetoric about a conflict that internal government strategy documents — including Bush's own 2002 National Strategy for Homeland Security — acknowledged could never be definitively "won," since some baseline level of terrorist risk was described internally as "a permanent condition" the country would have to accept.

This produces a self-reinforcing political bind rooted in Vietnam-era precedent: presidents fear the domestic political cost of appearing to "lose" a war more than they fear the strategic incoherence of promising an unattainable total victory, so they keep promising victory — and then are attacked by political opponents whenever events fail to validate that rhetoric. Bush's May 2003 "Mission Accomplished" banner became a symbol of exactly this dynamic when the Iraqi insurgency intensified in the following years; his 2006 "National Strategy for Victory in Iraq" was later acknowledged by his own aide Peter Feaver to have been, in substantial part, a public relations document rather than a strategic reassessment.

Obama's 2013 National Defense University speech attempted to break the cycle by redefining "victory against terrorism" in modest, normalized terms — describing success as ordinary life continuing (parents taking children to school, fans attending ballgames) rather than the eradication of the threat. Conservative critics attacked the speech as prematurely declaring victory, and ISIS's dramatic rise the following year was widely cited as vindicating those critics. Trump repeatedly declared premature victory over ISIS in Syria, in December 2018 and again in October 2019, both times contradicted almost immediately by his own senior officials and reversed under bipartisan pressure. Biden's 2021 Afghanistan withdrawal, intended to escape the trap entirely by declaring "neither victory nor defeat," was instead overwhelmed by the chaotic optics of the Kabul evacuation — suggesting the trap operates independently of any single administration's rhetorical strategy.

From al-Qaeda to ISIS

The doctrine's clearest failure on its own terms is the emergence of ISIS in 2014 — a direct organizational and ideological descendant of the War on Terror's Iraq campaign that grew, at its peak, into a far more territorially ambitious and administratively sophisticated movement than al-Qaeda had ever been. Understanding why requires comparing the two organizations' underlying ideological priorities.

Shared Foundation, Different Priorities

Both al-Qaeda and ISIS share a Salafi-jihadist theological foundation, but comparative framing analysis shows their diagnostic priorities diverged sharply: al-Qaeda's antagonist framing centered overwhelmingly on external oppressors — the "Zionist-Crusader alliance," meaning primarily the United States and Israel. ISIS's diagnostic framing, by contrast, centered overwhelmingly on the alleged religious "deviance and apostasy" of rival Muslim and jihadist groups — the single largest category in ISIS propaganda by a wide margin — reflecting its self-conception as the sole legitimate representative of "true Islam" rather than primarily a movement against the West.

Caliphate Versus Vanguard

Where al-Qaeda's propaganda emphasized "hijrah and jihad" — joining a mobile, transnational armed struggle — far more heavily than calls to build a physical caliphate, ISIS inverted this priority entirely, making territorial statehood and caliphate governance its central prognostic goal. This reflects the two organizations' different origins: al-Qaeda emerged from a mobile, foreign-fighter mujahideen network with no fixed territory to defend, while ISIS emerged from an insurgency that had, at various points, held and administered Iraqi and later Syrian territory directly.

Institutional Inheritance from Ba'athist Iraq

ISIS's administrative and military effectiveness at its 2014 peak owed substantially to former Iraqi Ba'athist army and intelligence officers — men stripped of their careers and social position by the 2003 de-Baathification order — who provided planning, intelligence, and bureaucratic capacity that the jihadist movement alone had never possessed. This is a direct institutional link between a specific War on Terror policy decision (de-Baathification) and the operational sophistication of the organization that decision helped produce.

The War on Terror's Response to ISIS

The U.S.-led coalition campaign against ISIS from 2014 onward relied on a mix of airpower, Kurdish and Iraqi ground partners, and continued reliance on "decapitation" strikes against senior leadership — culminating in the 2019 killing of ISIS founder Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi, announced by Trump in terms that echoed the same "victory" framing and the same subsequent disappointment (ISIS's continued insurgent activity after Baghdadi's death) that had characterized the bin Laden killing eight years earlier.

Why It Matters Today

The Doctrine Outlived Its Own "End"

The August 2021 U.S. withdrawal from Afghanistan is often treated as the War on Terror's conclusion, but scholars studying the doctrine describe it as a "forever war" whose beginning predates 9/11 (in earlier U.S. counterterrorism operations) and whose ending is not a clean historical break — surveillance infrastructure, detention policy precedents, and military basing arrangements built under the doctrine's justification persist well past any declared endpoint.

  • Guantanamo Bay remained open with detainees held without trial decades after the doctrine's stated peak, an enduring legal and diplomatic complication
  • The 2001 and 2002 Authorizations for Use of Military Force remain the legal basis for U.S. counterterrorism operations against groups that did not exist when the authorizations were passed

The Iraq Precedent Shapes Every Subsequent Intervention Debate

The gap between the WMD justification offered for the 2003 Iraq invasion and the reality discovered afterward has become the central cautionary case invoked in every subsequent U.S. debate over military intervention, from Libya in 2011 to Syria in 2013 to Iran nuclear diplomacy — a credibility deficit the War on Terror's doctrinal overreach created and that persists in American foreign policy discourse.

  • Bruce Hoffman's characterization of the Iraq invasion as counterterrorism's "worst failure" remains a widely cited reference point in terrorism studies
  • The Pakistan "double game" precedent — a nominal ally covertly sponsoring the enemy — is now a standard analytical category applied to other complicated counterterrorism partnerships

The "Victory Trap" Still Structures Presidential Rhetoric

Every major U.S. counterterrorism decision since 2001 — from surge strategies to withdrawal timelines to individual leader "decapitation" strikes — continues to be evaluated by domestic political audiences through a "victory or defeat" binary that the doctrine's own strategic architects privately understood not to apply to a transnational, non-state threat. This dynamic will likely shape how future administrations discuss any residual terrorism threat for as long as counterterrorism remains a live domestic political issue.

  • Both the 2011 bin Laden killing and the 2019 Baghdadi killing were used by their respective administrations as evidence of "victory," and both were followed by continued organizational activity that complicated that framing

The Racialization and Legitimacy Costs Are Still Being Assessed

Scholars examining the War on Terror's broader legacy have highlighted its role in the racialization of Muslim communities within the United States and allied countries, its colonial-era intellectual roots, and the erosion of American democratic legitimacy through Guantanamo, CIA black sites, and the Abu Ghraib torture scandal — costs that critics argue were never adequately weighed against the doctrine's stated security benefits, and that continue to shape debates over immigration, surveillance, and civil liberties long after the doctrine's initial post-9/11 peak.

  • Area-studies scholars have specifically interrogated how academic knowledge production about Afghanistan and Iraq was itself shaped by the conditions of indefinite war the doctrine created

Legacy & Debate

Assessing the War on Terror as a doctrine, rather than as a set of individual military campaigns, requires weighing genuinely contested claims about intention, effectiveness, and cost that scholars have not resolved and likely cannot fully resolve given the counterfactual nature of the underlying questions.

Sitakanta Mishra & Neeraj Singh Manhas
The International History Review, 2025

Their analysis, applying just-war theory and orthodox terrorism theory, is the most systematically critical of the doctrine's coherence, arguing it was "selective" and country-specific rather than genuinely global, and that the 2003 Iraq invasion fails just-war criteria almost entirely. They are particularly detailed on Pakistan's documented double game as the doctrine's central and most damaging contradiction.

Jonny Hall
Foreign Policy Analysis, 2024

Hall's "victory trap" framework treats the doctrine's persistence less as a matter of strategic conviction and more as a structural political incentive problem facing any U.S. president — a more institutionally sympathetic reading that locates the doctrine's flaws in domestic political incentives rather than in any single administration's ideology or bad faith.

Marc Hetherington & Elizabeth Suhay
American Journal of Political Science, 2011

Their survey research reframes the doctrine's domestic durability as a mainstream psychological phenomenon rather than an elite imposition, showing that threat-driven support for expansive government power among ordinary Americans — not just committed hawks — was central to sustaining the doctrine's political viability through its most controversial years.

The case that the doctrine achieved real security gains
  • No successful large-scale, centrally-directed al-Qaeda attack on the U.S. homeland occurred after September 11, a outcome supporters attribute partly to the surveillance, intelligence, and military pressure the doctrine authorized
  • Removing the Taliban government that had hosted al-Qaeda addressed a direct and demonstrated threat with a defensible just-war rationale, at least in its initial phase
  • The eventual killing of bin Laden and later Baghdadi removed the founding leadership of two of the era's most consequential terrorist organizations
  • Broad public support for the doctrine's core measures reflected genuine, widely shared threat perception rather than manipulation of a passive public
The case that the doctrine was a strategic and moral failure
  • The 2003 Iraq invasion, justified through the doctrine's own logic despite no operational al-Qaeda link, is widely regarded by terrorism scholars as having caused more strategic damage than 9/11 itself by creating the conditions for ISIS's emergence
  • The doctrine's "decapitation" strategy, applied against bin Laden, Zarqawi, and Baghdadi in turn, is not supported by the empirical evidence as an effective long-term deterrent against organizational resilience
  • Guantanamo Bay, CIA black sites, and the Abu Ghraib torture scandal inflicted lasting damage on U.S. moral authority and international legitimacy that outlasted any specific tactical counterterrorism gain
  • The doctrine's selective application — tolerating Pakistani state complicity with the Haqqani Network for two decades while invading Iraq on a false WMD premise — undermines any claim that it was principled rather than opportunistic

The most defensible synthesis is that the War on Terror succeeded at its narrowest possible goal — preventing a repeat of a centrally-planned, mass-casualty al-Qaeda attack on U.S. soil — while failing badly at the much larger goals its own architects articulated: eliminating terrorism as a strategic threat, stabilizing Afghanistan and Iraq, and demonstrating that democracy promotion could be achieved through military intervention. As one assessment puts it, the doctrine managed terrorism rather than eliminating it, with ISIS's 2014 emergence standing as the clearest evidence that "management" is a more accurate description of the outcome than "victory" ever was.

"We have to accept some level of terrorist risk as a permanent condition."
2002 National Strategy for Homeland Security — a private acknowledgment that sat uneasily alongside two decades of public "victory" rhetoric
