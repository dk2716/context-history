Origins & Rise to Power

Ruhollah Khomeini was born in 1902 in the small town of Khomein, in central Iran, to a family of Shia clerics. His father was killed when he was five months old; he was raised by his mother and aunt, both of whom died when he was fifteen. These early losses are rarely dwelled on in his hagiography, but they shaped a man who would prove extraordinarily self-contained, emotionally austere, and difficult to read — even to those closest to him. He was educated in the traditional clerical system, studying at the seminary in Arak and then Qom, the centre of Shia Islamic scholarship in Iran.

By the 1940s and 1950s, Khomeini had established himself as a respected religious scholar — but not yet a political one. Iranian Shia clerical tradition had historically been quietist: the senior clerics stayed out of politics, believing temporal government was inherently corrupt and that the clergy's role was to preserve religious law until the return of the Hidden Imam. Khomeini broke decisively with this tradition. He came to believe that waiting was a betrayal — that Islamic scholars had a duty to govern, not merely to advise.

His public opposition to the Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi began in earnest in 1963, when he denounced the Shah's "White Revolution" — a package of land reforms, women's suffrage, and secularisation measures that Khomeini saw as an attack on Islam and a capitulation to American influence. His arrest triggered riots in Qom and Tehran in June 1963 (the "15 Khordad" uprising) in which the government killed hundreds. The Shah chose exile over martyrdom: Khomeini was expelled to Turkey in 1964, then moved to Najaf in Iraq, where he spent most of the next fourteen years.

It was from Najaf that Khomeini developed his theory of Velayat-e Faqih — the Guardianship of the Islamic Jurist — in a series of lectures in 1970. The theory held that in the absence of the Hidden Imam, the most senior and qualified Islamic scholar had a duty and a right to govern. This was not a conservative reading of Shia tradition; it was a revolutionary innovation that overturned centuries of clerical quietism. When the revolution came in 1979, this intellectual framework was ready to be instantiated as a constitution.

Worldview: Velayat-e Faqih and Political Islam

Khomeini's worldview was a synthesis of Shia theology, anti-colonial politics, and a specific theory of legitimate government that had no real precedent in Islamic history. Understanding it is essential to understanding the Islamic Republic — because Iran's constitutional structure is his direct creation and remains in force today.

Velayat-e Faqih — Clerical Rule

Khomeini's core innovation: the most qualified Islamic jurist (faqih) must govern the state, not merely advise it. This was not the traditional Shia view. His 1970 lectures argued that Islam was a complete political system, not just a private faith, and that the clergy had an obligation to govern directly. The Islamic Republic's constitution enshrined this as the office of Supreme Leader — above the elected president, the parliament, and all other institutions.

Neither East nor West

"Neither East nor West, only the Islamic Republic" was the revolution's slogan, and it was more than rhetoric. Khomeini genuinely opposed both American capitalism and Soviet communism as alien systems being imposed on Muslim societies. He saw the Shah as an American puppet and the Soviet-aligned Tudeh Party as equally dangerous. The revolution initially drew support from leftists, liberals, and Islamists united against the Shah — Khomeini systematically destroyed the first two after winning.

Anti-Imperialism

Khomeini framed Iranian politics — and global politics — as a struggle between the "oppressors" (mustakberin) and the "oppressed" (mustaz'afin). America was the "Great Satan," not for cultural reasons but because of its support for the Shah and its broader dominance of Muslim-majority countries. This anti-imperial frame gave the revolution a universalist appeal — not just to Iranians, but to Muslims globally — which is why Hezbollah in Lebanon and Houthi militants in Yemen still operate within Khomeini's ideological framework.

Islamic Unity vs. Shia Sectarianism

Khomeini spoke the language of Islamic unity — Sunni and Shia together against imperialism — but the Islamic Republic he built was unmistakably Shia in its theology, law, and power structure. This contradiction haunted the revolution. Most Arab-majority Sunni countries viewed his revolution with alarm, not inspiration. Saudi Arabia — the other great pole of political Islam — saw the Islamic Republic as an existential sectarian challenge. The fault line runs to today's proxy wars.

The 1979 Revolution

1977–January 1979
The Shah Falls — How a Secure Monarch Lost Everything in Months
▼

By 1977, Mohammad Reza Shah appeared one of the most secure rulers in the world. He commanded a 400,000-strong military, a feared secret police (SAVAK) trained by the CIA and Mossad, and the support of the United States. Iran's oil revenues made it the fifth-largest military spender on earth. President Carter visited Tehran on New Year's Eve 1977 and called it "an island of stability in one of the more troubled areas of the world." Fifteen months later, the Shah had fled and an elderly cleric had taken power.

The speed of the collapse reflects two things: how brittle the Shah's modernisation had been and how broadly unpopular he was. The White Revolution had forced rapid urbanisation that created a huge class of poor, devout migrants in Tehran's shanty towns who had none of the Shah's secular modernity and all of the inflation. Oil wealth was being distributed upward, not downward. SAVAK's brutality had made the Shah's government feared and hated. When the street protests began in 1978, the military was unwilling to massacre enough people to stop them — and the Shah, weakened by lymphatic cancer he had concealed, lost his nerve for the necessary repression.

October 1978 – February 1, 1979
From Najaf to Paris — The Exile Who Became the Revolution
▼

In October 1978, Saddam Hussein expelled Khomeini from Iraq under pressure from the Shah. Khomeini moved to a suburb of Paris — Neauphle-le-Château — where he gave daily press conferences to an international media who were fascinated by the spectacle: a white-bearded 76-year-old cleric, sitting cross-legged on a carpet under an apple tree, promising to overthrow one of America's most important allies. From Paris, cassette tapes of his sermons were smuggled into Iran and played in mosques across the country. His message reached millions in a country with no internet, no social media — only revolutionary infrastructure built on religious networks.

The Shah left Iran on January 16, 1979, ostensibly for "a vacation." He never returned. On February 1, Khomeini landed at Mehrabad Airport in Tehran. An estimated five million people lined the route from the airport to the city centre. When asked by a French journalist what he felt returning to Iran after fifteen years, he replied: "Nothing." The answer — chilling in its detachment — told you something important about the man who had just become the most powerful person in his country.

February – December 1979
Destroying the Coalition — How Khomeini Outmanoeuvred His Allies
▼

The revolution that overthrew the Shah was a coalition: Islamists, Marxists, liberal nationalists, women's rights advocates, the bazaar merchants, and secular intellectuals had all marched together against the Shah. Khomeini had presented himself in Paris as a unifying figure who would establish democracy, not a theocracy. The constitution he had written in exile — Velayat-e Faqih — was not widely understood outside the clerical circles who had studied it. The secular coalition believed they had used Khomeini as a symbol; Khomeini intended to use them as foot soldiers.

Over the course of 1979, he neutralised every competing power centre. The left was initially welcomed into the revolutionary councils, then accused of atheism and dismantled. Liberal nationalist Prime Minister Mehdi Bazargan, who had assumed Khomeini genuinely supported parliamentary democracy, resigned in November 1979 when Khomeini endorsed the hostage seizure at the American embassy over his explicit objections. A constitutional referendum on the Velayat-e Faqih structure was held with a heavily restricted electorate. By the end of 1979, the Islamic Republic was a fact, and Khomeini held a position with more authority than the Shah had ever formally claimed.

Building the Islamic Republic

The Iran-Iraq War (1980–1988) defined Khomeini's decade in power at least as much as the revolution itself. Saddam Hussein, calculating that revolutionary Iran was weak and chaotic, invaded in September 1980. The war lasted eight years, killed an estimated 500,000 people on both sides, and used chemical weapons supplied by Western countries against Iranian troops. Khomeini's decision to continue fighting after 1982 — when Iranian forces had repelled the invasion and recaptured lost territory — is the most condemned strategic decision of his rule. He refused ceasefire after ceasefire, insisting on Saddam's removal as a precondition, allowing a war that could have ended in 1982 to grind on for six more years.

Key Institution

The Revolutionary Guards (IRGC) — Khomeini's lasting military legacy

Khomeini distrusted the regular Iranian military — it was the Shah's creation and had been led by officers trained in the United States. He established the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps in 1979 as a parallel military loyal to the revolution and to him personally. Today the IRGC controls an estimated 40% of Iran's economy, commands proxy forces in Iraq, Syria, Lebanon, and Yemen, operates Iran's ballistic missile programme, and is designated a terrorist organisation by the United States. It is one of the most consequential institutional legacies of the revolution — a state within a state that outlasted Khomeini and controls Iran's regional ambitions today.

November 1979 – January 1981
The Hostage Crisis — 444 Days
▼

On November 4, 1979, student militants seized the American embassy in Tehran and took 52 American diplomats hostage. Khomeini, who had not planned the seizure, recognised immediately what it gave him: a crisis that destroyed the moderate government of Mehdi Bazargan (who resigned within days), unified the country around anti-Americanism, and outflanked the left by making the Islamists appear the most radical anti-imperialists. He endorsed the seizure and the students became heroes of the revolution.

The 444-day hostage crisis destroyed Jimmy Carter's presidency, helped elect Ronald Reagan, and established a template of confrontation with America that Iran's government has maintained ever since. The hostages were released minutes after Reagan was inaugurated — the timing was not coincidental — which fed years of conspiracy theory about a deal between Reagan's campaign and Tehran. The crisis also established a dynamic that would define U.S.-Iran relations for decades: Iran learned that hostage-taking and proxy force projection produced political results in Washington.

July 1988
Accepting the Ceasefire — "Like Drinking Poison"
▼

In July 1988, Khomeini accepted UN Resolution 598 and agreed to a ceasefire with Iraq. His statement was one of the most remarkable self-condemnations any leader has ever issued: "Happy is he who has departed from this world and has not witnessed my humiliation. Taking this decision was more deadly than drinking hemlock. I submitted myself to God's will and drank this drink for His satisfaction." Iran was exhausted, militarily stalemated, and facing domestic food shortages. The war ended where it had begun, with the same borders, a half-million dead, and no political change whatsoever. It had been, by any measure, a catastrophic failure of strategic judgement.

Axworthy (2013)

The Dark Record

Mass Killing

The 1988 Prison Massacres — Iran's hidden atrocity

In the summer of 1988, following the ceasefire, Khomeini issued a fatwa ordering the execution of political prisoners who had not repented of their opposition to the Islamic Republic. A "Death Commission" of three judges — one of whom, Ebrahim Raisi, would become Iran's president in 2021 — went through the prisons conducting summary hearings. Prisoners were asked whether they accepted the Islamic Republic and would renounce their political affiliations. Those who refused were hanged. Estimates of those killed range from 2,500 to 5,000; human rights organisations believe the true figure may be higher. The executions were carried out in secret; families were not notified; bodies were buried in unmarked mass graves. The massacres are among the least-known mass atrocities of the 20th century — the Iranian government has never acknowledged them.

Dark Record

The Salman Rushdie Fatwa — weaponising religious authority against a writer

In February 1989, Khomeini issued a fatwa calling for the killing of British-Indian novelist Salman Rushdie, whose novel The Satanic Verses was deemed blasphemous. The Iranian government offered a bounty of $3 million. Rushdie spent a decade in hiding under British police protection. His Japanese translator was murdered; his Italian translator was stabbed; his Norwegian publisher was shot and nearly killed. The fatwa established a precedent that has never been withdrawn — as of 2022, the bounty had risen to $3.5 million and Rushdie was stabbed on stage in New York by an attacker who cited the fatwa as motivation. It is perhaps the only death sentence in history issued by a head of government against a foreign national for a work of fiction.

Why It Matters Today

Khomeini has been dead since 1989. But the political system he designed — the Islamic Republic, the Supreme Leader's constitutional supremacy, the IRGC as an ideological army — ran for 46 years on the logic he established. In 2025 and 2026, that system entered its most severe crisis since its founding. The question now is not whether Khomeini's legacy shapes the world, but whether the state he built is surviving the pressure of that legacy's contradictions.

The 12-Day War — June 2025

On June 13, 2025, Israel launched preemptive strikes on Iran's nuclear and missile infrastructure — the culmination of the low-intensity confrontation that had escalated since the 2024 direct exchange of strikes. The United States joined the operation on June 22, striking nuclear facilities at Natanz, Fordow, and Isfahan with bunker-busting munitions. A ceasefire was reached on June 25, mediated by Qatar and Russia. The strikes set back Iran's nuclear programme by an estimated several years — but the 12-Day War also killed hundreds of IRGC personnel and struck facilities the Islamic Republic had spent decades building.

  • The nuclear programme Khomeini quietly initiated in the 1980s — and that he believed was Iran's ultimate guarantee against regime change — was the primary target
  • The IRGC's air defence systems, built on Khomeinite logic of self-sufficiency and strategic depth, failed to prevent strikes on the facilities they were designed to protect
  • Iran's "Axis of Resistance" proxy networks — Hezbollah, the Houthis, Iraqi PMU — were unable to provide meaningful deterrence during the 12 days
  • The ceasefire was negotiated without the Islamic Republic achieving any of its stated war aims — the first time Iran had accepted such terms under direct military pressure

The 2025–2026 Uprising — The Largest Since 1979

Beginning December 28, 2025, nationwide protests erupted across Iran — spreading to more than 200 cities within weeks. The immediate trigger was economic: the combination of post-war sanctions, the currency's collapse, and fuel price rises. But the protests rapidly took on a political character that recalled 2022's "Woman Life Freedom" movement, and went further: crowds in multiple cities called explicitly for the end of the Islamic Republic, not reform of it. The crackdown that followed killed thousands — the largest death toll in any Iranian protest since the founding of the Republic.

  • The IRGC's domestic repression role — institutionalised by Khomeini precisely to protect the revolution from its own population — was deployed at full scale
  • Internet shutdowns, mass arrests of journalists and activists, and confirmed reports of executions of protest leaders followed the same playbook used in 2009 and 2019 and 2022 — but at greater scale
  • The protests revealed that the Islamic Republic's social base had contracted dramatically: the religious working class that sustained Khomeini was not defending the system at the scale it once had
  • International condemnation was issued by dozens of governments; no state provided material support to protesters — a pattern that repeated Khomeini's own observation about external powers and internal revolts

February 2026 — The Killing of Khamenei

On February 28, 2026, U.S. and Israeli forces launched a large-scale strike campaign explicitly aimed at regime change. In the course of those strikes, Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei — who had held the office Khomeini created since 1989, longer than Khomeini himself — was killed, along with key figures in the IRGC leadership. It was the first time a sitting Supreme Leader had died violently since Khomeini institutionalised the position. The constitutional succession mechanism Khomeini designed — the Assembly of Experts electing a successor — was triggered under active military pressure and internal chaos for the first time in its history.

  • Khomeini's constitution provides for exactly this scenario: the Assembly of Experts appoints a successor if the Supreme Leader dies or is incapacitated. Whether that mechanism can function under these conditions is unresolved.
  • The IRGC, which Khomeini designed as the guardian of the revolution rather than the state, faced the first real test of whether it could act independently of a Supreme Leader's authority
  • The Islamic Republic's survival as a political form — Velayat-e Faqih intact — remains contested as of this writing; what Khomeini built may endure in some form, be reformed, or collapse
  • If the Islamic Republic falls, Khomeini will be the only leader in modern history to have built an ideological state that outlasted him by nearly four decades before collapsing under external military and internal popular pressure

What Khomeini's System Could and Couldn't Do

The Islamic Republic's crisis is, in retrospect, legible from Khomeini's original design choices. He built a system optimised for surviving external pressure and suppressing internal dissent — not for managing a long-term social contract with a population that had modernised economically and demographically around it. He assumed the revolution would remain its own source of legitimacy indefinitely. It did not. The generation that grew up inside the Islamic Republic has largely concluded that it failed on its own terms.

  • Iran's youth population — among the most educated in the region — has faced some of the highest youth unemployment rates in the Middle East: a system that could not deliver the material life it promised
  • The mandatory hijab, which Khomeini imposed in 1979, became the central flashpoint of the 2022 and 2025 uprisings — a symbol of a system that chose ideological control over social consent
  • The IRGC's economic empire — built to sustain the revolution without dependency on state budgets — made it the dominant actor in the Iranian economy while also making it the target of the sanctions that impoverished ordinary Iranians
  • Khomeini's doctrine held that a just Islamic state would not need to fear its own people. The 46 years of repression that followed suggest the doctrine and the reality were irreconcilable from the start

Legacy & Debate

Khomeini is remembered in contradictory registers. In Iran, the official narrative is hagiographic: he is "Imam Khomeini," the founding father of the only political system since 1945 that successfully overthrew a U.S.-backed order and survived. For his supporters, this is almost miraculous — an elderly cleric against the CIA, SAVAK, and 40 years of American support for the Shah. For his opponents, both inside Iran and outside, he is among the most destructive political figures of the 20th century: the man who turned a genuinely popular revolution into a theocracy, killed his former allies, prolonged a catastrophic war, and built an authoritarian system that brutally represses the women, students, and secular intellectuals who made his revolution possible.

What he achieved
  • Overthrew one of the world's most secure U.S.-backed monarchies using non-military mass mobilisation — a remarkable feat of political organising
  • Created the only political system in the modern Middle East that successfully resisted American dominance and survived
  • Velayat-e Faqih was a genuine intellectual innovation — whatever its consequences, it was an original theory of Islamic governance
  • The Islamic Republic, for all its repression, has higher female literacy, better healthcare infrastructure, and broader democratic participation (within its constraints) than most of its regional neighbours
  • Inspired the global political Islam movement — demonstrated that an Islamic revolution was possible, affecting politics from Indonesia to Nigeria
What he destroyed
  • Betrayed the secular and leftist allies who made the revolution — systematically imprisoned and executed them after taking power
  • Prolonged the Iran-Iraq War for six years after the military necessity had passed, causing hundreds of thousands of deaths
  • 1988 prison massacres: the systematic execution of thousands of political prisoners — hidden atrocity, never acknowledged
  • The Rushdie fatwa: the only head of state in modern history to issue a death sentence against a novelist for the content of a book
  • Built a system of systematic oppression of women, minorities, and political dissidents that continues 35 years after his death
  • Created the IRGC, which has metastasised into a parallel state that controls Iran's economy, military, and foreign policy beyond any democratic accountability
The Contested Questions
  • Was the Islamic Republic what Khomeini always intended, or did the revolution radicalise him? The evidence is mixed: his Paris presentations emphasised democracy, but Velayat-e Faqih had been written a decade earlier. He appears to have been deliberately ambiguous about his intentions until power was secured.
  • Would Iran have been more stable without the revolution? The Shah's monarchy was not a stable system either — it was suppressing reform movements, using SAVAK to torture dissidents, and sitting atop massive inequality. The alternative to Khomeini was not liberal democracy but some other form of authoritarian modernisation.
  • Is the Islamic Republic reformable from within? This is the defining question of Iranian politics. The reform movement believed yes; the 2009 crackdown on the Green Movement suggested no. The "Woman Life Freedom" uprising of 2022 tested it again and found the same answer.
"Khomeini's greatest act of revolutionary deception was not overthrowing the Shah. It was convincing his allies that he would share power with them."
— Ervand Abrahamian, historian of Iran, Khomeinism (1993)
