Middle East · November–December 2024

The Fall of the Assad Regime

Eleven days. A 54-year dynasty. How a lightning rebel offensive out of Idlib ended the Assad family's rule of Syria — and rearranged the Middle East's balance of power in the process.

Overview

On December 8, 2024, Bashar al-Assad fled Damascus for Moscow, ending 54 years of Assad family rule over Syria and 13 years of civil war that had killed an estimated 600,000 people and displaced half the country's pre-war population. The regime did not fall to a foreign invasion or a negotiated transition. It fell to a rebel offensive that began in the northwestern province of Idlib on November 27, 2024, and reached Damascus in eleven days — a collapse so fast that even the rebels who led it, and the foreign governments watching, seemed genuinely surprised by how little resistance the Syrian Arab Army offered.

The offensive was led by Hay'at Tahrir al-Sham (HTS), a formerly al-Qaeda-aligned Islamist faction that had spent years rebranding itself as a nationalist, governance-focused movement inside its Idlib enclave, under the command of Ahmed al-Sharaa — better known by his nom de guerre, Abu Mohammed al-Jolani. Alongside HTS, Turkish-backed Syrian National Army (SNA) factions pushed against Kurdish-held territory in the north, the U.S.-backed Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF) seized the Albu Kamal border crossing with Iraq, and a separate Southern Front captured Daraa and Suwayda. Assad's government, hollowed out by over a decade of war, sanctions, and corruption, and abandoned by an exhausted army and a Russia distracted by Ukraine, simply did not fight.

What followed was not a clean transition. Sectarian violence resurfaced within months — attacks on Alawite communities on the Syrian coast, an attack on a church in Hama, and clashes between government-aligned forces and the Druze in Suwayda in mid-2025 that drew Israeli airstrikes on Damascus. Al-Sharaa, now interim president, has made real concessions — a new constitution in March 2025, outreach to religious minorities, a "zero-problems" foreign policy toward Turkiye, the Gulf, and even Israel — while facing a hardline faction within his own movement that considers those concessions a betrayal of HTS's founding jihadist project. The story of Assad's fall is inseparable from the unresolved story of what comes after him.

Why the Regime Was Vulnerable

Assad's government had survived the most dangerous years of the Syrian Civil War — the 2012–2016 period when rebel forces, including HTS's predecessor, controlled large stretches of the country and appeared close to Damascus itself. It survived because Russia intervened militarily in 2015 and Iran and Hezbollah provided ground forces and financing from 2012 onward. By late 2024, both lifelines had weakened. Israel's 2024 campaign against Hezbollah had decapitated much of its senior leadership, cutting off the fighters and logistics that had propped up Assad's ground forces for over a decade. Russia, absorbed in its war in Ukraine and reportedly irritated at Assad's resistance to a Turkiye reconciliation process Moscow had been brokering, offered only limited air support once the offensive began.

The regime's internal condition was, by every account, closer to a collapsing mafia state than a functioning government. Syria's economy had shrunk by an estimated 87% of pre-war GDP over the course of the conflict. Corruption was endemic at every level of the military and civil administration. Assad's own inflexibility at Astana peace process talks — his refusal to make even cosmetic concessions to Turkiye or to the opposition — left him with no political off-ramp when the military situation turned. Army morale, after 13 years of war, defections, and unpaid wages, had collapsed to the point that many units simply abandoned their positions rather than fight the advancing rebel columns.

The Synergy Nobody Planned

Turkiye, Israel, Saudi Arabia, and the U.S. did not coordinate — but their interests aligned anyway

Analysts studying the collapse have emphasized that no single foreign actor engineered Assad's fall. Instead, a set of otherwise conflicting external interests happened to point in the same direction simultaneously: Turkiye wanted leverage over the Kurdish-held northeast and had grown tired of an intransigent Assad; Israel had already crippled Hezbollah and had no interest in propping up an Iranian client; Gulf states had reduced their financial and diplomatic investment in Assad's survival; and the U.S., with 2,000 troops still in eastern Syria, was in a lame-duck transition period with limited appetite for intervention either way. It was this uncoordinated alignment — not any deliberate coalition — that gave HTS's offensive room to succeed.

The Eleven-Day Collapse

November 27–30, 2024
The Offensive Begins — Aleppo Falls in Four Days
▼

HTS forces broke out of their Idlib enclave on November 27, advancing on Aleppo — Syria's second city, which the regime had spent years and enormous casualties recapturing from rebels in 2016. This time, government forces barely contested it. Aleppo fell to HTS on November 30, a stunning reversal that immediately signaled the depth of the Syrian Arab Army's degradation. Government soldiers reportedly abandoned tanks and equipment rather than engage.

Gajić (2026)Hall, N. (2025)
December 1–5, 2024
A Failed Counteroffensive, Then Hama Falls
▼

The government attempted a counteroffensive around December 1 that failed to slow the rebel advance. Meanwhile, forces in the south began moving independently: the Southern Front captured Daraa — the city where the 2011 uprising had begun — and the Druze-majority Suwayda region, while the SDF seized the Albu Kamal crossing on the Iraqi border, cutting off a key Iranian supply route. On December 5, Hama, a strategically vital central city, fell to HTS with minimal resistance.

Gajić (2026)
December 6–7, 2024
Homs Surrenders — the Road to Damascus Opens
▼

Homs, the city that controls the strategic corridor between Damascus and the Alawite coastal heartland, was surrounded and surrendered on December 6–7 with little fighting. Its fall severed the regime's ability to retreat toward its traditional Alawite support base on the coast in good order and effectively opened the highway to the capital. Assad fled Damascus on December 7, reportedly departing for Russia the same night his palace guard abandoned their posts.

Gajić (2026)Hall, N. (2025)
December 8, 2024
Damascus Falls — the Assad Dynasty Ends
▼

HTS-led forces entered Damascus on December 8 without a defense of the capital materializing. State television announced the "liberation" of Syria; celebratory crowds opened the notorious Sednaya prison, releasing thousands of political detainees whose testimony over the following weeks would document torture and mass killing under Assad's security services at an industrial scale. Assad's 54-year family dynasty, and 61 years of Ba'ath Party rule in Syria, ended in a single day, with the government offering no organized resistance in the capital at all.

Winners & Losers

Assad's fall reshuffled a regional balance of power that had been frozen for over a decade. Analysts broadly agree that no single actor "won" decisively enough to permanently reshape the region, but the relative gains and losses were significant and immediate.

Iran — the Biggest Loser

Syria was the physical corridor connecting Iran to Hezbollah in Lebanon — the backbone of what analysts called the "Shiite arc" or "axis of resistance." Assad's fall severed that corridor overnight, isolating Hezbollah from its principal resupply route and eliminating Iran's most important Arab state ally. Tehran now faces pressure to lean more heavily on remaining proxies in Iraq and Yemen and, per some analyses, faces greater incentive to accelerate its nuclear program as compensation for the loss of conventional deterrence capacity in the Levant.

Russia — A Costly Setback

Moscow's 2015 intervention had been the single most important factor keeping Assad in power; his fall represents a major loss of prestige and strategic reach. Russia's naval facility at Tartus and air base at Hmeimim — its only military footholds on the Mediterranean and in the Middle East — became immediately precarious, dependent on the goodwill of a new government Moscow had no real relationship with, having backed the regime that new government had just overthrown.

Israel — "Mowing a Bigger Lawn"

Israel moved immediately and aggressively to exploit the power vacuum: roughly 500 airstrikes destroyed the bulk of Syria's remaining military infrastructure — air defenses, naval vessels, chemical weapons facilities — in the days after the collapse, denying any successor government (or Iran, opportunistically) the ability to reconstitute a conventional threat. Israeli forces also seized the UN-monitored buffer zone in the Golan Heights and occupied the strategic high ground of Mount Hermon, moves it described as temporary and defensive but which functionally expanded Israeli-controlled territory.

Turkiye — The Ambiguous Beneficiary

Turkiye is widely seen as the most likely beneficiary given its longstanding ties to Syrian opposition factions and its Syrian National Army proxies, and it moved quickly to press its advantage against Kurdish-held territory in the north. But HTS's relationship with Ankara has always been tactical rather than fully aligned, and Turkish leverage over an HTS-led government in Damascus is real but limited — Ankara is wary that al-Sharaa's government may prove less pliable than a purely Turkish-backed faction would have been.

The United States — Reluctant Gatekeeper

With roughly 2,000 troops still stationed in eastern Syria and considerable sanctions leverage over any successor government, Washington held significant cards without using many of them. The Trump administration's general instinct that Syria was "not our fight" limited direct engagement, even as the collapse curtailed Iranian influence and outmaneuvered Russia — outcomes broadly favorable to U.S. interests achieved with minimal U.S. investment or risk.

The Gulf States — Hedging Their Bets

Qatar emerged as the new government's most trusted Gulf partner, reportedly promising a 400% increase in Syrian civil servant salaries to help stabilize the transition. Saudi Arabia and the UAE's positions were murkier: the UAE in particular was reported to be exploring relationships with rival factions and warlords as a hedge against an HTS-dominated Damascus it did not fully trust, echoing its pattern of backing strongman alternatives elsewhere in the region.

HTS and the Radicals' Dilemma

Ahmed al-Sharaa's HTS did not emerge from nowhere. It traces its lineage through Jabhat al-Nusra — originally founded in 2012 as ISIS's Syrian branch before switching allegiance to al-Qaeda and then formally severing ties with al-Qaeda in 2016 in a rebrand that culminated in the 2017 formation of HTS. In its Idlib enclave before the 2024 offensive, HTS imposed a rigid form of Sharia governance: mandated Islamic dress codes, banned alcohol and gender-mixing, and ruled through a parallel "Salvation Government" that functioned as a proto-state.

Scholarship on post-civil-war Islamist movements describes what has been called "the radicals' dilemma": a rebel group that wins power on the strength of an uncompromising ideological project faces two competing pressures once it actually governs — external pressure (from foreign patrons, international legitimacy needs, and a war-weary population) to moderate, and internal pressure from "true believer" hardliners who joined the movement specifically because of its radicalism, and who view any softening as betrayal.

The Concessions and the Backlash

Sharaa has moved fast to moderate — and it has cost him internally

Since taking power, al-Sharaa's government adopted a new constitution in March 2025 that formally guarantees personal freedoms and women's rights while retaining Islamic jurisprudence as a source of legislation; reopened bars in Damascus; met with minority religious delegations; and offered Christmas greetings despite objections from more conservative clerics. Internationally, Sharaa pursued a "zero-problems" foreign policy — courting Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and Turkiye, engaging cautiously with Russia and Iran, and meeting President Trump in Riyadh in May 2025, after which the U.S. delisted HTS as a terrorist organization within two months in exchange for the group expelling foreign Palestinian militant factions. These moves provoked a backlash inside HTS's own ranks: a hardline splinter calling itself Saraya Ansar al-Sunnah emerged, reportedly denouncing Sharaa as an apostate for abandoning the movement's founding jihadist project.

Sharaa's government has tried to manage this tension through what scholars have termed "ideological hybridization" — distributing institutional power across three constituencies to keep each invested in the state's survival rather than in undermining it: HTS Salafis, who control the new national army of an estimated 40,000 fighters including several thousand foreign veterans; mainstream Islamists, represented in a new Supreme Fatwa Council established in March 2025 under a Sufi-leaning Grand Mufti; and nationalist pragmatists, who staff the Foreign Ministry and drive the "zero-problems" realist foreign policy. The regime's public narrative increasingly invokes a "neo-Umayyadist" framing — evoking Damascus's history as the seat of the Umayyad Caliphate — to try to unify Salafi, mainstream Islamist, and secular-nationalist Syrians under one historical story.

Comparative cases suggest the stakes of this balancing act. The Taliban's rigid 1996 governing model cost it international legitimacy for two decades; its somewhat more pragmatic post-2021 return still struggled with the same tension. Zimbabwe's Mugabe made genuine reconciliation gestures, at least initially, before reverting to authoritarian consolidation. Iran's Khomeini maintained a fragile liberal-religious institutional balance for barely two years before factional struggle resolved in favor of clerical hardliners. Whether Sharaa's Syria follows any of these trajectories, or forges a genuinely new path, remains the central open question of the post-Assad period.

Why It Matters Today

Sectarian Violence Has Already Resurfaced

Within months of the transition, violence broke out along the same sectarian lines the Assad regime had spent decades manipulating for its own survival. Killings targeting Alawite communities on the Syrian coast in March 2025, an attack on a church in Hama, and clashes between government-aligned forces and Druze militias in Suwayda in mid-2025 — the latter drawing Israeli retaliatory airstrikes on Damascus in defense of the Druze — all point to unresolved fault lines that a new constitution alone cannot settle.

  • Analysts identify at least three plausible escalation vectors going forward: Sunni-Alawite, Sunni-Druze, and Sunni-Kurdish/Turkish tension
  • Remnant Assad-loyalist militia, organized around former "Tiger Forces" commander Suheil al-Hassan, has reportedly regrouped in Alawite coastal areas and drawn renewed interest from Russia and Iran
  • The Kurdish-led SDF signed a nominal integration agreement with Damascus in March 2025, but a renewed Turkish-backed offensive against Kurdish forces at the turn of 2026 resulted in significant Kurdish territorial losses, underscoring how fragile that arrangement is

A Former al-Qaeda Affiliate Now Governs a State

HTS's transformation from an al-Qaeda franchise to an internationally recognized governing authority — accompanied by a U.S. terrorist delisting within eight months of taking power — is a case study with implications well beyond Syria for how the international community treats jihadist-descended movements that moderate their behavior once in power, and how durable that moderation can be judged to be.

  • The U.S. delisted HTS as a terrorist organization within roughly two months of the Trump-Sharaa Riyadh meeting; the UK and UN followed with their own delistings later in 2025
  • Sharaa's government controls Syria's oil-producing areas only partially — an estimated 70% of Syria's oil wealth sits in SDF-controlled territory, giving the Kurds significant leverage in negotiations over national integration
  • How Syria's transitional justice process handles former regime officials, including the Sednaya prison apparatus, will shape whether reconciliation or renewed cycles of revenge define the country's next decade

Iran's Regional Strategy Has Been Set Back a Generation

The severing of Iran's land corridor to Hezbollah via Syria is arguably the single most consequential strategic fact to emerge from Assad's fall. Decades of Iranian investment in Syria as forward-operating territory for its "axis of resistance" strategy were undone in eleven days, forcing a reassessment in Tehran of how it projects power in the absence of a friendly government in Damascus.

  • Israel's prior 2024 campaign against Hezbollah's leadership had already weakened the militant group before Assad's fall compounded the isolation
  • Some analysts argue the loss of the Syrian corridor increases incentives inside Iran to accelerate nuclear weapons development as a substitute deterrent for lost conventional and proxy capability
  • Reports of roughly 260 unemployed former Syrian chemical-weapons scientists have raised proliferation concerns independent of the broader geopolitical realignment

A Case Study in How Fast Authoritarian States Can Hollow Out

The speed of the collapse — a army with hundreds of thousands of nominal personnel disintegrating in eleven days without a serious defense of the capital — is now a reference case in the study of authoritarian state resilience, alongside the Taliban's 2021 takeover of Kabul. Both cases suggest that decades of apparent stability under repressive rule can mask an institutional hollowness that becomes visible only once real military pressure is applied.

  • Syria's economy had contracted an estimated 87% from pre-war levels by the time of the collapse — a material precondition for the army's disintegration
  • The regime's reliance on external patrons (Russia, Iran, Hezbollah) rather than domestic legitimacy meant that once those patrons' attention or capacity was diverted, no substitute source of cohesion existed

Legacy & Debate

Because the transition remains active and unresolved as of this writing, historical assessment of Assad's fall is necessarily provisional. But several analytical threads are already well established among scholars studying the collapse.

Natasha Hall
Survival, 2025

Hall's analysis frames the collapse through a "winners and losers" lens, emphasizing that Assad's regime had become a militarily weak, mafia-like state propped up almost entirely by external patrons whose attention and capacity had simultaneously eroded — Russia distracted by Ukraine, Iran's Hezbollah lifeline severed by Israeli strikes. She is notably cautious about declaring any single actor a clean winner, describing Israel's post-collapse posture as "mowing a bigger lawn" rather than achieving a decisive strategic breakthrough.

Raphaël Lefèvre
Survival, 2026

Lefèvre's "radicals' dilemma" framework is the most influential analytical lens applied to Sharaa's post-takeover governance. He situates HTS's moderation within a comparative pattern seen in other post-civil-war Islamist transitions — the Taliban, Khomeini's Iran, Mugabe's Zimbabwe — arguing that the tension between external moderation pressure and internal hardliner backlash is structural to this kind of transition, not unique to Syria, and that HTS's use of "ideological hybridization" across constituencies is a genuinely novel attempt to manage it.

Aleksandar Gajić
Politika nacionalne bezbednosti, 2026

Gajić's geopolitical analysis situates the collapse within longer-run classical-geopolitics framing of Syria and the Levant as a historically contested "intermediate space." He is the most skeptical of the sources examined here about the durability of Sharaa's government, describing the new regime as a "fragile coalition" whose ultimate loyalty to any of its external patrons — Turkiye, the U.S., Israel, or Russia — remains doubtful, and ranking Iran as the geopolitical actor that lost the most from the transition, with Turkiye and Israel gaining the most without either doing so decisively.

The case that the transition represents real progress
  • A 54-year dictatorship responsible for chemical weapons use against its own population and the Sednaya torture apparatus has ended
  • The new constitution's guarantees of personal freedoms and women's rights, whatever their limits, represent a formal improvement over Assad-era law
  • Sharaa's outreach to religious minorities and pragmatic foreign policy suggest a genuine, if incomplete, break from HTS's founding jihadist project
  • Iran's loss of its Syrian corridor reduces the risk of a wider regional war built around Hezbollah-Israel escalation
The case for deep skepticism
  • A formerly al-Qaeda-aligned organization now controls the Syrian state and its security services, whatever its rebranding
  • Sectarian violence against Alawites, Druze, and Christians within months of the takeover suggests the new government's control over its own armed factions is limited or its restraint is selective
  • Internal hardliner backlash (Saraya Ansar al-Sunnah) demonstrates that Sharaa's moderation is contested even within his own movement and could reverse under pressure
  • The speed with which Western governments delisted HTS and engaged diplomatically may reflect geopolitical convenience — curtailing Iran, outmaneuvering Russia — more than confidence in the new government's actual character
"None of Syria's external patrons — not Turkiye, not the United States, not Israel, not Russia — can be fully confident of the new government's long-term loyalty. It is a fragile coalition, assembled at speed, out of actors who agree on almost nothing except that Assad had to go."
Paraphrased from Aleksandar Gajić's analysis, Politika nacionalne bezbednosti, 2026
