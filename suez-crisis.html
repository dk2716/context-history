The Suez Crisis

The week Britain and France invaded Egypt to retake a canal — and the United States forced them to leave, ending the age of European empire and announcing that the Cold War's real superpower was in Washington, not London or Paris.

Background: A Canal, an Empire, and a Revolution

The Suez Canal opened in 1869 after ten years of construction, largely funded by French capital and Egyptian forced labour. It cut 7,000 kilometres off the sea route from Europe to Asia and almost immediately transformed global trade, naval strategy, and imperial competition. Britain had not been involved in building it; by 1875 it had bought a controlling stake from Egypt's bankrupt Khedive, and by 1882 it had invaded and occupied Egypt to protect its investment. For the next 70 years, the canal was managed by the Suez Canal Company — nominally an Egyptian-registered corporation, substantively a Franco-British enterprise that employed almost no Egyptians in senior positions and paid Egypt a modest royalty on the profits it generated from Egyptian soil.

When Gamal Abdel Nasser came to power in Egypt following the 1952 revolution, reclaiming the canal was a matter of both economic necessity and national pride. Egypt was poor; the canal was profitable; the canal was in Egypt. Nasser had spent years trying to negotiate a better arrangement with the British. He had also spent years trying to secure financing for the Aswan High Dam — the centrepiece of his economic programme, intended to control the Nile's annual flooding, generate electricity, and transform Egyptian agriculture. The dam required external financing on a scale Egypt could not supply.

In 1955, Nasser accepted a Soviet arms deal — purchasing Czech weapons to rearm the Egyptian military. The United States responded by withdrawing its offer to fund the Aswan Dam in July 1956, citing Egypt's "neutralist" alignment. The British followed. The World Bank withdrew its loan offer. Nasser's response was immediate and theatrical: on July 26, 1956 — the fourth anniversary of King Farouk's abdication — he delivered a three-hour speech in Alexandria. Buried in the speech was the word "de Lesseps" — the name of the canal's original French engineer, and the pre-arranged signal for Egyptian army units to seize canal installations across the country. The Suez Canal Company was nationalised. The shareholders would be compensated at market value. The canal's revenues would fund the Aswan Dam.

Key Figures

Gamal Abdel Nasser
President of Egypt
Nasser's nationalisation speech on July 26 was one of the great acts of political theatre of the 20th century. He had gambled that Britain and France would not risk a war that America would not support. The gamble proved correct. His prestige — already high in the Arab world after the 1955 Bandung Conference and his Czech arms deal — became enormous. Suez transformed him into the symbol of Arab anti-colonialism, a status that outlasted his actual military and political achievements.
Anthony Eden
British Prime Minister
Eden's hatred of Nasser was personal and irrational, his advisors recalled. He repeatedly compared Nasser to Mussolini and Hitler — comparisons that reflected his experience of appeasement in the 1930s but had little analytical value in 1956. Eden authorised the secret collusion with France and Israel, concealed it from Parliament, lied about it publicly, and destroyed his health in the process. He resigned in January 1957, officially for health reasons, actually because the crisis had broken him politically. The collusion was only officially admitted by Britain in the 1990s.
Dwight D. Eisenhower
President of the United States
Eisenhower was furious — genuinely, personally furious — that Britain and France had launched a war without informing him, nine days before a U.S. presidential election. He refused to support them at the UN, instructed Treasury Secretary George Humphrey to refuse IMF support that would stabilise the pound, and told his allies they could have a ceasefire or a currency crisis. They chose the ceasefire. Eisenhower's intervention was the clearest demonstration in history that American financial power could override European military power — a lesson that defined the post-imperial order.
Guy Mollet
French Prime Minister
Mollet's motivation was partly the canal itself but more immediately Algeria: Nasser was supplying the Algerian FLN with arms, and Mollet believed that toppling Nasser would collapse the Algerian rebellion. The calculation was wrong — the Algerian War continued for six more years — but it explains why France was the driving force behind the collusion with Israel, and why the Protocol of Sèvres was drafted at a villa outside Paris. France accepted the ceasefire reluctantly and with lasting bitterness toward what it saw as American betrayal of an ally.
David Ben-Gurion
Prime Minister of Israel
Israel's motivation for joining the collusion was more straightforward than Britain's or France's: Egypt had been blockading Israeli shipping through the Suez Canal and the Straits of Tiran since 1950, and fedayeen raids from Egyptian-controlled Gaza had killed hundreds of Israelis. Ben-Gurion agreed to Israel attacking first, creating the pretext for the Anglo-French "intervention," in exchange for French arms and a guarantee of British air cover. Israel achieved its military objectives — the Sinai was taken in 100 hours — but was forced to withdraw under U.S. pressure.
Dag Hammarskjöld
UN Secretary-General
Hammarskjöld negotiated the ceasefire, established the first UN Emergency Force (UNEF) — the first armed peacekeeping operation in UN history — and oversaw the withdrawal of British, French, and Israeli forces. His insistence that Egypt consent to UNEF's deployment rather than have it imposed was a principled stand for sovereignty that would have fateful consequences: Nasser's withdrawal of UNEF consent in May 1967 removed the buffer that had kept the peace, contributing to the Six-Day War.

The Crisis Unfolds

JULY 26, 1956
Nasser Nationalises the Canal — A Three-Hour Speech Changes Everything
▼

Nasser's Alexandria speech lasted three hours. Egyptian army units received their orders through a radio signal — the word "de Lesseps" spoken during the live broadcast — and moved simultaneously on canal facilities across the country. The nationalisation was presented as Egypt reclaiming its own sovereignty over its own territory. The Suez Canal Company, Nasser told the crowd, had been a tool of exploitation: it had taken from Egypt far more than it had given back, and now Egypt would take it back.

The reaction in London and Paris was immediate and violent. Eden told his Cabinet that Nasser could not be allowed "to have his hand on our windpipe." He ordered military planning to begin. The problem was that military planning required weeks, and world opinion — including American opinion — was moving quickly toward diplomatic resolution. The United States proposed an international Suez Canal Users' Association to manage the canal collectively. Nasser rejected it. Three months of fruitless negotiation followed while Britain and France secretly planned a military operation.

Kyle (1991)Heikal (1986)
OCTOBER 22–24, 1956
The Protocol of Sèvres — The Secret Collusion
▼

At a villa in Sèvres outside Paris, the foreign ministers of Britain, France, and Israel met in secret over three days. They agreed: Israel would attack Egypt on October 29, advancing toward the canal. Britain and France would issue an ultimatum to both sides to withdraw from the canal zone — a demand Egypt would obviously reject and Israel could easily comply with, since it was advancing. Britain and France would then intervene as peacekeepers, occupy the canal zone, and present the world with a fait accompli.

The plan required deceiving the United States, the United Nations, and Parliament. Eden was aware that American support was not forthcoming; he chose to proceed without it, calculating that the U.S. would not actively intervene against its closest allies. Eden was also aware that the operation might fail politically even if it succeeded militarily. He chose to proceed anyway. Three copies of the Protocol were signed. Eden later had the British copy destroyed. The French kept theirs. It was not officially acknowledged by Britain until 1996 — forty years after the events.

Shlaim (1997)Kyle (1991)
OCT 29 – NOV 5, 1956
The War — Israel Seizes the Sinai, Britain and France Bomb Egypt
▼

Israeli paratroopers dropped into the Sinai on October 29. Within 100 hours, Israeli forces had reached the canal and were in control of the entire Sinai Peninsula. Egyptian forces, outnumbered and surprised, fought harder than the Israelis had expected — particularly in the Gaza Strip — but could not hold the desert. The military result was swift and decisive.

On October 30, Britain and France issued their planned ultimatum: both sides must withdraw to ten miles from the canal. Egypt rejected it. Anglo-French aircraft began bombing Egyptian airfields on November 1, destroying most of the Egyptian air force on the ground. Port Said was attacked from the sea on November 5. British paratroopers landed on November 5–6. Egyptian resistance was fierce; approximately 3,000 Egyptians died in Port Said alone. The operation was militarily successful and politically catastrophic.

Kyle (1991)Laron (2017)
NOVEMBER 6–7, 1956
Eisenhower Forces a Ceasefire — Financial Pressure Ends the War
▼

Eisenhower had been kept in ignorance of the collusion. When the invasion began — nine days before the U.S. presidential election — he was furious. He immediately moved against Britain at the United Nations, sponsoring a General Assembly resolution calling for a ceasefire. He refused to send oil supplies to replace the European reserves that the war was disrupting. Most decisively, he directed Treasury Secretary Humphrey to block IMF emergency credits that Britain desperately needed to support the pound sterling, which was in freefall as investors fled in response to the crisis.

The message to British Chancellor Harold Macmillan was explicit: the United States would not support the pound until there was a ceasefire. Britain had spent £279 million in sterling reserves — roughly 15% of its total holdings — in the first week of the operation. Without American support, a sterling collapse was imminent. Eden called a ceasefire on November 7. The British and French withdrew by December 22. The Israeli withdrawal from the Sinai took until March 1957, completed under U.S. pressure and a UN guarantee of access to the Straits of Tiran.

Kunz (1991)Kyle (1991)
1957 ONWARD
The Aftermath — An Empire Ends, a Symbol Is Born
▼

Eden resigned in January 1957, succeeded by Harold Macmillan — who, as Chancellor, had delivered the financial ultimatum to Eden that made the ceasefire inevitable, and who then rebuilt the Anglo-American relationship by becoming one of the closest allies John F. Kennedy would have. The collusion was covered up for decades. Britain's imperial pretensions, never entirely realistic after 1945, were definitively ended. The psychological effect — on British political culture, on the British understanding of its own power — was enormous and lasting.

For Nasser, Suez was a transformational victory. He had lost the military battle; his air force had been destroyed, his army had been routed in the Sinai, and Port Said had been occupied. But he had won the political war — kept the canal, humiliated two European powers, and survived. His popularity across the Arab world reached its zenith. The Suez victory made pan-Arabism a genuine mass movement, not merely an elite ideology. For the next decade, every Arab leader who wanted popular legitimacy had to position themselves in relation to Nasser's achievement at Suez.

Turning Points

Turning Point 1
Eisenhower Was Not Informed — And Did Not Forgive It

The entire Anglo-French strategy depended on American acquiescence — on the assumption that Washington would not actively oppose its closest allies once the operation was underway. Eden made this calculation knowing it was a gamble. Eisenhower's fury at being kept in ignorance — particularly during an election week when Soviet suppression of the Hungarian uprising was also dominating the news — was genuine and decisive. Had Britain consulted Washington in advance, the operation would almost certainly have been vetoed before it began. The decision to deceive the United States was the decision that ensured failure.

Turning Point 2
The Protocol of Sèvres — Collusion as Strategy

The secret agreement that Israel would attack first, creating the Anglo-French pretext, was a masterpiece of tactical cleverness and strategic recklessness. It gave the operation a legal cover that fooled no one, required three governments to lie in public simultaneously, and handed Eisenhower a propaganda gift at precisely the moment the United States was condemning Soviet intervention in Hungary. The collusion was the crisis's original sin — it meant that Britain and France could never defend their action on the merits, because their stated justification (peacekeeping) was a fiction.

Turning Point 3
The Sterling Crisis — Financial Power Defeats Military Power

The British pound lost 15% of its reserves in a week. Eisenhower's refusal to support the IMF credit that would have stabilised it was the decisive intervention — more decisive than anything that happened on the battlefield. Harold Macmillan, who had been one of the most aggressive advocates for military action, immediately became one of the most urgent advocates for a ceasefire once he understood the financial position. The Suez Crisis established permanently that in the post-war world, the United States could translate financial dominance into political outcomes that military operations could not achieve.

Turning Point 4
Nasser Kept the Canal — Egypt's Sovereignty Transformed Arab Politics

Had the Anglo-French operation succeeded — had Nasser been toppled, the canal retaken, a more compliant Egyptian government installed — the entire trajectory of Arab nationalism might have been different. Instead, Nasser kept the canal. Egypt kept its sovereignty. The revenues from the canal funded the Aswan Dam. Arab populations across the Middle East drew the lesson that American power would constrain European imperialism — a lesson with complicated implications for their own subsequent relations with the United States. The outcome at Suez set the terms of Arab politics for the next decade.

"We are not at war with Egypt. We are in an armed conflict."
— Anthony Eden, November 4, 1956, in the House of Commons — attempting to avoid the legal and political implications of the word "war" for an operation whose justification was already collapsing

Why It Matters Today

The Suez Crisis is one of those events that looks like a footnote but was actually a hinge — the moment when the post-war world's power structure became visible and the European empires discovered that their military power no longer translated into political autonomy.

The End of European Imperial Autonomy

Suez demonstrated that European powers could no longer act independently in the world against American wishes. The lesson was absorbed differently in London and Paris: Britain chose the "special relationship" — subordinating its foreign policy to close alignment with Washington. France drew the opposite conclusion — it needed an independent defence capability and an independent foreign policy, leading eventually to de Gaulle's withdrawal from NATO's military command structure in 1966.

  • Britain accelerated its decolonisation programme after Suez — Macmillan's 1960 "Wind of Change" speech was the direct ideological consequence
  • France developed its own nuclear deterrent partly as a result of Suez
  • The crisis accelerated European integration as a counterweight to U.S. dominance

Nasser's Legacy and the Pan-Arab Dream

Suez made Nasser the dominant figure in Arab politics for the next decade. The United Arab Republic — the 1958 union of Egypt and Syria — was the high-water mark of pan-Arab nationalism, driven directly by Nasser's post-Suez prestige. The dream collapsed with the 1967 Six-Day War, in which Nasser's Egypt was catastrophically defeated by Israel. The arc from Suez triumph to 1967 defeat defines the rise and fall of pan-Arab nationalism as a governing ideology.

  • The UAR (Egypt-Syria union) lasted only 1958–1961 before Syria seceded
  • Nasser's removal of UN peacekeepers from the Sinai in 1967 directly triggered the Six-Day War
  • Post-Suez Arab nationalism shaped every Middle Eastern political movement for two decades

The Canal Remains a Chokepoint

The Suez Canal is still one of the world's most strategically important waterways — handling roughly 12% of global trade and 30% of global container traffic. Its vulnerability was demonstrated in March 2021 when the Ever Given container ship ran aground and blocked the canal for six days, temporarily disrupting an estimated $9.6 billion per day in trade. The 1973 Arab-Israeli War closed the canal for eight years. These disruptions reveal the same structural vulnerability that made the canal worth fighting over in 1956.

  • The Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping in 2023–24 forced major shipping companies to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope
  • Canal revenues are now Egypt's third-largest foreign exchange earner after tourism and remittances
  • Egypt expanded the canal in 2015 — a $8.2 billion project seen as a statement of sovereignty

The UN Emergency Force and Peacekeeping's Limits

The first UN peacekeeping force — UNEF — was created specifically for Suez. It established the template for UN peacekeeping: consent-based, lightly armed, interposed between parties that had already agreed to stop fighting. The model's central limitation was exposed in 1967 when Nasser demanded UNEF's withdrawal and the UN complied: a peacekeeping force that depends on host-nation consent cannot protect against a host nation that chooses war. Every subsequent debate about peacekeeping has returned to this tension.

  • UNEF remained in place from 1956 to 1967 — eleven years of successful deterrence
  • Its withdrawal in May 1967 directly preceded the Six-Day War
  • The consent-versus-enforcement tension in peacekeeping doctrine dates to Suez

Legacy & Debate

Historians broadly agree on the basic facts of Suez: the collusion happened, Eisenhower stopped it, and the result was a humiliation for Britain and France that accelerated decolonisation. The debates are about motivation, counterfactuals, and the crisis's place in the long history of Western intervention in the Middle East.

Keith Kyle
Chatham House · Suez: Britain's End of Empire in the Middle East (1991)
The definitive British account — comprehensive, archivally rigorous, and balanced. Kyle traces the crisis from Nasser's nationalisation through Eden's planning, the Sèvres collusion, the military operation, and the American intervention. He is particularly strong on the internal British decision-making process and Eden's psychological deterioration during the crisis. Essential reading. Available in major libraries.
Avi Shlaim
Oxford University · The Protocol of Sèvres, 1956 (1997, International Affairs)
Shlaim's account of the secret collusion, based on newly available Israeli and French documents, is the most precise reconstruction of what happened at the villa in Sèvres. He argues that the collusion was not a conspiracy of last resort but a plan that the French had been nurturing for months, with Israel as the willing instrument. His account makes clear that Eden's public denials were straightforwardly false. Available in academic databases.
Guy Laron
Hebrew University · The Six-Day War: The Breaking of the Middle East (2017)
Laron situates Suez within the longer arc of Cold War competition in the Middle East, arguing that the crisis's real significance was not the Anglo-French humiliation but the Soviet-American competition for influence it revealed. Both superpowers used the crisis — Eisenhower to assert hegemony over Europe, Khrushchev to issue nuclear threats against Britain and France — and the crisis accelerated the militarisation of Arab-Israeli conflict that produced 1967. A valuable corrective to accounts that focus exclusively on the European dimension.

  • Nasser had blocked Israeli shipping through the canal since 1950 in violation of the 1888 Constantinople Convention — Shlaim (1997)
  • Fedayeen raids from Egyptian-controlled Gaza had killed hundreds of Israeli civilians — Ben-Gurion archives
  • Britain had legitimate security interests in unobstructed passage through a canal handling 40% of its trade — Kyle (1991)
  • The collusion aside, the legal basis for international management was not unreasonable given canal's global importance — contested
