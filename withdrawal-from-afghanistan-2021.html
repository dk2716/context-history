The Withdrawal from Afghanistan (2021)

A peace deal that excluded the Afghan government, a president who fled by helicopter, and a republic that didn't lose a war so much as dissolve — how eleven days ended twenty years.

Overview

On August 15, 2021, Afghan President Ashraf Ghani fled Kabul by helicopter as Taliban fighters entered the capital without firing a shot. Eleven days earlier, the Taliban had controlled a handful of provincial capitals; by the time Ghani left, they controlled the country. The Afghan National Defense and Security Forces — a 300,000-strong force the United States had spent nearly two decades and tens of billions of dollars building — did not, for the most part, fight. It dissolved. This page picks up where Context's page on the War in Afghanistan (2001–2021) leaves off: the February 2020 Doha Agreement between the United States and the Taliban, the failed peace process that followed, the mechanics of the republic's collapse, and its aftermath. It does not re-narrate the preceding two decades of invasion, counterinsurgency, and state-building — that history is covered in full on the linked page.

The scholarship gathered here converges on an uncomfortable point: Afghanistan's government did not lose a battlefield war in 2021. Political scientist Anthony King's account is blunt — "the Taliban takeover was not a military victory but a political one." The collapse is better explained by the unraveling of a patronage network than by combat outcomes, compounded by a peace process that Omar Sadr's research shows was structurally designed to fail, and by domestic political choices — documented by Farhad Sultani — that hollowed out the republic's legitimacy well before the Taliban reached Kabul's gates.

The Doha Agreement

After more than a year of U.S.–Taliban talks in Doha, Qatar, led by U.S. envoy Zalmay Khalilzad, the two sides signed the Agreement for Bringing Peace to Afghanistan on February 29, 2020. Its terms bound the United States to an initial reduction from roughly 13,000 to 8,600 troops within 135 days, and a full withdrawal of all U.S. and coalition forces within fourteen months — by May 1, 2021 — contingent on Taliban counterterrorism guarantees, chiefly a commitment to prevent al-Qaeda from using Afghan soil to threaten the United States or its allies.

What made the Doha process distinctive — and, in Omar Sadr's analysis, structurally compromised from the outset — was its design as a two-stage negotiation. The first stage was bilateral: the United States and the Taliban alone, with the internationally recognized government of Afghanistan excluded entirely from the table. The second stage was meant to be "intra-Afghan" talks between the Taliban and the republic. But by the time those talks nominally began in September 2020, the most consequential decisions — troop withdrawal timelines, a prisoner exchange, the Taliban's removal from UN sanctions lists — had already been settled in the stage the Afghan government had no part in.

Key Structural Flaw

Washington negotiated concessions on the Afghan Republic's behalf — without its consent.

As part of the Doha framework, the U.S. committed the Afghan government to releasing 5,000 Taliban prisoners and supported removing Taliban leadership from UN sanctions lists — both major concessions to an adversary the Republic itself had not agreed to release or delist. Sadr's analysis is direct: this was "a concession to the Taliban" that the Taliban then used to reinforce their own narrative that the Kabul government was a "puppet regime," not a sovereign party worth negotiating with in good faith. The result, Sadr argues, was that "the Taliban did not genuinely enter the peace process" as an equal party at all.

Why the Peace Process Failed

Omar Sadr's 2024 study identifies seven interlocking causes behind the collapse of the Afghanistan peace process, several of which compounded one another in the deal's final year.

A Flawed Design

Beyond excluding the Republic from the first stage, the agenda itself front-loaded the most consequential decisions — troop withdrawal and an effective ceasefire with the Taliban — into the U.S.-only stage, while deferring the harder political questions (power-sharing, a permanent ceasefire between the Taliban and the Republic) to the intra-Afghan stage that followed. This left the Republic negotiating from a position that had already been substantially weakened before its delegation sat down.

No Mediator, No Guarantor

Sadr's analysis draws on Barbara Walter's research on civil war settlements, which finds that a credible third-party guarantor is often decisive in enforcing peace agreements. No such guarantor emerged: Ghani rebuffed international offers to play that role, preferring to negotiate through what Sadr calls informal "facilitators" (chiefly Qatar) whose status and authority were never clearly defined — creating space for rent-seeking behavior among the many states (Russia, China, Pakistan, Norway, and others) that all sought their own channels to the Taliban.

Costly, Indivisible Stakes

Sadr argues the core stakes were indivisible in a way that made rational bargaining difficult: the Taliban sought the reestablishment of an Islamic Emirate, a form of governance the Republic's leadership and constitution could not accommodate without dissolving itself. Concessions that might have been "cheap" in a divisible dispute over resources or territory were existential for both sides here, making genuine compromise structurally elusive.

Ideology, Identity, and Mutual Distrust

Sadr resists reducing the failure to ideology alone, but finds it a necessary part of the explanation: the Taliban's religious-political project and the Republic's (nominally) liberal-democratic constitutional order were not merely competing interests but competing identities, making the kind of trust-building compromise required for peace settlements especially difficult to sustain under pressure.

Sadr's verdict is not that peace was impossible in principle, but that the process as designed and executed foreclosed it: "a design that would have specified a mechanism for the mediator and guarantor would have avoided such pitfalls." Instead, he writes, the Taliban's own conduct compounded the design flaws — escalating violence through 2020 even as talks continued, and never fulfilling the counterterrorism commitments that were the deal's stated justification in the first place.

The August 2021 Collapse

May – early August 2021
The Final U.S. Withdrawal and the Taliban's Rural Advance
▼

President Biden, having missed the Doha deal's original May 1 deadline, announced U.S. forces would fully withdraw by September 11, 2021 — later moved up to August 31. As American troops departed through the summer, the Taliban moved rapidly through rural districts, frequently negotiating local surrenders rather than fighting for them. Anthony King's account locates the mechanism here: the "light but decisive centripetal force" of the U.S. presence had held together a fractious network of warlords and local patrons who nominally backed the Ghani government. Once that force was visibly withdrawing, King writes, "centrifugal tendencies took over."

August 6–15, 2021
Eleven Days: Provincial Capitals Fall in Sequence
▼

Zaranj, a provincial capital, fell to the Taliban on August 6 — the first of many. Over the following days, Kunduz, Kandahar, Herat, and other major cities followed in rapid succession, often with minimal fighting. By August 15, Taliban forces had entered Kabul. President Ghani departed the country by helicopter that same day, later stating he left to avoid bloodshed; critics characterized the departure as an abandonment of the government he led. The Republic of Afghanistan, twenty years and roughly $2 trillion in international investment after its founding, ceased to exist in barely a week and a half.

August 2021
A Political Collapse, Not a Military Defeat
▼

King's central finding is that the Afghan National Army was never actually defeated in the field at the scale the speed of the collapse implied. Instead, the same local warlords and patrons who had provided the ANA's provincial units with their real chain of command — a patrimonial arrangement the West had built around, rather than replaced, throughout the war — re-aligned to the Taliban as U.S. support ended, and "instructed client Afghan Army units in their provinces to surrender, disarm or simply to go home." Only well-resourced Special Operations units, insulated from local patronage networks, kept fighting. "The Ghani regime collapsed from within," King writes; "the political elite defected, taking the security forces with them."

King (2023)

Domestic Drivers of the Republic's Fall

Farhad Sultani's 2025 study pushes back against explanations that stop at U.S. withdrawal and Taliban military pressure, arguing that two specifically domestic dynamics — largely absent from the U.S. government's own SIGAR postmortem — were instrumental to the collapse: the Ghilzai-led Pashtunization of the republic under Ghani, and structural weaknesses baked into the 2004 Afghan Constitution.

Ghilzai-Led Pashtunization

Ashraf Ghani, a Ghilzai Pashtun — a Pashtun sub-confederation historically positioned as a rival to the politically dominant Durrani Pashtuns who had ruled Afghanistan since its founding in 1747 — used his presidency to redistribute power toward his own tribal network. Sultani documents senior appointments (the Ministry of Defense, Ministry of Public Health, the Attorney General's office among them) concentrated among Ghilzai Pashtuns during Ghani's tenure, a pattern that progressively marginalized Durrani Pashtuns and non-Pashtun ethnic groups alike, eroding the broad multi-ethnic coalition any Afghan government needs to project legitimacy nationally.

A "Three-Man Government"

Citing SIGAR's own 2022 evaluation, Sultani notes that Kabul under Ghani developed what SIGAR itself termed a "three-man government" — a small, personalized, family- and tribal-based inner circle operating from the presidential palace (the Arg) that concentrated decision-making and sidelined broader institutional input, deepening both the legitimacy crisis and the corruption that scholars across the literature identify as central to the republic's weakness.

The 2004 Constitution's Structural Weakness

Sultani argues the 2004 Constitution's hyper-centralized presidential system — concentrating power in Kabul with few effective checks or mechanisms for regional political participation — made the state unusually dependent on whoever occupied the presidency personally, rather than on durable institutions. When that individual pursued narrow tribal patronage rather than genuine national coalition-building, the constitutional structure offered few internal correctives.

Rejecting Power-Sharing

SIGAR's own November 2022 evaluation — cited extensively by Sultani — found that Ghani rejected a power-sharing arrangement with the Taliban, opting instead to try to integrate them into the existing republic under his own continued leadership. Combined with the Republic's exclusion from the Doha talks, this left Kabul with neither a negotiated settlement nor a credible plan to win outright.

Sultani's broader argument compares Ghani's republic unfavorably to the Soviet-backed Najibullah government of the early 1990s, which survived roughly three years after Soviet troops withdrew in 1989 — considerably longer than the Ghani government's survival after the U.S. drawdown. The comparison, in Sultani's framing, underscores that external military withdrawal alone does not explain the speed of the 2021 collapse; the internal political choices Ghani made in the years beforehand narrowed his government's base of support well before the Taliban's final offensive began.

Pakistan, Iran, and Regional Actors

Sultani's account gives particular weight to a factor he argues the U.S. government's own lessons-learned reporting has consistently underplayed: the active role of neighboring states, chiefly Pakistan, in sustaining the Taliban's insurgency and enabling its final offensive.

Pakistan's Inter-Services Intelligence (ISI) had supported the Taliban since the movement's founding in the 1990s, and — after a brief post-2001 wariness — resumed active backing from around 2003 as it came to see the India-friendly Karzai government as contrary to Pakistani strategic interests. Sultani documents financial, logistical, and ideological support flowing from Pakistan's tribal border regions throughout the war, providing the sanctuary and resourcing that allowed the Taliban to rebuild after their near-total defeat in 2001 — a pattern Anthony King's account of the war's earlier phases corroborates independently. Then-Pakistani Prime Minister Imran Khan's own public statement following Kabul's fall — reportedly celebrating it as breaking "the chains of slavery" — is cited by Sultani as confirmation of how directly Islamabad viewed the Taliban's return as a strategic victory for Pakistani interests in the region, rather than a destabilizing outcome to be feared.

Humanitarian Aftermath

The Watson Institute's Costs of War project at Brown University has tracked the human toll of the collapse in the years since, documenting sharp deteriorations across multiple indicators of Afghan welfare. The UN Development Programme projected in September 2021 that as many as 97% of Afghans could fall below the poverty line by mid-2022 absent a major reversal in economic conditions. UNICEF projected that fall that roughly half of Afghan children under five would face acute malnutrition as the economy collapsed and international development funding — which had underwritten much of the country's basic services — was abruptly suspended. By January 2022, the United Nations had launched its largest-ever single-country humanitarian aid appeal specifically for Afghanistan.

Women's rights indicators, tracked by the same Costs of War research, show severe reversals from the two decades of gains — however uneven — made under the Republic: restrictions on women's education, employment, and freedom of movement were reinstated by the Taliban government within its first years back in power, echoing the restrictions Costs of War researchers had documented under the Taliban's pre-2001 rule. The scale and speed of these reversals is, in the Costs of War framing, a direct consequence of the political and economic collapse examined throughout this article — not a separate or subsequent development.

Why It Matters Today

A Case Study in Negotiated Settlement Design

Sadr's diagnosis of the Doha process — excluded parties, undefined mediator roles, front-loaded concessions — has become a widely cited cautionary case study in conflict-resolution scholarship for how peace process architecture itself can determine outcomes independent of the parties' stated goodwill.

Patronage Networks as a Strategic Blind Spot

King's finding that the West spent two decades building security forces without ever fully reckoning with Afghanistan's underlying patrimonial political structure is now a central reference point in debates over U.S. and allied security-force-assistance missions elsewhere — a structural lesson distinct from, but reinforcing, the war-page's findings on rule-of-law assistance.

Ethnic Patronage and State Legitimacy

Sultani's Ghilzai-Pashtunization findings feed into a broader and ongoing debate about whether externally designed, hyper-centralized presidential systems can function in ethnically heterogeneous states without deliberate power-sharing safeguards — a question with direct relevance well beyond Afghanistan.

The Human Cost of Rapid State Collapse

The scale of humanitarian deterioration documented by Costs of War — food insecurity, malnutrition, reversed women's rights — illustrates how quickly the gains of a two-decade international state-building project can be undone once that project's central political scaffolding gives way.
