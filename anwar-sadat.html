Origins & Rise

Anwar Sadat was born in 1918 in the Nile Delta village of Mit Abu al-Kum, the ninth of thirteen children of a low-ranking civil servant. His father was Egyptian; his mother was Sudanese. He grew up in a rural setting far from Cairo's elite — a background that would shape both his political instincts and his sense of being an outsider in the world of Egyptian power. He idolised two figures in his childhood: Mahatma Gandhi, for his resistance to British colonialism through moral force, and Adolf Hitler, whom he wrote an admiring letter to in 1953 (a fact that became a biographical embarrassment later in life, though it reflected the pan-Arab tendency to see Hitler as an enemy of Britain rather than a genocidal antisemite).

Sadat graduated from the Egyptian Military Academy in 1938 and immediately fell in with Gamal Abdel Nasser and a group of young officers dedicated to ending British occupation and the corrupt monarchy of King Farouk. They called themselves the Free Officers. During World War II, Sadat attempted to make contact with German agents — he wanted the British expelled from Egypt and saw Berlin as a useful ally. He was arrested twice by the British, escaped from prison once, and spent time in detention. The experience hardened his anti-colonialism and his willingness to take radical action.

When the Free Officers' coup succeeded in July 1952, deposing King Farouk and effectively ending British control, Sadat was a peripheral figure compared to Nasser, who quickly became the revolution's dominant personality. For the next eighteen years, Sadat served as Nasser's loyal subordinate — Speaker of the National Assembly, editor of the government newspaper Al-Gomhuria, a reliable second-tier presence. He was so thoroughly overshadowed that when Nasser died of a heart attack on September 28, 1970, Egypt's political establishment expected Sadat to be a transitional figurehead — easily manipulated, easily replaced. Within months, Sadat had outmanoeuvred every faction that underestimated him, purging the "centres of power" in what he called the "Corrective Revolution" of May 1971. The man nobody had taken seriously was now in complete control of Egypt.

Worldview

Sadat's political worldview was harder to categorise than Nasser's. Where Nasser was an ideological architect — pan-Arabism, socialism, non-alignment — Sadat was a pragmatist who moved between frameworks based on what he calculated would achieve his goals. The constant underneath the shifts was Egyptian national interest, narrowly defined: restoring Egyptian dignity, recovering lost territory, and generating the economic resources to build the Egypt he wanted.

Egyptian Nationalism Over Pan-Arabism

Sadat broke with Nasser's pan-Arab vision in practice if not always in rhetoric. Where Nasser had sacrificed Egyptian resources for the broader Arab cause — in Yemen, in the confrontation with Israel — Sadat believed Egypt had paid too high a price for an Arab solidarity that hadn't delivered. His peace with Israel was, at its core, an act of Egyptian nationalism: securing the Sinai and ending the economic drain of perpetual war, regardless of what other Arab states thought.

Infitah — The Open Door

Sadat's economic policy, announced in 1974, was a sharp break from Nasserist socialism. "Infitah" (opening) dismantled state controls, courted foreign investment, encouraged the private sector, and reoriented Egypt toward the United States and Gulf Arab petrodollars. It created a new wealthy class and fuelled corruption and inequality, laying the social conditions for the Islamist opposition that would eventually kill him.

Strategic Realism

Sadat was willing to make moves of breathtaking audacity when he calculated the strategic moment was right — and to reverse course entirely when circumstances changed. He expelled 20,000 Soviet military advisers from Egypt in 1972, a year before attacking Israel with Soviet weapons. He launched a surprise war to create diplomatic leverage, then used that leverage to make peace. His 1977 trip to Jerusalem was the single most dramatic unilateral diplomatic gesture in modern Middle East history.

Islam as Political Tool

Early in his presidency, Sadat cultivated Egypt's Islamist movements — the Muslim Brotherhood and the emerging Salafist networks — as a counterweight to the Nasserist left. He released Brotherhood prisoners, allowed Islamic student associations to dominate universities, and gave himself the title "the Believer President." The strategy backfired catastrophically: by the late 1970s, the Islamist networks he had empowered turned against the regime he had built, and one of their cells murdered him.

The 1973 War

The October War — known in the Arab world as the Ramadan War and in Israel as the Yom Kippur War — is the central military event of Sadat's presidency and the key to understanding why the peace that followed was possible. Sadat launched the war not to destroy Israel but to change the political situation: to break the post-1967 deadlock in which Israel sat comfortably behind the Suez Canal with no incentive to negotiate, and to demonstrate that Egypt could fight effectively and reclaim its self-respect.

1971–1972
Expelled Soviets, Prepared for War — Sadat Repositions Egypt Toward the West
▼

In July 1972, Sadat made one of the most startling decisions of the Cold War: he expelled approximately 20,000 Soviet military advisers from Egypt virtually overnight. The move shocked both Moscow and Washington. Egypt was dependent on Soviet weapons, Soviet training, and Soviet diplomatic protection. Expelling the advisers appeared to leave Egypt defenceless — but Sadat had calculated that the Soviets were blocking him from acting militarily (they feared escalation) and that a dramatic break with Moscow might open a door to Washington. Nixon and Kissinger were intrigued but not yet ready to engage.

Sadat spent 1972 and early 1973 in secret war planning with Syrian President Hafez al-Assad. The plan was to attack simultaneously: Egypt crossing the Suez Canal, Syria attacking the Golan Heights. The goal was not conquest — Sadat knew he could not destroy Israel — but to create a military crisis that would force the superpowers to impose a diplomatic settlement and compel Israel to negotiate.

October 6, 1973
The Crossing — Egypt Storms the Bar-Lev Line and Changes the War's Terms
▼

At 2:05 p.m. on Yom Kippur — Judaism's holiest day, when most Israeli soldiers were fasting and off duty — Egyptian forces crossed the Suez Canal in a meticulously planned assault. High-pressure water cannons demolished the sand ramparts of Israel's Bar-Lev Line; bridges and boats carried 100,000 soldiers across in hours. The Israeli military, which had dismissed Egyptian military capability after 1967's Six-Day War debacle, was caught almost entirely by surprise. Within two days, Egyptian forces had captured most of the east bank of the canal.

The initial Egyptian success was remarkable. Soviet-supplied anti-aircraft missiles neutralised Israel's air force over the canal zone; anti-tank weapons inflicted heavy losses on Israeli armour. The first week of the war shattered the Israeli assumption of permanent military superiority and sent shockwaves through Washington, which scrambled to mount an emergency resupply airlift. Sadat had achieved his first objective: Egypt was no longer a humiliated loser but a credible military actor. That achievement — restoring Egyptian honour — was the precondition for everything that followed.

October 15–22, 1973
The Tide Turns — Israeli Counterattack and the Superpower Ceasefire
▼

Israeli General Ariel Sharon found a gap between Egyptian armies and pushed Israeli forces through it, crossing back over the canal and surrounding Egypt's Third Army. The military situation reversed sharply: Israeli tanks were 101 kilometres from Cairo. Sadat, who had refused to push his forces beyond their air defence umbrella, now needed the Soviets and Americans to impose a ceasefire before his encircled army was destroyed.

UN Security Council Resolution 338 halted the fighting on October 22 — with Egyptian forces in place on the east bank but with an Israeli force that had effectively encircled the Third Army. Sadat had not won the war militarily. But he had won it politically. Egypt had fought well enough to negotiate; Israel had been surprised badly enough to take negotiation seriously; Kissinger, who flew between Cairo and Jerusalem seventeen times over the following months, now had a genuine diplomatic process to manage. The war created the conditions for everything Sadat wanted.

The Peace with Israel

Sadat's journey from the architect of a war against Israel to a man shaking hands with Israeli leaders on the White House lawn took four years — and culminated in one of the most audacious diplomatic gestures of the twentieth century. The logic was always the same: Egypt needed the Sinai back, and it needed to escape the economic stranglehold of permanent war. Making peace was the only way to achieve both.

November 19, 1977
Sadat Flies to Jerusalem — A Gesture That Broke Every Rule of Arab Diplomacy
▼

On November 19, 1977, Sadat landed at Ben Gurion Airport — the first Arab head of state to officially set foot in Israel. He addressed the Israeli Knesset the following day. The speech was careful and conditional, stopping well short of immediate peace, but the symbolism was shattering. No Arab leader had recognised Israel's existence in person. The visit broke the psychological barrier that had made any negotiation appear impossible and wrong simultaneously. It enraged most of the Arab world — Egypt was expelled from the Arab League two years later — and it electrified Israeli public opinion.

Sadat's calculation was that gesture could accomplish what years of diplomacy had not. By going to Jerusalem, he demonstrated to the Israeli public and to Menachem Begin's government that Egypt genuinely wanted peace, not just a temporary armistice. He also put the ball entirely in Israel's court: if peace failed now, the failure would be unambiguously Israel's. Carter, who had been pushing for a comprehensive regional peace conference, was forced to accelerate and focus on an Egypt-Israel bilateral deal.

September 5–17, 1978
Camp David — Thirteen Days That Produced a Nobel Prize
▼

President Jimmy Carter invited Sadat and Israeli Prime Minister Menachem Begin to the presidential retreat at Camp David for what became thirteen days of gruelling, often near-collapse negotiations. Begin and Sadat barely spoke to each other — Carter shuttled between their cabins. At multiple points the talks appeared irretrievably broken. Begin was prepared to leave; Carter drafted a personal letter to Sadat listing the names of Begin's grandchildren, appealing to his legacy for future generations.

The framework that emerged — the Camp David Accords, signed September 17, 1978 — had two parts: a framework for peace between Egypt and Israel, and a vaguer framework for Palestinian autonomy in the West Bank and Gaza. The Egyptian-Israeli framework was specific and binding: Israel would return the entire Sinai Peninsula to Egypt in stages; Egypt would establish full diplomatic relations and open embassies; the Israeli military would withdraw from Sinai within three years. The Palestinian framework was a diplomatic footnote that Israel was never serious about implementing. Sadat and Begin were awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in October 1978. The Egypt-Israel peace treaty was signed on the White House lawn on March 26, 1979.

Domestic Rule

Sadat's international reputation as a peacemaker coexisted uneasily with an increasingly authoritarian domestic record. The Infitah economic liberalisation created a new consumerist class in Cairo and Alexandria but also worsened inequality, fuelled corruption, and generated a resentful urban poor for whom the "open door" had opened only to foreign goods they couldn't afford. The bread riots of January 1977 — triggered by IMF-mandated cuts to food subsidies — were the largest popular uprising Egypt had seen since the 1952 revolution. Sadat rescinded the cuts and declared a state of emergency.

His approach to political opposition grew harsher as his peace with Israel alienated the Arab world and energised domestic critics from both the left and the Islamist right. In September 1981 — just one month before his assassination — Sadat ordered the arrest of over 1,500 political opponents, journalists, academics, and religious figures, including Coptic Christian leaders and prominent Islamist clerics. The sweep was sweeping enough to alarm even his American backers. Two weeks later, he was dead.

Dark record

Empowering Islamism, Then Paying the Price

In the early 1970s, Sadat systematically cultivated Egypt's Islamist movements as a political counterweight to Nasserists and communists. He released Muslim Brotherhood members imprisoned by Nasser, allowed Islamic student groups (Gama'a Islamiyya) to take over Egyptian universities, and promoted a public piety that his secular predecessor had always resisted. By the late 1970s, those same networks had radicalised beyond his control. Egyptian Islamic Jihad — which assassinated him — grew directly out of the Islamist infrastructure Sadat had allowed to flourish. His death was, in part, a consequence of his own political strategy.

Assassination

On October 6, 1981 — the eighth anniversary of the crossing of the Suez Canal — Sadat sat in the reviewing stand at a military parade in Cairo, watching jets fly overhead. A truck in the parade slowed, and four soldiers leaped out and rushed the stand, throwing grenades and firing automatic weapons. Sadat stood up as they approached — witnesses later disagreed about whether he was trying to flee or trying to face the attackers. He was shot multiple times and died at Maadi Military Hospital several hours later.

The assassins were members of Egyptian Islamic Jihad, a radical Islamist cell led by Khalid Islambouli. At his trial, Islambouli declared: "I have killed Pharaoh." The plotters objected to the peace with Israel, to Sadat's close relationship with the United States, and to the September 1981 arrests. Islambouli was executed in April 1982. Other members of the cell, including Ayman al-Zawahiri — who would later become Osama bin Laden's deputy and eventually al-Qaeda's leader — were imprisoned and tortured, emerging more radicalised than when they had gone in.

Long shadow

Sadat's Killers and the Road to 9/11

The network that killed Sadat did not end with his death. Ayman al-Zawahiri, imprisoned after the assassination, was radicalised further in Egyptian jails and eventually made his way to Afghanistan, where he linked up with Osama bin Laden. Egyptian Islamic Jihad merged with al-Qaeda in 2001. The ideology of violent jihad against "apostate" Muslim rulers — developed explicitly as a response to Sadat's peace with Israel — became the intellectual foundation for the global jihadist movement that produced the September 11 attacks. Sadat's assassination is, in this sense, a direct precursor to 9/11.

Why It Matters Today

Sadat was assassinated in 1981. The peace treaty he signed with Israel in 1979 has held for 45 years — the longest-standing peace agreement between Israel and any Arab state, and the only one that involved the return of significant occupied territory. Whether that durability vindicates his gamble or simply reflects Egypt's continued dependence on American military aid is the central argument his legacy provokes.

The Egypt–Israel Peace and Its Regional Isolation

The Camp David Accords produced the only comprehensive land-for-peace agreement in the Arab-Israeli conflict's history. Israel returned the entire Sinai to Egypt; Egypt became the first Arab state to recognise Israel. Every subsequent peace process — the Oslo Accords, the Jordan-Israel peace treaty, the Abraham Accords — has occurred in the shadow of Camp David, either building on its model or explicitly distinguishing itself from it. And every subsequent failure to extend peace to the Palestinians has retroactively complicated Sadat's achievement: he got Sinai back, but the Palestinians whose cause he claimed to represent got nothing durable.

  • The Arab League expelled Egypt and moved its headquarters from Cairo to Tunis after Camp David — Egypt remained isolated until 1989
  • The Jordan-Israel peace treaty (1994) used Camp David as its legal and diplomatic template — the only other comprehensive bilateral peace Israel has with an Arab state
  • The Abraham Accords (2020) normalised Israeli relations with UAE, Bahrain, Sudan, and Morocco — but explicitly without resolving the Palestinian question, a departure from the Camp David model that Sadat would likely have rejected
  • Egypt's peace with Israel survived Mubarak, the Muslim Brotherhood's Morsi, and Sisi — but it remains a "cold peace": diplomatic and security cooperation without popular acceptance

American Military Aid and Egypt's Political Trajectory

Camp David locked Egypt into a $1.3 billion annual U.S. military aid relationship that has continued without interruption for 45 years. That aid has been the primary financial support for Egypt's military — which has governed the country, directly or indirectly, since 1952. Critics argue that U.S. aid, provided regardless of Egypt's human rights record, has been the single most important external factor enabling Egyptian authoritarianism. The Sadat deal created the financial architecture that sustained Mubarak for 30 years and Sisi for a decade after him.

  • The U.S. has never conditioned Egypt's military aid on human rights performance in any sustained way — Congress has periodically legislated conditions; successive administrations have waived them
  • Sisi's 2013 coup against the elected Morsi government — which the Obama administration declined to call a coup, because doing so would have legally required suspending aid — was enabled by the military's financial independence from electoral politics
  • Egypt is the second-largest recipient of U.S. military aid after Israel; the two-country framework Sadat created at Camp David has structured U.S. Middle East military spending for four decades
  • When the Arab Spring reached Egypt in 2011, the military's calculation that it could remove Mubarak without losing American support was based on the structural importance of the Sadat-era relationship

Political Islam and the Assassination's Legacy

Sadat's assassination by members of Egyptian Islamic Jihad — a group whose ideological descendants include al-Qaeda — was not an isolated event. It was the product of the very forces Sadat had helped create: he had released Islamist prisoners Nasser had jailed, allowed Islamist student groups to operate on campuses as a counterweight to leftists, and inserted Islamic references into Egypt's constitution. The assassination illustrated a pattern that would repeat across the Muslim world: leaders who use Islamism as a political tool against secular opponents, then find that the tool turns against them.

  • Ayman al-Zawahiri — who became the operational and ideological leader of al-Qaeda — was arrested after Sadat's assassination, tortured in Egyptian prisons, and emerged radicalised into global jihad
  • The same Egyptian Islamic Jihad network that killed Sadat merged with bin Laden's organisation in the 1990s to form al-Qaeda; Sadat's assassination is therefore a direct link in al-Qaeda's genealogy
  • Mubarak drew the lesson that any concession to political Islam was dangerous — and governed for 30 years accordingly, banning the Brotherhood from politics and driving it underground
  • The Brotherhood's brief democratic opening after 2011 and its violent suppression under Sisi in 2013 is the latest iteration of the same cycle Sadat's assassination set in motion

The Land-for-Peace Model — What It Proved and Didn't

Camp David demonstrated one thing conclusively: a comprehensive land-for-peace agreement between Israel and an Arab state is achievable when a leader is willing to pay the political cost, when American pressure is sustained, and when Israel offers full territorial withdrawal. It has not been replicated with the Palestinians for 45 years. Whether that failure is because the model is inapplicable to the Palestinian case, because no Palestinian or Israeli leader has been willing to pay Sadat's price, or because American pressure has never been applied at the required intensity, is the central unresolved question of Middle East diplomacy.

  • Every U.S. administration since Carter has attempted a comprehensive Israeli-Palestinian peace process; none has produced a final status agreement
  • The Oslo Accords (1993) were modelled on Camp David's step-by-step framework; they produced interim agreements but collapsed before reaching final status
  • The 2000 Camp David Summit between Clinton, Barak, and Arafat explicitly attempted to replicate Sadat's breakthrough — its failure is still debated: who said no, and why
  • The Abraham Accords' explicit separation of normalisation from Palestinian statehood was a deliberate reversal of the Sadat model — an acknowledgment that Arab governments no longer need to make Palestinian progress a condition of their own relationships with Israel

Legacy & Debate

Sadat's legacy is genuinely bifurcated in a way that few leaders' legacies are. In the West, and particularly in the United States, he is remembered as a courageous peacemaker who risked everything for reconciliation. In much of the Arab world, and among many Egyptians, he remains a controversial or condemned figure — the man who abandoned the Palestinians, legitimised Israel, and sold Arab solidarity for American aid. Both assessments contain real truth.

What he achieved
  • Recovered the entire Sinai Peninsula through diplomacy after Egypt had failed to recover it through three wars
  • Ended the cycle of Egypt-Israel wars that had cost tens of thousands of Egyptian lives since 1948
  • Demonstrated that Arab-Israeli peace was possible — a precedent Jordan later followed in 1994
  • The 1973 war restored Egyptian military credibility and national dignity after the humiliation of 1967
  • Shifted Egypt from Soviet dependency to U.S. patronage, securing $1.3 billion in annual military aid that continues to this day
  • The Egypt-Israel peace treaty has held for over 45 years — the longest sustained peace in the modern Middle East
What he failed or damaged
  • The Camp David Palestinian autonomy framework was never implemented — Sadat traded Palestinian rights for Egyptian territorial recovery
  • Egypt was expelled from the Arab League and isolated regionally for a decade after the peace treaty
  • Infitah created deep inequality; the crony capitalism it spawned persists as a defining feature of the Egyptian economy
  • His empowerment of Islamist networks created the infrastructure that ultimately killed him and contributed to Egypt's subsequent Islamist insurgencies
  • The September 1981 mass arrests showed an authoritarian instinct as severe as any of his predecessors'
  • His successor Mubarak used the "emergency state" Sadat established to rule Egypt for 30 years
The genuinely contested questions
  • Did Sadat abandon the Palestinians — or was he right that Egypt could not indefinitely sacrifice itself for a cause other Arab states were unwilling to fund or fight for?
  • Was the peace durable because it was built on genuine reconciliation, or because Egypt's military dependency on the U.S. made breaking it strategically impossible?
  • Would a different Egyptian president have achieved peace on better terms — or was Sadat's willingness to take enormous personal and political risks the indispensable ingredient?
