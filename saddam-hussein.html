Saddam Hussein

صدام حسين

The village orphan who clawed into the Ba'ath Party at 20, masterminded a coup at 22, ruled the most oil-rich country in the Arab world for 24 years — and whose removal by the United States created a chaos that killed far more people than he did.

Origins: From a Village Ditch to the Presidential Palace

Saddam Hussein was born in 1937 in Al-Awja, a small village near Tikrit in northern Iraq. His father either died before his birth or abandoned the family — accounts differ. His mother, Sabha, remarried a man named Ibrahim Hassan who was, by most accounts, brutal and uninterested in his stepson's education. Saddam spent part of his childhood sleeping in a ditch, and later lived with his maternal uncle Khairallah Talfah, a military officer and fiery Arab nationalist whose worldview would become Saddam's own. "Three things God should not have created: Persians, Jews, and flies" was the title of a pamphlet Khairallah eventually published — a crude summary of the ideology that shaped young Saddam's politics.

He moved to Baghdad at 16 and joined the Ba'ath Party at 20. Ba'athism — from the Arabic word for "renaissance" — was a secular, pan-Arab nationalist movement that believed the Arab world had been humiliated by colonialism and could recover only through unified socialist government that transcended tribal and religious divisions. The movement attracted young men from poor provincial backgrounds who felt excluded from power by Iraq's Ottoman-era elite and the British-installed monarchy. Saddam fit the profile exactly.

In 1959, at 22, he participated in a Ba'athist assassination attempt on Prime Minister Abd al-Karim Qasim. The attempt failed — Saddam was wounded but escaped — and he spent the next years in exile in Egypt, where he continued his political education. When a Ba'athist coup succeeded in 1963, he returned to Iraq. When that government collapsed months later, he was imprisoned. When Ba'athists regained power in 1968, he emerged as the regime's enforcer, running its secret police and systematically eliminating rivals before becoming president in 1979.

Ba'athist Doctrine: Arab Nationalism with an Iron Fist

Ba'athism provided Saddam with his intellectual framework, but he reshaped it around his own personality to a degree unusual even among Arab autocrats. By the 1980s, Iraqi Ba'athism was less a coherent ideology and more a vehicle for Saddam's personal rule — what political scientists call a "sultanistic" regime, where loyalty to the leader, not the party or ideology, was the primary political reality.

Arab Nationalism

Saddam positioned Iraq as the natural leader of the Arab world — heir to ancient Mesopotamian civilisation and Saladin (the Kurdish-born medieval hero whom Saddam, problematically, claimed as an Arab). He poured money into museums, archaeology, and monumental architecture. During the Gulf War, he drew explicit parallels between himself and Nebuchadnezzar II — a comparison that said a lot about his self-image.

Secular Governance

The Ba'ath Party was explicitly secular — Christians, Shia, and Sunnis could all in theory participate. In practice Saddam's regime was dominated by Sunni Arabs from the Tikrit region, but he resisted sectarian politics as a framework and executed Islamist opposition groups brutally. This is why the U.S. overthrow of him, which empowered the Shia majority, transformed Iraq's religious and political geography so drastically.

Socialist Economics (Early)

In the 1970s, under Ba'athist governance, Saddam oversaw genuine development: oil nationalisation in 1972 brought massive revenues, literacy rates rose from 30% to 70% during the 1970s, free healthcare expanded, and women entered the workforce and universities at rates unprecedented in the Arab world. The UN awarded Iraq an award for literacy achievement in 1982. These facts tend to get lost in accounts focused solely on his violence.

Cult of Personality

Saddam's image was everywhere: on murals, statues, money, and wristwatches. He had multiple doubles trained to impersonate him for security. He wrote several novels, including a romance titled Zabibah and the King. He presented himself alternately as a modern statesman, a devout Muslim (convenient after the 1991 Gulf War damaged his secular nationalist credentials), and an ancient warrior-king. The multiple identities were not incoherence — they were an attempt to claim every available source of legitimacy simultaneously.

"He who does not live for others does not live for himself."
— Saddam Hussein, 1979 victory speech, shortly before purging dozens of senior Ba'athists in the room

How He Ruled: Fear, Oil, Tribe, and Paranoia

The purge of 1979

Saddam became president in July 1979 when he effectively forced his predecessor, Hassan al-Bakr, to resign. One week later, he convened a meeting of senior Ba'ath Party officials, accused sixty-six of treason, and had them removed from the room one by one. He then had those men executed — some killed by other party officials who were made to participate as proof of their loyalty. Video footage of the meeting was circulated within the party. The message was unambiguous: loyalty meant more than ideology, tenure, or prior service. This was not a paranoid overreaction. It was calculated political theatre designed to atomise potential opposition before it could organise. From this moment, his rule was secure in the most literal sense — anyone capable of threatening him was either dead or too frightened to act.

The Mukhabarat and the surveillance state

Iraq under Saddam maintained multiple overlapping intelligence and security services that monitored each other as much as they monitored the population. The Mukhabarat (General Intelligence Directorate), the Amn (internal security), and Saddam's own Special Security Organization created a system where no single agency had enough independent power to threaten him, and where each had incentives to report on the others. Informers were embedded at every level of Iraqi society — workplaces, neighbourhoods, mosques. Criticism of Saddam, even in private, could result in imprisonment or death. Family members of suspected dissidents were routinely detained to ensure compliance.

Documented Atrocity

The Anfal Campaign and Halabja, 1986–1989

The Anfal campaign — named after a Quranic verse about war spoils — was a systematic military offensive against Iraq's Kurdish population in the north, carried out between 1986 and 1989 under the direction of Saddam's cousin Ali Hassan al-Majid ("Chemical Ali"). Villages were emptied, civilians killed or expelled, and infrastructure destroyed. An estimated 50,000 to 182,000 Kurds were killed — the higher estimates come from Kurdish sources, the lower from more conservative analyses. On March 16–17, 1988, Iraqi aircraft dropped chemical weapons — mustard gas, nerve agents — on the Kurdish town of Halabja. Between 3,200 and 5,000 civilians died; thousands more suffered long-term health effects. It was the largest chemical weapons attack on a civilian population in history. At the time, the United States — then using Iraq as a counterweight to Iran — significantly downplayed the attack and blocked UN condemnation. American diplomats in Baghdad reported the attack; Washington's public response was muted.

Oil and development — the other side of the ledger

After the nationalisation of Iraq's oil industry in 1972, Saddam used oil revenues to fund a genuine modernisation programme through the late 1970s. Iraq built roads, schools, and hospitals. The Ba'ath Party's literacy campaign reduced illiteracy dramatically; by 1979 Iraq had one of the best-educated populations in the Arab world. Women's participation in the workforce was strongly encouraged by Ba'athist ideology's commitment to gender equality in public life — a fact that stands in strange contrast to the regime's systematic torture of political prisoners. This complexity is important: Saddam's Iraq was not simply a chamber of horrors. For many Iraqis, particularly urban Sunnis and members of the growing middle class in the 1970s, it was a period of genuine material improvement and social mobility. That this modernisation coexisted with state terror is part of what makes his era genuinely hard to assess.

Wars: Eight Years of Trenches, Then a Catastrophic Miscalculation

September 22, 1980
Iran-Iraq War Begins — Eight Years and One Million Dead
▼

Saddam invaded Iran in September 1980, calculating that the Islamic Revolution had left the Iranian military in disarray and that a quick war would secure Iraq's disputed border along the Shatt al-Arab waterway and establish him as the dominant power in the Gulf. He was catastrophically wrong about the quick part. Iran mobilised mass armies of volunteers, including children sent across minefields, and the war ground into a stalemate of trench warfare eerily reminiscent of World War I.

The United States, France, and several Gulf states backed Saddam with intelligence, loans, and weapons — Washington provided satellite imagery of Iranian troop positions and looked away from Iraq's chemical weapons use. By the time a ceasefire was reached in 1988, an estimated 500,000 to 1,000,000 people on both sides had died. Iraq had taken on enormous debt to finance the war — debt that would directly motivate Saddam's 1990 invasion of Kuwait, which he argued had stolen Iraqi oil and refused to forgive Iraq's wartime borrowing.

August 2, 1990
Invasion of Kuwait — The Miscalculation That Ended Everything
▼

Saddam invaded Kuwait on August 2, 1990, annexing it as Iraq's "nineteenth province." His stated grievances included Kuwaiti slant-drilling into disputed oil fields and Kuwait's refusal to forgive Iraq's Iran-Iraq War debt. He may also have believed, based on a meeting with U.S. Ambassador April Glaspie in which she apparently indicated American non-intervention in Arab-Arab disputes, that Washington would tolerate the annexation.

He was wrong. The United States assembled a coalition of 35 nations and launched Operation Desert Storm in January 1991. Within six weeks, Iraqi forces were routed from Kuwait. President George H.W. Bush stopped short of marching to Baghdad, a decision later criticised by some and defended by others on the grounds that regime change would have produced exactly the chaos that the 2003 invasion ultimately did produce. Saddam survived the Gulf War militarily weakened but politically intact in Baghdad — and then brutally suppressed Shia and Kurdish uprisings that the United States had encouraged but failed to support.

Freedman & Karsh (1993)
1991–2003
Sanctions, Inspections, and the WMD Question
▼

After the Gulf War, the UN imposed sweeping economic sanctions on Iraq that remained until 2003. The humanitarian consequences were severe: a 1995 study in The Lancet estimated that 576,000 Iraqi children under five had died due to sanctions-related health failures — a figure disputed but widely cited. UNICEF's 1999 report concluded that child mortality had doubled in southern and central Iraq during the sanctions period. Secretary of State Madeleine Albright, asked in 1996 whether the deaths of 500,000 Iraqi children were worth it, said "the price is worth it" — a statement that resonated catastrophically across the Arab world and remains a recruiting tool for anti-American extremism.

Meanwhile, UN weapons inspectors dismantled Iraq's WMD programmes — Saddam had possessed chemical and biological weapons and had a nuclear programme before the Gulf War. By the late 1990s, most of these programmes had been destroyed. But Saddam refused to cooperate fully with inspections, a strategic ambiguity some analysts believe he cultivated deliberately to deter Iran. When the Bush administration made WMD the central justification for the 2003 invasion, it was building on a real history of weapons use and evasion — but the specific claim that Iraq had reconstituted its WMD programmes was not supported by the evidence then available to serious intelligence analysts, as subsequent investigations confirmed.

Blix (2004)UNICEF (1999)
March 20, 2003
U.S. Invasion — "Shock and Awe"
▼

The United States invaded Iraq on March 20, 2003, with a coalition that notably excluded France and Germany. The conventional military phase lasted 21 days; Baghdad fell on April 9, symbolised by the toppling of Saddam's statue in Firdos Square. Saddam fled, and for eight months his location was unknown — a period during which an insurgency rapidly developed against the occupying forces.

The occupation's early decisions proved catastrophic: L. Paul Bremer's Coalition Provisional Authority dissolved the Iraqi military (putting 400,000 trained armed men out of work and out of loyalty to the new state) and implemented a sweeping "de-Ba'athification" that excluded most educated administrators, teachers, and civil servants from public life. The result was not liberation but state collapse — a vacuum filled by sectarian militias, Al-Qaeda in Iraq, and eventually ISIS. The Chilcot Inquiry (2016) in the UK concluded that the UK (and by extension the US) invasion had been undertaken "before the peaceful options for disarmament had been exhausted" and that post-war planning was "wholly inadequate."

Capture, Trial, and Execution

December 13, 2003
Found in a Hole in Adwar
▼

U.S. forces captured Saddam Hussein hiding in a "spider hole" — a small underground pit — in Adwar, near Tikrit. He was dishevelled, bearded, and apparently defeated. The images of his medical examination — mouth open, hair tangled — were broadcast globally and were intended to convey his humiliation. His capture did not, as U.S. military commanders hoped, significantly reduce the insurgency, which was driven by forces independent of him.

Gordon & Trainor (2006)
October 2005 – November 2006
Trial Before the Iraqi Special Tribunal
▼

Saddam was tried before the Iraqi Special Tribunal — a court established under U.S. occupation. The trial was chaotic: three of his defence lawyers were assassinated, a judge resigned citing government interference, and Saddam used proceedings as a platform for defiant speeches, frequently refusing to recognise the court's legitimacy. He was charged for the 1982 Dujail massacre — the execution of 148 Shia men and boys following an assassination attempt — rather than the larger Anfal genocide, partly because prosecutors believed the Dujail case was simpler and faster to conclude. Human Rights Watch criticised the trial as procedurally flawed and politically rushed.

Human Rights Watch (2006)
December 30, 2006
Executed on the First Day of Eid al-Adha
▼

Saddam was hanged at dawn on December 30, 2006 — the first day of Eid al-Adha, the Islamic festival of sacrifice. The timing was controversial among Sunni Muslims worldwide, who saw it as a deliberate sectarian provocation by the Shia-led Iraqi government. Mobile phone footage of the execution — showing guards taunting Saddam with chants of "Muqtada! Muqtada!" (a reference to Shia cleric Muqtada al-Sadr) — circulated immediately online, turning what was meant to be a closure into further evidence of the sectarian score-settling that was tearing Iraq apart. Saddam's last words were a prayer; he did not plead. He was 69.

Burns (NYT, 2006)

Why It Matters Today

Saddam Hussein was hanged in December 2006. The consequences of his removal are still unfolding. The 2003 invasion that ended his rule did not end the story — it opened a new chapter whose events have been more catastrophic, and more consequential for the world, than almost anyone predicted. Understanding Saddam is inseparable from understanding what came after him.

ISIS Was Born in the Vacuum He Left

The Islamic State — which at its 2014 peak controlled territory the size of the United Kingdom and carried out terrorist attacks across three continents — emerged directly from the chaos of post-invasion Iraq. Its founding leadership came from al-Qaeda in Iraq, which was created in the power vacuum after 2003. Without the invasion, without the de-Baathification that threw hundreds of thousands of trained military officers into unemployment and humiliation, ISIS in its 2014 form could not have existed.

  • Abu Musab al-Zarqawi, ISIS's founding ideological father, set up in Iraq only after the invasion provided the space to operate
  • Paul Bremer's de-Baathification order (2003) dissolved the Iraqi Army overnight — an estimated 400,000 trained military personnel, many of whom joined insurgent groups
  • ISIS's 2014 blitzkrieg succeeded partly because it recruited former Iraqi Army officers who provided military competence the jihadist movement alone lacked
  • ISIS controlled Mosul — Iraq's second city — from 2014 to 2017, requiring a multi-year military campaign involving U.S., Iraqi, Kurdish, and Iranian-backed forces to dislodge

Iran Became the Region's Dominant Power

Saddam Hussein was, for 22 years, Iran's primary regional counterweight. He was Sunni Arab, anti-Persian, secular, and militarily capable. The 2003 invasion removed him and handed Iraq to a Shia Arab political class with deep ties to Tehran. This was not an accident of implementation — it was a structural consequence of removing the one Arab leader capable of containing Iranian power.

  • Iran's "Shia crescent" — allied political and military networks running from Tehran through Baghdad, Damascus, and Beirut to Hezbollah — was only possible after Saddam's removal
  • Iraqi Prime Ministers Nouri al-Maliki and subsequent leaders maintained deep Tehran ties while formally allied with Washington — a structural consequence of who the invasion empowered
  • Iran-backed Popular Mobilisation Units (PMU) became the dominant military force in Iraq after the ISIS crisis, with formal state recognition — an Iranian proxy army within the Iraqi state
  • Saudi Arabia's military intervention in Yemen (2015) was directly driven by fear of Iran's expanded regional reach after Saddam's removal

The WMD Failure Broke American Intelligence Credibility

The U.S. intelligence community concluded with "high confidence" that Saddam had active WMD programmes. There were none. This failure — whether caused by politicisation, analytical error, or deliberate deception by Iraqi defectors — has permanently altered how the world assesses U.S. intelligence assessments. The damage is structural, not reputational: it affects actual policy outcomes decades later.

  • When the U.S. presented intelligence on North Korea's nuclear programme, South Korean and Japanese officials privately questioned whether the pattern of 2002 was repeating
  • The 2007 National Intelligence Estimate on Iran's nuclear programme was released publicly — an unprecedented step, driven partly by the need to demonstrate post-Iraq transparency
  • European allies' scepticism about Iraq's WMD (specifically France and Germany's refusal to join the coalition) has shaped NATO burden-sharing debates ever since
  • The "we know what we don't know" epistemology Colin Powell presented to the UN in 2003 is now a cautionary case taught in every intelligence training programme

Iraq's Sectarian Template

Before 2003, Iraqi political identity was primarily national or ideological — Ba'athist, Communist, Islamist. After 2003, the U.S.-designed political system was built on explicit ethno-sectarian power sharing: a Shia president, Sunni speaker, Kurdish prime minister. This institutionalised sectarian identity as the primary axis of Iraqi politics — a template that persists and that made every subsequent political crisis a sectarian crisis.

  • The Sunni political exclusion under Maliki (2006–2014) is directly cited by analysts as the condition that made ISIS's 2014 Sunni tribal support possible
  • Iraq's political system has produced repeated government formation crises that have lasted months — in 2022, it took over a year after elections to form a government
  • The Kurdish autonomous region, empowered after 2003, has periodically moved toward independence, creating ongoing constitutional crises within Iraq and geopolitical tension with Turkey and Iran

Legacy & Debate: The Hardest Historical Calculation

Assessing Saddam's legacy requires separating at least four distinct questions: How bad was his rule? Was the 2003 invasion justified? Was the invasion competently executed? And what do we owe to the Iraqis who lived under him and under the chaos after him? These questions are often collapsed into one, which is why debates about Saddam are almost impossible to have without turning into debates about George W. Bush — or vice versa.

The 1970s development record
  • Literacy rates rose from ~30% to over 70% during the 1970s; Iraq won UN recognition for literacy achievement in 1982
  • Free healthcare and education expanded significantly
  • Women entered universities and the workforce in significant numbers — Iraq had female doctors, engineers, and civil servants at a rate unusual in the Arab world
  • Oil nationalisation in 1972 channelled revenues into public infrastructure
  • Secular Ba'athist governance protected Christians and other minorities from the sectarian violence that followed his removal
The documented record of atrocities
  • Anfal campaign killed an estimated 50,000–182,000 Kurdish civilians in 1986–1989
  • Halabja chemical attack killed 3,200–5,000 civilians in a single operation — the largest chemical weapons use against civilians in history
  • 1982 Dujail massacre: 148 Shia men and boys killed in collective punishment after assassination attempt
  • Suppression of 1991 Shia uprising killed tens of thousands following the Gulf War
  • Political imprisonment, torture, and disappearances were routine tools of governance throughout his 24-year rule
  • The Iran-Iraq War, which he started, killed 500,000–1,000,000 people on both sides
  • The 1990 invasion of Kuwait and the resulting Gulf War and decade of sanctions devastated Iraq's civilian infrastructure
Genuinely contested
  • Whether the 2003 invasion, which removed him, produced better or worse outcomes for Iraqis than continued sanctions and containment — a question that cannot be answered without knowing the counterfactual, but the evidence of post-2003 Iraq is not encouraging
  • The Chilcot Inquiry found the invasion was not a last resort and post-war planning was inadequate — but does this mean Saddam should have been left in power indefinitely?
  • His trial was criticised by human rights groups as procedurally flawed, raising questions about whether the form of his ending matched the severity of his crimes
  • Some analysts argue the "stability" of Saddam's Iraq was real for Sunni urban Iraqis but a permanent nightmare for Kurds, Shia, and political opponents — raising the question of whose stability counts

The most honest thing that can be said about Saddam Hussein's legacy is that it is inseparable from the legacy of his removal. Roughly 200,000 people died under his direct rule in identifiable atrocities. Estimates of violent deaths in Iraq since the 2003 invasion — from insurgency, sectarian war, ISIS, and ongoing conflict — range from 200,000 to over 600,000. This is not an argument that he should have been left in power. It is an argument that the people who removed him had an obligation to replace his order with something better, and they didn't.

"We had to try him for the crime that was clearest and most winnable in court. But Halabja — the case that actually described what he was — we never finished prosecuting."
— Paraphrase of criticism by multiple human rights observers of the Iraqi Special Tribunal process
