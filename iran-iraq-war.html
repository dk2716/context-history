Middle East · 1980–1988

The Iran-Iraq War

Eight years of industrialised slaughter between two revolutionary states — and how the United States armed both sides.

Overview

The Iran-Iraq War lasted from September 22, 1980, to August 20, 1988 — eight years, making it the longest conventional war of the 20th century. The death toll is contested but almost certainly exceeded 500,000 people; some estimates place it at over a million. Iraq used chemical weapons on a scale not seen since World War I. Iran sent hundreds of thousands of young men — including boys as young as twelve — into human-wave attacks against dug-in Iraqi positions. And when it was over, neither side had gained a single inch of territory beyond where they started: the ceasefire restored the pre-war borders almost exactly.

The war was not simply a conflict between two states. It was a collision between two revolutionary projects: Saddam Hussein's Ba'athist Iraq, which defined itself as the defender of Arabism and secular Arab modernity, and Ayatollah Khomeini's Islamic Republic, which had just overthrown a U.S.-backed monarchy in 1979 and was calling for Islamic revolution across the Muslim world. Both men believed the other's revolution was an existential threat. Both were right, in different ways. Saddam feared that Khomeini's call for Shia uprising would destabilise Iraq's 60% Shia majority population. Khomeini believed that defeating the Ba'athist "infidel" state would demonstrate that Islam could defeat both Western-backed Arab nationalism and American imperialism simultaneously.

The United States played a role that was simultaneously cynical, strategically incoherent, and consequential: tilting toward Iraq to prevent an Iranian victory, while secretly selling weapons to Iran during the Iran-Contra affair. Washington provided Iraq with satellite intelligence, agricultural credits used to buy weapons components, and diplomatic cover when Iraq used chemical weapons against both Iranian soldiers and Kurdish civilians. The war's end left Iraq militarily powerful, economically bankrupt, and looking for a quick way to pay its debts — a logic that led directly to the 1990 invasion of Kuwait and the Gulf War.

Origins & Causes

Saddam Hussein made the decision to invade Iran, but the conditions that made him think it would work — and the conditions that made him think he had to — deserve careful unpacking. Several distinct causes were operating simultaneously, and the war cannot be reduced to any one of them.

The immediate trigger was geopolitical and strategic. Khomeini's Islamic Revolution in February 1979 terrified Saddam for reasons that were entirely rational. Iraq's population was approximately 60% Shia Muslim; the Dawa Party (an Iraqi Shia Islamist organisation with ties to Iran) had been building a clandestine network inside Iraq for years. In April 1980, Dawa operatives attempted to assassinate Tariq Aziz, Iraq's foreign minister; Saddam responded by executing Dawa's leader, Ayatollah Muhammad Baqir al-Sadr, and expelling over 100,000 Shia Iraqis of Iranian descent. Khomeini was simultaneously calling explicitly for the overthrow of Ba'athist "apostates" in Baghdad. Saddam genuinely believed — not unreasonably — that a consolidating Khomeinist Iran would fund, arm, and inspire an insurgency that could bring down his government.

The strategic opportunity was equally real. Post-revolutionary Iran was in chaos: its military officer corps had been decimated by Khomeini's purges (most senior officers were seen as loyal to the Shah); its U.S.-supplied equipment was running out of spare parts under the arms embargo; its government was consumed by internal power struggles and the hostage crisis. Saddam consulted with advisers and concluded — correctly, at that moment — that Iran's military was weak. What he failed to account for was Iranian popular mobilisation: the revolution had generated enormous reserves of ideological fervour that could put hundreds of thousands of motivated fighters into the field, regardless of equipment shortfalls.

There were also territorial ambitions: Saddam wanted to renegotiate or abrogate the 1975 Algiers Agreement, which had required Iraq to recognise Iranian sovereignty over the eastern half of the Shatt al-Arab waterway in exchange for Iran stopping its support for Iraqi Kurds. He also wanted Khuzestan (which Ba'athist rhetoric called "Arabistan") — Iran's Arab-majority, oil-rich southwestern province. And he wanted to position himself as the leader of the Arab world following Egypt's diplomatic isolation after Camp David: a quick military victory over revolutionary Iran would make Saddam the protector of the Arab nation against Persian aggression.

Key Miscalculation

Saddam expected a war of weeks. He got one of years.

Iraqi military planning assumed a lightning campaign of 3–4 weeks: a rapid seizure of Khuzestan, the fall of the revolutionary government under military pressure, and a negotiated settlement that recognised Iraqi gains. This was the model of the 1967 Arab-Israeli war — a rapid, overwhelming strike that forced a quick capitulation. The model was wrong for Iran. The revolutionary government did not fall; the Iranian public rallied; and the Iraqi advance stalled in the urban fighting around Abadan and Khorramshahr. By December 1980, the war had already stopped being what Saddam planned.

Phases of the War

September 1980 – June 1982
Iraqi Invasion: The "Blitzkrieg" That Became a Siege
▼

On September 22, 1980, Iraqi forces crossed the border on five fronts simultaneously, supported by airstrikes against Iranian air bases. Initial gains were significant: Iraqi forces seized territory up to 30 kilometres inside Iran and surrounded the key port of Khorramshahr. But they failed to take either Khorramshahr or Abadan quickly, and the urban fighting — street by street through civilian neighbourhoods — ground the Iraqi advance to a halt. Khorramshahr finally fell in late October after intense fighting; Iranian defenders called it "the City of Blood."

Iraq's military doctrine was not equipped for what it encountered: Iranian soldiers and Revolutionary Guard fighters who were motivated by religious and revolutionary fervour to fight to the death rather than retreat or surrender. By early 1981, the front had largely stabilised. Iraq held a strip of Iranian territory but had not achieved its war aims. Iran was organising a counter-offensive. The war that Saddam had planned for weeks was entering its second year with no end in sight.

1981 – May 1982
Iranian Counteroffensive: Driving Iraq Back
▼

Iran's counteroffensive combined regular army units, Revolutionary Guard (Pasdaran) forces, and the Basij — a volunteer militia of poorly trained but ideologically committed fighters including teenagers who had been told that martyrdom in battle was the highest religious duty. The Basij's role was particularly grim: they were used in human-wave assaults to clear Iraqi minefields — literally walking through them — before regular forces advanced.

By May 1982, Iran had retaken virtually all the territory Iraq had seized in 1980, including Khorramshahr. Saddam announced a unilateral ceasefire and offered to withdraw to pre-war borders. This was the decision point at which the war's character changed entirely. Khomeini rejected the ceasefire. He believed — and explicitly stated — that accepting peace now, before the Ba'athist "infidel" government had been punished and Saddam removed, would betray the Islamic Revolution. Iran would cross into Iraq. The war that could have ended in May 1982 after the core Iraqi aggression had been repelled instead continued for six more years.

Razoux (2015)Murray & Woods (2014)
July 1982 – 1986
War of Attrition: Human Waves and Chemical Weapons
▼

After Iran crossed into Iraq in July 1982 with Operation Ramadan (aimed at Basra, Iraq's second city), the war settled into a pattern of attritional grinding that recalled the Western Front of World War I more than any modern conflict. Iran would launch massive infantry assaults — sometimes involving tens of thousands of fighters across a narrow front — accepting enormous casualties to penetrate Iraqi defensive lines. Iraq would defend with fortifications, artillery, and increasingly with chemical weapons.

The human cost of this phase was catastrophic. Individual operations cost Iran tens of thousands of dead in single engagements. The Basij provided a reservoir of fighters who were, by any military standard, being sent to their deaths in exchange for small territorial gains that were frequently reversed in Iraqi counterattacks. Iran's military leadership understood the casualties it was accepting; its political leadership — particularly Khomeini — believed the revolutionary commitment of fighters compensated for material disadvantage and that God would ultimately ensure an Iranian victory.

Iraq's response to being unable to match Iranian manpower was to escalate to chemical weapons. From 1983 onward, Iraq used mustard gas and later nerve agents (tabun, sarin, and cyclosarin) against Iranian positions with growing frequency. The weapons were effective: a single chemical attack could incapacitate or kill hundreds of fighters who had no adequate protection. The international response was muted — the UN condemned Iraq in 1984 but took no action, and the United States, which was tilting toward Iraq, actively worked to minimise the diplomatic consequences for Baghdad.

Hiltermann (2007)Murray & Woods (2014)
1984–1988
The Tanker War: Bringing the World In
▼

Unable to break the land stalemate, both sides began attacking each other's economic infrastructure — and, crucially, third-country oil shipping. Iraq attacked Iranian oil terminals and tankers loading at Kharg Island; Iran retaliated by attacking tankers belonging to Kuwait and Saudi Arabia, which were financing Iraq's war effort. This "tanker war" directly involved the Persian Gulf's oil shipping lanes, through which a significant share of global oil exports passed.

Kuwait formally requested protection from both the Soviet Union and the United States in 1986. The Reagan administration, which had been tilting toward Iraq, decided that protecting Kuwaiti tankers was strategically necessary. In 1987, the U.S. reflagged Kuwaiti tankers under the American flag and deployed naval forces to escort them — Operation Earnest Will. This put the U.S. Navy in direct confrontation with Iran: there were exchanges of fire, the USS Stark was hit by an Iraqi Exocet missile (apparently by mistake), and the USS Vincennes shot down Iran Air Flight 655, killing 290 civilians, which the U.S. initially defended as a necessary act of self-defence in a combat environment.

Razoux (2015)Pollack (2004)
February–August 1988
Iraq's Reconquest: The War of the Cities and the Halabja Massacre
▼

By early 1988, the military balance had shifted decisively toward Iraq. Iraqi forces, rebuilt and re-equipped with Soviet and Western weapons and now extensively trained in combined-arms warfare, launched a series of major offensives that drove Iranian forces back from all the territory they had held inside Iraq. The "War of the Cities" — in which both sides fired missiles at each other's urban areas — killed thousands of civilians in Tehran and Baghdad. Iraq's conventional military superiority was now decisive.

The Anfal campaign, conducted simultaneously against Iraqi Kurdistan (whose Kurdish guerrillas — the Peshmerga — had cooperated with Iran throughout the war), included the chemical weapons attack on Halabja on March 16–17, 1988. Aircraft dropped mustard gas and nerve agents on the town, killing between 3,200 and 5,000 people — overwhelmingly Kurdish civilians, including women and children. It was the largest chemical weapons attack on a civilian population in history. The international response was almost nonexistent: the U.S. State Department initially blamed Iran (a claim that was false and that the CIA contradicted internally), and no significant diplomatic consequences followed for Iraq.

Khomeini accepted UN Security Council Resolution 598, which called for a ceasefire, on July 17, 1988 — describing the decision as "more deadly than taking poison" (the phrase is sometimes translated as drinking "a poisoned chalice"). He died less than a year later, in June 1989, without publicly explaining his decision to continue the war for six years after Iran had retaken its territory. The ceasefire took effect on August 20, 1988. The borders were restored to their pre-war positions almost exactly.

Chemical Weapons

Iraq's use of chemical weapons during the Iran-Iraq War was the most extensive since World War I — and the first time nerve agents were used in warfare. Understanding this history is not optional for understanding the subsequent history of the Middle East: the Halabja massacre shaped Kurdish politics for a generation; the absence of international accountability shaped Saddam's confidence in using similar methods later; and the U.S. decision to minimise the political consequences of Iraq's chemical weapons use directly undermined the Chemical Weapons Convention's credibility.

Documented War Crime

Halabja, March 16–17, 1988 — the largest chemical weapons attack on civilians in history

Iraqi aircraft dropped bombs containing mustard gas, tabun, sarin, and possibly VX nerve agent on the Kurdish town of Halabja, which had been occupied by Iranian forces and Kurdish Peshmerga fighters. The death toll is estimated at 3,200–5,000 people — the vast majority Kurdish civilians who had no warning and no protection. Survivors reported seeing people drop dead in the streets, children lying dead in their parents' arms, and the smell of apples and almonds (the scent signatures of different nerve agents). The survivors continue to experience higher rates of cancer, reproductive disorders, and birth defects decades later. The commanding general of the Anfal campaign, Ali Hassan al-Majid ("Chemical Ali"), was convicted of genocide by an Iraqi court in 2007 and executed in 2010.

Iraq's chemical weapons programme began in the 1970s with Soviet and Western assistance and was operational by 1983. The weapons were used in at least 40 documented attacks during the war, with frequency and scale increasing through 1987–88. The agents used included mustard gas (a blistering agent that causes severe skin, eye, and respiratory damage) and the nerve agents tabun, sarin, and cyclosarin (which disrupt the nervous system and cause death through asphyxiation). Iran lacked effective protective equipment for most of the war; soldiers exposed to nerve agents died quickly or suffered permanent injuries.

The United States knew. Declassified CIA documents confirm that the Reagan administration had detailed intelligence on Iraq's chemical weapons use from at least 1983 and chose not to act on it. In 1988, the State Department had internal analyses confirming Iraqi responsibility for Halabja within days of the attack — and then publicly suggested Iran might be responsible, to minimise diplomatic damage to an Iraqi government Washington was still backing. The Senate passed a bill imposing sanctions on Iraq for chemical weapons use; the Reagan administration lobbied against it, and it did not become law.

The U.S. Role

The United States was not a direct participant in the Iran-Iraq War, but its involvement shaped the conflict's course, its outcomes, and its long-term consequences more than any other external actor. The U.S. role was also internally contradictory to a degree unusual even by Cold War standards: Washington simultaneously tilted toward Iraq to prevent an Iranian victory and secretly sold weapons to Iran in violation of its own stated policy.

Tilting Toward Iraq

From 1982, when Iran began to gain the upper hand, the Reagan administration provided Iraq with satellite intelligence on Iranian military positions, extended agricultural credits that freed up Iraqi cash for weapons purchases, facilitated the sale of dual-use technology with weapons applications, and diplomatically shielded Iraq from UN condemnation for chemical weapons use. The U.S. also restored full diplomatic relations with Iraq in 1984 — suspended since 1967 — and removed Iraq from the State Department's terrorism list. The policy calculation: an Iranian victory would be worse for U.S. interests than an Iraqi one.

Iran-Contra — Arming Iran

While publicly backing Iraq, the Reagan administration was simultaneously and secretly selling weapons to Iran — TOW anti-tank missiles and Hawk surface-to-air missile spare parts — in exchange for Iranian assistance in securing the release of American hostages held in Lebanon by Hezbollah. The proceeds from the arms sales were then illegally diverted to fund the Nicaraguan Contras, circumventing a congressional ban. When revealed in 1986, the Iran-Contra affair produced a constitutional crisis. It also confirmed that U.S. policy toward both combatants was entirely opportunistic — directed by hostage politics and covert operations rather than any coherent strategic principle.

Operation Earnest Will

From 1987, the U.S. Navy began escorting reflagged Kuwaiti tankers through the Persian Gulf, putting American forces in de facto combat against Iran. The operation resulted in several exchanges of fire: U.S. forces destroyed Iranian oil platforms used as military bases, sank or damaged Iranian naval vessels, and — in the war's most controversial U.S. action — the USS Vincennes shot down Iran Air Flight 655 on July 3, 1988, killing all 290 people aboard. The U.S. government defended the action as a legitimate mistake in a combat environment; Iran called it deliberate murder. No U.S. official was criminally charged; the Vincennes' captain received a commendation medal.

Intelligence Sharing and Chemical Weapons

The most damaging long-term U.S. policy was providing Iraq with satellite and signals intelligence while knowing that it would be used to direct chemical weapons attacks on Iranian forces. A 2013 Foreign Policy investigation, based on declassified CIA documents, confirmed that U.S. officials knew in real time that Iraq was using chemical weapons against Iranian positions and continued providing intelligence anyway. The Reagan administration's calculation was explicit: preventing an Iranian military victory was more important than enforcing the Chemical Weapons Convention. The precedent set — that chemical weapons use is tolerable if the user is a U.S. ally — would haunt subsequent non-proliferation diplomacy.

Why It Matters Today

The Iran-Iraq War ended in 1988, but it created the political, military, and economic conditions that have driven Middle Eastern conflict ever since. Almost every major crisis in the region from 1990 onward is traceable in some way to decisions made — or not made — during those eight years.

The Debt That Led to Kuwait — and Everything After

Iraq ended the war with approximately $80 billion in foreign debt, owed primarily to Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and the Gulf states that had financed the war to prevent an Iranian victory. Saddam expected those debts to be forgiven or written off as the price of Iraq having defended the Arab world from Khomeini. When Kuwait instead demanded repayment and, in Saddam's view, drove down oil prices through overproduction (reducing Iraq's oil revenues), it became a casus belli. The August 1990 invasion of Kuwait — which led to the Gulf War, which led to permanent U.S. military presence in Saudi Arabia, which Bin Laden cited as justification for al-Qaeda's attacks — is a direct consequence of the Iran-Iraq War's financial outcome.

  • Iraq's $80bn debt in 1988 was roughly equivalent to its entire annual GDP — an impossible financial position that made aggressive action to solve the debt crisis almost inevitable
  • The Gulf War of 1991 established the permanent U.S. military presence in Saudi Arabia that Osama bin Laden cited as his primary grievance against the United States
  • The 2003 invasion removed Saddam, but the chain of consequences — ISIS, Iranian expansion, regional instability — runs directly back to 1980

Chemical Weapons Without Accountability — the Syrian Template

Iraq used chemical weapons against Iranian soldiers and Kurdish civilians and suffered no significant international consequences. The lesson was not lost on other governments. When Bashar al-Assad began using chemical weapons against Syrian civilians from 2012 onward, the international response — condemnation, a red line, and then failure to enforce the red line — directly echoed the 1980s precedent. The Iran-Iraq War established that chemical weapons use could be tolerated if the political costs of punishment were high enough.

  • Bashar al-Assad's chemical weapons programme was partly inherited from Saddam Hussein's — Syrian and Iraqi Ba'ath both developed chemical weapons in the same Cold War environment
  • Obama's failure to enforce the 2013 "red line" on Syrian chemical weapons use was compared by critics to Reagan's failure to act on Halabja — both involving calculation that the political costs of action outweighed the costs of inaction
  • The Organization for the Prohibition of Chemical Weapons has documented over 50 confirmed chemical weapons attacks in Syria — none have resulted in criminal prosecution of Syrian officials

Iran's Permanent Distrust of the United States

Iran's leaders have an exact accounting of what the United States did during the Iran-Iraq War: supported the country that invaded them, provided intelligence used to target their forces with chemical weapons, shot down a civilian airliner, and then defended the action. This history is not ancient grievance; it is the foundation on which every Iranian government since 1988 has built its foreign policy calculus. When Iranian officials refuse to trust U.S. assurances in nuclear negotiations, they are responding to a specific historical record, not to abstract anti-Americanism.

  • The JCPOA negotiations (2013–2015) were conducted under a shadow of Iranian demands for verification mechanisms that reflected distrust of U.S. commitments — a distrust rooted partly in the Reagan-era record
  • Iran's investment in proxy forces and regional deterrence (Hezbollah, Houthis, Iraqi PMU) is explicitly designed to prevent a recurrence of the isolation it experienced during 1980–88, when no great power would defend it against chemical attack
  • The USS Vincennes incident — the shootdown of Iran Air 655 — is taught in Iranian schools; the commanding officer's subsequent commendation is specifically cited in Iranian government statements about U.S. bad faith

Kurdish Memory and Political Identity

Halabja created a political fact that has not diminished: the Kurdish people of Iraq experienced genocide — legally, not just rhetorically — at the hands of the Iraqi Arab state, with the passive complicity of the international community. This experience is foundational to Kurdish political identity and to the Kurdish demand for autonomy or independence that has structured Iraqi politics since 2003. It also explains why the Kurdish Regional Government's relationships with both the United States and Iran are so carefully calibrated — they have learned not to rely on outside guarantors.

  • An Iraqi court found the Anfal campaign constituted genocide in 2006; international courts have also recognised Halabja as genocide, though no international prosecution followed
  • The Kurdish Peshmerga's role in fighting ISIS (2014–2017) was partly premised on a U.S. promise of political support — a promise that was not fully kept when Turkey objected to Kurdish territorial gains
  • Halabja Remembrance Day (March 16) is the most significant national commemoration in Iraqi Kurdistan — 35 years after the event, it remains the defining political memory

Legacy & Debate

The Iran-Iraq War presents historians with a distinctive problem: it was a conflict in which almost every actor — Iraq, Iran, the United States, the Soviet Union, France, the Gulf states — behaved badly, in which accountability has been almost entirely absent, and in which the consequences have been so interwoven with subsequent events that it is difficult to assess the war independently of what came after it. The central historical debate is not really about what happened but about who bears primary responsibility — and that debate reflects ongoing political conflicts as much as historical disagreement.

Dilip Hiro
Journalist, author of The Longest War

Hiro's 1989 account — the most comprehensive contemporaneous history — places primary responsibility on Saddam Hussein as the aggressor while documenting U.S. complicity in detail. He is particularly thorough on the material support the Reagan administration provided to Iraq and on the political calculations behind it. His account is sympathetic to the Iranian perspective without endorsing Khomeini's decision to continue the war after 1982.

Williamson Murray & Kevin Woods
Military historians, authors of The Iran-Iraq War: A Military and Strategic History

Murray and Woods produced the first major account based on captured Iraqi military documents from 2003. Their work gives the most detailed picture of Iraqi military planning and command, and it complicates the simple narrative of Iraqi aggression: they show that Iraqi military commanders were often deeply uncertain, that Saddam's micromanagement of operations produced some of the war's worst tactical decisions, and that the Iraqi military's performance improved dramatically over the eight years.

Joost Hiltermann
Human Rights Watch, author of A Poisonous Affair

Hiltermann's account of the chemical weapons campaign — based on extensive fieldwork in Iraqi Kurdistan and declassified U.S. documents — is the definitive study of the Anfal genocide. He argues that U.S. complicity in the chemical weapons programme was not passive but active: Washington understood what the intelligence it provided would be used for and provided it anyway. His work is the primary source for arguments about U.S. legal responsibility for Halabja.

The weight of evidence supports several conclusions that are now largely uncontested among serious historians: Saddam Hussein was the aggressor in 1980; Khomeini's refusal to accept a ceasefire in 1982 prolonged the war and cost hundreds of thousands of additional lives for no territorial gain; the United States violated the principles it claimed to defend by providing intelligence to a state actively using chemical weapons; and Halabja was genocide under international law. What remains contested is how to weigh these factors against each other and how to attribute systemic rather than individual responsibility for the war's continuation.

The case for Iraqi primary responsibility
  • Iraq was the aggressor — it crossed an internationally recognised border on September 22, 1980, with no legal justification
  • Iraq used chemical weapons against both enemy combatants and civilian populations — a violation of international law regardless of the military context
  • The Anfal campaign against the Kurds constituted genocide — systematic, intentional destruction of an ethnic group, documented in Iraq's own captured military documents
  • Saddam's 1990 invasion of Kuwait confirms a pattern: military aggression as a policy tool whenever internal pressures required an external release
The case for shared and systemic responsibility
  • Iran's refusal to accept the May 1982 ceasefire — after it had retaken its territory — transformed what had been a war of Iraqi aggression into a war of Iranian expansionism
  • The U.S., France, Soviet Union, and Gulf states all provided material support that prolonged the conflict; responsibility cannot be attributed to Iraq alone
  • Khomeini's decision to continue the war cost Iran hundreds of thousands of additional deaths for zero territorial gain — a strategic failure for which the Iranian leadership bears direct responsibility
  • The Chemical Weapons Convention's failure to prevent or punish Iraqi chemical weapons use reflects systemic weaknesses in international law that no single state created
