Dick Cheney

The most powerful vice president in American history — a Wyoming conservative who spent four decades accumulating institutional power, drove the decision to invade Iraq, and built the legal architecture for a "War on Terror" that included waterboarding and warrantless surveillance.

Origins & Rise to Power

Richard Cheney grew up in Casper, Wyoming — oil country, politically conservative, far from the centres of American power. He was a high school football star and a solid student, but he failed out of Yale University twice in the early 1960s, eventually completing his undergraduate and graduate education in political science at the University of Wyoming. The Yale experience — dropping out of an elite institution — left a mark. Cheney spent much of his career cultivating a persona of no-nonsense Wyoming pragmatism that stood in pointed contrast to the Ivy League backgrounds of many of the officials around him. The contrast was partly real, partly constructed.

He obtained five deferments from the Vietnam War draft between 1963 and 1966, the last of which came when his wife Lynne became pregnant with their first child. Asked about this in a 1989 confirmation hearing for Secretary of Defense, he said: "I had other priorities in the '60s than military service." It was a statement of fact presented without apology, and it became one of the most quoted lines associated with him — particularly given that he would later be the primary driver of decisions sending hundreds of thousands of Americans to war in two countries.

His entry into politics came through a fellowship with the Office of Economic Opportunity in 1969, where he worked under Donald Rumsfeld — the relationship that would define both men's subsequent careers. When Rumsfeld became Ford's White House Chief of Staff in 1974, Cheney came with him as deputy. When Rumsfeld moved to the Defense Department in 1975, Cheney became Chief of Staff himself, at age 34, managing the White House under a president trying to restore credibility after Watergate. He served one term in Congress from Wyoming (1979–89), rising to House Minority Whip — the most powerful Republican leadership position in the House. He was then confirmed as Secretary of Defense under George H.W. Bush, where he oversaw the military's reduction after the Cold War and its dramatic deployment in the Gulf War.

Worldview & Ideology

Cheney's worldview is not easily reduced to a single ideology, but several consistent principles ran through his four decades in government. He believed in an exceptionally powerful executive branch, was deeply skeptical of congressional oversight and freedom of information, thought the US military should be used proactively to shape the international order rather than reactively to defend against threats, and regarded the post-Watergate, post-Vietnam limits on presidential power as a dangerous overcorrection that had weakened America.

Unitary Executive Theory

Cheney believed the president's power as Commander-in-Chief was nearly unlimited in wartime — that Congress and the courts could not meaningfully constrain executive action in national security matters. This theory underpinned the legal memos authorizing "enhanced interrogation," warrantless surveillance, and the indefinite detention of "unlawful combatants" without trial.

The One Percent Doctrine

Journalist Ron Suskind documented Cheney's operating principle: if there was even a one percent chance that a threat to the United States was real, it had to be treated as a certainty. The doctrine justified extraordinary preventive action against low-probability but high-consequence threats — and it made the accumulation of power easier to justify than its restraint.

Neoconservatism

Cheney signed the 1998 PNAC (Project for the New American Century) letter to President Clinton arguing that removing Saddam Hussein by force was necessary to protect American interests in the Middle East. This was three years before 9/11. The letter's other signatories included Donald Rumsfeld, Paul Wolfowitz, and Elliott Abrams — virtually the entire senior foreign policy team of the Bush administration that took office in 2001.

Institutional Secrecy

Cheney was obsessive about protecting executive privilege. He refused to disclose the members of his 2001 National Energy Task Force to Congress or the courts. He conducted much of his most sensitive business through his Chief of Staff Scooter Libby, who was convicted of perjury and obstruction in the investigation into the outing of CIA officer Valerie Plame. Cheney's influence operated largely in the space between official decisions and official records.

What distinguishes Cheney from most ideologues is the degree to which he combined firm convictions with bureaucratic expertise. He knew how Washington worked — which offices to control, which deputies to place where, which information flows to monitor — in a way that most politicians and even most cabinet officials do not. His influence over the Bush administration's first term was structural as much as rhetorical: he shaped the choices Bush was given, the people appointed to carry them out, and the information that reached the Oval Office.

Driving the Iraq War

On September 11, 2001, Cheney was evacuated from his West Wing office to the Presidential Emergency Operations Center — a bunker beneath the East Wing of the White House. He spent much of the following months in "undisclosed locations," a phrase that became both security policy and political symbol. The immediate response to the attacks — the invasion of Afghanistan, the creation of the Department of Homeland Security, the Patriot Act — had wide support. The pivot to Iraq was Cheney's specific contribution.

Within hours of the September 11 attacks, according to multiple accounts, Cheney and his advisers were already asking whether Iraq was involved. There was no evidence that it was. The 9/11 Commission's 2004 report found no "collaborative operational relationship" between Iraq and al-Qaeda. But Cheney continued to assert such a link in public statements throughout 2002 and 2003 — most notably in a September 2003 interview on NBC's Meet the Press, after the invasion, when he said it was "pretty well confirmed" that September 11 hijacker Mohamed Atta had met with an Iraqi intelligence official in Prague. The claim was based on a single intelligence report that the CIA had assessed as unconfirmed; the 9/11 Commission subsequently concluded the meeting had probably not occurred.

Cheney was also the most aggressive voice arguing that Saddam Hussein was reconstituting his nuclear programme. In a series of speeches in August 2002, he went beyond what the CIA's current assessments supported — asserting that there was "no doubt" Saddam had weapons of mass destruction and was "amassing" them "to use against our friends, against our allies and against us." The CIA's analysis at the time expressed significant uncertainty about the nuclear programme. Cheney's public assertions were understood within the intelligence community as pressure to harden those assessments. Zinn (2016)

AUGUST 26, 2002
"There Is No Doubt": The VFW Speech
▼

In a speech to the Veterans of Foreign Wars in Nashville, Cheney delivered the most aggressive public case for the Iraq War yet made by a senior administration official. "There is no doubt that Saddam Hussein now has weapons of mass destruction," he stated. "There is no doubt he is amassing them to use against our friends, against our allies, and against us." He dismissed weapons inspections as futile: "A return of inspectors would provide no assurance whatsoever of his compliance with UN resolutions."

The speech alarmed CIA Director George Tenet, who felt it went well beyond what the intelligence supported. It alarmed Colin Powell, who felt Cheney was making it impossible to build a credible diplomatic case at the UN. It alarmed Brent Scowcroft, who had served as National Security Advisor under both Ford and George H.W. Bush and who published an op-ed titled "Don't Attack Saddam" three days later in the Wall Street Journal. None of these reactions changed Cheney's position.

MARCH–MAY 2003
Invasion and De-Baathification: Cheney's Policy Wins
▼

When the invasion began on March 20, 2003, Cheney's preferred approach had triumphed over the alternatives: no large international coalition (France and Germany refused to join), no second UN resolution, a relatively small invasion force (Rumsfeld's "transformation" doctrine), and a plan built on the assumption that Iraqis would welcome US forces as liberators and that the existing state structures would be left in place.

The Coalition Provisional Authority, established after Baghdad fell, issued two orders in May 2003 that reversed the latter assumption dramatically. CPA Order 1 removed the top four tiers of Ba'ath Party members from all government positions — dismissing 30,000 to 50,000 people overnight. CPA Order 2 dissolved the entire Iraqi military, putting 400,000 trained soldiers out of work simultaneously. The CIA station chief in Baghdad warned CPA Administrator Paul Bremer that 30,000 to 50,000 Baathists would "go underground by sundown." His warning proved accurate. Zinn (2016)

MAY 23, 2003
CPA Order 2: 400,000 Soldiers Dismissed
▼

CPA Order 2 dissolved the entire Iraqi military — including the Republican Guard, the Navy, and the Air Force — effective immediately, along with the entire intelligence apparatus. All officers at the rank of colonel and above were classified as "Senior Party Members" and were barred from future government employment, regardless of their actual involvement with the Ba'ath Party. The order affected roughly 400,000 military personnel and 100,000 intelligence service members. CFR Backgrounder (2005)

Franklin C. Miller at the National Security Council warned that it was "dangerous to put 300,000 men on the street with guns, without jobs." Research on ISIS leadership has found that approximately 8 of 13 top ISIS leaders were former Iraqi military officers dismissed under CPA Order 2. The order was issued by Bremer, but was consistent with the neoconservative framework promoted by Cheney's office — which regarded the entire Ba'athist state structure as irredeemably contaminated and wanted to start from scratch. Enhanced Interrogation & Abu Ghraib

The Abu Ghraib photographs — published in April 2004, showing members of the 372nd Military Police Company abusing, sexually humiliating, and torturing Iraqi detainees between October and December 2003 — produced a political and moral crisis for the Bush administration. The army's own investigation, the Taguba Report, found "systemic and illegal abuse" and concluded that military intelligence and CIA officers had "actively requested" that MP guards help "set physical and mental conditions for favorable interrogation." The International Committee of the Red Cross, in a February 2004 confidential report, found that abuse was "tantamount to torture" and was "widespread." AJIL (2004)

The policy framework that produced Abu Ghraib was built by the Office of the Vice President and the Department of Defense, not by accident but by deliberate legal architecture. In the weeks after September 11, Cheney's counsel David Addington and White House Counsel Alberto Gonzales developed a set of legal theories holding that the President's authority as Commander-in-Chief could override statutory prohibitions on torture and that the Geneva Conventions did not apply to "unlawful combatants." These theories were formalised in a series of memos from John Yoo and Jay Bybee at the Justice Department's Office of Legal Counsel in 2002 — the "Torture Memos." AJIL (2004)

Key Document

The April 2003 DoD Working Group Report

On April 14, 2003, a DoD working group — whose recommendations were developed under the authority of Rumsfeld's department, with Cheney's office closely involved — delivered a report on interrogation techniques for use at Guantanamo. It concluded that the Convention Against Torture applied, but identified narrow exceptions and asserted that the Commander-in-Chief's constitutional authority overrode criminal statutes prohibiting torture. Secretary Rumsfeld approved 24 interrogation techniques on April 16, 2003 that went beyond what the Army Field Manual permitted. The Taguba Report found these authorizations contributed to the conditions that produced Abu Ghraib. AJIL (2004)

Cheney was the most visible and persistent defender of "enhanced interrogation" both within the administration and, after leaving office, in public. He argued that waterboarding — a technique that simulates drowning and that US law courts had previously treated as torture — was not torture, was effective, and had saved American lives. "I would do it again in a minute," he told Fox News in 2014. The Senate Intelligence Committee's 2014 report on the CIA's detention and interrogation programme — a 6,700-page study whose 500-page executive summary was publicly released — concluded that enhanced interrogation techniques were "not an effective means of acquiring intelligence," that the CIA had repeatedly misled Congress about the programme's scope and results, and that the programme produced far less actionable intelligence than the CIA had claimed. Cheney rejected the committee's findings. AJIL (2004)

The EBSCO Research Starters analysis of Abu Ghraib contextualises the scandal within a broader pattern: the photographs created a "chasm" between US forces and the Iraqi public, caused "real and deep damage" to American standing in the Muslim world (as UN Secretary-General Kofi Annan said on May 13, 2004), and significantly complicated the administration's ability to argue that the invasion was about liberation rather than dominance. Seven soldiers from the 372nd MP Company were charged; ultimately 17 service members were dishonorably discharged. None of the senior officials whose policy decisions created the interrogation framework were charged with any offence. EBSCO (2019)

Late VP Years: Plame, the Hunting Accident & the End

In July 2003, former ambassador Joseph Wilson published an op-ed in the New York Times arguing that the administration had exaggerated evidence that Iraq was seeking uranium from Niger — evidence that had appeared in Bush's 2003 State of the Union address. Someone in the administration then leaked to journalist Robert Novak that Wilson's wife, Valerie Plame, was an undercover CIA officer. Outing an undercover officer was a potential federal crime. The investigation that followed led to the conviction of Cheney's Chief of Staff, Scooter Libby, for perjury and obstruction. Libby was sentenced to 30 months in prison; Bush commuted the sentence. Cheney pushed for a full pardon, which Bush refused to grant. Trump pardoned Libby in 2018.

On February 11, 2006, Cheney shot Harry Whittington — a 78-year-old Texas attorney and hunting companion — in the face, neck, and chest during a quail hunt on a Texas ranch. Whittington was hospitalised and survived, though pellet shot near his heart later caused a minor heart attack. The incident's handling — Cheney's office did not notify the White House press corps for nearly 24 hours, and the ranch owner informed a local newspaper before the Secret Service informed the press secretary — provided a near-perfect illustration of Cheney's relationship to transparency and accountability.

By 2007 and 2008, Cheney's influence within the administration had waned somewhat as Bush sought to rehabilitate his foreign policy legacy by engaging diplomatically with Iran and North Korea — approaches Cheney strongly opposed. Their relationship ended on a sour note when Bush, in his final weeks in office, refused Cheney's sustained lobbying campaign for a presidential pardon for Scooter Libby. Cheney later said it was the one act of the Bush presidency he found "very disappointing."

Legacy & Debate

Dick Cheney's legacy is among the most contested in American political history, and the terms of the debate have shifted significantly over time. In his own era, he was understood by supporters as the indispensable architect of a strong post-9/11 national security response; by critics as the manipulative bureaucratic operator who pushed an unnecessary war and authorised a torture programme. Since Trump's rise — which split the Republican Party — the question of Cheney's legacy has taken on a new dimension: his daughter Liz Cheney's leadership of the House January 6th Committee, and Dick Cheney's own 2022 video endorsing his daughter and stating that "there has never been a greater threat to our republic" than Trump, placed the Cheneys, improbably, as defenders of democratic norms against the party they had served for decades.

What Supporters Argue
  • No second large-scale terrorist attack on US soil after September 11 — Cheney's defenders attribute this partly to the aggressive security measures he championed
  • The Gulf War (1991), conducted under his oversight as Defense Secretary, was one of the most successfully executed US military operations of the modern era
  • Cheney was consistent across decades in his view of what US national security required — he did not shift positions for political convenience
  • His daughter Liz Cheney's leadership on January 6th accountability represented a form of institutional courage that contrasted with most Republicans
What Critics Argue
  • Iraq War: pushed the invasion on false intelligence, misrepresented the Iraq-al-Qaeda connection publicly for years after it was disproven
  • CPA Orders 1 and 2: the de-Baathification policy created the conditions for the insurgency and provided much of the leadership of ISIS Zinn (2016)
  • Authorised "enhanced interrogation" framework that the Senate Intelligence Committee found was ineffective, misleading to Congress, and caused lasting damage to US standing AJIL (2004)
  • Halliburton's KBR subsidiary received over $10 billion in Iraq reconstruction contracts after Cheney left the company to become VP — an unresolved conflict of interest
  • Warrantless surveillance programme: the NSA programme authorised after 9/11, bypassing the FISA court, was later found by a federal judge to be illegal
  • Five draft deferments while publicly championing military action that others' children were sent to fight
Contested Points
  • Was Cheney ideologically driven or primarily motivated by institutional power? His defenders say he had genuine convictions about American security; critics note his views consistently served to expand executive power and benefit the industries he had led.
  • The "ticking time bomb" argument for enhanced interrogation — that extreme measures were justified to prevent imminent mass casualty attacks — is philosophically contested. The Senate report found the specific techniques used were not effective in practice, but the moral argument is distinct from the empirical one.
  • Cheney's post-2016 positioning as a defender of democratic norms against Trump has led some to argue that his institutional conservatism — his genuine belief in constitutional order, even expansively interpreted — was distinct from Trumpian authoritarianism. Others regard this as a distinction without a practical difference.
Barton Gellman
Washington Post · Angler: The Cheney Vice Presidency (2008)
Gellman's Pulitzer Prize-winning investigation found Cheney operated an "alternative government" within the executive branch, routing around normal interagency processes. He describes how Cheney and Addington built the legal architecture for expanded executive power systematically, and how Cheney's influence depended on controlling information flows to Bush rather than on transparent deliberation.
Ron Suskind
The One Percent Doctrine (2006)
Suskind documents Cheney's operating framework seriously rather than simply dismissing it. The One Percent Doctrine reflects a genuine — if extreme — response to the post-9/11 threat environment, and Cheney's framework had internal logic even where Suskind finds it dangerous. Understanding why intelligent people accepted it matters as much as understanding why it was wrong.
Liz Cheney
Former Wyoming Congresswoman; daughter
Argues her father made decisions based on genuine beliefs about American security, in the specific post-9/11 context where another attack seemed imminent. The policy disagreements can be had; what cannot be disputed, she argues, is the sincerity of the threat assessment from which everything followed.
The Case For Cheney
  • The post-9/11 security environment was genuinely unprecedented — the administration did not know what further attacks were coming, or how soon. The One Percent Doctrine was a defensible response to catastrophic uncertainty. Suskind, 2006
  • Saddam Hussein had used chemical weapons against civilians in 1988, had invaded Kuwait in 1990, and had deceived weapons inspectors throughout the 1990s. The intelligence failure was a collective failure across Western agencies, not a Cheney fabrication. Senate Intelligence Committee, 2004
  • The Gulf War (1991) demonstrated Cheney's capacity for disciplined, strategically coherent military planning — a track record that gave his Iraq assessments surface credibility they may not have deserved. Woodward, 2004
The Case Against Cheney
  • Cheney asserted an Iraq-9/11 connection publicly for years after the 9/11 Commission concluded it did not exist — a sustained campaign of public misinformation. 9/11 Commission Report, 2004
  • De-Baathification policies created the insurgency and provided ISIS with much of its leadership — consequences that were warned against and proceeded regardless. Zinn (2016)
  • The Senate Intelligence Committee found the enhanced interrogation programme was not effective, misled Congress, and damaged US standing — yet Cheney continues to defend it without engagement with these findings. Senate Intelligence Committee, 2014
  • The legal architecture built by Cheney's office — unitary executive theory, commander-in-chief authority over criminal statutes — set precedents that have outlasted the specific policies it was used to justify. Gellman, 2008
