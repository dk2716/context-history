Middle East · 2003–2011

The Iraq War

How contested intelligence, neoconservative ideology, and a divided UN Security Council led to an invasion that cost hundreds of thousands of lives and remade the Middle East.

Background

When Colin Powell took his seat at the United Nations Security Council on February 5, 2003, he knew the stakes. Armed with satellite photographs, intercepted phone calls, and a vial of white powder — a theatrical prop meant to evoke anthrax — America's most trusted diplomat made the case that Saddam Hussein was hiding weapons of mass destruction. Millions watched. Fewer questioned. Within six weeks, the bombs fell on Baghdad.

Iraq had been in the international crosshairs since August 1990, when Saddam Hussein ordered the invasion of Kuwait. The resulting Gulf War (January–February 1991) expelled Iraqi forces, but the U.S.-led coalition stopped short of Baghdad — a deliberate decision by President George H.W. Bush and his National Security Advisor Brent Scowcroft, who feared the chaos that regime change would produce. Instead, Iraq was placed under the most comprehensive sanctions regime in UN history. Between 1990 and 2002, the Security Council passed 16 resolutions demanding Iraq disarm and allow weapons inspections. Iraq's compliance was partial, deceptive, and at times openly defiant — feeding a genuine uncertainty about what Saddam still possessed, even among intelligence services that would later be proven wrong. Simuziya (2023)

The September 11, 2001 attacks created a political environment that the architects of a second Iraq war had been waiting for. A group of senior officials — Vice President Dick Cheney, Defense Secretary Donald Rumsfeld, his deputy Paul Wolfowitz, and National Security Council staffer Elliott Abrams, among others — had been publicly advocating for Saddam's removal since 1998, when they signed a letter to President Clinton calling it "the only acceptable strategy." Their ideological home was the Project for the New American Century (PNAC), which argued that American dominance required not just containing adversaries but eliminating them. Hinnebusch (2007) 9/11 did not cause these officials to want to remove Saddam; it gave them the political cover to try.

The stated justification — weapons of mass destruction — was treated by key officials as real but was also understood, by some, as a useful framing. Hinnebusch argues the real drivers were plural: neoconservative ideology seeking to reshape the Middle East, control over the world's second-largest oil reserves, domestic political pressure, and what he calls the "Israel lobby" — a cluster of pro-Likud neoconservatives who saw Saddam's Iraq as Israel's most threatening regional adversary. The senior CIA career staff and several retired generals opposed the invasion. Both the head of UK weapons inspections and the UN's chief inspector, Hans Blix, would later say the case for war was manufactured from ambiguous evidence. Hinnebusch (2007)

Key Figures

George W. Bush
U.S. President, 2001–2009
Bush authorized the invasion and staked his presidency on the WMD claim. He was not the primary ideological architect — that role belonged to Cheney and the neoconservatives around him — but his decisions shaped every phase of the war. His "Mission Accomplished" speech on May 1, 2003 became one of history's most embarrassing premature victories.
Dick Cheney
U.S. Vice President; principal war architect
More than any other single figure, Cheney drove the push for war. He had been lobbying for Saddam's removal since the 1990s and used his access to intelligence agencies to build the WMD case. He asserted publicly that Saddam was reconstituting his nuclear program — a claim the CIA regarded as uncertain at best. Hinnebusch (2007)
Saddam Hussein
President of Iraq, 1979–2003
Saddam's decades of defiance, his documented use of chemical weapons against Kurdish civilians, and his deliberate ambiguity about WMD possession — apparently maintained to deter Iran even as he had actually dismantled his programs — created the conditions the Bush administration exploited. He was captured in December 2003 and executed by hanging on December 30, 2006.
Hans Blix
Executive Chairman, UNMOVIC (UN weapons inspectors)
Blix led the UN Monitoring, Verification and Inspection Commission into Iraq from November 2002 until the invasion. His teams inspected more than 700 sites and found no active WMD programs. On January 27, 2003, he reported to the Security Council that "no smoking gun" had been found and requested more time. The U.S. and UK invaded six weeks later without a second UN resolution. Simuziya (2023)
Colin Powell
U.S. Secretary of State, 2001–2005
Powell was the most credible voice the Bush administration had, and his UN presentation on February 5, 2003 was designed to persuade skeptical allies. He later called it "a blot on my record." The intelligence he presented — including claims about mobile bioweapons labs and aluminum tubes for uranium enrichment — was either wrong or had been flagged as uncertain by the CIA analysts whose caveats were stripped out. Hinnebusch (2007)
Tony Blair
UK Prime Minister, 1997–2007
Blair's support gave the invasion a degree of international legitimacy it would otherwise have lacked. He commissioned a "dossier" on Iraqi WMD that was later found to have been "sexed up" to strengthen the case for war — including the claim, later retracted, that Iraq could deploy WMD within 45 minutes. Three senior cabinet ministers resigned over the war, including Foreign Secretary Robin Cook. Simuziya (2023)

Key Events

November 2002
The Security Council's Last Warning: Resolution 1441
▾

On November 8, 2002, the UN Security Council unanimously passed Resolution 1441, declaring Iraq in "material breach" of its disarmament obligations and giving it "a final opportunity to comply." The resolution warned of "serious consequences" if Iraq failed to cooperate fully. Crucially, the resolution did not authorize the use of force directly — it required the Security Council to reconvene and consider what those consequences should be. The U.S. and UK ambassador to the UN, John Negroponte, explicitly confirmed this in his statement after the vote. Simuziya (2023)

Iraq accepted the resolution and allowed UNMOVIC inspectors to return. Hans Blix's teams began operating in late November. The U.S. and UK interpreted 1441 as a "trigger" that, once pulled by Iraqi non-compliance, would automatically authorize force. France, Russia, China, and Germany took the opposite view: that a second resolution explicitly authorizing military action was required. This disagreement would fracture the Security Council — and NATO — and ultimately be decided not by diplomacy but by invasion. Simuziya (2023)

January–February 2003
Inspectors Find Nothing; the World Takes to the Streets
▾

On January 27, Hans Blix reported to the Security Council that after inspecting more than 200 sites, his team had found no evidence of active WMD programs. He noted continued problems with Iraqi cooperation on documentation but said the inspectors needed more time. The following week, on February 5, Colin Powell presented the U.S. intelligence case to the Security Council — satellite images, intercepted communications, and claims about mobile bioweapons labs and aluminum tubes for uranium centrifuges. Most of Powell's claims were later found to be based on faulty or fabricated intelligence. Hinnebusch (2007)

On February 15, 2003 — a month before the invasion — an estimated 10 to 15 million people marched in protests across more than 600 cities worldwide. In London, 750,000 took to the streets — the largest demonstration in British history. In Rome, the crowd was estimated at 3 million. The protests had no effect on the timeline. France, Russia, and Germany introduced a memorandum to the Security Council proposing to substantially strengthen UNMOVIC and extend inspections. The U.S. and UK lobbied hard to prevent a Security Council vote they knew they would lose. Simuziya (2023)

March 17–20, 2003
Robin Cook Resigns; "Shock and Awe" Begins
▾

On March 17, 2003, Robin Cook resigned as Leader of the House of Commons — the most senior British cabinet minister to quit over Iraq. His resignation speech, delivered to a hushed House of Commons, remains one of the most precise political indictments of the war: "Iraq probably has no weapons of mass destruction in the commonly understood sense of the term — namely a credible device capable of being delivered against a strategic city target. It probably still has biological toxins and battlefield chemical munitions, but it has no nuclear weapons programme, no long-range missiles, it has probably forgotten more about anthrax and VX than it currently knows." Simuziya (2023)

On March 20, the invasion began. The opening "shock and awe" air campaign struck Baghdad with thousands of precision munitions intended to destroy Saddam's command structure and break Iraqi will to resist. Hinnebusch notes this campaign had a secondary purpose beyond military efficiency: it was designed to demonstrate the overwhelming power of American arms and establish a new deterrent precedent — a display of "full spectrum dominance" that would discourage future challengers. The regular Iraqi army largely collapsed within three weeks. Hinnebusch (2007)

The speed of the military victory concealed how narrow the planning had been. The Office of Reconstruction and Humanitarian Assistance (ORHA), the body tasked with post-war governance, was given eleven weeks to prepare — compared to the years of planning that preceded the post-WWII occupation of Germany and Japan. The State Department's "Future of Iraq" project, which had spent eighteen months preparing detailed governance plans, was sidelined by the Pentagon. Hinnebusch (2007)

April–May 2003
Baghdad Falls; Bremer's Orders Ignite the Insurgency
▾

Baghdad fell on April 9, 2003. The toppling of Saddam's statue in Firdos Square — partly stage-managed for television — became the defining image of apparent victory. Saddam Hussein himself went into hiding; he was found in a hole near Tikrit on December 13, 2003, disheveled and subdued. On May 1, President Bush landed on the aircraft carrier USS Abraham Lincoln and delivered his "Mission Accomplished" speech, declaring that "major combat operations in Iraq have ended."

Within days, widespread looting stripped Baghdad of government ministries, hospitals, and the National Museum. Coalition forces, under orders not to interfere, stood by as Iraq's infrastructure was dismantled. Then came two decisions by L. Paul Bremer, head of the Coalition Provisional Authority, that most analysts now regard as catastrophic. On May 16, Bremer issued CPA Order 1 — de-Ba'athification — which purged anyone who had held a senior position in Saddam's party from public employment, instantly removing tens of thousands of teachers, doctors, engineers, and civil servants. Two weeks later, CPA Order 2 dissolved the entire Iraqi military. Roughly 400,000 armed, trained soldiers — many of them Sunni men with no alternative employment — were turned loose into a power vacuum. Within months, the insurgency was burning. Hinnebusch (2007)

2004–2006
Abu Ghraib, Sectarian War, and the Rise of Al-Qaeda in Iraq
▾

In April 2004, photographs from Abu Ghraib prison showed U.S. soldiers humiliating, torturing, and sexually abusing Iraqi detainees. The images — many of them taken by the soldiers themselves — were broadcast worldwide and became the most powerful single piece of evidence that the war's stated moral purpose was hollow. Simuziya notes that the Abu Ghraib abuses, along with the Guantanamo detention regime and the CIA's extraordinary rendition programme, constituted clear violations of the Geneva Conventions and the UN Convention Against Torture. Simuziya (2023)

By 2006, the war had transformed into something the Bush administration had not planned for: a multi-front sectarian civil war. The February 22, 2006 bombing of the al-Askari mosque in Samarra — one of Shia Islam's holiest sites — triggered waves of retaliatory killings between Sunni and Shia militias. Al-Qaeda in Iraq, a jihadist organization that had not existed before the invasion, was now a major force. Abu Musab al-Zarqawi, its leader, had been killed in June 2006, but the organization metastasized. By late 2006, Baghdad was producing roughly 100 bodies a day. The Iraq Study Group, a bipartisan commission, concluded in December that the situation was "grave and deteriorating." Spagat (2024)

2007–2008
The Surge Reduces Violence — at a Political Cost
▾

In January 2007, President Bush announced a "surge" of 20,000 additional troops, combined with a new counterinsurgency strategy under General David Petraeus. The surge coincided with the "Anbar Awakening" — a movement of Sunni tribal leaders who had turned against Al-Qaeda in Iraq and began cooperating with coalition forces. Violence fell sharply. By 2008, monthly death counts had dropped from their 2006–2007 peaks, and Baghdad's neighborhoods were no longer experiencing daily massacres. Spagat (2024)

The reduction in violence was real, but it came partly through ethnic cleansing that had already run its course by 2007. Baghdad's mixed neighborhoods had been almost entirely segregated, often violently. The Sunni Awakening was bought partly with weapons and cash payments that empowered militia commanders whose long-term loyalties were uncertain. The political reconciliation the surge was supposed to enable — an agreement among Shia, Sunni, and Kurdish factions on sharing oil revenues and government power — never happened. The military situation improved; the political conditions that had produced the insurgency did not. Hinnebusch (2007)

December 2011
The Last U.S. Troops Leave — Leaving the Power Vacuum
▾

On December 15, 2011, the last U.S. combat troops crossed the border into Kuwait, formally ending the American military presence in Iraq under a Status of Forces Agreement negotiated by the Bush administration in 2008. President Obama had campaigned on withdrawal and fulfilled the commitment. Iraq, under Prime Minister Nouri al-Maliki, refused to grant U.S. forces the legal immunity that would have been required for a longer stay. The withdrawal was criticized from the right as a strategic abandonment and from the left as arriving too late. Hinnebusch (2007)

What the withdrawal left behind was a state that functioned in name only. Al-Maliki's government systematically excluded Sunni politicians from power, arresting rivals and dismantling the Anbar Awakening by cutting off payments to the tribal militias that had helped defeat Al-Qaeda in Iraq. The Islamic State of Iraq — Al-Qaeda in Iraq's successor — had been weakened but not destroyed. In 2013, Syria's civil war gave it a new theater, new recruits, and a new name: ISIS. By June 2014, ISIS had seized Mosul — Iraq's second city — with a force of a few thousand fighters, routing an Iraqi army that outnumbered them ten to one. U.S. forces returned to Iraq the same year. Spagat (2024)

Turning Points

Turning Point 1
The Decision Not to Seek a Second Resolution

By February 2003, it was clear France, Russia, and China would veto any resolution explicitly authorizing force. Bush and Blair chose to invade anyway, citing SCR 1441 and earlier resolutions as sufficient legal basis. If they had waited for Blix's inspections to conclude — which the inspectors themselves requested — the absence of WMDs would have become undeniable and the political case for war would have collapsed. The choice to act before that evidence emerged determined everything that followed. Simuziya (2023)

Turning Point 2
Bremer's De-Ba'athification and Army Dissolution Orders

L. Paul Bremer issued CPA Orders 1 and 2 in May 2003 over the objections of the CIA, the State Department, and the U.S. military. Dissolving the Iraqi army created 400,000 armed, unemployed men with grievances. Had the orders been reversed — as was eventually attempted, too late — the insurgency's core would have lacked both its organizational backbone and much of its manpower. Many of the senior ISIS commanders who seized Mosul in 2014 were former Iraqi army officers. Hinnebusch (2007)

Turning Point 3
Al-Maliki's Sectarian Governance and the Anbar Betrayal

The U.S. surge of 2007–2008 succeeded militarily partly because Sunni tribal leaders turned against Al-Qaeda in Iraq. The survival of that coalition depended on Nouri al-Maliki's government integrating Sunni fighters into the Iraqi security forces and sharing political power. Al-Maliki did neither, instead arresting Sunni politicians and cutting off the tribal militias. His decisions directly recreated the conditions that allowed ISIS to recruit and operate in Sunni communities after 2011. Spagat (2024)

Turning Point 4
The 2011 Withdrawal Without a Political Settlement

The Status of Forces Agreement required U.S. forces to leave by December 31, 2011. Obama honored it. Critics — including some of his own generals — argued that a residual U.S. force of 10,000 troops could have stabilized the political situation and prevented the ISIS resurgence. Supporters of withdrawal argued that no amount of U.S. presence could substitute for the political reconciliation that Iraqi leaders refused to pursue. Either way, the departure removed the external pressure that had temporarily restrained al-Maliki's sectarian impulses. Hinnebusch (2007)

"The case for war was based on assertions about capabilities that were wrong, and on links between al-Qaeda and Iraq that did not exist. The intelligence was, at best, ambiguous, and the policy was shaped by ideology rather than evidence."
— Raymond Hinnebusch, Critique: Critical Middle Eastern Studies, 2007

Why It Matters Today

The Iraq War did not end in 2011. Its consequences — ISIS, Iranian dominance of Iraqi politics, a broken Arab state system, and a global crisis of institutional trust — have structured every major Middle East conflict since. Understanding the war means understanding why the region looks the way it does now.

ISIS Emerged Directly from the Power Vacuum the Invasion Created

The Islamic State is not an aberration — it is a direct organizational successor to Al-Qaeda in Iraq, which was itself created by the invasion. Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi, who declared the "caliphate" in 2014, was radicalized in a U.S. detention facility at Camp Bucca after 2003. The senior military commanders who seized Mosul were former Iraqi army officers dismissed by CPA Order 2. Spagat (2024)

  • Camp Bucca held 100,000 Iraqis between 2003–2009; veterans called it "the academy" for jihadist networking
  • ISIS at its peak in 2014–2015 controlled territory the size of the United Kingdom
  • The anti-ISIS coalition has required ongoing U.S. military presence in Iraq since 2014
  • An estimated 150,000 died in the fight against ISIS across Iraq and Syria

Iran Became the Dominant Power in Iraq — the Opposite of What Was Planned

The neoconservatives who planned the Iraq War believed a democratic Iraq would transform the Middle East and weaken Iran. The opposite happened. Removing Saddam eliminated Iran's primary regional adversary. The Shia-majority government that took power in Baghdad developed deep ties to Tehran. Iran now exercises more influence in Iraq than the United States does. Hinnebusch (2007)

  • Iran-backed militias (Hashd al-Sha'abi / PMF) have 150,000 fighters embedded in the Iraqi state
  • Iraqi oil revenues help Iran evade international sanctions
  • Iranian advisors operate openly in Baghdad ministries
  • The January 2020 U.S. killing of Iranian General Qasem Soleimani took place in Baghdad

Intelligence Failures and "Sexed-Up" Dossiers Reshaped Public Trust

The WMD failure was the most consequential intelligence error in modern history. It did not occur because the intelligence agencies were stupid — it occurred because policymakers selected, amplified, and in some cases fabricated evidence to support a predetermined conclusion. The damage to institutional trust extended far beyond Iraq. Simuziya (2023)

  • The UK Chilcot Inquiry (2016) concluded Blair had "misrepresented" the intelligence to Parliament
  • Public trust in Western intelligence agencies fell to historic lows after the WMD failure
  • The "Iraq effect" made Western publics skeptical of later intervention arguments in Libya, Syria, and Iran
  • CIA Director George Tenet called the WMD case a "slam dunk" — a characterization he later disputed

The War Tested — and Damaged — the UN System of Collective Security

The UN Charter's prohibition on unilateral military action was designed to prevent exactly what the U.S. and UK did in 2003. The war was undertaken without Security Council authorization and against the explicit opposition of three permanent members. UN Secretary-General Kofi Annan said in September 2004 that the war was "illegal." The precedent has been cited by Russia in justifying its own military actions. Simuziya (2023)

  • Kofi Annan's September 2004 statement was the first time a sitting UN Secretary-General had explicitly declared a war illegal
  • Russia cited "regime change" precedent and humanitarian justifications in 2008 (Georgia) and 2022 (Ukraine)
  • The Security Council's inability to prevent the Iraq War weakened reform efforts for two decades
  • Elizabeth Wilmshurst, the UK Foreign Office's deputy legal adviser, resigned rather than sign off on the war's legality

Legacy & Debate

The central scholarly argument about the Iraq War has shifted over two decades. In the early years, debate focused on whether the WMD intelligence was genuinely mistaken or deliberately manipulated. That question has largely been settled — the Chilcot Inquiry, Senate Intelligence Committee investigations, and academic research all found that the intelligence was at minimum aggressively shaped and at worst fabricated. The live scholarly argument now concerns causation: why did the war happen, and what does that reveal about American power, international institutions, and the limits of democratic accountability?

Kenneth Pollack
Brookings Institution — The Threatening Storm (2002)
Argued before the invasion that the WMD threat was genuine and the case for war was defensible on national security grounds, given the intelligence available. Later acknowledged his analysis was wrong. His pre-war case represented the "liberal hawk" position that the war could be legitimate if conducted carefully.
Raymond Hinnebusch
University of St Andrews — Critique: Critical Middle Eastern Studies (2007)
Argues the war's real causes were structural: neoconservative ideology, U.S. hegemonic strategy, oil interests, and the influence of pro-Likud officials who wanted Saddam removed for Israel's strategic benefit. WMD was a post-hoc justification for a decision already made. The war represented the breakdown of the international order built after 1945.
Michael Spagat
Royal Holloway, University of London — PLOS ONE (2024)
Does not address causation directly but provides the most rigorous assessment of the war's human cost. Finds that mainstream estimates of ~150,000–300,000 violent deaths are well-supported, while the much-publicized Lancet studies (600,000+) and ORB survey (1 million) are methodologically flawed. His analysis complicates both minimizing narratives and maximizing ones.

Was the Iraq War Legal?

The legality question has three parts, corresponding to the three grounds under international law on which force might be justified. First: UN Security Council authorization. The U.S. and UK argued that Resolution 1441 (2002) and earlier resolutions — particularly Resolution 678 (1990), which authorized force to expel Iraq from Kuwait — provided sufficient authority for the 2003 invasion. This "revival" argument was rejected by France, Russia, China, Germany, and most international law scholars. SCR 1441 explicitly required the Security Council to reconvene before any military action; the U.S. and UK bypassed that step. Simuziya (2023)

Second: self-defense. The UN Charter permits unilateral military action in cases of genuine armed attack or imminent threat. Iraq had not attacked the United States or UK, and UNMOVIC had found no evidence of active WMD programs that could constitute an imminent threat. The Bush administration's novel "preemptive self-defense" doctrine — the right to attack a state before it acquires dangerous weapons — had no basis in existing international law and was explicitly rejected by the International Court of Justice's own jurisprudence. Simuziya (2023)

Third: humanitarian intervention. Some defenders of the war argued retrospectively that removing Saddam — a dictator who had used chemical weapons against his own citizens — was morally legitimate even if legally ambiguous. This argument faces the problem that humanitarian intervention, even under the Responsibility to Protect framework adopted by the UN in 2005, requires Security Council authorization. It also faces the empirical problem that the invasion created far more suffering than it ended. The death toll, displacement figures, and the rise of ISIS make the humanitarian case very difficult to sustain on consequentialist grounds. Simuziya (2023)

The Case For the Invasion
  • Iraq had defied 16 UN Security Council resolutions over 12 years — unprecedented non-compliance Simuziya (2023)
  • Genuine uncertainty about WMD — Saddam deliberately maintained ambiguity to deter Iran, even after dismantling programs Hinnebusch (2007)
  • Saddam had used chemical weapons against Kurdish civilians (Halabja, 1988) — documented atrocities
  • SCR 678 (1990) and SCR 1441 (2002) together could be read as authorizing force — the "revival" argument
  • The removal of Saddam eventually allowed the Kurdish region to flourish and a nominal democracy to function
The Case Against the Invasion
  • UNMOVIC inspectors found no active WMD programs in 700+ site visits and requested more time Simuziya (2023)
  • No second UN resolution; three permanent members opposed — the Security Council system was bypassed Simuziya (2023)
  • No imminent threat — self-defense argument fails under existing international law Simuziya (2023)
  • Real drivers were ideological and strategic, not genuinely about WMD Hinnebusch (2007)
  • ~150,000–300,000 violent deaths, 4.7 million displaced, and ISIS as direct consequences Spagat (2024)
  • Kofi Annan (Sept 2004), UK Foreign Office legal advisors, and most international law scholars concluded the war was illegal

The weight of legal and scholarly evidence is not equally balanced. Simuziya's careful legal analysis concludes that the war violated international law on all three grounds on which authorization was claimed. Hinnebusch's political analysis finds that the real decision to go to war preceded the intelligence by years. Spagat's death toll methodology finds that the mainstream estimates — roughly 150,000 to 300,000 violent deaths — are well-supported, while the most dramatic figures are not. None of these findings are seriously contested within their disciplines. The more genuinely live debate is about political accountability: why democratic systems with free presses, opposition parties, and independent intelligence agencies failed so completely to prevent a war that most experts now regard as a catastrophe. Hinnebusch (2007)

"I have indicated it was not in conformity with the UN Charter. From our point of view and from the Charter point of view it was illegal."
— Kofi Annan, UN Secretary-General, September 16, 2004
