Tony Blair

The Labour leader who ended 18 years of Tory rule with a historic 1997 landslide — then spent his political capital on an Iraq War that the Chilcot Inquiry found was built on flawed intelligence and overstated certainty.

Origins & Rise to Power

Tony Blair grew up in a household that didn't match the Labour movement he would come to lead. His father Leo was a Conservative-leaning barrister and law lecturer — a man who had pulled himself up from working-class Glasgow roots and harboured ambitions of his own for parliament that a stroke in 1964 cut short. The experience shaped his son in a particular way: Blair inherited his father's drive and his father's belief that effort and ability, not birth, determined what you could become. He was educated at Fettes College, Edinburgh's most prestigious private school, then read law at Oxford, where he played in a rock band called Ugly Rumours and converted to a devout, if privately held, Anglican Christianity under the influence of Australian theology student Peter Thomson.

His politics solidified in his early twenties, but they were always unusually centrist for Labour. He was not formed by the trade union movement, by the experience of industrial poverty, or by the socialist ideological traditions that had shaped figures like Neil Kinnock or Michael Foot. What drew him to Labour was a combination of communitarian Christianity and a conviction that markets, properly managed, could serve social ends. He entered parliament in 1983 as MP for Sedgefield, County Durham — the same year Labour suffered its worst general election result since 1935 under Michael Foot's hard-left manifesto. Blair spent the next decade watching the party's leadership try to modernise fast enough to catch a country that seemed to have moved permanently rightward under Thatcher.

His moment came with the sudden death of Labour leader John Smith from a heart attack in May 1994. Smith had been rebuilding Labour's credibility after the Kinnock years, but he was seen by Blair's circle as too cautious and too close to the unions. Blair, at 41, ran for the leadership on an explicit modernisation agenda, won easily, and immediately set about dismantling what remained of Labour's socialist commitments — most symbolically by rewriting Clause IV of the party's constitution, which had since 1918 committed Labour to common ownership of the means of production. The rewrite was largely symbolic, but the symbolism was the point: New Labour was announcing itself as a different kind of party, one that could govern from the centre.

New Labour: The Third Way

Blair's governing philosophy, developed with Gordon Brown and the think-tank Demos in the mid-1990s, was known variously as "New Labour," the "Third Way," or simply "Blairism." The core argument was that the old left-right divide — state ownership versus the market, redistribution versus growth — was obsolete. A modern social democratic party, Blair believed, could accept market mechanisms while using state power to invest in education, healthcare, and opportunity. The goal was not to eliminate inequality, but to guarantee that everyone had a fair chance to succeed in a competitive economy.

The Third Way

Blair rejected both Old Labour's statism and Thatcherite laissez-faire. Markets would remain; the state would steer them toward social ends through investment, regulation, and public-private partnership. The rhetoric was of "modernisation" rather than socialism or capitalism.

Ethical Foreign Policy

Foreign Secretary Robin Cook announced in 1997 that Labour would conduct an "ethical foreign policy" — one that placed human rights at its centre. The doctrine justified interventions in Kosovo and Sierra Leone. It was later put under enormous strain by Iraq.

Humanitarian Interventionism

Blair's 1999 Chicago speech articulated a doctrine of "liberal interventionism": the international community had a responsibility to intervene when governments committed atrocities against their own people. Kosovo was the test case. Iraq would be its undoing.

The Special Relationship

Blair invested heavily in the UK-US relationship, believing that British influence with Washington gave London leverage in Europe and at the UN. The logic worked in Kosovo. With Bush, it led Blair into decisions he could not reverse without breaking his most consequential diplomatic commitment.

The domestic record of Blair's first two terms was genuinely substantial. The Good Friday Agreement of April 1998 — which Blair personally pushed across the line in a gruelling late-night negotiation — ended most of the violence in Northern Ireland after 30 years. Scottish and Welsh devolution created new parliaments and assemblies. The national minimum wage was introduced over fierce Tory opposition in 1998. Investment in schools and hospitals was substantial. Child poverty fell by a quarter. The Human Rights Act incorporated the European Convention into British law.

But the governance style carried its own pathologies. Blair centralised decision-making in Downing Street to a degree that sidelined Cabinet. Senior figures — including Robin Cook, Clare Short, and Jack Straw's predecessor as Foreign Secretary — felt that by 2002 the real decisions were being made in bilateral meetings between Blair and his close advisers, with the Cabinet reduced to a ratifying function. This mattered enormously when it came to Iraq, because a Cabinet that functioned as an independent deliberative body might have slowed or stopped the march toward war.

The Road to Iraq

Blair's commitment to the Iraq invasion was not, as is sometimes suggested, a simple surrender to American pressure. By the time George W. Bush was seriously contemplating regime change in mid-2002, Blair had already formulated his own reasons for wanting Saddam Hussein removed. He had read the intelligence assessments about Saddam's alleged biological and chemical weapons programmes, believed them to be broadly accurate, and had concluded — in the post-9/11 environment — that a leader who had used chemical weapons against his own population could not be allowed to retain or rebuild WMD capacity indefinitely. The intervention in Kosovo had also hardened Blair's view that sometimes military force was the only moral answer to a sufficiently dangerous situation.

The critical moment came in April 2002 at Crawford, Texas, where Blair met Bush at his ranch and privately assured him of British support for military action. The Chilcot Inquiry — the seven-year British government investigation that reported in 2016 — found that Blair had, in effect, written a blank cheque to Bush before the Cabinet had been consulted, before Parliament had voted, and before any serious diplomatic effort at the UN had been made. A note Blair sent to Bush after Crawford stated: "I will be with you, whatever." The Inquiry found this was "a fundamental" decision that should have been made by the full Cabinet, not by Blair alone.

The September 2002 "Iraq dossier" — a government publication designed to make the public case for the threat posed by Saddam — became the most controversial document in British political history. It included the claim that Iraqi forces could deploy biological or chemical weapons within 45 minutes of an order. The intelligence behind this claim was, as later emerged, based on a single uncorroborated source and referred specifically to battlefield weapons on the Iraqi front line — not, as many readers assumed, to missiles capable of striking neighbouring countries. The dossier was drafted by Alastair Campbell, Blair's communications director, working alongside the Joint Intelligence Committee. A BBC journalist's report in May 2003 that the dossier had been "sexed up" against intelligence officers' wishes triggered a crisis that ended with the suicide of government weapons expert Dr. David Kelly and an inquiry by Lord Hutton — which exonerated the government, though most observers found Hutton's conclusions unconvincing.

Key Controversy

The Cabinet Resignations

Robin Cook, Leader of the House of Commons and former Foreign Secretary, resigned from Cabinet on March 17, 2003, the day before the parliamentary vote on Iraq. His resignation speech remains one of the most powerful delivered in the modern Commons: "I cannot accept collective responsibility for the decision to commit Britain now to military action in Iraq without international agreement or even a last attempt to use an ultimatum to Saddam to secure the threat of force." Clare Short, International Development Secretary, resigned after the invasion began. Elizabeth Wilmshurst, a senior FCO legal adviser, resigned over her view that the invasion was unlawful under international law.

The parliamentary vote on March 18, 2003 was won, but only with Conservative support. A majority of Labour MPs voted with the government, but 139 Labour members voted against — the largest rebellion against a sitting government in modern British parliamentary history. Blair survived politically, but the rebellion revealed the depth of unease within his own party about what he was doing and why.

Blair met Bush at his Crawford, Texas ranch and privately committed the UK to supporting military action against Iraq. The Chilcot Inquiry found this was done before Cabinet consultation and before any UN process. A memo Blair sent Bush after the meeting contained the phrase "I will be with you, whatever" — a commitment that Chilcot described as having "foreclosed options" for UK policy.

Blair's supporters argue he believed that being "with" Bush was the only way to influence American policy — that a Britain outside the coalition would have had no leverage over how the war was prosecuted or what followed. Critics argue this gets the logic backward: that unconditional commitment removed any British leverage over whether to go to war at all.

SEPTEMBER 2002
The "Dodgy Dossier" and the 45-Minute Claim
▼

The British government published "Iraq's Weapons of Mass Destruction: The Assessment of the British Government," a publicly-released intelligence document. Blair's foreword stated: "I believe the assessed intelligence has established beyond doubt" that Saddam had continued to produce chemical and biological weapons. The document's headline claim — that Iraq could deploy such weapons within 45 minutes of an order — was highlighted in newspaper front pages across Britain the next morning.

The claim was later found to refer to battlefield munitions already deployed in the field, not strategic weapons capable of threatening British interests in Cyprus or beyond. The Joint Intelligence Committee chairman John Scarlett told the Hutton Inquiry that the 45-minute claim came from "a single source" and that its inclusion in the dossier — and the way it was presented — was a matter of judgement that he would not revisit. Most post-inquiry analyses have concluded that the dossier overstated the intelligence to make the public case for a decision Blair had already privately made.

MARCH 2003
Parliament Votes; Invasion Begins
▼

On March 18, Parliament voted 412 to 149 to authorise military action, with 139 Labour MPs rebelling. Robin Cook had resigned the previous day. On March 20, US and UK forces began the invasion of Iraq. Baghdad fell on April 9. Blair flew to Washington and addressed a joint session of Congress in July, where he received 17 standing ovations — an indication of how his stock had risen in the United States even as it fell at home.

No weapons of mass destruction were found. The Iraq Survey Group, sent to search for them after the invasion, reported in September 2004 that Saddam had effectively abandoned his WMD programmes in 1991 and had not resumed them. The intelligence on which Blair had staked his government's credibility, his personal integrity, and British foreign policy was wrong.

The War & Its Aftermath

Blair had sold the invasion partly on a version of humanitarian interventionism — that removing Saddam would liberate the Iraqi people. But the post-invasion planning, which the Chilcot Report found was "wholly inadequate," produced chaos rather than liberation. The Coalition Provisional Authority, headed by Paul Bremer and reporting to Washington rather than London, issued two orders in May 2003 that would define the next decade of Iraqi history. CPA Order 1 removed the top four tiers of Ba'ath Party members from all government positions — dismissing 30,000 to 50,000 people from civil service and public life overnight. CPA Order 2 dissolved the entire Iraqi military, putting 400,000 trained, armed men out of work simultaneously. Zinn (2016)

The CIA station chief in Baghdad warned Bremer before CPA Order 1 was signed that 30,000 to 50,000 Baathists would "go underground by sundown." Franklin C. Miller at the National Security Council called it "dangerous to put 300,000 men on the street with guns, without jobs." These warnings were not heeded. The Sunni insurgency that emerged in 2003–2004, and the sectarian civil war that followed the bombing of the Al-Askari mosque in Samarra in February 2006, drew heavily on the network of former officers and Ba'ath Party cadres who had been stripped of their livelihoods and their status. Research on the leadership of ISIS has found that roughly 8 of 13 top figures were former Iraqi military officers dismissed under CPA Order 2. Zinn (2016)

Britain's specific contribution was the occupation of Basra, in the south. British forces initially tried a softer approach than US forces in the Sunni triangle, with less visible footprint and more emphasis on engagement. But by 2006 Basra had effectively been ceded to Shia militia groups, and British soldiers were confined to their bases. When British forces finally withdrew from the city in 2007, US commanders openly said the British had "lost" Basra. It was a humiliating end to a commitment that had been sold on the promise of professional, experience-tested British military competence.

The Abu Ghraib photographs, published in April 2004, produced a political crisis that cut across the entire coalition. British forces had their own abuse scandal — the Baha Mousa case, in which an Iraqi hotel worker died in British military custody in Basra in September 2003 after being hooded and subjected to "conditioning techniques." The subsequent inquiry found that the death resulted from "an appalling episode of serious, gratuitous violence." Blair's government faced the same question that confronted Washington: how far up the chain of command did the authorisation for abuse extend? AJIL (2004)

Fall & Later Career

Blair won a third general election in May 2005, but with a reduced majority of 66 seats and the lowest popular vote share (35%) ever to produce an outright parliamentary majority. The Iraq War had cost Labour millions of votes and had cost Blair his personal authority within his own party. The internal pressure from Gordon Brown's camp — who believed Brown had been promised the leadership and was being denied it — intensified throughout 2006. In September 2006, a group of Labour MPs publicly called on Blair to name a departure date. He announced he would leave within a year. On June 27, 2007, he resigned and Gordon Brown became prime minister.

The Chilcot Report, published in July 2016 after seven years of investigation, was the definitive accounting. It found that Blair had presented the intelligence on Iraqi WMDs with "a certainty that was not justified." It found the post-war planning was "wholly inadequate." It found that the decision to join the invasion was made "before the peaceful options for disarmament had been exhausted." Blair responded the same day with a 90-minute press conference in which he stated he took "full responsibility" but maintained he had made the right decision with the information available. The Inquiry's findings did not change his position.

After leaving Downing Street, Blair was appointed Quartet Representative to the Middle East — the official envoy of the EU, UN, US, and Russia to the Israeli-Palestinian peace process. He served in this role from 2007 to 2015, but achieved very little: Israeli settlement construction accelerated, the Gaza blockade continued, and no peace process moved forward on his watch. Critics noted that his proximity to the Israeli government and his avoidance of Hamas — the elected government of Gaza — made him structurally incapable of brokering anything. He resigned the role in 2015.

Post-2015, Blair built an extensive consultancy and advisory network through the Tony Blair Institute for Global Change. He advised African governments, signed lucrative deals with Middle Eastern governments including Kazakhstan, and remained a prominent voice in British politics — particularly on Brexit, which he opposed vigorously, arguing for a second referendum. For many in the Labour movement, his continued interventions were unwelcome. The party he had reshaped had moved in a very different direction under Jeremy Corbyn, and Blair's repeated interventions from outside were seen by Corbyn supporters as attempts to relitigate the internal battles he had already won and already lost.

Legacy & Debate

Blair's legacy is among the most hotly contested of any postwar British leader. He won three elections, held power for ten years, and enacted a substantial domestic programme. He also took Britain into a war of choice on the basis of intelligence that proved false, and that produced a catastrophe whose consequences — ISIS, the Syrian crisis, the fragmentation of the Middle East state system — were still being felt two decades later. How those two things are weighed against each other depends heavily on what you think politics is ultimately for.

Contested Points
  • Did Blair genuinely believe the intelligence, or did he know it was weak and choose to proceed anyway? Chilcot found he "overstated" it, but stopped short of calling him a liar.
  • Was the "special relationship" strategy — staying close to the US to influence Washington — ever a realistic basis for UK foreign policy? The Iraq experience suggests the influence was largely illusory.
  • New Labour's economic model, which accepted financial deregulation and rising inequality alongside public investment, is now seen by many as having contributed to the conditions for the 2008 financial crisis.
  • Blair's domestic record — real achievements on poverty, healthcare, peace in Northern Ireland — is rarely credited today by people who opposed Iraq, or by left-wing critics who see his economic legacy as Thatcherism with better branding.

The Case For Blair
  • He genuinely believed the intelligence about Iraqi WMDs — a belief shared by the CIA, MI6, and most Western intelligence agencies at the time. Rentoul, 2001
  • Kosovo and Sierra Leone demonstrated that military intervention could succeed and could save lives — a track record that made Iraq seem more plausible. Blair, Chicago Speech 1999
  • Saddam Hussein had used chemical weapons against Kurdish civilians at Halabja (1988) — a documented atrocity that made the WMD threat credible. Human Rights Watch
  • British influence over American conduct in Iraq was real, if limited: without UK pressure, there might have been no second UN resolution attempt, less post-war international involvement. Powell, 2010
The Case Against Blair
  • Blair committed the UK at Crawford in April 2002 "before the peaceful options were exhausted" — making his later UN diplomacy performative rather than genuine. Chilcot Report, 2016
  • The 45-minute claim was based on a single uncorroborated source and was presented as established fact; intelligence professionals warned at the time that the dossier overstated the evidence. Gilligan/BBC, 2003
  • Post-war planning was "wholly inadequate" — the chaos that followed was foreseeable and forecast by the Foreign Office and military advisers who were not listened to. Chilcot Report, 2016
  • The consequences — insurgency, civil war, ISIS, regional destabilisation — were catastrophic in scale and were warned against by those with expert knowledge of Iraq. Zinn, 2016
