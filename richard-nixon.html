Origins: The Outsider Who Always Felt Like One

Richard Nixon was born in 1913 in Yorba Linda, California, in a house his father built with his own hands. The family was poor — his father ran a series of struggling businesses — and the shadow of financial precarity never fully left Nixon even after he reached the White House. Two of his brothers died of tuberculosis. He worked his way through school and earned a scholarship to Duke Law School, graduating third in his class in 1937 but being rejected by every New York law firm he applied to. He was from the wrong side of everything that mattered in East Coast establishment America: the wrong class, the wrong school, the wrong region, the wrong kind of ambition — too naked, too hungry, too obvious about wanting.

This sense of resentment toward elites — a feeling that the establishment looked down on him — ran through Nixon's entire political career. It drove his best instincts (his genuine ability to connect with working-class Americans, his willingness to take bold foreign policy risks that "respectable" opinion would have rejected) and his worst (the enemies lists, the abuse of power, the paranoia that eventually destroyed him). He was, as historian Richard Reeves put it, "a man who thought he was being persecuted — and he was right, because he had made himself so threatening to so many people."

He served in the Pacific during World War II, returned to California, and was elected to Congress in 1946 on the strength of an aggressive Red-baiting campaign against his opponent. His pursuit of Alger Hiss — a senior State Department official whom Nixon suspected of Soviet espionage — made him nationally famous and established his reputation as a relentless, ruthless political operator. By 36 he was a senator; by 39, Eisenhower's vice president. He lost the 1960 presidential election to Kennedy by one of the narrowest margins in history, lost the 1962 California gubernatorial race, told the press "you won't have Nixon to kick around anymore" — and came back eight years later to win the presidency.

Worldview: Power Without Sentiment

Nixon's foreign policy instincts were deeply realist — he and Kissinger were ideological soulmates in their conviction that American power should be deployed strategically, not morally. But Nixon added something Kissinger's framework didn't quite account for: a genuine populist instinct, a sense of what ordinary Americans wanted that was often more accurate than the Ivy League advisors around him. He understood that Americans were tired of Vietnam in a way that many in the foreign policy establishment still did not.

Strategic Realism

Nixon believed the United States could not sustain an open-ended global commitment to contain Communism everywhere. The "Nixon Doctrine" (1969) stated that while the U.S. would honour its treaty commitments, it would not commit ground troops to defend allies in future; instead it would provide material support and let allied nations supply their own soldiers. This was a frank admission that America's reach had exceeded its grasp in Vietnam.

Détente

Nixon pursued détente — a deliberate relaxation of tensions — with both the Soviet Union and China simultaneously. This was strategically sophisticated: by engaging both simultaneously, he prevented them from coordinating against the United States and gave Washington leverage over both. The China opening was the masterstroke; it changed the fundamental balance of the Cold War.

The Silent Majority

Nixon's political genius was identifying and speaking to Americans who felt ignored by the media, universities, and political establishment — people who thought the Vietnam War protesters were unpatriotic, that the counterculture was decadent, that their country was changing too fast. His 1969 "Silent Majority" speech gave this constituency a name and made them feel seen. This political strategy became the template for American conservative populism for the next fifty years.

Paradoxical Domestics

Nixon's domestic record defies his conservative reputation. He created the EPA, proposed a guaranteed national income, signed the Clean Air Act, opened trade with China (which eventually moved manufacturing jobs abroad), ended the military draft, and presided over some of the most significant federal spending increases since the New Deal. Many of these policies are to the left of anything his Democratic successors achieved. His conservatism was primarily cultural and political, not economic.

What He Actually Built

The Opening to China

In February 1972, Nixon flew to Beijing and met with Mao Zedong, ending 23 years of diplomatic isolation between the United States and the People's Republic of China. The visit was the culmination of eighteen months of secret negotiations, including Kissinger's clandestine 1971 trip. The Shanghai Communiqué, signed at the end of the visit, acknowledged that "all Chinese on either side of the Taiwan Strait maintain there is but one China" — a deliberately ambiguous formulation that sidestepped the impossible question of Taiwan's status while opening a relationship with the world's most populous nation. No prominent U.S. politician could have made this opening to a Communist government without Nixon's unimpeachable anti-Communist credentials. A liberal Democrat doing the same would have been destroyed politically.

Arms Control with the Soviet Union

The SALT I treaty, signed in Moscow in May 1972, was the first agreement to actually limit nuclear weapons rather than just regulate them. It froze the number of ballistic missile launchers at existing levels and was accompanied by the ABM Treaty, which prevented both sides from building nationwide missile defence systems. These were imperfect agreements — they didn't reduce existing weapons — but they established the principle that strategic weapons could be negotiated, not just accumulated, and created the diplomatic architecture that all subsequent arms control built upon.

The EPA and Environmental Policy

In January 1970, Nixon signed the National Environmental Policy Act. Later that year he created the Environmental Protection Agency by executive order. The Clean Air Act of 1970 — one of the most consequential pieces of environmental legislation in American history — was signed under his administration. The Endangered Species Act followed in 1973. Nixon was not an environmentalist by conviction — he viewed environmental regulation as something the public wanted and that he could deliver — but the institutional legacy of his environmental legislation has outlasted almost everything else he did.

The Dark Side: Cambodia, Chile, and Paranoia

Documented

The Secret Cambodia Bombing

Beginning in March 1969, Nixon authorised a secret bombing campaign — "Operation Menu" — against Vietnamese supply lines in neutral Cambodia. The bombing was kept secret from Congress, from the American public, and from most of the U.S. government; the military falsified records. Over 14 months, more than 2,700 B-52 sorties dropped approximately 110,000 tons of bombs on Cambodia. When the bombing was discovered by the press in May 1969, Nixon and Kissinger had reporters and government officials wiretapped to find the source of the leak. The bombing, and the subsequent ground invasion of Cambodia in 1970, contributed to the destabilisation of Cambodia's government and the conditions in which the Khmer Rouge came to power, killing 1.5 to 2 million Cambodians between 1975 and 1979.

Chile

Nixon's personal reaction to Allende's election in September 1970 was recorded by Kissinger: "I don't see why we need to stand by and watch a country go Communist due to the irresponsibility of its own people." He authorised a $10 million CIA programme to prevent Allende taking office and ongoing covert operations throughout Allende's presidency. When the 1973 coup removed Allende, the Nixon administration promptly recognised the Pinochet government. These decisions — to subvert a democratic election in a sovereign country — are, by any conventional standard of international law, serious abuses of power that happened to succeed. Their consequences (3,200+ Chileans killed under Pinochet) are not Nixon's sole responsibility, but the role of his administration in creating the conditions is thoroughly documented.

The Enemies List and Abuse of Power

Nixon maintained literal lists of political enemies — journalists, academics, politicians — and directed the IRS to audit them, instructed agencies to deny them government contracts, and used the FBI to investigate them. The White House "Plumbers" unit — created to stop leaks — conducted illegal wiretaps and break-ins. These were not aberrations but systematic abuses of federal power against political opponents. They were also, ultimately, the institutional pattern that produced Watergate.

Watergate: The Scandal That Ended Him

June 17, 1972
The Break-In at the Watergate
▼

Five men were arrested breaking into the Democratic National Committee headquarters at the Watergate complex in Washington D.C. They were employees of Nixon's re-election committee. Nixon did not order the break-in — it was the initiative of overzealous subordinates. What Nixon did was cover it up. Within days of the break-in, he was on tape discussing how to use the CIA to obstruct the FBI investigation. That decision — to obstruct justice rather than fire the people responsible — was the one that destroyed his presidency.

Woodward & Bernstein (1974)
1973
The Senate Hearings and the Saturday Night Massacre
▼

The Senate Watergate Committee hearings in the summer of 1973 revealed the existence of Nixon's White House taping system. Special Prosecutor Archibald Cox subpoenaed the tapes. Nixon ordered Attorney General Elliot Richardson to fire Cox; Richardson resigned rather than comply. Deputy Attorney General William Ruckelshaus also resigned. Solicitor General Robert Bork carried out the firing — an event immediately named the "Saturday Night Massacre." The episode made Nixon appear guilty to much of the country even before the tapes were heard. Nixon eventually released edited transcripts, then the tapes themselves — one of which had an unexplained 18.5-minute gap.

Emery (1994)
August 8–9, 1974
Resignation — The Only President to Quit
▼

When the "smoking gun" tape was released in August 1974 — recording Nixon discussing the cover-up six days after the break-in — his remaining congressional support collapsed. Republican leaders told him he faced certain impeachment and conviction. On the evening of August 8, Nixon addressed the nation from the Oval Office and announced his resignation, effective at noon the next day. He flew to California on Air Force One — he was no longer president when he landed. His farewell speech in the East Room that morning, rambling and emotional, contained the line: "Always remember, others may hate you. But those who hate you don't win unless you hate them — and then you destroy yourself." It was, in its way, an insight he had spent his entire career failing to act on.

What Watergate Actually Was

Why it matters beyond just Nixon

Watergate is often reduced to "a president who lied." What actually happened was broader: a systematic campaign by the executive branch to use federal agencies — the FBI, CIA, IRS, and Department of Justice — as instruments of political warfare against opponents, combined with obstruction of a criminal investigation. The constitutional crisis it produced — resolved only by Nixon's resignation before the Senate could remove him — tested and ultimately validated the American system of checks and balances. It is also the reason that all subsequent political scandals have the suffix "-gate" appended to them, which is an annoying linguistic legacy but an accurate measure of its impact.

Why It Matters Today

Nixon left office in disgrace in August 1974. But the two things that define his presidency — the strategic architecture he built with Kissinger, and the constitutional crisis Watergate produced — have both outlasted him in ways that are directly relevant to the present. The world order he designed is unravelling. The precedents Watergate set are being relitigated in every subsequent abuse-of-power crisis.

The China Opening — and Its Collapse

Nixon's 1972 opening to China was the foundational act of the post-Cold War international order — the decision that brought China into the global economic system and made possible four decades of relative great-power peace. The premise of that opening — that engagement would liberalise China and create mutual interests in stability — is now widely regarded as having failed. The U.S.-China relationship Nixon designed is now one of the world's most dangerous rivalries, and the debate about what went wrong is a direct interrogation of Nixon's strategic assumptions.

  • Nixon and Kissinger's bet was that integrating China into the international order would give it a stake in that order's survival — Xi Jinping's China has consistently sought to revise that order rather than protect it
  • The deliberate ambiguity of the Shanghai Communiqué on Taiwan — which Nixon accepted to get the deal done — is now the central source of the most dangerous potential conflict in the world
  • Henry Kissinger's final visits to Beijing in 2023, explicitly defending engagement with China, were a defence of the Nixon-era framework against its bipartisan critics in Washington
  • The decoupling debate — whether the U.S. should try to disentangle its economy from China's — is a debate about whether Nixon's core strategic bet was a mistake that should be reversed

Watergate's Constitutional Precedents — Still Being Tested

Watergate established that a president can be forced from office through the constitutional process. It also established — through Nixon's near-success in obstruction — exactly how far a president can push institutional resistance before it breaks. Every subsequent American president who has faced impeachment proceedings, abuse-of-power allegations, or attempted obstruction of congressional or judicial oversight has been measured against the Watergate template. The template's continued relevance suggests the crisis it resolved was not fully resolved.

  • The Saturday Night Massacre — Nixon's firing of the special prosecutor — established the principle that a president who fires the investigator investigating him will face overwhelming congressional and public backlash; it did not establish that he cannot do it
  • Nixon's pardon by Gerald Ford — which Ford said was necessary to end the national trauma — remains the most contested presidential pardon in U.S. history and set a precedent that has been invoked in every subsequent debate about presidential accountability
  • The Supreme Court's unanimous ruling in United States v. Nixon (1974) — that executive privilege does not protect a president from criminal subpoenas — remains the foundational precedent on presidential immunity, though subsequent courts have narrowed and contested it
  • The two Trump impeachments, the debates about presidential immunity in criminal proceedings, and the question of whether a sitting president can be indicted all trace their legal genealogy to Watergate

The Imperial Presidency He Expanded

Nixon's presidency was the high-water mark of what historian Arthur Schlesinger called "the imperial presidency" — the accumulation of executive power beyond constitutional limits that accelerated through the Cold War. His abuses prompted the War Powers Act (1973), the Congressional Budget Act, FISA courts, and campaign finance reform. But those constraints were never fully enforced, and the executive power he claimed — particularly in national security — has been incrementally expanded by every subsequent president regardless of party.

  • The War Powers Act required presidential notification of Congress within 48 hours of military action and withdrawal after 60 days without authorisation — no president has ever fully complied; no court has ever enforced it
  • Nixon's secret Cambodia bombing (Operation Menu) established the template for covert military operations that subsequent administrations used to expand drone warfare, special operations, and cyber operations without congressional authorisation
  • The NSA surveillance programmes revealed by Edward Snowden in 2013 were downstream of the same executive power claims Nixon made — filtered through subsequent legal frameworks but sharing the same DNA

The Southern Strategy — American Politics Remade

Nixon's 1968 and 1972 electoral strategies — appealing to white Southern voters alienated by the Civil Rights Act through coded racial messaging, "law and order" rhetoric, and opposition to busing — are the founding act of the modern American political alignment. The Republicans are a Southern-dominated party and the Democrats are an urban-and-minority coalition largely because of choices Nixon made and operatives he trained. This is not a historical curiosity; it is the structure of every American election since.

  • Nixon's domestic adviser Kevin Phillips laid out the Southern Strategy explicitly in The Emerging Republican Majority (1969) — a book written as a governing blueprint, not a prediction
  • The realignment Nixon set in motion transformed the Solid South from a Democratic bloc into a Republican one over the following two decades — completing by 1994 what Nixon began in 1968
  • The "war on drugs," launched by Nixon in 1971, was later revealed by Nixon aide John Ehrlichman to have been designed partly to criminalise Black communities and anti-war protesters — a domestic political weapon dressed as public health policy
  • Every Republican presidential candidate since Nixon has had to decide whether to build on or distance from his racial-political framework; most have built on it

Legacy & Debate: The Most Complex President

Nixon may be the hardest American president to assess because the gap between his achievements and his failures is wider than for almost anyone else. He accomplished things in foreign policy that no other president managed. He also committed crimes in office. These facts coexist and neither cancels the other out.

The record of accomplishment
  • Opening to China — the most significant diplomatic achievement of the Cold War era after Truman's NATO
  • SALT I and ABM Treaty — established the framework for all subsequent nuclear arms control
  • Created the EPA; signed the Clean Air Act and Endangered Species Act
  • Ended the military draft — one of the most consequential domestic decisions of the era
  • Nixon Doctrine reduced U.S. ground commitments abroad — a realistic reassessment after Vietnam
  • Proposed a guaranteed national income (Family Assistance Plan) — more progressive than anything Congress passed
The documented abuses
  • Obstruction of justice in the Watergate cover-up — resigned before he could be impeached and convicted
  • Secret Cambodia bombing without congressional authorisation; falsified military records
  • Destabilisation of Allende's democratic government in Chile
  • Abuse of federal agencies (FBI, CIA, IRS) against political enemies
  • Systematic wiretapping of staff and journalists without warrants
  • Extended the Vietnam War by four years — an estimated 20,000+ additional American deaths, and hundreds of thousands of Vietnamese — in pursuit of "peace with honour" that ultimately achieved the same outcome
The unresolved argument
  • Did Nixon's crimes disqualify his achievements? The China opening and SALT I mattered regardless of what he did at Watergate
  • Would Watergate have been discovered without the taping system Nixon himself installed? The tapes were his own undoing, which is perhaps the most Nixonian thing about the whole affair
  • His Vietnam "peace with honour" strategy — including the Christmas 1972 bombing of Hanoi — produced a 1973 ceasefire that North Vietnam broke in 1975. Was it an achievement or a prolonged, costly delay of inevitable defeat?
