Ba'athism

The Arab nationalist ideology that brought Saddam Hussein and the Assad dynasty to power — and whose collapse reveals what happens when a revolutionary idea becomes cover for clan rule.

What Is Ba'athism?

Ba'athism — from the Arabic البعث, meaning "renaissance" or "resurrection" — is the Arab nationalist ideology founded in Damascus in 1947 that claimed the Arabic-speaking world constitutes a single nation, artificially divided by European colonialism, and that this nation must be reunited under a secular, socialist state. At its peak, Ba'athist parties controlled two of the Arab world's most powerful states simultaneously: Syria from 1963 and Iraq from 1968. Its two most consequential leaders — Hafez al-Assad in Syria and Saddam Hussein in Iraq — became among the 20th century's most durable and brutal dictators.

The ideology was shaped by a specific historical crisis: the 1940s, when most Arab lands were still under French or British control, when the borders of the modern Arab states had just been drawn by European powers, and when the 1948 Arab defeat in the first Arab-Israeli war seemed to confirm that a fragmented Arab world could not defend itself. Michel Aflaq — the party's founding theorist — believed that Arabs shared a spiritual essence that transcended their differences of religion, region, and class, and that a unified secular Arab state would achieve the modernisation and dignity that colonial division had denied them.

The gap between this vision and what Ba'athist states actually became is the central historical fact about the ideology. In practice, Ba'athism in both Syria and Iraq became the political cover for narrow sectarian and tribal cliques — Alawites under Assad, Sunni Tikritis under Saddam — to monopolise state power while using the rhetoric of Arab unity to suppress political opposition. The ideology that claimed to transcend sectarianism produced some of the most nakedly sectarian regimes in modern Arab history. Understanding how that happened is essential for understanding the Middle East today.

Definition

Ba'athism vs. Pan-Arabism — not the same thing

Ba'athism is one version of pan-Arab nationalism, not the whole movement. Pan-Arabism — the broader belief that Arabs form one nation — was also expressed through Gamal Abdel Nasser's Egyptian-centred Nasserism, the Arab Nationalist Movement (founded by George Habash), and various smaller parties. Ba'athism was distinctive in being organised as a formal, multi-country party structure with a single command, in explicitly combining nationalism with socialism, and in achieving state power. Nasser's Egypt and Ba'athist Syria briefly merged into the United Arab Republic (1958–61) before Egypt's dominant position provoked Syria's secession — an early sign that Arab unity was easier to invoke than to implement.

Origins

Michel Aflaq was born in 1910 in Damascus to a Greek Orthodox family — a detail that would matter enormously. As a Christian Arab, he had a personal stake in defining Arab identity in non-Islamic terms: if Arabism were defined by Islam, Christian Arabs would be permanent outsiders in their own homeland. Aflaq studied at the Sorbonne in the 1930s alongside his co-founder Salah al-Din al-Bitar, a Sunni Muslim, where both were influenced by French socialist thought, German romantic nationalism (the idea of a Volk defined by culture and language, not blood), and the anti-colonial left. They returned to Damascus in 1934 as schoolteachers, began meeting with small groups of students, and in 1940 formally established what they initially called the Arab Ba'ath Movement.

The party's first national congress in April 1947 produced a constitution that laid out the core principles: one Arab nation with an eternal mission; freedom from colonialism and foreign domination; and Arab socialism. The phrase "eternal mission" (risala khalida) is worth noting — Ba'athism was conceived not just as a political programme but as a quasi-metaphysical civilisational project. Aflaq wrote in a prose style that deliberately echoed religious scripture, and the party demanded the kind of total commitment — ideological, personal, national — usually associated with religious movements. This combination of secular politics and quasi-religious intensity would prove both a strength and a weakness: it generated genuine fervour in recruits and later enabled the personality cults of Hafez and Saddam.

Ba'athism's crucial early growth came not through electoral politics but through systematic infiltration of the Syrian military officer corps. Akram Hourani — a shrewd rural politician who merged his Arab Socialist Party with the Ba'ath in 1954 to create the Arab Socialist Ba'ath Party — worked to recruit officers from minority communities (Alawites, Druze, Ismailis) who saw the secular nationalist party as a vehicle for advancement against the urban Sunni merchant establishment that had dominated Syrian society. This strategy would ultimately destroy the civilian leadership's control of the party: the military officers who joined were interested in power, not ideology, and when they had enough guns they removed the founders.

The Three Core Ideas

The Ba'ath Party's constitution expressed its programme as a triad: Unity (al-Wahda), Freedom (al-Hurriya), and Socialism (al-Ishtirakiya). The slogan "One Arab nation with an eternal mission — unity, freedom, socialism" appeared on party buildings across Syria and Iraq. These three principles are worth understanding precisely, because the distance between what they claimed and what Ba'athist governments delivered reveals the ideology's fundamental failures.

Arab Unity (al-Wahda)

The foundational claim: the Arabic-speaking world from Morocco to Iraq is one nation, and existing borders are colonial impositions that must eventually be dissolved into a single unified Arab state. The Ba'ath Party was conceived as a pan-Arab organisation with branches in every Arab country rather than a national party for any single state. In practice, the Iraqi and Syrian Ba'ath branches became bitter rivals after the 1966 split and spent decades actively trying to destabilise each other's governments. "Unity" under Ba'athism always meant unity under one party's leadership — and each wing believed it should lead.

Freedom (al-Hurriya)

Freedom from colonialism, foreign military presence, and Western or Soviet domination — what Aflaq called the external dimension of Arab liberation. This meant expulsion of British and French bases, nationalisation of foreign-owned industries, and formal nonalignment. Ba'athist rhetoric consistently framed both Western imperialism and Soviet communism as threats to Arab authenticity. In practice, both Syrian and Iraqi Ba'ath governments maintained deep military relationships with the Soviet Union while invoking this anti-imperial principle. "Freedom" applied to the Arab nation as a collective; individual political freedom was never part of the programme and was actively suppressed.

Socialism (al-Ishtirakiya)

Arab socialism — explicitly not Marxist internationalism. Ba'athism rejected class struggle and rejected the idea that Arab workers had more in common with Soviet workers than with Arab capitalists. Its socialism was nationalist: redistribution within the Arab nation, using the state to develop the economy and provide welfare rather than to abolish private property. In Ba'athist governments, this meant land reform, nationalisation of oil and banking sectors, free education and healthcare, and state direction of economic development. In practice, it produced state capitalism that enriched party members and military officers while delivering genuine welfare improvements to rural populations — until oil revenue fluctuations and war spending made the welfare state unsustainable.

Secularism (al-'Almaniyya)

Though not one of the official three pillars, secularism was Ba'athism's most socially radical claim — and the one that most directly produced violent conflict. Aflaq insisted that Arab identity was based on language, culture, and history, not religion. Christians, Muslims, and Druze were equally Arab; the state would not privilege Islam. This was genuine for Aflaq (a Christian), strategic for many Muslim Ba'athists, and threatening to the Muslim Brotherhood and Islamist movements throughout. Both Syrian and Iraqi Ba'ath governments engaged in systematic, often brutal suppression of Islamist opposition — the Syrian regime's 1982 massacre of the Brotherhood in Hama being the most extreme case.

Structural Contradiction

The civilian-military split that destroyed the founders

Ba'athism's fatal structural flaw was built in from the beginning: the party needed military officers to take power, but military officers had no inherent loyalty to the party's ideology. Once in power, the officer cadres who had staged coups in Syria (1963) and Iraq (1968) had no reason to defer to civilian party theorists like Aflaq. In Syria, the founders were expelled in 1966; in Iraq, they were tolerated as figureheads while real power passed to security chiefs like Saddam Hussein. Every Ba'ath government was, in practice, a military junta using Ba'athist ideology as legitimising rhetoric — not a party government subject to ideological accountability.

Ba'athism in Practice

March 8, 1963 — Syria
The First Ba'ath Coup: Ideology Takes Power
▼

After years of infiltrating Syria's military, Ba'athist and Nasserist officers seized power on March 8, 1963 — a date the Ba'ath Party would call the "8th of March Revolution." The civilian leadership — Aflaq and Bitar — found themselves in nominal authority but with real power held by the Military Committee, a clandestine group of mostly Alawite officers who had built their own parallel organisation within the party. The civilians quickly realised they had been used: the officers had joined the party for its network and its anti-establishment rhetoric, not its ideology.

The first years of Syrian Ba'ath rule were chaotic, marked by rapid nationalisation, land reform, and brutal suppression of the Muslim Brotherhood and traditional landed elites. The party simultaneously fractured between Nasserists (who wanted to merge with Egypt again), civilian Ba'athists (loyal to Aflaq), and the military committee's younger, more radical Alawite faction. In 1966, the younger officers staged a coup within the party, expelled Aflaq and Bitar, and took complete control.

February 8, 1963 — Iraq
Nine Months in Baghdad: Murder Without Governance
▼

Eight days before the Syrian coup, the Iraqi Ba'ath Party also seized power, killing Prime Minister Abd al-Karim Qasim. The new regime immediately launched a systematic massacre of the Iraqi Communist Party, whose strength it feared. Lists of communists were reportedly provided by the CIA, which had identified Qasim's growing ties with Moscow as dangerous. Hundreds of communists were killed in the first weeks; thousands more were imprisoned and tortured in the headquarters of the Ba'ath Party's paramilitary force, the National Guard.

The Iraqi Ba'ath held power for only nine months before being removed in a counter-coup in November 1963 by President Abd al-Salam Arif, who had used the Ba'ath to take power and then had no further use for them. The episode demonstrated the party's capacity for organised violence and its inability to govern: it had no coherent economic programme, it had made enemies of most of the urban professional class, and its internal divisions between Iraqi nationalists and pan-Arab unionists had paralysed its decision-making. It would take five more years of patient organisation before the Ba'ath retook Iraq.

February 1966
The Founders Are Expelled: Ba'athism Devours Itself
▼

In February 1966, the younger Alawite officers led by Salah Jadid staged a coup within the Ba'ath Party itself, overthrowing and expelling the civilian founders Aflaq and Bitar. It was the definitive moment in Ba'athism's institutional history: the ideology was now fully separated from its creators. Aflaq fled to Beirut, then Beirut to Brazil, then eventually — humiliatingly — to Baghdad, where the Iraqi Ba'ath Party (rivals of the Syrian Ba'ath) gave him nominal status as Secretary-General to embarrass Damascus.

The 1966 split permanently divided the Ba'ath movement into two hostile national factions. The Syrian wing (first under Jadid, then from 1970 under Hafez al-Assad) and the Iraqi wing (from 1968 under Ahmad Hassan al-Bakr and then Saddam Hussein) would spend the next three decades in near-open conflict. Each claimed to be the legitimate Ba'ath; each accused the other of betraying the movement; each provided sanctuary to the other's dissidents and funded each other's opposition groups. The ideology of Arab unity had produced two states that despised each other more than either despised Israel.

July 17–30, 1968 — Iraq
The Ba'ath Returns to Baghdad — This Time to Stay
▼

The Iraqi Ba'ath retook power in a two-stage coup in July 1968. The key figure was General Ahmed Hassan al-Bakr, but the decisive operator was his young cousin Saddam Hussein, who had spent the preceding years building a security apparatus loyal to the party rather than the state. As head of internal security, Saddam systematically identified and eliminated rival power centres — military officers who might stage a counter-coup, Shia religious networks, Kurdish political leadership, communist remnants — using a combination of surveillance, infiltration, torture, and execution that was more sophisticated than anything the previous Iraqi government had deployed.

By 1979, when Bakr "retired" in circumstances that remain murky (he was possibly ill; he was possibly pressured), Saddam had neutralised every potential challenger. His first act as president was theatrical and deliberate: he convened a meeting of 400 senior Ba'ath officials, had a "confessed traitor" read out a list of alleged conspirators — mostly Shia Ba'athists who had maintained ties with Syria — and had them removed from the room and executed by firing squads composed of other senior party members, who were thus made complicit. The scene was filmed and copies distributed to Ba'ath branches across Iraq. It was a performance designed to communicate one thing: the party now existed to serve Saddam, not the other way around.

November 1970 — Syria
Hafez al-Assad: Ba'athism as Alawite Statecraft
▼

Hafez al-Assad, Syria's defence minister and air force commander, seized power in November 1970 in what he called the "Corrective Movement." His coup ended the Jadid period, which had pursued radical Ba'athist policies (nationalisation, support for Palestinian fedayeen guerrillas) at the cost of catastrophic military defeat in 1967 and economic stagnation. Assad was a pragmatist: he wanted a stable, powerful Syria, and he was willing to adapt the Ba'athist ideology to serve that goal.

Under Assad's rule, Syrian Ba'athism became increasingly a vehicle for Alawite power. Alawites — about 12% of Syria's population, a heterodox Shia-adjacent sect that had historically been among the country's poorest communities — were systematically placed in command of every sensitive military unit, intelligence service, and internal security apparatus. The party remained formally secular and multi-confessional; the reality was confessional domination masked by Ba'athist ideology. Assad also expanded the welfare state, built infrastructure in the rural areas the Alawites came from, and maintained Syria's formal alliance with the Soviet Union while proving willing to make tactical deals with Israel-aligned Jordan and with the U.S. during the 1973 war.

This arrangement survived for 54 years. It ended in December 2024, when Bashar al-Assad — Hafez's son, who had inherited the state in 2000 — fled to Russia as rebel forces led by Hay'at Tahrir al-Sham (HTS) swept across Syria and took Damascus in a matter of days. The speed of the collapse was itself revealing: the Ba'ath Party's "popular" support had been a fiction maintained by the security services; when those services disintegrated, so did the regime.

1979–2003 — Iraq
Saddam's Ba'athism: From Party Rule to Clan Rule
▼

By the time Saddam Hussein became president in 1979, Ba'athism in Iraq had become something very specific: political cover for a network of Sunni Arab tribes from the town of Tikrit — connected by blood and marriage to Saddam's family — who controlled the security services, the Republican Guard, and the party's real levers of power. The ideology was still invoked: Aflaq's portraits lined government buildings; "Unity, Freedom, Socialism" appeared on every official document. But the actual logic of power was tribal, familial, and personal. Party membership was required for professional advancement; party membership was increasingly a means of surveillance of the population, not of ideological commitment.

Saddam used Ba'ath ideology selectively where it served him and discarded it where it did not. He invoked Arab nationalism to justify the 1980 invasion of Iran (protecting the Arab nation from Persian aggression) and to suppress Kurdish autonomy (no sub-national identities within the Arab nation). He invoked socialism to nationalise oil revenues, which he then used to fund a military and a security state. He abandoned the ideology's anti-imperialism when convenient — seeking Western support against Iran through the early 1980s — and reclaimed it when convenient again, positioning Iraq as the defender of the Arab nation against American imperialism during the Gulf War.

April 2003
De-Baathification: When Dismantling an Ideology Creates an Insurgency
▼

Following the U.S.-led invasion of Iraq in March 2003, Coalition Provisional Authority administrator Paul Bremer issued two orders that together dismantled the Iraqi state: Order 1 disbanded the Ba'ath Party and barred its members from public employment; Order 2 dissolved the Iraqi Army, the Republican Guard, and the intelligence services. The stated logic was to remove the instruments of Saddam's authoritarian rule. The consequence was to throw an estimated 400,000 trained military and security personnel — most of them Sunni Arab men with military skills and no income — out of work, out of their social position, and out of their professional identity overnight.

Many joined the insurgency that began within months. Senior Ba'athist officers provided organisational structure and military competence that the insurgency and, later, ISIS would not have had otherwise. The de-Baathification order is now regarded by most analysts as one of the most consequential policy errors in the post-invasion period — not because the Ba'ath Party should have been preserved, but because dismantling it without any transition plan or alternative employment for its members guaranteed that those men would fight. Ba'athism in Iraq ended as a state ideology in 2003; its human cadre continued to shape Iraqi violence for the next two decades.

Why It Matters Today

Ba'athism as a governing ideology is functionally over: the Iraqi party was dissolved in 2003, and the Syrian party's collapse in December 2024 ended the last Ba'athist state. But the consequences of Ba'athism's rise, rule, and fall are live political facts across the Middle East.

The December 2024 Collapse — What Ba'athism Left Behind in Syria

When HTS-led rebels took Damascus in December 2024 and Bashar al-Assad fled to Russia, they inherited a state that Ba'athism had systematically hollowed out over 61 years. Every independent institution had been subordinated to the party and the security services; every civil society organisation had been co-opted or crushed; every professional and economic network ran through regime patronage. The Ba'ath's destruction of alternative power centres — so effective at maintaining control — left nothing capable of governing the day after.

  • Syria's state institutions had been so thoroughly Ba'athified that distinguishing "the state" from "the party" was nearly impossible — a problem every post-Ba'ath government faces
  • The Alawite communities that staffed the security services now face potential reprisals — a sectarian reckoning that Ba'athism's confessional power structure made inevitable
  • Reconstruction requires institutions Syria doesn't have: functioning courts, an independent civil service, municipal governments with real authority — all destroyed by 60 years of Ba'ath centralisation
  • The speed of the regime's collapse (weeks, not months) confirmed that the Ba'ath "popular base" had been a security-maintained fiction for at least a decade

Former Ba'athists and the Genealogy of ISIS

The most direct institutional consequence of Ba'athism's Iraqi collapse was the contribution of former Ba'athist officers to the insurgency and eventually to ISIS. This is not a marginal point: ISIS's military effectiveness at its 2014 peak — its ability to plan operations, coordinate multiple columns, administer territory, and run intelligence services — was substantially the product of former Iraqi Army and Ba'athist security officers who had been stripped of their careers and social position in 2003.

  • Senior ISIS military figures including "Haji Bakr" (Samir Abd Muhammad al-Khlifawi) were former Iraqi Army colonels who provided strategic planning capacity the jihadist movement alone lacked
  • The administrative structure ISIS imposed on captured territory — including a functional intelligence service and a systematic extortion economy — bore the hallmarks of Ba'athist bureaucratic methods
  • De-Baathification created the condition; Sunni political marginalisation under Maliki (2006–2014) created the community that provided ISIS its support base
  • The question of how to reintegrate former regime members is now directly relevant to Syria's transition — the Iraqi precedent is a clear warning about what happens when exclusion is the answer

The Failure of Secular Arab Nationalism — and What Filled the Vacuum

Ba'athism was the most institutionally successful form of secular Arab nationalism. Its failure — not just to achieve Arab unity, but to provide the dignity, development, and meaningful political participation it promised — created the vacuum that Islamist movements rushed to fill from the 1970s onward. Understanding why Ba'athism failed is inseparable from understanding why the Muslim Brotherhood and its descendants became the dominant opposition politics across the Arab world.

  • Ba'athism promised pan-Arab unity; it produced two states that nearly went to war with each other and did go to war with their Arab neighbours (Iraq's invasion of Kuwait, 1990)
  • It promised socialism and development; it produced rentier oil economies distorted by war spending and enriching the party elite while rural and poor populations stagnated
  • It promised freedom from colonialism; it produced police states with surveillance and repression mechanisms more sophisticated than the colonial authorities they replaced
  • When the Arab Spring reached Syria in 2011, the opposition that mobilised most effectively was the Muslim Brotherhood — the same organisation Ba'athism had spent decades trying to destroy

The Secular-Islamist Divide Ba'athism Defined

Ba'athism's aggressive secularism — Aflaq's insistence that Arab identity transcended religion — placed it in permanent, violent conflict with Islamist movements that argued the opposite. The contest between these two visions of Arab political identity is the deepest structural divide in modern Arab politics, and it is not resolved. Ba'athism lost the argument: neither Syria nor Iraq is being rebuilt on Ba'athist lines. But the Islamist alternative that has filled parts of that space (HTS in Syria, various Islamist factions in Iraq) faces its own crisis of governance.

  • The 1982 Hama massacre — in which the Assad regime killed between 10,000 and 40,000 people to suppress a Muslim Brotherhood uprising — is the most extreme expression of the secular-Islamist conflict Ba'athism epitomised
  • Saddam's suppression of Shia Islamist networks (the Dawa Party, the Supreme Council for Islamic Revolution in Iraq) and Kurdish nationalist movements was continuous throughout his rule
  • Post-Ba'ath Iraq became an Iranian-influenced Shia Islamist state — the opposite of everything Ba'athism stood for — as the direct result of Ba'athism's collapse
  • Post-Ba'ath Syria's trajectory remains uncertain, but the leading rebel faction (HTS) is a former al-Qaeda affiliate that has moderated its public positions while governing Idlib — a very different political vision from Ba'athism's secular nationalism

Legacy & Debate

The central question about Ba'athism's historical legacy is whether it was ever a genuine ideology — a coherent set of beliefs that shaped what its adherents actually did — or whether it was, from the beginning, merely a vehicle for power. The evidence points toward a complex answer: Ba'athism was a genuine ideology in its founding decade that was systematically instrumentalised and hollowed out by the military officers who used it to take and maintain power. By the 1980s, the ideology had become largely window dressing for clan rule in Iraq and sectarian domination in Syria, but those ruling arrangements required the Ba'athist institutional structure (the party cells, the security services organised around ideological criteria) to function.

Adeed Dawisha
Political scientist, author of Arab Nationalism in the Twentieth Century

Dawisha offers the most sympathetic serious account of Ba'athism as a genuine intellectual tradition. He argues that Aflaq's synthesis of romantic nationalism and Arab socialist thought was coherent and responded to a real historical problem — the fragmentation of Arab political life after colonialism. The failure of Ba'athism, in his account, was not inherent in the ideology but in the specific conditions of military coup politics in Syria and Iraq, which repeatedly stripped power from civilian ideologues and handed it to officers with no ideological commitment.

Kanan Makiya
Iraqi dissident, author of Republic of Fear

Makiya's analysis of Iraqi Ba'athism is the most influential critical account. He argues that Ba'athism in Iraq was never genuinely ideological — it was always a cover for power, and the specific power of Saddam Hussein's Tikriti clan. The "ideology" served a functional purpose: it required party membership and ideological performance from every state employee, creating a society-wide surveillance network in which everyone was implicated in the regime and in which dissent was structurally impossible. Ba'athism, for Makiya, was a technology of totalitarian control, not a political vision.

Hanna Batatu
Historian, author of The Old Social Classes and the Revolutionary Movements of Iraq

Batatu's monumental study of Iraqi politics offers the most structurally sophisticated analysis. He locates Ba'athism's appeal in the specific social position of its recruits — rural, minority, upwardly mobile officers and students who found in the party a secular, modern, nationalist vehicle for advancement against urban Sunni and Shia religious establishments. For Batatu, Ba'athism's degeneration into clan rule was predictable from its social base: the party never had genuine mass roots, only a disciplined minority using state power to maintain itself.

The weight of historical evidence supports a verdict somewhere between these positions. Ba'athism began as a genuine intellectual project — Aflaq's writings show real engagement with questions of Arab identity, modernity, and political organisation. The party attracted ideologically committed members in its early decades who genuinely believed in Arab unity and who were willing to sacrifice for it. But the structure of Ba'athist organisation — vanguard party, cell structure, requirement for total ideological commitment — proved identical to the structure of a totalitarian party, and once military officers used that structure to take power, the ideology became a tool of repression rather than liberation.

The case for Ba'athism's genuine significance
  • Ba'athism provided the first serious political framework for Arab nationalism that could organise across religious lines — Aflaq's Christian Arab identity was not incidental but foundational
  • The welfare states built by Ba'ath governments in Syria and Iraq — free education, free healthcare, land reform, state employment — produced genuine improvements in rural living standards for their first two decades
  • Ba'athist secularism maintained space for women in professional and public life that religious alternatives explicitly opposed
  • The party's critique of colonial borders has been vindicated by subsequent history: the Sykes-Picot borders have indeed produced states that do not correspond to social, ethnic, or cultural realities
  • Aflaq's intellectual synthesis — however distorted in practice — represents the most serious attempt in the 20th century to articulate a specifically Arab political modernity distinct from both Western liberalism and Soviet communism
The case against Ba'athism's legacy
  • The ideology's promise of Arab unity produced the Iran-Iraq War (in which Ba'ath Iraq killed hundreds of thousands of Muslims, Arab and Persian), the invasion of Kuwait, and near-war between Syria and Iraq
  • Ba'athism's hostility to civil society, independent media, and political pluralism left Syria and Iraq without the institutions needed to govern themselves after the regimes fell
  • The systematic suppression of the Muslim Brotherhood in Syria produced the exact radicalisation it sought to prevent: survivors of Hama became the seed of jihadist movements that later threatened both states
  • The "socialism" that was supposed to develop the Arab economy produced oil-dependent rentier states whose non-oil sectors were stunted by decades of state monopoly
  • Ba'athism's secularism was never genuine pluralism — it was the imposition of a specifically secular vision by force, which is why it collapsed the moment the security services disintegrated

The deepest problem with Ba'athism as an ideology was its conception of politics: the idea that a vanguard party could represent the Arab nation's "eternal mission" without democratic accountability, and that this mission justified whatever was necessary to achieve and maintain power. This logic — which Aflaq borrowed from both Leninism and European fascism without acknowledging either — meant that the ideology contained no internal correction mechanism. When Ba'athist governments failed (economically, militarily, politically), there was no legitimate way to say so and no legitimate way to change course. The ideology required always claiming success while eliminating anyone who said otherwise.

"The Arab people is summoned today to combat on two fronts: against the remnants of the old order and against the forces of reaction in the world. But the combatant who fights must be inspired, not just organised."
Michel Aflaq, 1944 — written 60 years before the ideology's institutional collapse
