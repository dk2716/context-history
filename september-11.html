United States · September 11, 2001

September 11, 2001

The morning nineteen al-Qaeda operatives hijacked four commercial aircraft and killed 2,977 people — an attack that ended one era of American foreign policy and launched another whose full costs are still being counted.

Background: Al-Qaeda Before the Attacks

Osama bin Laden founded al-Qaeda — "the Base" in Arabic — in the late 1980s from the networks of Arab volunteers who had fought the Soviet occupation of Afghanistan. The organisation's ideology combined Salafi-jihadist theology with a geopolitical grievance: the presence of U.S. military bases in Saudi Arabia, which bin Laden had framed, since 1996, as a sacred duty to expel. His 1996 fatwa declared war on Americans. His 1998 fatwa — co-signed with Egyptian Islamic Jihad's Ayman al-Zawahiri — declared that killing Americans and their allies was "an individual duty for every Muslim."

These declarations were not made in secret. The U.S. intelligence community was aware of them. What it struggled with was translating a diffuse threat — an organisation with cells in dozens of countries, recruiting through mosques, funded through Saudi charitable networks, operating training camps in Taliban-controlled Afghanistan — into specific actionable intelligence about specific plots. The Clinton administration had come close to killing bin Laden with cruise missiles in 1998 after the East Africa embassy bombings, which killed 224 people, and again in 1999. Neither strike succeeded.

The al-Qaeda cell that executed the September 11 attacks was planned from 1999 onward. Mohamed Atta, an Egyptian citizen who had studied in Hamburg and received flight training in Florida, was the operational lead. The 19 hijackers entered the United States legally, trained at American flight schools, and lived openly in American cities for months. Fifteen of the nineteen were Saudi citizens. Two were from the UAE, one from Egypt, one from Lebanon. None were Iraqi or Afghan. The 9/11 Commission found that the plot cost roughly $400,000 to $500,000 to execute.

The Bush administration, which had taken office in January 2001, was briefed extensively on the al-Qaeda threat in its first months. CIA Director George Tenet later said his "hair was on fire" with concern about an imminent attack. National Security Advisor Condoleezza Rice was briefed. On August 6, 2001 — thirty-six days before the attacks — President Bush received a Presidential Daily Brief titled "Bin Laden Determined to Strike in US." He did not convene a principals meeting in response.

Key Figures

Osama bin Laden
Al-Qaeda founder and operational planner
A Saudi millionaire radicalised during the Soviet-Afghan War, bin Laden conceived and financed the September 11 attacks from Afghanistan. He evaded capture until May 2, 2011, when U.S. Navy SEALs killed him at a compound in Abbottabad, Pakistan — a hundred meters from the Pakistani Military Academy. He had been hiding there for years, apparently unknown to Pakistani intelligence, or concealed by elements of it.
Mohamed Atta
Lead hijacker, AA Flight 11
An Egyptian architecture student who became radicalised in Hamburg in the mid-1990s, Atta was the operational commander of the cell. He flew American Airlines Flight 11 into the North Tower. The 9/11 Commission portrait of him — methodical, humorless, deeply ideological — is the standard account of how a middle-class professional becomes a mass murderer through ideological immersion.
George W. Bush
President of the United States
Bush was reading to schoolchildren at Emma E. Booker Elementary in Sarasota, Florida, when the attacks occurred. His initial response was widely criticised as slow and directionless; his September 14 speech at Ground Zero — "I can hear you! The rest of the world hears you! And the people who knocked these buildings down will hear all of us soon!" — was a defining presidential moment. The decisions he made in the months that followed — Afghanistan, the PATRIOT Act, the eventual pivot to Iraq — defined his presidency.
Dick Cheney
Vice President
Cheney was evacuated to the Presidential Emergency Operations Center beneath the White House on the morning of September 11 and effectively managed the executive response while Bush remained airborne. He was the dominant voice within the administration for the most expansive possible interpretation of the emergency — enhanced interrogation techniques, mass warrantless surveillance, and the eventual decision that Iraq was the priority theatre of the War on Terror.
George Tenet
CIA Director
Tenet later claimed he had been sounding alarms about al-Qaeda throughout 2001 — the metaphor he used was that "the system was blinking red." He also presided over the CIA's post-9/11 enhanced interrogation programme, including waterboarding, which was later found by the Senate Intelligence Committee to have produced little actionable intelligence. His memoir, At the Center of the Storm, is a sustained exercise in partial accountability.
The passengers of Flight 93
Civilian resisters
After learning by phone that hijacked aircraft had been used as weapons, passengers including Todd Beamer, Mark Bingham, Tom Burnett, and Jeremy Glick voted to storm the cockpit. The aircraft crashed into a field near Shanksville, Pennsylvania, at 10:03 a.m. The intended target — most likely the U.S. Capitol — was spared. The final cockpit voice recording captures the passengers' assault on the door and the hijackers' decision to crash the plane before it was retaken.

The Day Itself

8:14 – 8:46 A.M.
American Airlines Flight 11 — The First Strike
▼

American Airlines Flight 11 departed Boston Logan at 7:59 a.m. bound for Los Angeles with 92 people aboard, including five hijackers. At 8:14 a.m., the hijackers moved. Mohamed Atta and his team used box cutters — allowed under pre-9/11 security rules — to subdue the crew. At 8:19 a.m., flight attendant Betty Ong called the airline's reservation center and reported the hijacking in a remarkably calm 25-minute call that became the first real-time account of the attacks. At 8:46 a.m., Atta flew the aircraft into the North Tower of the World Trade Center at roughly 440 mph. The impact between floors 93 and 99 killed everyone aboard and hundreds inside the building instantly.

Initial reports described a small plane or accident. The scale of the damage — a massive fireball, floors destroyed, thousands of gallons of jet fuel burning — quickly made clear this was not an accident. But the U.S. air defence system, NORAD, had not been notified of the hijacking in time to respond. The FAA and NORAD protocols of the time were designed for threats originating outside U.S. airspace, not for domestic aircraft turned into weapons.

9:03 A.M.
United Airlines Flight 175 — The World Watches
▼

By the time United Airlines Flight 175 struck the South Tower at 9:03 a.m., television cameras from across New York were trained on the burning North Tower. The second impact — broadcast live worldwide — removed all remaining ambiguity: the United States was under attack. The aircraft hit between floors 77 and 85. Both towers now had structural damage that their designers — who had accounted for accidental aircraft strikes — had not anticipated at this fuel load and speed.

The South Tower, which was hit at a lower floor and at an angle that distributed load unevenly, would collapse first — at 9:59 a.m., 56 minutes after impact. The North Tower followed at 10:28 a.m. Both collapses were caused by the combination of impact damage to load-bearing columns and the sustained fire from burning jet fuel weakening steel at temperatures the fireproofing could not contain. The design worked exactly as intended up to a point no designer had designed for.

9:37 A.M.
American Airlines Flight 77 — The Pentagon
▼

American Airlines Flight 77, en route from Washington Dulles to Los Angeles, was hijacked and turned around over Ohio. At 9:37 a.m., Hani Hanjour flew the aircraft into the west facade of the Pentagon at 530 mph, killing 184 people — 64 on the aircraft and 125 in the building. The impact and fire destroyed a newly renovated section of the Pentagon that had been reinforced with blast-resistant windows and kevlar-lined walls. The reinforcements almost certainly saved lives. Five hijackers flew a 757 into the headquarters of the world's most powerful military without being intercepted.

Vice President Cheney, watching from the Presidential Emergency Operations Center, was informed of the Pentagon strike shortly after it occurred. He had already authorised fighters to shoot down unresponsive aircraft — an order whose legal basis was disputed, since it had not come directly from the President. The question of who authorised what and when on the morning of September 11 remains a subject of historical disagreement.

9/11 Commission Report (2004)Woodward (2002)
10:03 A.M.
United Airlines Flight 93 — The Passengers Fight Back
▼

United Airlines Flight 93 departed Newark 41 minutes late. The delay proved consequential: by the time the four hijackers seized the aircraft over Ohio, passengers and crew were able to make phone calls learning that hijacked planes had already been used as missiles. A vote was taken. "Are you guys ready?" Todd Beamer asked. "Let's roll." The passengers used a food cart as a battering ram against the cockpit door. The cockpit voice recording — reviewed by families and the 9/11 Commission — captured the hijackers deciding to crash the plane at 10:03 a.m. rather than risk it being retaken, 20 minutes' flying time from Washington.

The intended target of Flight 93 has never been definitively confirmed. The 9/11 Commission concluded it was most likely the U.S. Capitol, based on statements by Khalid Sheikh Mohammed and the flight path. The White House was also a possibility. The passengers' revolt — spontaneous, unglamourised, effective — prevented whatever was planned. It is one of the few moments in the story of September 11 where individual decision-making made a direct, documentable difference.

SEPT–OCT 2001
The Aftermath — From Ground Zero to Afghanistan
▼

The immediate aftermath involved extraordinary scenes of rescue and recovery in Lower Manhattan, where 343 firefighters and 71 police officers had died in the collapses. The smell of Ground Zero — burning metal, plastic, and something worse — persisted for months. Thousands of first responders developed respiratory illnesses; hundreds have since died from cancers linked to their exposure. The James Zadroga 9/11 Health and Compensation Act, providing healthcare for first responders, was not passed until 2010 — and required Jon Stewart's public shaming of Congress to extend it permanently in 2019.

Within days, Congress passed the Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF), which authorised the President to use "all necessary and appropriate force" against those responsible for the attacks. The resolution passed 420 to 1 in the House — the lone dissenter was Barbara Lee of California, who warned that "military action will not prevent further acts of international terrorism against the United States." It remains on the books today, cited as legal authority for military operations in dozens of countries its 2001 authors never contemplated.

On October 7, 2001, the United States and the United Kingdom launched Operation Enduring Freedom, striking Taliban and al-Qaeda targets in Afghanistan. The Taliban government fell within months. Osama bin Laden escaped into Pakistan at the battle of Tora Bora in December 2001, when a decision was made to rely on Afghan militias rather than U.S. ground forces to seal the mountain passes. He would not be found for another decade.

Turning Points

Turning Point 1
The August 6 PDB — "Bin Laden Determined to Strike in US"

Thirty-six days before the attacks, President Bush received a Presidential Daily Brief specifically warning that al-Qaeda intended to strike inside the United States, potentially using hijackings. Bush asked no follow-up questions and convened no principals meeting. National Security Advisor Condoleezza Rice later described the PDB as "historical" rather than operational — a characterisation disputed by the CIA briefer who delivered it. Whether this constitutes a culpable failure of attention or a reasonable prioritisation in a sea of threat warnings remains the central contested question about U.S. government responsibility for September 11.

Turning Point 2
Tora Bora — The Decision Not to Use U.S. Ground Forces

In December 2001, with bin Laden cornered in the Tora Bora mountain complex on the Afghan-Pakistan border, the decision was made to rely on Afghan militias — some of whose commanders were negotiating with al-Qaeda — rather than commit U.S. Army Rangers or Special Forces to seal the passes. Bin Laden escaped into Pakistan. General Tommy Franks and Defense Secretary Rumsfeld later disputed that bin Laden was definitively at Tora Bora; subsequent Senate investigations found that he was. The decision not to commit U.S. forces — whether driven by ideology about using local proxies, concern about repeating the Soviet experience, or genuine uncertainty — allowed the architect of September 11 to survive for another decade.

Turning Point 3
The "Wall" Between CIA and FBI

In August 2001, a CIA analyst identified two of the future hijackers — Nawaf al-Hazmi and Khalid al-Mihdhar — as al-Qaeda operatives who had entered the United States. The information was not shared with the FBI in time to locate them. A legal interpretation of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act, implemented in the 1990s, had erected a procedural "wall" limiting intelligence sharing between CIA foreign operations and FBI domestic investigations. Both men died in the Pentagon attack six weeks later. The 9/11 Commission found this "wall" was the single most important structural failure that allowed the plot to proceed undetected.

Turning Point 4
The Iraq Decision — Connecting Unconnected Dots

Within hours of the September 11 attacks, Deputy Defense Secretary Paul Wolfowitz raised the possibility of striking Iraq — a country with no involvement in the attacks. Within days, Defense Secretary Rumsfeld was asking his staff for "best info fast. Judge whether good enough hit S.H. [Saddam Hussein] at same time." The decision to invade Iraq in 2003 — justified partly by false claims of an Iraq–al-Qaeda connection — redirected the War on Terror toward a country that bore no responsibility for September 11, destabilised the region, and created conditions that gave rise to ISIS. It is one of the most consequential non sequiturs in the history of American foreign policy.

"We're at war. Someone has declared war on us, and we will respond accordingly."
— President George W. Bush, diary entry, September 11, 2001

Why It Matters Today

The September 11 attacks set in motion changes to American law, foreign policy, and civil liberties whose effects are still unfolding more than two decades later. The question is no longer what happened on September 11 — it is what the response revealed, and cost.

The AUMF Still Governs Military Operations

The 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force — passed three days after the attacks in a climate of shock and unanimity — has been invoked to justify military operations in at least 19 countries. It was used to justify the 2011 killing of bin Laden in Pakistan, drone strikes in Yemen and Somalia, and operations against groups that did not exist in 2001. Congress has never updated or repealed it.

  • Cited in legal filings for Guantánamo detentions still ongoing
  • Used to authorise U.S. operations against ISIS — an organisation that didn't exist in 2001
  • Multiple reform attempts have stalled in Congress since 2013

Mass Surveillance Became Permanent Infrastructure

The NSA's post-9/11 surveillance programmes — revealed by Edward Snowden in 2013 — showed that the response to September 11 included building a mass surveillance architecture that collected data on hundreds of millions of people, including American citizens, with minimal judicial oversight. The programmes were authorised secretly under FISA Court orders and the PATRIOT Act.

  • NSA bulk phone metadata collection ran from 2001 to 2015
  • PRISM programme collected internet data from nine major U.S. tech companies
  • The Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board found in 2014 that bulk phone collection had produced no unique counterterrorism leads

Guantánamo Bay Remains Open

President Bush opened the Guantánamo Bay detention facility in January 2002 to hold "enemy combatants" outside the reach of U.S. courts. At its peak it held 780 detainees. As of 2024, 30 remain — some never charged with any crime, held for more than two decades. Obama promised to close it on his first day as president and never did. The facility has become a symbol of the legal architecture built in the shadow of September 11.

  • 9 detainees have died at Guantánamo, including 3 ruled suicides in 2006
  • The 9/11 mastermind Khalid Sheikh Mohammed has been awaiting trial there since 2006
  • Annual cost: approximately $13 million per detainee

The Wars' Human Cost

The Watson Institute at Brown University estimates that the post-9/11 wars — in Afghanistan, Iraq, Pakistan, Syria, and elsewhere — have killed between 897,000 and 929,000 people directly, including 387,000 civilians, and displaced 38 million. The financial cost exceeds $8 trillion when projected future veterans' care is included. The September 11 attacks killed 2,977 people. The proportionality of the response is a live moral and strategic question.

  • 7,052 U.S. military deaths in Afghanistan and Iraq
  • 30,177 active-duty U.S. military suicides since 9/11 — four times the combat death toll
  • Afghanistan returned to Taliban control in August 2021

Legacy & Debate

The historiography of September 11 has produced genuine scholarly disagreement on several fronts: whether the attacks were preventable, whether the intelligence community failed or was simply overwhelmed, whether the civil liberties trade-offs of the response were necessary or cynical, and whether the Iraq War — the most consequential downstream decision — was a sincere mistake or a premeditated misdirection. These debates have not been resolved; they have deepened.

The 9/11 Commission
National Commission on Terrorist Attacks · Final Report (2004)
The most authoritative account, based on 1,200 interviews and 2.5 million pages of documents. The Commission found systemic failures of imagination, policy, capabilities, and management — but distributed blame widely rather than assigning individual culpability. It concluded the attacks were not the result of any single failure but of a system designed for a different world. The report is rigorous and readable; its recommendations on intelligence reform were implemented unevenly.
Richard Clarke
National Coordinator for Counterterrorism · Against All Enemies (2004)
Clarke, the senior counterterrorism official in both the Clinton and Bush administrations, argues that the Bush team systematically downgraded the al-Qaeda threat in its first eight months — preoccupied with missile defence and Iraq — despite persistent warnings. He describes briefing Condoleezza Rice in January 2001 with what he characterises as a sense that she had never heard of al-Qaeda. His account is self-serving in parts but broadly corroborated by the Commission's findings on the August 6 PDB and the principals' meeting record.
Jane Mayer
The New Yorker · The Dark Side (2008)
Mayer's account of the post-9/11 response is the most detailed examination of how Cheney, Addington, Yoo, and the "war council" built the legal architecture for enhanced interrogation, warrantless surveillance, and indefinite detention within weeks of the attacks — and how that architecture was shielded from review by other parts of the executive branch. Her conclusion is that the response to September 11 damaged the United States institutionally in ways that outlasted the security gains, if any were made.

The Central Contested Questions

Was September 11 preventable? The honest answer is: possibly, but the conditions required were not in place. Preventing it would have required a different legal framework for intelligence sharing, a different set of priorities within the new administration, and either the successful assassination of bin Laden in the late 1990s or a successful disruption of the Hamburg cell before it trained in Afghanistan. Each of these was possible in retrospect; none was straightforward in prospect.

Did the response make the United States safer? The absence of a comparable domestic attack in the twenty-three years since is cited as evidence that it did. The creation of conditions that produced ISIS, the radicalisation of hundreds of thousands of people in Iraq and elsewhere, and the documented failure of programmes like the NSA bulk collection to produce unique counterterrorism leads are cited as evidence against. Both observations can be simultaneously true: some measures worked, some were counterproductive, and distinguishing between them has been made harder by classification and the absence of the counterfactual.

The case that the response was proportionate
  • No comparable domestic attack has occurred since 2001 — DHS/FBI
  • Al-Qaeda's central organisation was degraded significantly by 2011 — DNI assessments
  • The PATRIOT Act's roving wiretaps and delayed-notice provisions were tools already used in drug cases — DOJ
  • Afghanistan under the Taliban had been a direct state sponsor of the attack — 9/11 Commission
The case that the response was disproportionate
  • Iraq had no connection to September 11; its invasion killed hundreds of thousands — 9/11 Commission
  • NSA bulk collection produced no unique counterterrorism leads — PCLOB (2014)
  • Torture produced little actionable intelligence per Senate Intelligence Committee — SSCI Report (2014)
  • U.S. military suicides since 9/11 (30,177) are 4× combat deaths — VA statistics
