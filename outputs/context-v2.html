Gaddafi, the Arab Spring,
and Why Libya Still Burns

How one man's 42-year grip on a desert nation created a power vacuum the world is still trying to fill — and why its consequences are arriving on Europe's shores.

The one-paragraph version

Libya was ruled by a dictator named Muammar Gaddafi for 42 years. In 2011, inspired by uprisings across the Arab world, Libyans rebelled. NATO intervened with air strikes, and Gaddafi was overthrown and killed. But instead of democracy, Libya collapsed into chaos: rival governments, dozens of armed militias, no state, no police, no army. That collapse still matters — it's why hundreds of thousands of migrants cross Libya to reach Europe, why weapons looted from Gaddafi's arsenals are fuelling terrorism across West Africa, and why Russia and Turkey are now fighting a proxy war on Libyan soil.

Background: Gaddafi's Libya

To understand why Libya fell apart so catastrophically in 2011, you have to understand what Gaddafi spent 42 years building — or more precisely, what he spent 42 years deliberately not building. Most dictators, however brutal, construct some version of a state: ministries, courts, a professional army, a bureaucracy. Gaddafi did the opposite. He created what political scientists call a "stateless state" — a system of patronage, tribal loyalties, and personal rule so deliberately fragmented that no institution could ever challenge him.

When Gaddafi seized power in September 1969, Libya was a newly independent kingdom sitting on enormous oil reserves but with almost no educated class, no political tradition, and a population of barely two million people divided among dozens of tribes. He was 27 years old and charismatic, framing himself as a revolutionary in the mould of Egypt's Nasser — an Arab nationalist who would free Libya from Western imperialism and use its oil wealth for the people. For a generation of Libyans, especially rural families who had lived in poverty under the Italian colonial era and the brief monarchy that followed, Gaddafi was genuinely popular.

He nationalised oil production and used the revenues to build infrastructure, provide free healthcare, and make university education accessible. By some measures, Libya had the highest standard of living in Africa. But this welfare state came with a total absence of political freedom. Critics were tortured in Abu Salim prison or executed on live television. A 1984 assassination attempt at the Libyan embassy in London — during which a British policewoman, Yvonne Fletcher, was shot dead — showed the world how far Gaddafi's reach extended.

Gaddafi created not a state, but an anti-state — a system designed to prevent the emergence of any institution that could challenge his personal authority. — Dirk Vandewalle, A History of Modern Libya (2012)

On the international stage, Gaddafi's Libya became a sponsor of global terrorism. He funded the Irish Republican Army during The Troubles, provided weapons to Palestinian militant groups, and ordered the 1988 bombing of Pan Am Flight 103 over Lockerbie, Scotland, killing 270 people. International sanctions in the 1990s isolated Libya, crippling its economy. By the 2000s, facing the aftermath of the Iraq War and pressure from the West, Gaddafi renounced his weapons of mass destruction programme and paid compensation to the Lockerbie families. He was welcomed back into the international community — even visited by Tony Blair in 2004.

That rehabilitation was still fresh when protesters took to the streets in 2011. The West had a complicated recent history with Gaddafi: they'd just done business with him, and now they were about to bomb him.

Key figures

🎖️

Muammar Gaddafi

DICTATOR · IN POWER 1969–2011

A 27-year-old army officer who overthrew Libya's king in a 1969 coup. He built a cult of personality and a deliberately fragmented state designed to prevent any rival from emerging. He funded terrorism, including the Lockerbie bombing, then rehabilitated himself in the 2000s. His violent crackdown on 2011 protesters — threatening to hunt them "house by house" — triggered NATO's intervention.

⚖️

Mustafa Abdul Jalil

REBEL LEADER · CHAIR OF NTC 2011

Gaddafi's former Justice Minister who defected in February 2011, becoming the public face of the National Transitional Council. A measured, religiously conservative judge from the east, he struggled to hold together an extremely diverse coalition of rebels — secular liberals, Islamists, tribal leaders, and defecting military officers — who agreed on little except that Gaddafi had to go.

🌐

Khalifa Haftar

WARLORD · LIBYAN NATIONAL ARMY (LNA)

A former Gaddafi general who was captured in Chad in the 1980s, spent years in the US with reported CIA ties, and returned to Libya after 2011. By 2014 he had built the Libyan National Army in the east and launched "Operation Dignity" — a military campaign to seize the entire country. He is backed by Russia, the UAE, and Egypt. As of the mid-2020s, he controls most of eastern and southern Libya, and the standoff continues.

What happened, step by step

Sep 1969 Gaddafi seizes power in a bloodless coup ▶

At 2 a.m. on 1 September 1969, a group of around 70 young military officers called the "Free Officers Movement" seize key communications and military installations across Libya while King Idris is in Turkey receiving medical treatment. There is almost no resistance. By morning, Gaddafi — just 27 years old and holding the rank of captain — is announced as head of the new Revolutionary Command Council.

The coup is welcomed by many Libyans, particularly younger people frustrated with the monarchy's dependence on Western powers and the slow distribution of oil revenues. Gaddafi immediately nationalises the Libyan oil industry, expels the US Wheelus Air Base and British military installations, and announces a new political philosophy: Libyan Arab nationalism combined with a version of socialism. He publishes this in the Green Book — a short manifesto that becomes the ideological foundation of his rule.

The West watches nervously but does not intervene. Libya's oil is too important, and the coup presents itself as nationalist rather than communist. The Soviet Union is also cautious — Gaddafi is no Marxist.

1970s–2000s 42 years of oil wealth, terror, and repression ▶

Over four decades, Gaddafi builds one of the strangest political systems in modern history. He calls it the Jamahiriya — a word he invents meaning "state of the masses" — governed in theory by "People's Committees" at every level. In practice it is total personal rule, maintained through a parallel system of Revolutionary Committees that spy on and terrorise the official structures. There is no constitution, no independent judiciary, no political parties. The army is deliberately kept weak, fragmented into competing units, so that no general can ever stage the coup Gaddafi himself staged.

Internationally, Libya becomes a pariah state. Gaddafi funds the IRA in Northern Ireland, Palestinian militant groups, and various African liberation movements — some legitimate, some not. In 1986, US President Reagan launches air strikes on Tripoli and Benghazi in response to Libyan involvement in the Berlin discotheque bombing that killed two US soldiers. In 1988, Libyan agents blow up Pan Am Flight 103 over Lockerbie, Scotland, killing all 270 people aboard. International sanctions follow, crippling Libya's economy through the 1990s.

By the 2000s, Gaddafi makes a dramatic pivot. Facing the example of Saddam Hussein's overthrow in 2003, he renounces his WMD programme and opens negotiations with the West. He pays $2.7 billion in compensation to the Lockerbie victims' families. Tony Blair visits Gaddafi in his Bedouin tent in 2004. BP signs major oil contracts. European leaders queue up to shake his hand. The rehabilitation is complete — until the moment it isn't.

📚 Vandewalle (2012) 📚 St John (2011)
Jan–Feb 2011 The Arab Spring arrives — and Gaddafi reaches for his gun ▶

In December 2010, a Tunisian street vendor named Mohamed Bouazizi sets himself on fire to protest police harassment. The act triggers mass protests that topple Tunisia's long-serving dictator Ben Ali within weeks. The revolution spreads: Egypt's Hosni Mubarak falls in February 2011. Yemen and Bahrain erupt. Across the Arab world, a generation that grew up under autocracy looks at Tunisia and Egypt and asks: why not us?

In Libya, protests begin in the eastern city of Benghazi on 15 February 2011 — triggered partly by the arrest of a human rights lawyer representing families of the Abu Salim prison massacre, in which an estimated 1,270 prisoners were killed in 1996. Benghazi has always resented Tripoli; eastern Libya felt economically marginalised and politically excluded under Gaddafi's rule. The protests are initially small but spread rapidly as security forces open fire on demonstrators.

Gaddafi's response removes any ambiguity about his intentions. In a rambling live television address on 22 February, flanked by an umbrella in what appears to be a bombed building, he calls the protesters "rats" and "cockroaches" and threatens to hunt them "house by house, alley by alley." His son Saif al-Islam appears separately on TV warning of "rivers of blood." Libyan ambassadors begin resigning in protest. Parts of the military defect. The rebellion explodes across the east.

📚 Pack (2013)
Mar 2011 The UN authorises a no-fly zone — NATO goes further ▶

By early March, Gaddafi's forces have retaken most of the towns the rebels initially captured, and his army is advancing on Benghazi with tanks and artillery. The rebel fighters — mostly untrained civilians with light weapons — are being massacred. A humanitarian catastrophe appears imminent in a city of over 600,000 people.

On 17 March 2011, the UN Security Council passes Resolution 1973, authorising "all necessary measures" to protect civilians and establishing a no-fly zone over Libya. Russia and China, who might have vetoed the resolution, abstain. NATO assumes command of the operation on 31 March. The alliance interprets the mandate broadly: rather than simply preventing air strikes, NATO aircraft begin systematically attacking Gaddafi's ground forces, command and control centres, and military equipment wherever they are found. Critics argue this transforms "protecting civilians" into actively helping the rebels win.

The air campaign is decisive. Gaddafi's conventional military — armed with Soviet-era tanks and artillery but with no air defence against modern Western jets — crumbles. Rebel militias, each loyal to different cities and commanders (Misrata, Zintan, Benghazi), sweep westward. The war that seemed lost in February is suddenly winnable.

📚 Chivvis (2014) 📚 Pack (2013)
Oct 2011 Gaddafi found in a drainpipe. Beaten. Killed. ▶

After months of fighting, Tripoli falls to rebel forces in August 2011. Gaddafi disappears. NATO jets, using signals intelligence, track his convoy as it tries to flee his hometown of Sirte on 20 October 2011. Air strikes disable the convoy. Gaddafi is found hiding in a drainage culvert by rebel fighters. He is beaten, sodomised with a bayonet, shot, and killed. The entire ordeal is filmed on mobile phones and broadcast worldwide.

Western leaders react with careful statements about the "end of an era." President Obama calls it "a momentous day in the long journey to democracy." But the manner of Gaddafi's death — a mob killing of a captured man — should have been a signal about what kind of post-war order was coming. There were no negotiations, no trial, no process. The war ended not with a political agreement but with a killing.

With Gaddafi gone, there was no plan. NATO had focused entirely on the military operation and almost nothing on post-conflict stabilisation. There was no functioning government, no agreement on disarming the dozens of militias that had fought the war, and no Libyan institution capable of filling the void. The vacuum was immediate and total.

📚 Chivvis (2014) 📚 Pack (2013)
2012–2014 False dawn: elections, optimism, and collapse ▶

Libya holds elections in July 2012 — the first free elections in the country's history. Turnout is high and the mood is cautiously optimistic. Secular liberals do better than Islamists, in contrast to Egypt and Tunisia. The Western press runs optimistic headlines. But behind the scenes, the transitional government has almost no ability to govern. It cannot disarm the militias — it barely controls its own capital.

The militias that won the war have no intention of dissolving. Armed groups from Misrata, Zintan, and Benghazi occupy government ministries, oil facilities, and airports, extracting salaries from a government too weak to refuse. In September 2012, the US Ambassador to Libya, Chris Stevens, is killed in Benghazi in an attack by Islamist militants — an early sign of the jihadist presence that is growing in the security vacuum.

By 2014, Libya has fractured into two competing governments: one based in Tripoli (eventually backed by Turkey and Qatar) and one in the eastern city of Tobruk (backed by Egypt, the UAE, and later Russia). A civil war within the post-revolution chaos begins.

📚 Pack (2013)
2015–now ISIS, slave markets, Wagner, and a war that won't end ▶

Into the vacuum steps ISIS. In 2015, the organisation seizes Gaddafi's hometown of Sirte and establishes a Libyan "province" — carrying out public beheadings and operating training camps. A US-backed Libyan operation, supported by American air strikes, eventually recaptures Sirte in December 2016. But ISIS fighters simply scatter into the desert and across the Sahara to neighbouring Mali, Niger, and Chad, where they join existing jihadist networks armed with weapons looted from Gaddafi's abandoned arsenals.

In 2017, CNN broadcasts footage of what appear to be migrant slave auctions in Libya — men being sold for $400 in a country with no government to stop it. The images shock the world. By this point Libya has become the primary transit route for migrants from sub-Saharan Africa trying to reach Europe. Smuggling networks — often run by the same militias that fought the 2011 war — charge thousands of dollars per person for dangerous sea crossings in inflatable boats.

Russia's Wagner Group arrives in Libya around 2019, deploying mercenaries and laying land mines in support of Haftar's eastern forces. Turkish drones, deployed in support of the Tripoli government, prove decisive in halting Haftar's 2019–2020 assault on the capital. The country is now effectively partitioned, with foreign powers entrenched on both sides, and no peace agreement on the horizon.

Was NATO's intervention the right call?

This is one of the most contested foreign policy questions of the 2010s. Historians, policymakers, and strategists are still arguing about it. The debate matters not just for Libya, but because it set the terms for how the West thinks about military intervention, "Responsibility to Protect" (R2P), and regime change.

✓ The case for intervention

Gaddafi's forces were days from entering Benghazi when NATO acted. The massacre of a city of 600,000 people appeared imminent. Intervention saved tens of thousands of lives. The UN Security Council authorised the action. To stand aside while a government slaughters its own people — after the failures of Rwanda and Srebrenica — would have been a moral catastrophe. The subsequent chaos was Libya's failure to build institutions, not NATO's failure to intervene.

✗ The case against

The UN mandate was for civilian protection, not regime change — but NATO's air campaign went far beyond that, systematically destroying Gaddafi's military to ensure rebel victory. There was no post-war plan, no commitment to stabilisation, and no effort to negotiate a ceasefire. Obama later called failing to plan for the day after the intervention "the worst mistake" of his presidency. The chaos that followed killed more people than the intervention saved, and created crises — migration, terrorism, proxy war — that destabilised half a continent.

📖 Key concept: Responsibility to Protect (R2P)

R2P is an international norm adopted at the UN in 2005 which says that when a government is unable or unwilling to protect its own civilians from mass atrocities, the international community has a responsibility to intervene. Libya in 2011 was the first case where the UN Security Council invoked R2P to authorise military force.

The Libyan aftermath severely damaged R2P's credibility. Russia and China, who abstained on Resolution 1973, argued that NATO had used R2P as cover for regime change — and used this as a reason to block similar resolutions on Syria in 2012, where the humanitarian situation was arguably far worse.

The aftermath: why Libya couldn't rebuild

To understand why Libya failed to rebuild after 2011, you have to understand what Gaddafi's 42 years had actually destroyed. He hadn't merely suppressed political opposition — he had deliberately prevented the formation of any institution capable of governing. There were no independent courts, no professional civil service, no functioning police force. The universities were underinvested; professional expertise was distrusted. Even the army was kept deliberately fragmented so it could never challenge him.

When Gaddafi fell, there was nothing to replace him with. The NTC — a coalition of lawyers, academics, defected officials, and tribal leaders — had legitimacy but no power. The power lay with the militias: armed groups loyal to specific cities, tribes, or commanders, who had actually won the war. These groups had no incentive to disarm and no institution with the authority to make them. The NTC tried to co-opt them by putting them on the government payroll — which meant militias were simultaneously drawing state salaries and operating as independent armed factions answerable to nobody.

Meanwhile, Gaddafi's enormous weapons stockpiles — tanks, artillery, anti-aircraft missiles, and small arms accumulated over four decades of oil-funded arms purchases — were simply looted. Weapons spread across the Sahara into Mali, Niger, and Chad within months, arming Tuareg rebels and jihadist groups who would go on to destabilise the entire Sahel region. This is why the Libyan war is not just a Libyan story — its consequences are playing out across 5,000 kilometres of West African desert.

We did not do enough to fill the void after Gaddafi fell. That's the lesson of Libya. — President Barack Obama, The Atlantic interview, 2016

Why this matters today

🚢

Europe's migration and asylum crisis

Ongoing

Libya sits directly across the Mediterranean from Italy and Malta. Under Gaddafi, it acted as a de facto border guard for Europe — an ugly arrangement in which the EU paid Libya to detain and deport migrants crossing from sub-Saharan Africa. When Gaddafi fell and the state collapsed, that arrangement disappeared overnight.

With no coast guard, no border police, and active smuggling networks run by the same militias that control the country, Libya became the world's busiest route for irregular migration. Between 2014 and 2017, over 600,000 people crossed the Mediterranean from Libya to Italy. More than 20,000 have drowned. The Italian coast guard rescues boats on a near-daily basis.

  • The 2015–2016 migration surge — when over a million people entered Europe via the Mediterranean and the Balkans — directly fuelled the rise of far-right and anti-immigration parties across Germany, Austria, Italy, Sweden, and France
  • Italy's political landscape has been transformed; parties like Giorgia Meloni's Brothers of Italy rose largely on an anti-migration platform rooted in frustration with Mediterranean crossings
  • The EU's migration deals with Tunisia, Turkey, and Libya — paying authoritarian governments to stop migrants — are a direct political consequence of 2011
🪖

Terrorism across the Sahel

Expanding

Gaddafi spent decades stockpiling weapons — partly because he had the oil money, partly because he was paranoid. His arsenals included hundreds of thousands of small arms, shoulder-fired anti-aircraft missiles (MANPADS), and military vehicles. When the state collapsed in 2011, these were looted and dispersed across the Sahara in weeks. A UN Panel of Experts report found weapons from Libyan stockpiles in at least 14 countries within two years of the war's end.

These weapons directly armed and enabled the 2012 Tuareg rebellion in northern Mali, which destabilised the entire country, triggered a military coup in Bamako, and opened the door for jihadist groups — Al-Qaeda in the Islamic Maghreb (AQIM) and its offshoots — to seize the northern half of the country. French forces intervened in 2013 to prevent Mali from becoming a jihadist state, and have been fighting there, on and off, ever since.

  • ISIS established a Libyan branch in 2014 and held the city of Sirte until 2016; fighters dispersed into the broader Sahel after its defeat
  • Wagner Group (now Africa Corps) used Libya as a springboard for operations in Mali, Central African Republic, Sudan, and Niger
  • Military coups in Mali (2021), Guinea (2021), Burkina Faso (2022), Niger (2023), and Gabon (2023) are all connected — directly or indirectly — to the security vacuum created by the Libyan collapse
🛢️

Global oil markets and energy security

Economic

Libya holds Africa's largest proven oil reserves — approximately 48 billion barrels — and before 2011 was producing around 1.6 million barrels per day. The civil war has repeatedly reduced production to a fraction of that: at its lowest point in 2011, Libya produced just 22,000 barrels per day. Competing factions routinely shut down oil fields and export terminals as a weapon of economic war or a bargaining chip in political negotiations.

Libya is a member of OPEC, and its production instability is a persistent variable in global oil price calculations. When Haftar's forces shut the "oil crescent" terminals in 2018, it contributed to a global price spike. Energy traders watch Libyan politics closely not because Libya is the world's largest producer, but because its instability creates unpredictable supply disruptions at critical moments.

  • European nations — particularly Italy, via ENI — are heavily dependent on Libyan oil and gas, giving them a strategic interest in Libyan stability
  • Control over oil revenues is one of the primary drivers of ongoing factional conflict; the UN-recognised government controls the central bank and National Oil Corporation, giving it a financial weapon against rivals
♟️

A great power proxy war

Geopolitical

Libya has become one of the clearest examples of 21st-century proxy warfare — a conflict in which outside powers fund, arm, and deploy forces on behalf of local factions, pursuing their own strategic interests without directly fighting each other. The alignment is striking: Russia, the UAE, and Egypt support Haftar's eastern forces; Turkey and Qatar support the Tripoli-based government. France has reportedly supported Haftar while officially backing the UN peace process.

Russia's interest is straightforward: a Haftar-controlled Libya would give Moscow a foothold on NATO's southern flank, potential basing rights on the Mediterranean, and leverage over European energy markets. Turkey's interest is partly economic (construction contracts) and partly strategic — it has used its Libyan presence to assert claims in the Eastern Mediterranean's disputed maritime zones.

  • Wagner Group (now "Africa Corps") operated in Libya before expanding to Mali, CAR, and Sudan — Libya was the template for the model
  • Turkish drone warfare in Libya was a proving ground; the same Bayraktar TB2 drones were later deployed in Syria, Azerbaijan, and Ukraine
  • The conflict demonstrates how the UN Security Council's permanent members can simultaneously back a peace process and arm opposing sides
