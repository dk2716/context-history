Origins: A Doctor Who Chose Politics

Salvador Allende was born in 1908 into a middle-class family in Valparaíso, the same port city that would produce Pinochet seven years later. The two men could not have been more different in temperament or politics. Allende's father was a lawyer and his family had a tradition of freethinking and mild radicalism — his grandfather had been a prominent Freemason. He studied medicine at the University of Chile in Santiago, qualifying as a doctor in 1933, and from the beginning combined medicine with politics in a way that made each inform the other. He believed, as a matter of medical conviction, that poverty and inequality were the primary causes of illness in Chile's population, and that the only lasting cure was political.

He joined the newly formed Chilean Socialist Party in 1933 — distinct from, and often rivalrous with, the Communist Party — and was elected to the Chamber of Deputies in 1937 at the age of 28. He served as Minister of Health under President Pedro Aguirre Cerda from 1939 to 1942, where he implemented Chile's first significant public health legislation. He ran for president four times — in 1952, 1958, 1964, and finally 1970. He lost the first three, each time narrowing the margin. His persistence was not stubbornness but a particular kind of democratic faith: that Chileans would eventually choose what he believed was right.

The Socialist Vision: "The Chilean Road to Socialism"

Allende's political project was explicitly revolutionary in its goals but explicitly democratic in its methods. He called it "la vía chilena al socialismo" — the Chilean road to socialism — and it was built on a simple, radical premise: that a socialist transformation of society could be achieved through elections and legislation rather than armed revolution. This put him at odds with orthodox Marxism (which held that the ruling class would never permit its own dispossession through law) and with the Cuban model (where Castro had taken power by force). Allende was making an argument about whether democracy and socialism were compatible — an argument that was never allowed to reach its conclusion.

Nationalisation of Copper

Chile's copper mines — producing the country's primary export and controlled largely by U.S. corporations Anaconda and Kennecott — were nationalised in July 1971 with unanimous congressional approval, including opposition votes. Allende called copper "the salary of Chile." The nationalisation was legally structured to compensate the companies, though disputes over the valuation led to the compensation being reduced to near zero. This single act set off the U.S. economic campaign against his government.

Land Reform

The Agrarian Reform Law accelerated land redistribution, breaking up large estates (latifundios) and transferring land to peasant cooperatives. By 1972, roughly 40% of Chile's agricultural land had been redistributed. The reform was legally based on legislation passed by his predecessor — Allende was accelerating an existing process rather than inventing a new one, which made it legally difficult for opponents to challenge.

Social Programmes

In his first year, Allende's government provided half a litre of free milk daily to every Chilean child, expanded healthcare and school meals, froze rents, and raised wages while freezing prices. GDP grew 8.9% in 1971, real wages rose 22%, and unemployment fell from 8.3% to 3.8%. These numbers are not in dispute — the question is whether they were sustainable, and by 1972 inflation was accelerating severely as the government printed money to fund them.

Democratic Commitment

Allende refused offers of armed support from Cuba and the radical left within his own coalition, insisting that his government would operate within Chile's constitutional framework. When an opposition-controlled Congress tried to remove him in August 1973, he challenged them in a plebiscite rather than dissolving the legislature. He had a scheduled plebiscite for September 11, 1973 — the day of the coup — which he believed he would win. We never found out.

"I am not the president of all Chileans. I am the president of those who voted for me."
— Allende, speaking with characteristic bluntness about the class character of his government — a quote his opponents used against him and his supporters regarded as refreshingly honest

The 1,007 Days: What His Government Actually Did

The Allende government's record is complicated by the fact that it operated under sustained economic warfare from the United States, active destabilisation from Chilean business interests, and growing polarisation within Chilean society — all simultaneously. Evaluating its achievements and failures requires separating what his government chose to do from what it was prevented from doing or forced into by external pressure.

The economic achievements of 1971 — the first full year of his presidency — were real. Growth, rising wages, and falling unemployment happened. They were partly funded by drawing down Chile's foreign currency reserves and by running budget deficits that would prove unsustainable. By 1972, inflation had reached 163% and was accelerating. The combination of declining copper prices, U.S. pressure on international lenders to refuse credit to Chile, and Allende's own expansionary policies created a genuine economic crisis by 1973.

The political crisis was equally severe. Chile's society became deeply polarised — women banging pots in the streets (the "cacerolazo" protests), truck drivers striking for months with U.S. funding, the opposition-controlled Congress declaring Allende's government illegal in August 1973. Allende was governing a country that was actively splitting, in a political system designed for consensus that had none.

Context

How much did U.S. interference actually cause the crisis?

Declassified CIA and State Department documents confirm that the Nixon administration authorised $10 million in September 1970 alone to prevent Allende taking office, with ongoing covert funding of opposition groups and media throughout his presidency. The "invisible blockade" — pressure on the World Bank and Inter-American Development Bank to refuse loans — was real and effective. Historians debate what proportion of Chile's economic crisis was caused by external sabotage versus Allende's own policy errors. The honest answer is: both, and separating them precisely is impossible.

The Destabilisation: What the U.S. Actually Did

September 1970
CIA Track I and Track II: Before He Even Took Office
▼

After Allende won the September 4 election, the CIA initiated two simultaneous covert programmes. Track I aimed to persuade the Chilean Congress — which was required to ratify the election of a candidate who failed to win an outright majority — not to confirm Allende. Track II, authorised by Nixon and Kissinger and conducted without the knowledge of the State Department or Defense Department, tasked the CIA with exploring a military coup. As part of Track II, the CIA was involved in an attempt to kidnap General René Schneider — the army's commander-in-chief, who had explicitly stated he would respect the constitutional process. The kidnappers shot Schneider, who died. The outrage over the attack actually strengthened Allende's position; Congress confirmed him. The CIA paid the plotters $35,000 after the fact.

1971–1973
The Economic Squeeze and Covert Funding
▼

Throughout Allende's presidency, the U.S. government pressured international financial institutions to restrict credit to Chile while maintaining military aid to the Chilean armed forces. The CIA funded opposition newspapers — particularly El Mercurio, Chile's largest newspaper — and provided money to opposition political parties. The 1972 truckers' strike, which paralysed Chile's economy for weeks, received CIA funding according to later Senate investigations. These interventions were not enough to cause Allende's economic problems by themselves, but they amplified them significantly and prevented him from accessing the foreign credit that might have stabilised his finances.

August 22, 1973
Congress Declares the Government "Illegal"
▼

The opposition-controlled Chilean Congress passed a resolution accusing Allende's government of systematic violations of the constitution and calling on the military to restore order. The resolution was legally ambiguous — it was not an impeachment, and Allende contested its authority — but it gave the military a political justification for intervention that they subsequently used. Allende announced a plebiscite for September 11 in which Chileans would vote directly on whether his government should continue. He believed he would win it. The military struck three weeks before the vote was held.

September 11, 1973: The Last Day

At 7:55 a.m. on September 11, 1973, Chile's navy seized the port city of Valparaíso. Allende was informed at his residence and drove to La Moneda, the presidential palace in central Santiago. He knew what was coming. He had received intelligence warnings but had not believed the military would move so decisively or so unanimously. He telephoned a radio station and broadcast a farewell address while the military surrounded the palace.

September 11, 1973

The Bombing of La Moneda

At approximately 11:55 a.m., Chilean Air Force jets bombed the presidential palace. Allende was inside, armed, with a small group of guards and loyalists. By early afternoon, military forces stormed the building. Allende died in his office — his body found with his AK-47 (a gift from Fidel Castro) nearby. For 25 years, the official military narrative claimed he was shot by soldiers; forensic examination conducted in 2011 confirmed he had shot himself, under circumstances that leave little doubt he chose death over capture. He was 65 years old.

His last radio broadcast, delivered while the palace was under bombardment, is one of the most remarkable political documents of the 20th century. He spoke calmly and with complete clarity about what was happening and what he believed:

"I will not resign. Placed at a historic crossroads, I will pay with my life for the loyalty of the people. And I say to them that I am certain that the seeds which we have planted in the good conscience of thousands and thousands of Chileans will not be shrivelled forever… The broad avenues through which free men will walk to build a better society will open again. Long live Chile! Long live the people! Long live the workers! These are my last words."
— Salvador Allende, final radio address, September 11, 1973

Why It Matters Today

Allende's 1,007-day presidency ended in September 1973. But the questions it raised — about whether democratic socialism can survive external sabotage, about what the United States owes to governments it helped destroy, about whether economic disruption justifies military intervention — have not been answered. They are live arguments in every country that tries to elect a government Washington distrusts.

The Template for Externally-Engineered Democratic Collapse

Chile 1973 became the definitive case study in how a great power can destabilise a democratically elected government without a direct military intervention. The tools used against Allende — economic strangulation, CIA support for opposition media and political organisations, encouragement of business strikes, cultivation of military officers — have since been documented in detail and provide a blueprint that analysts apply when assessing U.S. policy toward Venezuela, Bolivia, Ecuador, and other left-leaning governments in Latin America.

  • Declassified CIA documents confirmed direct U.S. funding of El Mercurio — Chile's largest newspaper — to run anti-Allende content throughout his presidency
  • The "invisible blockade" — U.S. pressure on international banks and export credit agencies to withhold lending to Chile — reduced foreign exchange reserves by 80% between 1970 and 1973
  • When Hugo Chávez was elected in Venezuela in 1998, analysts immediately reached for the Chile template to assess U.S. response — and the 2002 coup attempt against him validated that instinct
  • The U.S. acknowledged its role in the coup only partially; no official apology has been issued; no reparations have been paid to Chile

The Unfinished Accountability Question

Chile's transition to democracy was accompanied by an amnesty law that protected coup perpetrators — and by a negotiated democratic transition in which Pinochet retained the army command and later a Senate seat. Full accountability never came. The Valech and Rettig commissions documented the crimes; the prosecutions were partial; and the political debate about whether the Pinochet era represented a founding trauma or an unfortunate necessity continues to animate Chilean politics today.

  • Pinochet was arrested in London in 1998 on a Spanish warrant for crimes against humanity — the first time a former head of state had been arrested abroad under universal jurisdiction; he returned to Chile in 2000 without trial
  • Chile's 2022 constitutional referendum — which proposed a new constitution replacing Pinochet's 1980 document — failed by 62–38%. A second attempt also failed in 2023. Pinochet's constitution still governs Chile.
  • The Rettig Report (1991) and Valech Report (2004, 2011) documented 3,065 killings and 28,459 torture cases — but fewer than 100 people have been imprisoned
  • As of 2024, hundreds of human rights cases from the Pinochet era remain open in Chilean courts; ageing perpetrators die before trials conclude

The Democratic Socialism Question Is Still Open

Allende's core project — using democratic institutions to achieve a socialist transformation of a capitalist economy — failed. But historians debate whether it failed because democratic socialism is structurally unworkable, because the specific Chilean context made it impossible, or because external sabotage prevented a real test. This argument matters wherever left governments win elections in developing countries that have significant foreign economic exposure.

  • Allende's first year produced remarkable results — 8.9% GDP growth, 22% real wage increases, falling unemployment — before the economic crisis began
  • The degree to which the crisis was caused by his policies versus orchestrated external pressure is genuinely contested among economic historians
  • Gabriel Boric, elected Chilean president in 2021, ran as a democratic socialist explicitly in Allende's tradition — and then moderated substantially in office under similar economic pressure, raising the same structural questions
  • Allende is invoked by left governments from Bolivia to Greece to South Africa as a cautionary precedent about what external powers will do to constrain redistributive programmes

Copper, Resources, and Economic Sovereignty

Allende's copper nationalisation — the centrepiece of his economic programme, completed in July 1971 — was not reversed by Pinochet. CODELCO, the state copper company, remains state-owned today. It is the single most consequential structural change Allende made, and its survival through 50 years of political change — including 17 years of Pinochet — suggests that resource nationalism, when implemented in a sector with genuine strategic importance, can be politically durable in ways that other socialist measures are not.

  • CODELCO remains the world's largest copper producer; copper accounts for roughly half of Chile's export earnings
  • The lithium nationalisation debates in Chile in 2023 — around the world's largest lithium reserves — explicitly referenced Allende's copper model as the political and legal precedent
  • Allende's argument that natural resources belong to the nation rather than to foreign corporations has become the default framework in resource-rich developing nations, regardless of their ideological position

Legacy & Debate

The central historical question about Allende is not whether he was admirable — most historians across the political spectrum regard him as a genuine believer in democratic socialism who died for his convictions. The harder questions are: was his programme workable? Was the economic crisis primarily his fault or the product of destabilisation? And what does his 1,007-day presidency tell us about whether democratic socialism can survive in a world where powerful outside interests oppose it?

What he achieved
  • First full year: GDP up 8.9%, real wages up 22%, unemployment fell from 8.3% to 3.8%
  • Copper nationalisation secured Chile's primary resource from foreign corporate control — later governments kept this nationalisation
  • Expanded access to healthcare, nutrition (the half-litre of milk), and education for Chile's poorest
  • Demonstrated that a socialist programme could be pursued through democratic means — a model that influenced Latin American politics for decades
  • Maintained constitutional governance throughout his presidency despite extreme pressure, never suspending democracy even when it would have been politically convenient
Failures and contradictions
  • Inflation accelerated catastrophically — from 22% (1970) to 163% (1972) to 508% by the time of the coup in 1973
  • Unable to control the revolutionary left within his own coalition, which alienated the middle class and gave the opposition legitimate grievances
  • His government's pace of nationalisation exceeded what the legal framework comfortably supported, creating constitutional conflicts he could not resolve
  • The economic mismanagement was real, not solely a product of external sabotage — though the degree to which each contributed remains disputed
The unresolved question
  • Would "the Chilean road to socialism" have worked without U.S. interference? We cannot know — the experiment was ended before it could conclude
  • Could Allende have managed inflation without the credit squeeze imposed by Washington? This is debated; some economists say yes, others say the programme was fiscally unsustainable regardless
  • His legacy became more powerful through his death than it might have been through his success — the image of a man dying at his desk is politically indestructible in a way that a struggling government would not have been

What can be said with certainty is that the question Allende was asking — whether democratic socialism is possible — was never answered. The experiment was destroyed from outside before it reached its conclusion. The right uses this to argue his programme was unsustainable; the left uses it to argue no conclusion can be drawn. Both are, in their own way, correct. The honest position is that we don't know what Chile would have become, and we never will.
