Middle East & North Africa

Gaddafi, the Arab Spring,
and Why Libya Still Burns

How one man's 42-year grip on a desert nation created a power vacuum the world is still trying to fill.

Beginner-friendly ~8 min read 4 academic sources

The one-paragraph version

Libya was ruled by a dictator named Muammar Gaddafi for 42 years. In 2011, inspired by uprisings across the Arab world, Libyans rebelled. With NATO's help, Gaddafi was overthrown and killed. But instead of democracy, Libya collapsed into chaos — two rival governments, dozens of armed militias, and no functioning state. That chaos still matters: it's why hundreds of thousands of migrants cross Libya to reach Europe, why terrorist groups flourish nearby, and why Russia and Turkey are now fighting a proxy war there.

Key figures

🎖️

Muammar Gaddafi

DICTATOR · IN POWER 1969–2011

A 27-year-old army officer who overthrew Libya's king in a 1969 coup. He funded terrorist groups, including the IRA and the Lockerbie bombers, making Libya a pariah state. But he also used oil money to build schools, hospitals, and housing — meaning many Libyans genuinely mourned him. His brutal crackdown on 2011 protesters triggered the NATO intervention that ended his rule.

⚖️

The National Transitional Council (NTC)

REBEL GOVERNMENT · FORMED 2011

A coalition of lawyers, ex-ministers, and defecting army officers who formed the rebel government in Benghazi. Recognised by Western nations as the legitimate government of Libya, they received NATO air support. But after Gaddafi's fall, they struggled to disarm the dozens of militia groups that had actually fought the war — setting the stage for years of instability.

What happened, step by step

1969 Gaddafi seizes power in a bloodless coup ▶
A group of young military officers, calling themselves the "Free Officers Movement," topple King Idris while he's abroad for medical treatment. Gaddafi, just 27, becomes the face of the revolution. He immediately nationalises Libya's oil, expels US and British military bases, and frames himself as a pan-Arab nationalist hero. For a generation of Libyans, he is genuinely popular.

📚 Vandewalle, D. (2012). A History of Modern Libya. Cambridge University Press.
1970s–2000s 42 years of oil, terror, and repression ▶
Gaddafi builds a bizarre political system he calls the "Jamahiriya" — rule by the masses — but in practice it is brutal authoritarianism. Critics are executed on live television. He funds the IRA in Ireland, Palestinian militant groups, and the bombers who blow up Pan Am Flight 103 over Lockerbie, Scotland in 1988 (270 dead). International sanctions cripple the country for decades. Yet oil wealth funds free healthcare, education, and subsidised housing — creating a complicated legacy.

📚 St John, R. B. (2011). Libya: From Colony to Revolution. Oneworld Publications.
Jan 2011 The Arab Spring arrives in Libya ▶
Tunisia's dictator has just been toppled by street protests. Egypt's Hosni Mubarak is on the brink. Inspired, Libyans in Benghazi (the eastern city that always resented Gaddafi) take to the streets. Gaddafi's response is immediate and savage: security forces open fire on protesters. His son Saif al-Islam appears on state TV and warns of a civil war that will see "rivers of blood." The rebellion explodes.

📚 Pack, J. (Ed.) (2013). The 2011 Libyan Uprisings and the Struggle for the Post-Qadhafi Future. Palgrave Macmillan.
Mar 2011 NATO intervenes — air strikes begin ▶
The UN Security Council authorises a "no-fly zone" to protect civilians. NATO interprets this broadly, launching air strikes against Gaddafi's forces. France, the UK, and the US lead the campaign. Critics argue NATO crossed a line from protecting civilians to actively helping rebels win the war. Gaddafi's military — armed with Soviet-era tanks but no air power — crumbles. Rebel militias, each loyal to different towns and commanders, sweep westward.

📚 Chivvis, C.S. (2014). Toppling Qaddafi: Libya and the Limits of Liberal Intervention. Cambridge University Press.
Oct 2011 Gaddafi captured and killed by a mob ▶
Gaddafi is found hiding in a drainage pipe near his hometown of Sirte. He is beaten, shot, and his body displayed for days. His death is filmed on mobile phones and broadcast worldwide. Western leaders celebrate. But with Gaddafi gone, there is no plan — no post-war government structure, no deal to disarm the militias, and no functioning institutions. The vacuum is immediate and catastrophic.

📚 Pack, J. (Ed.) (2013). The 2011 Libyan Uprisings and the Struggle for the Post-Qadhafi Future. Palgrave Macmillan.
2014–now Two governments, dozens of militias — ongoing civil war ▶
Libya splits into two competing governments: one in Tripoli (backed by Turkey and Qatar) and one in the east (backed by Russia, the UAE, and Egypt). A warlord named Khalifa Haftar leads a military campaign to control the country. ISIS seizes the city of Sirte in 2015. Slave markets for migrants are discovered in 2017. By the 2020s, there is still no unified government, and foreign mercenaries from Russia's Wagner Group operate openly on Libyan soil.

📚 Vandewalle, D. (2012). A History of Modern Libya. Cambridge University Press.

Why this matters today

🚢

Europe's migration crisis

Libya is the main departure point for migrants crossing the Mediterranean to Europe. With no functioning coast guard or border control, smuggling networks thrive. Over 20,000 people have drowned making the crossing since 2014. This fuelled the rise of far-right parties across Europe who campaigned on migration.

Ongoing
🪖

The rise of ISIS & Sahel terrorism

When Gaddafi fell, his enormous weapons stockpiles — guns, missiles, explosives — were looted by militias and smuggled across the Sahara. These weapons armed jihadist groups across Mali, Niger, and Chad. ISIS established a Libyan branch. The terrorist insurgency now destabilising West Africa traces directly back to 2011.

Expanding
🛢️

Global oil & energy prices

Libya holds Africa's largest proven oil reserves. Before 2011, it exported 1.6 million barrels per day. Civil war has repeatedly shut down oil fields and terminals, contributing to global price spikes. Every time militias fight over an oil terminal, it sends ripples through petrol prices worldwide.

Economic
🌍

A proxy war for global powers

Libya is now a chessboard where Russia, Turkey, the UAE, Egypt, and France all back different factions. Russia's Wagner Group uses Libya as a base for broader African operations. Turkey uses it to project influence into the Mediterranean. What looks like a local conflict is actually a Cold War-style great power competition playing out in the desert.
