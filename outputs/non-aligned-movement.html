The Non-Aligned Movement

The Cold War project to build a third way between the American and Soviet blocs — and why over a hundred countries decided neutrality was more powerful than choosing sides.

What Is the Non-Aligned Movement?

The Non-Aligned Movement — the NAM — was the largest geopolitical project of the twentieth century that most people in the West have almost entirely forgotten. At its founding in 1961, it brought together twenty-five newly independent nations that refused to take sides in the Cold War. At its peak, it counted 120 member states — two-thirds of the world's countries and over half the world's population. Its members stretched from Cuba to Yugoslavia to India to Indonesia to Egypt, united by one core proposition: the world's poor and recently decolonised nations had no obligation to serve as pawns in a superpower conflict that wasn't theirs.

The word "non-aligned" is often mistaken for neutral, passive, or uncommitted. It was none of these things. The NAM was a loud, assertive, and often radical political project. Its founders believed that true independence meant more than flag-raising and seat-filling in the United Nations — it meant controlling your own foreign policy, your own economy, and your own destiny. They called themselves the "Third World," a term coined by French economist Alfred Sauvy in 1952 by analogy to France's pre-revolutionary Third Estate: the majority who did the work and bore the burden while two powerful factions quarrelled over who would rule them.

The NAM operated on multiple levels simultaneously: as a diplomatic bloc that tried to coordinate votes at the UN and speak with one voice in trade negotiations; as an ideological project that promoted decolonisation, racial equality, and economic sovereignty; and as a symbolic claim that the Global South had the right to exist as an independent political force, not merely as a theatre for superpower competition. How well it succeeded in any of these roles is the central contested question of its history.

Definition

Non-Alignment vs Neutrality — A Crucial Distinction

The NAM's founders were emphatic that they were not neutral. Nehru, Nasser, and Tito all took strong positions on the issues of their day — decolonisation, Palestinian rights, nuclear disarmament, apartheid South Africa. What they rejected was alignment: the automatic subordination of their foreign policy to either Washington or Moscow. Non-alignment meant the freedom to judge each issue on its merits, ally with whoever was right on that particular question, and refuse the Cold War's demand that the entire world organise itself around two poles. As Nehru put it: "We are not neutralists. We are the people who want peace."

Origins & Development

The intellectual origins of non-alignment predate the movement's formal founding by more than a decade. India, even before independence, had been arguing that the newly liberated nations of Asia and Africa owed no allegiance to the European order that had subjugated them — including its Cold War successor. When Jawaharlal Nehru became India's first Prime Minister in 1947, he made non-alignment the cornerstone of Indian foreign policy from the start, refusing to join either NATO or the Soviet-aligned Warsaw Pact. His foreign minister, V.K. Krishna Menon, articulated the position with particular sharpness: the Cold War was a quarrel between two variants of European power, and India had no stake in either side's victory.

The Bandung Conference of April 1955 was the first major gathering of non-European nations to act collectively on this principle. Twenty-nine countries from Asia and Africa — representing 1.5 billion people and covering a quarter of the Earth's land area — met in Bandung, Indonesia, convened by Indonesian President Sukarno. The conference produced the "Ten Principles of Bandung," which included respect for national sovereignty, non-aggression, and non-interference in each other's internal affairs. It was the moment the Third World announced it existed as a political actor.

The Non-Aligned Movement proper was formally constituted at the Belgrade Conference in September 1961. Its five founding architects were Nehru (India), Nasser (Egypt), Tito (Yugoslavia), Sukarno (Indonesia), and Kwame Nkrumah (Ghana). These five embodied the movement's contradictions from the start: Tito was a communist leading a socialist state; Nehru was a democratic socialist; Nasser was an Arab nationalist with authoritarian tendencies; Nkrumah was a pan-Africanist visionary who would be overthrown in a CIA-assisted coup in 1966. None of them was neutral. All of them believed non-alignment was a more powerful position than submission to either bloc.

April 1955
Bandung Conference — The Third World Announces Itself
▼

The Bandung Conference was the NAM's intellectual birth. Twenty-nine nations — mostly newly independent from European colonial rule — gathered in the West Java city of Bandung to assert a common identity and common interest. The agenda was decolonisation and Cold War non-involvement. The atmosphere was electric: these were countries that had been told for generations they were incapable of self-governance, now meeting as sovereign powers to set their own agenda.

The Ten Principles of Bandung codified what would become the NAM's philosophical core: respect for sovereignty, non-aggression, non-interference, equality of nations, and opposition to colonialism in all forms. China's Zhou Enlai attended as an observer and charmed the conference with his measured diplomacy — though China's own relationship to the NAM would remain fraught, given its alignment with Moscow until the Sino-Soviet split. The conference demonstrated that a Third World bloc was not just possible but politically compelling.

September 1961
Belgrade Conference — The NAM is Born, With Contradictions Baked In
▼

Twenty-five nations met in Belgrade under the chairmanship of Yugoslav President Josip Broz Tito — the one European leader who had successfully defied both superpowers, having broken with Stalin in 1948 while refusing to join NATO. The choice of venue and chair said everything about what the NAM was trying to be: independent of both Washington and Moscow, governed by its own logic.

The tensions were visible from the opening sessions. Nehru wanted the conference to focus on colonialism and peaceful coexistence; Nasser wanted a more explicitly anti-Western posture, given Egypt's confrontation with Britain and France over Suez in 1956; Sukarno wanted pan-Asian solidarity; Nkrumah wanted African unity. The final communiqué managed to paper over these differences with sufficiently vague language about peace, disarmament, and anti-colonialism. The NAM had been born — but its ability to maintain coherence across such different national interests would be tested almost immediately.

1964–1974
UNCTAD and the New International Economic Order — The NAM's Most Ambitious Agenda
▼

The NAM's most substantive political project was economic, not military. At the UN Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD) in 1964, the developing nations formed the "Group of 77" (G-77) to demand better terms of trade — the NAM's economic arm. In 1974, the G-77 pushed through the UN Declaration on the Establishment of a New International Economic Order (NIEO), which called for stable commodity prices, technology transfer from rich to poor nations, and the right to nationalise natural resources without interference.

The NIEO was the NAM at its most radical and its most doomed. The demands were economically coherent — the terms of trade genuinely did disadvantage commodity exporters — but the political power to implement them simply didn't exist. The United States and other wealthy nations refused to accept the NIEO framework. The oil shocks of 1973 and 1979 briefly appeared to shift power toward commodity-exporting nations, but the debt crisis of the 1980s reversed whatever gains had been made. By the mid-1980s, the NIEO was dead and the NAM's economic programme had been replaced by IMF structural adjustment — effectively the opposite of what the movement had demanded.

1979–1991
The NAM's Crisis — When Members Started Choosing Sides
▼

The Soviet invasion of Afghanistan in December 1979 put the NAM in an impossible position. The movement's official policy was anti-imperialism — but whose imperialism were they condemning? The Soviet invasion was a textbook case of a great power violating a small nation's sovereignty. Many NAM members (India, Cuba, Syria) refused to condemn it; others (most African and Asian states, with increasing discomfort) went along with Soviet diplomatic pressure. The NAM emergency session on Afghanistan passed a watered-down resolution that satisfied nobody.

The episode exposed the movement's central vulnerability: non-alignment worked as long as both superpowers were roughly equivalent threats. When one superpower (the Soviet Union) had closer economic and military ties to many NAM members than the other (the United States), "non-alignment" was a polite fiction. Cuba hosted the NAM summit in 1979 while simultaneously sending troops to Angola and Ethiopia in support of Soviet-backed governments. The incoherence was becoming impossible to ignore. By the late 1980s, as the Cold War wound down, the question of what the NAM was actually for was increasingly unanswerable.

Core Principles

The NAM's principles were articulated most fully at Bandung and Belgrade and elaborated at successive summits. They cluster around four core ideas that together constitute the movement's political philosophy — a philosophy that was both genuinely radical and frequently violated by members who found it inconvenient.

Sovereignty and Non-Interference

The foundational principle: every nation, however small and newly independent, possessed sovereignty equal in law to the great powers. No external force had the right to interfere in another country's internal affairs, to impose political or economic systems, or to use aid as a lever for compliance. This principle had obvious appeal to nations that had just escaped colonialism — and it meant the NAM resisted both American pressure to align against communism and Soviet pressure to align against capitalism. It also meant, in practice, that NAM members rarely criticised each other's human rights records.

Anti-Colonialism and Racial Equality

The NAM was born from decolonisation and remained obsessively focused on it. Opposition to apartheid in South Africa was the movement's most consistent and passionate cause — sustained long after the Cold War had faded as an organising principle. The NAM also demanded racial equality within the international system itself, arguing that the UN Security Council's permanent membership structure — five powers, all from the North — reflected the colonial world order rather than the actual distribution of humanity. The movement's most enduring political achievement was its role in delegitimising colonialism and apartheid as internationally acceptable practices.

Economic Sovereignty

Non-alignment was never just about military alliances — it was about economic independence too. NAM members argued that formal political independence meant nothing if their economies remained dependent on commodity exports whose prices were set in London and New York, their debt held by Western banks, and their natural resources extracted by foreign corporations. The demand for a New International Economic Order was an attempt to restructure global trade and finance in ways that gave developing nations genuine agency. It failed — but the underlying analysis of structural inequality in the global economy has proven remarkably durable.

Nuclear Disarmament and Peaceful Coexistence

The NAM was the first major international forum to demand nuclear disarmament as a political priority. Its founding communiqués called for banning nuclear weapons testing, halting proliferation, and eventually eliminating nuclear arsenals entirely. On this issue, the NAM was more an expression of moral pressure than a meaningful policy force — neither the U.S. nor the Soviet Union had any intention of disarming — but it gave voice to the majority of humanity's genuine terror at nuclear war. The movement's advocacy contributed to the Partial Nuclear Test Ban Treaty of 1963 and the Non-Proliferation Treaty of 1968, though whether the NAM's pressure was decisive is contested.

Structural tension

The Non-Alignment Paradox

The NAM's insistence on non-interference in members' internal affairs created an immediate problem: many of its members were authoritarian states that were themselves interfering massively in their citizens' lives. The principle of sovereignty — originally designed to protect small nations from great powers — became a shield behind which member states could ignore human rights without fear of collective criticism. When Uganda's Idi Amin chaired the NAM in 1975 while conducting a reign of terror at home, the movement's inability to say anything about it exposed the limits of sovereignty as an absolute principle. The NAM was better at criticising external imperialism than at confronting internal oppression.

The Non-Aligned Movement in Practice

1956
The Suez Crisis — Non-Alignment's First Great Test
▼

When Nasser nationalised the Suez Canal in 1956 and Britain, France, and Israel invaded Egypt in response, the Suez Crisis became the NAM's first major real-world test — even before the movement had been formally constituted. Nasser's Egypt was the archetype of what non-alignment was supposed to enable: a small, recently decolonised nation acting on its own interests, nationalising a foreign-controlled asset, and resisting imperial retaliation.

The outcome was encouraging for the non-aligned idea. The United States — in this case — sided against its British and French allies, pressuring them to withdraw. The Soviets threatened rocket attacks. Nasser survived and became a hero across the Arab world and the Global South. But the lesson was double-edged: Egypt's survival depended on superpower intervention (American pressure and Soviet threats), not on Third World solidarity. Non-alignment had been vindicated symbolically; as a practical survival strategy, it was more ambiguous than its proponents acknowledged.

Nkrumah (1963)Prashad (2007)
1962
The Cuban Missile Crisis — The Limits of Third World Influence
▼

Cuba was a NAM member while simultaneously being armed by the Soviet Union and receiving Soviet nuclear missiles. When the 1962 Cuban Missile Crisis brought the world to the edge of nuclear war, the NAM was essentially irrelevant. The crisis was resolved through direct U.S.-Soviet negotiation, with no meaningful input from the 60-plus non-aligned nations whose survival was at stake. U Thant, the UN Secretary-General from Burma, attempted to mediate — but the actual resolution was worked out by Kennedy and Khrushchev over his head.

The episode revealed what the NAM could and could not do. It could articulate moral positions, provide diplomatic cover, and amplify voices in the UN General Assembly. It could not prevent a superpower confrontation or insert itself into the negotiating room when the great powers decided to deal directly. The movement's influence was real but bounded by a fundamental asymmetry of power that its rhetoric sometimes obscured.

Willetts (1978)
1960s–1994
Anti-Apartheid — The NAM's Most Sustained and Successful Campaign
▼

If the NAM had one genuinely durable achievement, it was its role in the international isolation of apartheid South Africa. NAM members drove the expulsion of South Africa from the British Commonwealth in 1961, pushed for UN arms embargoes, and sustained three decades of diplomatic, sporting, and economic boycotts that made apartheid an internationally condemned practice rather than a legitimate domestic policy. The South African government repeatedly tried to frame apartheid as an internal matter — exactly the non-interference principle the NAM usually endorsed — and the NAM consistently rejected that framing on racial justice grounds.

The success was partial and slow. Britain and the United States for most of the period refused to impose comprehensive sanctions, and South Africa maintained economic relationships with Western countries throughout the apartheid era. But the NAM's moral pressure was a real factor in delegitimising the apartheid regime internationally, and the movement's consistent solidarity with the African National Congress gave the liberation movement diplomatic resources it might otherwise have lacked. Nelson Mandela acknowledged the NAM's contribution directly after South Africa's democratic transition in 1994.

1973–1974
The Oil Embargo — When NAM Logic Gave Way to Economic Power
▼

The 1973 oil embargo by Arab OPEC members briefly seemed to validate the NAM's economic logic: if developing nations controlled strategic resources, they could use that control to compel the rich world to change its policies. Oil prices quadrupled; Western economies were thrown into recession; the balance of global economic power seemed to be shifting. Arab oil producers used the embargo explicitly as a political weapon — withdrawing supply to pressure countries supporting Israel in the Yom Kippur War.

The episode also demonstrated the limits of Third World solidarity. The oil embargo benefited Arab oil exporters enormously; for oil-importing developing nations — most of Africa, South Asia, and Latin America — it was an economic catastrophe, raising their import bills far beyond what development aid could compensate. The G-77 tried to frame the oil crisis as a collective Third World assertion of economic power, but the reality was more fractured: OPEC's gains came partly at the expense of the same poor nations the NAM claimed to represent.

Prashad (2012)
2003–present
The NAM After the Cold War — Searching for Relevance
▼

After the Soviet Union's collapse in 1991, the NAM's original rationale — refusing to choose between two superpower blocs — had vanished. The movement continued to meet, continued to admit members, and continued to issue communiqués. But what it was actually for became increasingly unclear. In the 2003 Kuala Lumpur Summit, the NAM condemned the U.S. invasion of Iraq — which aligned it with much of world opinion but didn't change anything. At the 2012 Tehran Summit, Iran held the chairmanship while under UN sanctions for nuclear weapons development — a situation that highlighted the movement's inability to maintain any coherent political identity.

Some analysts argue the NAM has found a new role in the multipolar world that has emerged since the 2000s: as a forum for the Global South to collectively resist the "rules-based international order" that the United States and European Union promote — which, NAM members argue, is really a Western-designed order that serves Western interests. Others argue the movement is a diplomatic zombie: technically alive, incapable of effective action, maintained mostly by habit and the diplomatic prestige accumulated from its Cold War heyday. The emergence of alternative groupings like the BRICS nations and the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation may be doing more to build an alternative to Western-led multilateralism than the NAM has managed in three decades.

Prashad (2012)

Why It Matters Today

The Non-Aligned Movement as a Cold War institution is over. But the questions it tried to answer remain central to international politics. Who sets the rules of the international order? Do those rules serve humanity equally or do they encode the interests of the powerful? Does sovereignty require the strong to leave the weak alone? These questions animate global politics today as fiercely as they did in 1961.

The Return of Great Power Competition

As U.S.-China rivalry intensifies, the same dynamic the NAM was responding to is reasserting itself: great powers pressuring smaller states to choose sides, threatening economic consequences for non-compliance, offering alliances with strategic strings attached. Countries like India, Turkey, Saudi Arabia, and Brazil are explicitly reviving NAM-style logic — refusing to choose between Washington and Beijing, extracting concessions from both, maintaining strategic autonomy. The NAM's intellectual legacy is more relevant than the organisation itself.

India's refusal to condemn Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine is a direct expression of NAM-era reasoning
Turkey maintains NATO membership while purchasing Russian S-400 missiles — a 21st-century version of the non-alignment balancing act

Global South Solidarity and Trade

The NAM's economic project — the demand for fair terms of trade, commodity price stability, and debt relief — failed in the 1980s. But the underlying structural problem it identified has not been solved. Developing nations still depend heavily on commodity exports whose prices they do not control; still carry debt burdens that constrain their policy space; still struggle to develop domestic manufacturing against competition from established industrial powers. The G-20, the WTO Doha Round collapse, and the debt relief debates at successive G-7 summits are all, in different ways, continuations of the argument the NAM started in 1964.

The NIEO demand for technology transfer is directly echoed in current debates about green energy technology access
The debt crisis facing 60+ developing nations in 2023–2025 echoes the 1980s crisis that destroyed the NIEO agenda

Sovereignty vs. Humanitarian Intervention

The NAM's insistence on non-interference sits in direct tension with the Responsibility to Protect doctrine, which asserts that sovereignty is conditional on protecting citizens. Most NAM members have historically rejected R2P — or accepted it narrowly — precisely because it provides a legal framework for the kind of external intervention the movement was founded to resist. The Syria debate, the Libya debate, and the Myanmar debate are all, in part, arguments between NAM-era sovereignty logic and post-1990s humanitarian intervention norms.

119 of 120 NAM members voted against recognising Kosovo's independence — a sovereignty-first position at odds with Western policy
China and Russia, not formal NAM members, often vote with NAM positions at the UN Security Council on sovereignty grounds

The Memory Problem — Who Writes Cold War History

The NAM is nearly absent from Western Cold War historiography, which tends to organise events around the U.S.-Soviet confrontation and treat the developing world as a passive theatre. This is itself a political choice. The movement's leaders — Nehru, Nkrumah, Nasser, Tito — were major historical figures who shaped the postcolonial world, and their project had real effects on decolonisation, nuclear arms control debates, and international economic law. Recovering the NAM's history is part of a broader effort to write the Cold War as a global event rather than a European and American one.

Vijay Prashad's The Darker Nations (2007) is the most influential recent attempt to write Cold War history from the Global South's perspective

Legacy & Debate

The historical verdict on the Non-Aligned Movement is genuinely divided. Enthusiasts point to decolonisation, the anti-apartheid campaign, and the moral pressure for nuclear disarmament as real achievements. Critics argue the movement was largely performative — a club of authoritarian leaders who used anti-imperialist rhetoric to deflect criticism of their own governance, while their economic programme failed and their members repeatedly chose sides when it was convenient. The truth is somewhere between these positions, and depends heavily on which aspect of the NAM you're evaluating.

Vijay Prashad
Historian, Tricontinental Institute

The NAM's most influential modern champion. Prashad argues in The Darker Nations that the Third World project was a genuine and sophisticated attempt to restructure global power relations — and that its failure was not due to any inherent flaw in the project but to the deliberate sabotage of Western powers and international financial institutions. He reads the NIEO's collapse and the debt crisis of the 1980s as imposed defeats, not natural outcomes.

Peter Willetts
Emeritus Professor, City University London

Willetts' foundational study The Non-Aligned Movement (1978) is more measured. He finds real unity and political capacity in the movement's early years, particularly on decolonisation, but traces how the entry of new members with conflicting interests and the Soviet-backed Angola/Cuba intervention eroded coherence through the 1970s. His is the essential empirical account, even if its conclusions are now contested.

Kwame Nkrumah
President of Ghana (1957–1966) / Founding NAM figure

Nkrumah's Neo-Colonialism (1965) provides the NAM's sharpest economic critique from inside: political independence without economic independence was meaningless, he argued, and the machinery of international finance was designed to perpetuate colonial extraction under new management. His argument that the West would never voluntarily accept a New International Economic Order proved prescient — as did his subsequent overthrow in a CIA-assisted coup.

What the NAM achieved
  • Decolonisation: the movement gave political and diplomatic cover to independence movements across Africa and Asia, accelerating a process that the great powers had shown little interest in hurrying
  • Anti-apartheid: sustained, consistent pressure over three decades contributed materially to the international isolation of South Africa's regime
  • Nuclear pressure: the NAM's consistent advocacy for disarmament, while not decisive, helped build the international normative framework that produced the Partial Test Ban Treaty (1963) and NPT (1968)
  • Economic analysis: the movement's identification of structural inequality in the international trading system was intellectually serious and empirically grounded, even if the proposed remedies failed
  • Survival model: smaller nations that maintained NAM-style strategic autonomy (India, Yugoslavia) were often better positioned to extract concessions from both superpowers than those that formally aligned with one
Where it fell short
  • Many members were authoritarian states whose non-alignment protected them from external pressure to improve their human rights records, which the movement's sovereignty doctrine could not criticise
  • The NIEO — the movement's most ambitious economic project — was a complete failure; global trade and finance remained structured on Western terms, and the debt crisis of the 1980s imposed exactly the kind of economic subordination the NAM had sought to escape
  • The movement was effectively irrelevant during the Cold War's most dangerous moments (Berlin, Cuba, Afghanistan) — when it mattered most, the superpowers dealt with each other directly
  • Members repeatedly violated non-alignment when convenient: Cuba's military interventions in Africa, India's close Soviet alignment, Egypt's pivot to the U.S. after Camp David
  • After 1991, the movement failed to articulate a coherent post-Cold War identity or purpose — it has met and issued communiqués but not done anything of significance for three decades

The most honest assessment is probably this: the NAM was most successful when it operated as a moral and diplomatic pressure campaign on specific issues (decolonisation, apartheid) where it had strong normative ground and the patience to sustain pressure over decades. It was least successful when it tried to restructure global economic arrangements or maintain military neutrality in genuine crises. The movement was always more effective at saying "no" to the existing order than at building an alternative to it — and when its own members found non-alignment inconvenient, they abandoned it without apology.

"We are not neutralists. We are the people who want peace and who try to act in such a manner as to preserve peace. The neutralist attitude often means an acceptance of things as they are. We do not accept them."
— Jawaharlal Nehru, First Prime Minister of India, on the difference between non-alignment and neutralism
