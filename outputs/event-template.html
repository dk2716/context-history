Background

Opening paragraph: set the scene. Who ruled, how did they rule, what tensions existed? Avoid starting with "In [YEAR]" — start with a human or structural detail that draws the reader in. Be specific: names, numbers, dates.

Second paragraph: deepen the structural context. What made conflict or crisis possible here and not elsewhere? Economic conditions, historical grievances, ethnic or sectarian fault lines, external pressures. Show the reader the dry tinder before the spark.

Third paragraph: the immediate lead-up. What were the last months/years before the event? What warnings were ignored? What decisions were made that made the outcome more likely? This bridges background to the event itself.

Turning Points

A 1–2 sentence framing paragraph: what makes these moments significant as turning points rather than just events? What was the counterfactual — what was the road not taken?

Turning Point 1
The Decision / Moment That Changed Everything

2–3 sentences explaining why this was a hinge moment. What choice was made, what the alternative was, and how it foreclosed or opened certain futures. Cite specifically: a decision, a date, a person who made the call.

Turning Point 2
The Second Hinge

2–3 sentences. What happened here that locked in the outcome? Could be a military moment, a diplomatic failure, an unexpected death, a defection.

Turning Point 3
The Third Hinge

2–3 sentences. Often the most overlooked: an international decision, an economic shock, or a failure of communication that sealed the outcome.

Legacy & Debate

Opening paragraph: frame the debate. What is the core contested question? Don't list things that are uncontested. Find the live argument — the one where serious people who have studied this reach different conclusions — and put it front and centre.

The Contested Question

Prose paragraph diving into the specific debate. Use evidence and named sources. Don't just say "some argue X, others argue Y" — say who argues X, what their evidence is, and why Y is a compelling counter. Then explain where the weight of evidence sits.
