Origins & Rise

Saddam Hussein was born on 28 April 1937 in Al-Awja, a poor village on the banks of the Tigris a few miles south of Tikrit. His father had died or disappeared before his birth — accounts differ — and his mother remarried a man named Ibrahim al-Hassan, who by most accounts beat the boy and resented feeding him. Saddam was largely raised by his maternal uncle, Khairallah Tulfah, a former army officer who had participated in the failed 1941 pro-Nazi coup against the British-backed Iraqi government and served time in prison for it. Khairallah's fierce anti-British, pan-Arab nationalism became Saddam's first political education. When Saddam finally moved to Baghdad at around the age of ten to live with Khairallah, he arrived semi-literate, rough-mannered, and seething with the humiliation of a poverty-stricken childhood.

He joined the Ba'ath Party in 1957, aged about twenty. The Ba'ath — short for Hizb al-Ba'ath al-'Arabi al-Ishtiraki, or Arab Ba'ath Socialist Party — was a pan-Arab movement that envisioned a single unified Arab nation. At the time it was a small, conspiratorial organisation focused on seizing state power. Saddam proved himself useful in the only currency that mattered: willingness to commit violence. In October 1959, he participated in a failed assassination attempt on Iraqi Prime Minister Abd al-Karim Qasim. The plan went wrong, Qasim was wounded but survived, and Saddam was shot in the leg during the chaotic getaway. He fled to Syria and then Egypt, where he spent the next three years in exile in Cairo, enrolling in a law school but spending most of his energy on Ba'athist politics. He returned to Iraq after the Ba'ath Party successfully overthrew Qasim in February 1963, only to find that the party was itself ousted nine months later by military rivals.

The years that followed — imprisonment (1964–66), another failed power grab, and the eventual Ba'athist coup of July 17, 1968 — taught Saddam the essential lesson of Iraqi politics: no power is safe unless you control the security services yourself. While the formal president after 1968 was Ahmad Hassan al-Bakr, a general and Saddam's relative from Tikrit, it was Saddam who built and ran the party's security apparatus. He became deputy chairman of the Revolutionary Command Council, the real seat of power, and he used that position over the next decade to place loyalists — many of them Tikritis, many of them family — into every sensitive node of government and intelligence. By the time he publicly forced al-Bakr to resign on July 16, 1979, the transition was almost a formality. Saddam had already been the most powerful man in Iraq for years.

What followed his assumption of the presidency, just days later, is one of the most chilling episodes in modern political history. He convened a congress of senior Ba'ath Party members and, in a scene recorded on video, personally read out the names of 66 "traitors" accused of plotting with Syria. Those men were taken from the room and shot. A line had been drawn in blood: loyalty to Saddam Hussein was now the supreme obligation of Iraqi political life, and the price of disloyalty was death.

Ba'athist Ideology

Ba'athism gave Saddam a framework — pan-Arab unity, anti-imperialism, secular socialism — but he was never an ideologue in any sincere sense. He used ideology the way he used everything else: as a tool of power. By the 1980s, the Ba'ath Party's internal structures had become mere facades for his personal rule, and the ideology shifted with whatever served him at a given moment. He invoked Islam when he wanted to mobilise masses or signal hostility to Iran; he invoked Arab nationalism when he wanted to rally neighbours against Western influence; he invoked socialism when defending state ownership of oil wealth. What remained consistent throughout was the cult of Saddam himself.

Arab Nationalism

Ba'athism held that all Arabic-speaking peoples formed one nation, temporarily divided by colonialism and corrupt local elites. Saddam positioned himself as the heir to Gamal Abdel Nasser, the Egyptian leader who had united the Arab world in the 1950s. In practice, this nationalism served as justification for regional ambition — including the claim that Kuwait was historically part of Iraq.

Secular Socialism

The Ba'ath Party was explicitly secular — keeping religion out of law and government — and nominally socialist, which in Iraq meant state ownership of the oil industry after its nationalisation in 1972. Oil revenues funded a genuine expansion of public services in the 1970s: literacy programmes, free healthcare, subsidised food. These real material gains were central to Saddam's early legitimacy.

The Cult of the Leader

As Saddam's power consolidated, his personal image overwhelmed any party doctrine. Portraits, statues, and murals depicted him as a soldier, a farmer, a father, a Bedouin horseman. State media ran his speeches in full. Schools taught children to identify with him personally. The party became an instrument of this cult rather than the other way around — a pattern seen in Stalinist Russia and which Saddam studied carefully.

Sunni Arabism in Practice

Whatever the ideology's formal universalism, power in Ba'athist Iraq rested on an ethnic and sectarian skeleton. Sunni Arabs from the Tikrit region dominated the security services, the Republican Guard, and the inner circle. Iraq's Shia majority (roughly 60% of the population) and Kurdish minority in the north were systematically marginalised and, at times, subjected to mass violence.

"The leader-symbol is above criticism. The leader is one who makes no mistakes. It is not permitted to question his acts."
— Saddam Hussein, on the role of the leader in Ba'athist political culture, paraphrased from internal Ba'ath Party documents

How He Ruled

Saddam's system of rule combined two things that are more often seen in opposition: calculated generosity and systematic terror. For those inside the circle — tribal clients, loyal party members, Tikriti kinsmen — the regime was extraordinarily generous. Salaries, cars, houses, foreign educations for children, contracts, and business licences all flowed through networks of patronage that traced back to Saddam himself. This created a large class of people with a direct material stake in his survival. But the boundaries of that circle were never fixed, and crossing them — even accidentally, even by reputation — meant disaster.

The Security Apparatus

Saddam understood, from his earliest days as the party's intelligence chief, that power depends on information and the credible threat of violence. He maintained at least five overlapping intelligence and security agencies: the Mukhabarat (general intelligence), Amn al-Khass (special security), the Estikhbarat (military intelligence), the Amn (internal security), and the Special Republican Guard. These organisations were deliberately kept separate, competitive with each other, and answerable only to him. Each could monitor the others. No single general or intelligence chief could ever accumulate enough power to threaten the president. It was a system engineered for paranoia as a feature, not a bug.

Terror and Compliance

Political prisoners were common throughout the 1970s, but after 1979 the scale and brutality of repression increased sharply. The regime did not merely imprison dissidents — it tortured them, often in front of family members, and then killed them and charged the family for the bullets. Confessions extracted under torture were broadcast on state television. An Iraqi official who expressed the wrong opinion to a colleague could find that colleague had already reported him. The aim was not just to punish dissent but to make every act of social trust feel dangerous, rendering collective resistance nearly impossible.

Documented Atrocity

The Anfal Campaign and the Halabja Chemical Attack (1986–1989)

Between 1986 and 1989, the Iraqi state under Saddam launched a campaign of extermination against Iraqi Kurds, named "Anfal" (a Quranic term referring to war spoils) and commanded by Ali Hassan al-Majid — subsequently known internationally as "Chemical Ali." The campaign involved mass executions, the destruction of thousands of Kurdish villages, forced displacement, and the use of chemical weapons. The single most devastating incident was the attack on Halabja on 16–17 March 1988: Iraqi aircraft dropped mustard gas and nerve agents (including sarin and VX) on a civilian Kurdish town, killing approximately 3,200–5,000 people immediately and injuring thousands more, with long-term health consequences for survivors. Human Rights Watch and subsequent investigations estimated that the broader Anfal campaign killed between 50,000 and 182,000 Kurds. It constitutes one of the clearest documented instances of genocide in the post-World War II period.

Oil, Welfare, and Legitimacy

The 1970s were genuinely transformative for many Iraqis. After the nationalisation of the Iraq Petroleum Company in 1972 and the 1973 oil price shock, Iraq's revenues soared. The government built schools, hospitals, roads, and electrification projects at a pace that had no precedent. Female literacy rates rose steeply. The UN cited Iraq as having achieved near-eradication of illiteracy by 1982. This material improvement was real and cannot be dismissed — it formed a genuine part of the social contract that bound many urban, professional-class Iraqis to the regime, even as that same regime was torturing people in its basement prisons.

The Republican Guard and Tribal Networks

Alongside the party and the intelligence services, Saddam maintained his position through two other pillars: a praetorian military force (the Republican Guard, eventually the Special Republican Guard) equipped better than the regular army and drawn from loyal tribal areas; and a deliberately cultivated network of tribal sheikhs across Iraq who received money, weapons, and recognition in exchange for keeping their populations in line. This was in some ways a return to Ottoman and British colonial methods — ruling through intermediaries — but Saddam made it entirely personal. A sheikh's access to state resources depended on his personal relationship with the president, not on any institutional arrangement that could outlast either of them.

System Design

Overlapping Loyalties, Manufactured Insecurity

Political scientists who have studied the Ba'athist system — including Hanna Batatu and Kanan Makiya — emphasise that the regime's genius was making everyone simultaneously a beneficiary and a potential victim. Even senior officials could never be entirely certain of their status. This manufactured insecurity kept even the most powerful figures deferential. No one dared organise because no one could trust that any confederate was not already an informer.

Wars & Conflicts

Saddam Hussein fought three major wars during his presidency. Each was premised on a miscalculation — about Iranian vulnerability, about international tolerance for aggression, and about the extent to which the United States would actually invade in 2003. Each left Iraq weaker and more isolated. Together, they consumed hundreds of billions of dollars, killed hundreds of thousands of people, and ultimately provided the immediate context for his removal.

1980–1988
The Iran–Iraq War
▼

On 22 September 1980, Iraqi forces invaded Iran on a broad front along the Shatt al-Arab waterway. Saddam's calculation was straightforward, if catastrophically wrong: Iran was in post-revolutionary chaos, its officer corps purged by Khomeini, its international relationships in tatters after the hostage crisis. He expected a quick victory and border concessions. Instead, the invasion united Iranians behind the Islamic Republic and triggered eight years of grinding, attritional warfare reminiscent of World War I trench fighting.

By 1982, Iraq was on the defensive. Iran had pushed Iraqi forces back, and its "human wave" tactics — sending poorly armed soldiers, including teenage boys from the Basij militia, across minefields — was slowly exhausting Iraqi defences. Saddam's response included the use of chemical weapons against Iranian forces and Kurdish villages (the Halabja attack came in the closing months of this war, when Kurdish groups were collaborating with Iran). The United States, which feared a revolutionary Iran far more than Ba'athist Iraq, tilted toward Saddam: providing intelligence, agricultural credits, and diplomatic cover even as it became clear Iraq was using chemical weapons.

A UN-brokered ceasefire finally came into effect on 20 August 1988, restoring approximately the pre-war borders. The war had cost Iraq an estimated $450 billion, killed perhaps 250,000 Iraqi soldiers, and left the country deeply indebted to Saudi Arabia and Kuwait — a debt that would feed directly into the next crisis.

1990–1991
The Gulf War: Invasion of Kuwait and Desert Storm
▼

Emerging from the Iran–Iraq War with a massive army and massive debts, Saddam turned on Kuwait. He had long argued that Kuwait was illegitimately carved off from Iraq by British colonialism, and he now accused it of exceeding OPEC oil quotas and thereby depressing oil prices — an act of economic warfare, he claimed. On 2 August 1990, Iraqi forces invaded and occupied Kuwait within hours. Saddam declared Kuwait Iraq's 19th province.

The international response was swift and unified in a way that would not be repeated in 2003. The UN Security Council imposed immediate sanctions and, in Resolution 678, authorised the use of force if Iraq did not withdraw by 15 January 1991. A US-led coalition of 35 nations — including Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Syria, and the United Kingdom — assembled in the Gulf. Saddam refused to withdraw, miscalculating that the coalition would not stomach high casualties, or that Israel could be dragged into the conflict (he fired Scud missiles at Tel Aviv to try to provoke Israeli retaliation and split the Arab members of the coalition). Neither calculation bore out.

Operation Desert Storm began on 17 January 1991 with an air campaign of unprecedented intensity: 100,000 sorties over 42 days. On 24 February, the ground war began. Coalition forces crushed Iraqi resistance in 100 hours. President George H.W. Bush, crucially, stopped short of marching on Baghdad — a decision he justified on practical and legal grounds (the coalition's UN mandate was to liberate Kuwait, not overthrow Iraq) but which left Saddam in power. In the war's aftermath, Shia uprisings in the south and Kurdish revolts in the north were brutally suppressed by remaining Republican Guard units — with no intervention from the US forces that remained nearby, a betrayal that Kurdish and Shia Iraqis would not forget.

1991–2003
The Decade of Sanctions: Surviving Containment
▼

After the Gulf War, Iraq was placed under the most comprehensive sanctions regime in UN history. Saddam was required to dismantle his weapons of mass destruction programmes and submit to inspections by UNSCOM (later UNMOVIC). The sanctions prohibited virtually all trade and froze foreign assets. The impact on ordinary Iraqis was devastating: the UN estimated that by the mid-1990s, child mortality had increased substantially and the healthcare system had essentially collapsed. UNICEF reported in 1999 that an additional 500,000 children had died since 1991 compared to pre-sanctions trends — a figure that became one of the most contested statistics in the debate over Iraq policy.

Saddam survived through a combination of ruthless domestic repression and manipulation of the sanctions system. The Oil-for-Food Programme (1996–2003), intended to allow Iraq to sell oil for humanitarian goods, was systematically corrupted: Saddam's government extracted billions in kickbacks from contractors and used oil allocations as political rewards to countries and individuals it wanted to cultivate. Meanwhile, Saddam played a cat-and-mouse game with weapons inspectors — sometimes cooperating, sometimes blocking access — in ways that kept international suspicion alive without ever definitively settling whether WMD programmes remained active. This ambiguity would become fatal.

March–April 2003
Operation Iraqi Freedom: The US-Led Invasion
▼

After the September 11, 2001 attacks, the Bush administration — led by Vice President Dick Cheney and Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld — moved to connect Iraq to the new "War on Terror." The case rested on three claims: that Iraq possessed active WMD programmes; that it had links to al-Qaeda; and that Saddam's removal would produce a democratic domino effect across the Middle East. The WMD claim was the centrepiece of the public case, most dramatically presented by Secretary of State Colin Powell to the UN Security Council on 5 February 2003 — a presentation Powell later called a "blot" on his career, because much of the intelligence it relied on was fabricated or misread.

The invasion began on 20 March 2003 with a US-led coalition that notably excluded France and Germany (which opposed the war) but included the UK, Australia, and Poland. Iraqi military resistance collapsed with startling speed — Saddam had hollowed out the regular army through decades of underfunding and distrust, keeping combat-capable units under tight political control. Baghdad fell on 9 April 2003. The iconic image of the day — US Marines helping Iraqis topple a large statue of Saddam in Firdos Square — was later revealed to have been a staged media event involving a relatively small crowd, though the genuine jubilation of many Iraqis at the regime's fall was real.

No WMD were ever found. The CIA's Iraq Survey Group concluded in 2004 that Iraq had dismantled its nuclear, chemical, and biological weapons programmes in the early 1990s, though retaining some dual-use infrastructure and institutional knowledge. Whether Saddam's ambiguous posturing was intended to deter Iran (which he feared more than the US) or was simply a bluff that got out of hand remains debated. The result was the same: a regime built on fear collapsed in three weeks, and the US found itself administering a country it had no coherent plan to rebuild.

The Fall & Execution

When Baghdad fell, Saddam disappeared. The man who had cultivated an image of iron invincibility — who had survived assassination attempts, wars, and sanctions — went into hiding. US forces searched for him for months, following a trail of informants, intercepted communications, and intelligence from captured Ba'athist officials. He moved through safe houses in the Tikrit region, avoiding contact even with most of his own security apparatus.

13 December 2003
Capture at Ad-Dawr
▼

US forces from the 4th Infantry Division, acting on intelligence provided by a member of Saddam's extended family, raided a farmhouse near Ad-Dawr, a village south of Tikrit. Saddam was found hiding in a narrow underground hole — a "spider hole," as it became known — barely large enough for a man to lie flat, with a ventilation pipe and a small cache of money. He was dishevelled, with an unkempt beard, and offered no resistance. He told the soldiers, "I am Saddam Hussein. I am the president of Iraq and I want to negotiate." A soldier reportedly replied: "President Bush sends his regards."

The images of his medical examination — doctors probing his mouth and hair for lice — were broadcast around the world. For Iraqis who had lived under a regime that presented him as godlike, the pictures were deliberately chosen for their humiliating contrast with the cult of personality. The practical and psychological effect was significant: it confirmed the regime's permanent end in a way that mere military defeat had not.

2004–2006
Trial and Execution
▼

Saddam was tried before the Iraqi Special Tribunal, established by the Coalition Provisional Authority but staffed by Iraqi judges. His first trial focused on the 1982 massacre at Dujail, where the regime had executed 148 Shia men and boys after a failed assassination attempt against Saddam during a visit to the town. He was convicted and sentenced to death on 5 November 2006 — two days before the US midterm elections, a timing that drew criticism. A second trial related to the Anfal campaign was still under way when he was executed.

Saddam was hanged at Camp Justice, a former intelligence headquarters in north Baghdad, in the early hours of 30 December 2006. The execution was conducted by Iraqi government officials during the Eid al-Adha holiday, which many Sunni Muslims observed that day (Shia Muslims observed it a day later). A mobile phone video, captured by a witness and leaked within hours, showed guards taunting Saddam with chants of "Muqtada! Muqtada! Muqtada!" — a reference to the Shia cleric Muqtada al-Sadr, whose followers had been fighting US forces. Saddam responded with contempt. The manner of the execution drew widespread international criticism — not in sympathy for Saddam, but because it looked more like sectarian revenge than justice. Even some members of the Iraqi government condemned it.

After the Fall

The Chaos That Followed

Removing Saddam did not bring democracy, stability, or gratitude. The US decision to disband the Iraqi army and "de-Ba'athify" the civil service in May 2003 — stripping hundreds of thousands of trained, armed men of their livelihoods and status — is widely regarded as one of the most consequential foreign policy blunders of the post-Cold War era. It created a pool of aggrieved, experienced fighters who fuelled the insurgency. The sectarian civil war that followed (2006–2008) killed tens of thousands of Iraqis. Out of the chaos emerged ISIS, which in 2014 captured Mosul and declared a caliphate across large parts of Iraq and Syria, drawing heavily on former Ba'athist military and intelligence networks. By most measures, the 2003 invasion and its aftermath killed more Iraqis than Saddam's entire reign — a deeply contested claim that nevertheless must be part of any honest accounting.

Legacy & Debate

Few figures in modern history are easier to condemn and harder to assess honestly. Saddam Hussein was a mass murderer who used chemical weapons on his own citizens, built a state founded on terror, launched two catastrophic wars of aggression, and left Iraq physically devastated and politically fractured. That is not a contested claim — it is documented in meticulous detail by human rights organisations, UN bodies, and the testimony of survivors. The moral ledger on atrocities is clear.

The harder questions are the comparative and counterfactual ones. Was Iraq better or worse off under Saddam than it was before him? Is it better or worse off after him? What do the answers tell us about the relationship between stability and tyranny, between imposed order and self-determination? These questions do not have simple answers, and honest engagement with them does not require softening the judgement on Saddam himself.

What defenders (cautiously) note
  • Iraq under Ba'athism achieved genuine improvements in literacy and public health in the 1970s
  • The regime was secular, protecting Christian minorities and allowing women in professional roles
  • Iraq maintained a degree of regional stability and Arab nationalist counterweight to Israel
  • The state, however brutal, functioned — infrastructure, education, medicine all worked
  • He kept the Sunni-Shia-Kurdish tensions suppressed, preventing the overt sectarian violence that followed
  • Iraq under Saddam, despite everything, did not see the scale of chaos of post-2003 Iraq
Documented crimes and failures
  • Anfal genocide against Kurds: 50,000–182,000 killed, confirmed by HRW and ICC proceedings
  • Halabja chemical attack: 3,200–5,000 civilians killed in a single day in March 1988
  • Mass executions of Shia after the 1991 uprising: tens of thousands killed
  • Systematic use of torture, rape, and extrajudicial killing as instruments of state policy
  • Destruction of southern Iraq's marshlands — a unique ecosystem — to punish Marsh Arabs
  • Invasion of Iran: initiated a war killing ~500,000 people on both sides
  • Invasion of Kuwait: an act of aggression that destabilised the entire Gulf region
  • Corruption of the Oil-for-Food Programme, diverting humanitarian funds for personal and political use
Contested & Unresolved
  • The sanctions of the 1990s: Did they weaken Saddam or punish ordinary Iraqis? The answer is almost certainly "both"
  • WMD ambiguity: Saddam may have genuinely confused his own intelligence apparatus about the status of programmes that had been dismantled — he may not have known what he did or didn't have
  • The 2003 invasion: Was the world better off without him? The question cannot be answered without asking what Iraq became afterward — and whether that outcome was inevitable or a consequence of specific decisions (like disbanding the army)
  • His pan-Arab nationalism: did it offer a genuinely alternative vision to political Islam, or was it always just a cover for personal power?
  • Responsibility for post-2003 sectarianism: Did Saddam create the fault lines, or merely suppress them — with his removal revealing pre-existing fractures that would have emerged regardless?

The most honest account of Saddam Hussein is one that refuses to let the genuine catastrophe of post-2003 Iraq rehabilitate him. The argument "Iraq was more stable under Saddam" is statistically defensible in some narrow senses — and immediately complicated by asking "stable for whom?" Not for Kurds being gassed. Not for Shia men vanished in the night. Not for the hundreds of thousands killed in wars he started. Stability built on mass terror is not a political achievement — it is a humanitarian crime with a longer time horizon.

"He managed to make himself both indispensable and irreplaceable — not because he built good institutions, but because he destroyed every institution that might survive him."
— Kanan Makiya, author of Republic of Fear (1989), on the structural logic of Saddam's rule
