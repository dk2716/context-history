Dictators & Tyrants

Saddam Hussein

28 April 1937 – 30 December 2006

From a mud-brick village near Tikrit to the gilded palaces of Baghdad, Saddam Hussein spent four decades clawing toward absolute power — then spent the rest of his life proving how destructive absolute power can be. His story is one of ambition, cruelty, catastrophic miscalculation, and a chaotic aftermath that still shapes the Middle East today.

01

Origins: The Village Boy

Saddam Hussein Abd al-Majid al-Tikriti was born on 28 April 1937 in al-Awja, a poor village on the banks of the Tigris River near the town of Tikrit, about 160 kilometres north of Baghdad. His father died — or disappeared, accounts vary — before Saddam was born, and his mother, Sabha, fell into a deep depression. For the first few years of his life, Saddam was raised by his maternal uncle, Khairallah Tulfah, a nationalist army officer who filled the boy's head with Arab pride, hatred of Western colonialism, and admiration for strongmen.

When Saddam eventually returned to his mother, her new husband — his stepfather — treated him harshly. Contemporaries recalled a childhood without shoes, without books, and without safety. He did not attend school until he was about ten years old, which was unusual even by the standards of rural Iraq at the time. The humiliation of starting late, of being older and poorer than other students, seems to have sharpened something cold and acquisitive in him.

At sixteen, Saddam moved to Baghdad to live with his uncle Khairallah and attend secondary school. The uncle introduced him to Ba'athist circles — a pan-Arab socialist movement then competing fiercely for influence in Iraqi politics. Saddam, large, physically intimidating, and already skilled at projecting menace, found his place among its enforcers.

Context

Iraq in the 1950s was a constitutional monarchy under King Faisal II, but it was a brittle state — shaped by British colonial borders drawn after World War I, riven by tensions between Sunni Arabs, Shia Arabs, and Kurds, and increasingly inflamed by pan-Arab nationalism sweeping the region after Egypt's Gamal Abdel Nasser nationalised the Suez Canal in 1956. The ground was fertile for anyone who offered strength and certainty.

02

The Ba'ath Party & the Road to Power

The Ba'ath Party ("Ba'ath" means "resurrection" or "renaissance" in Arabic) was founded in Syria in the 1940s. Its ideology blended Arab nationalism — the belief that all Arabic- speaking peoples formed one nation artificially divided by colonialism — with socialism and secular government. In theory it was progressive; in practice, in both Syria and Iraq, it became a vehicle for one-party dictatorship.

Saddam joined the party in 1957, aged twenty. He proved himself useful in the way young men often prove themselves useful to revolutionary organisations: violence. In 1959, the Ba'ath Party assigned him to a team that attempted to assassinate Iraq's military ruler, General Abd al-Karim Qasim, in a Baghdad street ambush. The plot failed. Saddam was wounded in the leg — he later claimed he dug out the bullet himself — and fled first to Syria, then to Egypt, where he spent several years studying law in Cairo.

The exile was formative. In Egypt, Saddam observed how Nasser built a cult of personality, how state media could be weaponised, how fear and admiration could be manufactured simultaneously. He returned to Iraq after the Ba'ath Party successfully seized power in a 1963 coup that overthrew and executed Qasim. The Ba'athists were ousted again within months, but Saddam had tasted power's proximity.

He spent time in prison — where, reportedly, he organised the other Ba'athist detainees into a disciplined faction — and in the underground, building the party's internal security apparatus. When the Ba'ath Party seized power again in a July 1968 coup, Saddam was thirty-one years old and the second most powerful man in Iraq. His relative by marriage, Ahmad Hassan al-Bakr, became president. Saddam became deputy.

Key idea

Saddam's genius in these years was institutional. While al-Bakr was the public face, Saddam systematically took control of the party's security services, the intelligence apparatus, and the military chain of command — building a parallel structure of loyalty that ran through him rather than through the state itself.

03

Seizing Total Control (1979)

By the late 1970s, al-Bakr's health was failing. On 16 July 1979, Saddam persuaded — or pressured — him to resign. He was now president of Iraq, chairman of the Revolutionary Command Council, prime minister, commander-in-chief of the armed forces, and secretary-general of the Ba'ath Party. Every lever of state ran through his hands.

He moved immediately to eliminate potential rivals. Five days after taking power, he convened an extraordinary meeting of senior Ba'athists and announced that a Syrian-backed conspiracy had been uncovered within the party's own ranks. As he read out names from a list, security officers escorted each named man from the hall. The remaining delegates, visibly terrified, were then asked to form the firing squads that executed the accused. Saddam made senior party members complicit in the killings — ensuring their silence through shared guilt.

04

How He Ruled: Fear and Patronage

Saddam's system of control rested on two pillars that worked in opposite directions and reinforced each other: terror and reward.

The terror apparatus was elaborate. The Mukhabarat (general intelligence directorate), the Amn (internal security), and the Istikhbarat (military intelligence) operated as overlapping, competing bureaucracies of surveillance. Informers were everywhere — neighbours reported on neighbours, children were encouraged to report on parents. Political prisoners were tortured in Abu Ghraib and other facilities. Disappearances were routine. Public execution was not uncommon. Entire families could be punished for the perceived disloyalty of a single member.

But fear alone cannot sustain a government. Alongside it, Saddam built a genuine system of patronage centred on his home region. Tikritis — and especially members of his own tribe, the al-Bu Nasir — were elevated into positions of military command, intelligence leadership, and business privilege. His sons Uday and Qusay were groomed as princelings. Loyalty to Saddam personally, rather than to Iraq as a state, was the organising principle of Iraqi society.

For ordinary Iraqis who stayed quiet and out of politics, the 1970s actually brought real improvements. Iraq's nationalisation of its oil industry in 1972, combined with the 1973 oil-price shock, flooded the country with revenue. Saddam invested in schools, hospitals, electrification, and literacy campaigns. Iraq briefly became one of the most educated, urbanised societies in the Arab world. UNESCO awarded Iraq a prize for its literacy programmes in 1982. This is part of the historical record, and it matters — it explains why some Iraqis who remember the 1970s feel a complicated nostalgia, even knowing what came after.

The personality cult

Saddam modelled his self-presentation on a shifting cast of historical heroes. He compared himself to Nebuchadnezzar, the ancient Babylonian king who conquered Jerusalem. He posed in Kurdish traditional dress, in military uniform, in a business suit, in peasant clothes. His face appeared on every public building, his voice on every radio station, his image on currency. He wrote novels (published under a pen name). He was simultaneously the father of the nation and its most feared executioner.

05

The Iran–Iraq War (1980–1988)

In 1979, Iran's Islamic Revolution overthrew the Shah and installed Ayatollah Khomeini as supreme leader. For Saddam, this was both a threat and an opportunity. Khomeini — a Shia cleric — was openly calling on Iraq's majority-Shia population to rise up against their Sunni-dominated government. But Iran also looked, briefly, like it might be weak: its military was in disarray after the revolution, and it had been internationally isolated by the American hostage crisis.

On 22 September 1980, Iraq invaded Iran. Saddam expected a quick victory. He did not get one. The war became a grinding, catastrophic stalemate — eight years of trench warfare, poison gas, and mass casualties that bore an uncomfortable resemblance to the Western Front of World War I.

The international context is important. The United States and most of the West backed Saddam — not because they admired him, but because they feared Khomeini's Islamic revolution spreading further. The Reagan administration provided Iraq with satellite intelligence, financial credit, and turned a blind eye to Iraq's use of chemical weapons against Iranian forces. The Soviet Union sold Iraq weapons too. Saddam was, for a time, a useful man to powerful friends.

The war ended in August 1988 with UN-brokered ceasefire and no territorial gains for either side. Iraq was left with a shattered economy, enormous debt (much of it owed to Kuwait and Saudi Arabia), and a battle-hardened military that had nowhere left to go.

06

The Anfal Campaign & Halabja

Iraq's Kurdish population in the north had long resisted Ba'athist rule. During the Iran–Iraq War, Kurdish militias (the Peshmerga) allied with Iran against Baghdad. Saddam viewed this as unforgivable treason. His response was not military suppression — it was an attempt at ethnic cleansing.

The Anfal Campaign ("Anfal" is a Quranic word for spoils of war) was directed by Saddam's cousin and son-in-law, Ali Hassan al-Majid, who became notorious as "Chemical Ali." Between February and September 1988, Iraqi forces swept through Kurdish villages in a coordinated campaign of destruction.

Atrocity: The Halabja Chemical Attack — 16–17 March 1988

The town of Halabja, in Iraqi Kurdistan near the Iranian border, had fallen to Iranian and Kurdish Peshmerga forces. On 16–17 March 1988, Iraqi aircraft dropped chemical weapons — including mustard gas and nerve agents (sarin and possibly VX) — on the town. The attack killed an estimated 3,200 to 5,000 civilians immediately, and injured thousands more. It remains the largest chemical weapons attack against a civilian population in history. Survivors described yellow and white clouds drifting through streets where people had been shopping, cooking, and going about their lives. The long-term health consequences — cancers, birth defects, respiratory illness — have persisted for generations.

Atrocity: The Anfal Campaign — 1988

Across Iraqi Kurdistan, the Anfal operation destroyed an estimated 2,000–4,000 villages. Between 50,000 and 182,000 Kurds were killed — estimates vary widely, but the Iraqi government's own documents, recovered after 1991, described mass executions. Men and boys of fighting age were separated from their families and shot in the desert or at purpose-built execution sites. Women, children, and elderly people were transported to detention camps where many died from thirst, heat, and abuse. The campaign has been recognised as genocide by several governments and international bodies.

The Anfal Campaign received relatively little international attention at the time. The world was focused on the Iran–Iraq War's ceasefire, and many Western governments did not want to damage their relationship with Saddam by pressing the issue. This silence is part of the moral accounting that historians must make.

07

The Gulf War (1990–1991)

Iraq emerged from the Iran–Iraq War deeply in debt. Saddam was furious that Kuwait — which had lent Iraq money during the war — was now demanding repayment and simultaneously overproducing oil, driving prices down and depriving Iraq of revenue. He also claimed that Kuwait was "slant drilling" into Iraqi oil fields across the border.

On 2 August 1990, Iraqi forces invaded and occupied Kuwait. The move shocked the world, including most of Saddam's former patrons. The United States, under President George H.W. Bush, assembled a coalition of 35 nations and spent five months building up forces in Saudi Arabia.

Saddam misread the situation catastrophically. He believed the US would not risk American casualties for what he characterised as a commercial dispute. He was wrong. On 17 January 1991, the coalition launched Operation Desert Storm — a six-week air campaign followed by a ground offensive so swift it lasted 100 hours. Iraqi forces collapsed. Kuwait was liberated.

Critical decision

President Bush chose not to march on Baghdad. His administration feared that toppling Saddam would cause Iraq to fragment — handing Iran a strategic victory — and would leave the US occupying a hostile country with no clear exit strategy. Coalition forces stopped at the Iraqi border. This decision would haunt policymakers for the next decade, and its reversal in 2003 would prove them partially right.

Inside Iraq, the aftermath of the Gulf War was catastrophic. Saddam's defeat triggered simultaneous uprisings by Shia Arabs in the south and Kurds in the north. Both expected US support; neither received it. Saddam's Republican Guard crushed the Shia uprising with exceptional brutality — mass executions, draining of the southern marshes, destruction of ancient communities. Perhaps 50,000–100,000 Shia civilians were killed. The Kurds fared slightly better: a Western-enforced no-fly zone in the north gave them a degree of protection and eventually allowed the formation of a de facto Kurdish autonomous region.

08

The Sanctions Decade (1991–2003)

After the Gulf War, the United Nations imposed comprehensive economic sanctions on Iraq — intended to force Saddam to dismantle his weapons of mass destruction programmes and comply with arms inspectors. Saddam did comply with some demands, and dissembled, delayed, and obstructed others. The stand-off lasted twelve years.

The sanctions had devastating consequences for ordinary Iraqis. The UN's own estimates suggested that hundreds of thousands of children died from preventable illness and malnutrition in the 1990s, as the Iraqi health system — once among the best in the Middle East — collapsed. The Oil-for-Food Programme, introduced to allow Iraq to sell oil for humanitarian goods, was riddled with corruption — including by Saddam's own government, which skimmed billions.

Saddam used the sanctions strategically. He allowed suffering to be visible, directing Western journalists to hospitals and children's wards, building an international narrative in which the West — not his own misrule — was responsible for Iraqi misery. In the Arab world especially, this framing had considerable traction.

Meanwhile, the question of Iraq's weapons of mass destruction — chemical, biological, and potentially nuclear — remained unresolved. Saddam had genuinely possessed and used chemical weapons. He had pursued nuclear weapons before the Gulf War. UN inspectors dismantled much of these programmes during the 1990s, but Saddam's deliberate ambiguity about what remained was, in retrospect, a fatal miscalculation: he wanted to appear more dangerous than he was, to deter Iran and regional rivals. Instead, he convinced the United States that he actually was dangerous.

09

The 2003 Invasion

The attacks of 11 September 2001 transformed the political environment in Washington. The Bush administration — already focused on Iraq — began building a case for military action. The argument rested on three claims: that Iraq possessed weapons of mass destruction in violation of UN resolutions; that Saddam's government had links to al-Qaeda; and that removing Saddam would be a catalyst for democratic change across the Middle East.

The first claim was the central one. On 5 February 2003, US Secretary of State Colin Powell presented intelligence to the UN Security Council purporting to show active Iraqi WMD programmes. The presentation was meticulous and compelling. It was also, as subsequent investigation showed, largely wrong. The intelligence had been assembled under political pressure and overstated what was actually known.

On 20 March 2003, a US-led coalition — primarily American and British, with smaller contributions from other nations — invaded Iraq without UN Security Council authorisation. The military campaign was brief: Baghdad fell on 9 April 2003, famously marked by the televised toppling of a large statue of Saddam in Firdos Square. Saddam himself fled and disappeared.

No WMD

Subsequent searches by US-led inspection teams found no active weapons of mass destruction programmes in Iraq. The stockpiles that had been assumed to exist did not exist. Whatever had remained after the Gulf War had been destroyed in the 1990s — but Saddam had never clearly admitted this, maintaining deliberate ambiguity. The intelligence failure (and the political pressure applied to intelligence agencies) remains one of the most consequential in modern history.

10

The Chaos That Followed

The US and its allies had planned the invasion in detail. They had planned the aftermath almost not at all.

Within weeks of Baghdad's fall, the decision by US administrator L. Paul Bremer to dissolve the Iraqi Army and ban senior Ba'athists from public life (de-Ba'athification) put hundreds of thousands of armed, trained, humiliated men out of work with no income and no future. Many of them became the backbone of the insurgency that followed.

2013–2017
ISIS rises and is defeated. The Islamic State of Iraq and Syria (ISIS/ISIL/Daesh), growing partly from al-Qaeda in Iraq, seizes vast territory including Mosul. A second US military intervention, Iraqi army rebuilding, and Kurdish Peshmerga eventually defeat ISIS — but at enormous cost.

The total death toll from the 2003 invasion and subsequent conflict is contested and haunting. Estimates of Iraqi civilian deaths range from 100,000 to over 600,000, depending on the methodology used. Millions were displaced. Iraq's Christian community, one of the oldest in the world, was largely destroyed. The country's infrastructure, historical sites, and social fabric suffered damage that will take generations to repair.

A difficult question

Was Iraq better or worse after Saddam? This is a genuine moral question, not a simple one. Saddam was undeniably a genocidal dictator. But the chaos that followed his removal killed enormous numbers of people, produced ISIS, and left Iraq weaker and more fragmented than before. Acknowledging both truths simultaneously — that Saddam was monstrous, and that the invasion made things worse — is harder than picking a side, but it is the only honest position.

11

Capture, Trial & Execution

For months after the fall of Baghdad, Saddam evaded capture. His location remained unknown despite a massive US manhunt and a $25 million bounty on his head. On 13 December 2003, acting on a tip, US forces found him hiding in a small underground hole — a "spider hole" — on a farm near his hometown of Tikrit. He was dishevelled, with a long beard, and surrendered without resistance.

The sight of him — humbled, dirty, submissive — was broadcast around the world. It was a deliberate contrast to the image he had spent a lifetime constructing.

Saddam was handed over to the Iraqi interim government and tried by the Iraqi Special Tribunal. The trial focused first on the 1982 massacre of 148 Shia men and boys from the town of Dujail, following a failed assassination attempt against Saddam there. He was convicted of crimes against humanity and sentenced to death. The Anfal campaign — the graver charge of genocide against the Kurds — had not yet been reached in the proceedings when the sentence was carried out.

On 30 December 2006, the first day of the Muslim festival of Eid al-Adha, Saddam Hussein was hanged in a former military intelligence building in Baghdad. Witnesses filmed the execution on mobile phones. The footage — which showed some of those present taunting Saddam as he stood with the noose around his neck — was leaked and spread rapidly online. His manner in his final moments was calm and composed. He died reciting Islamic prayers.

12

Legacy

Saddam Hussein's legacy resists simple summary, which is precisely why it is worth studying carefully.

He was a mass murderer. The Halabja chemical attack, the Anfal campaign, the Dujail massacre, the suppression of the 1991 Shia uprising, the systematic torture and disappearance of political opponents — these are documented, evidenced, and cannot be softened by any other consideration.

He was also a moderniser, in the authoritarian mould: he built schools, electrified villages, and for a brief period in the 1970s, raised Iraqi living standards significantly. These achievements do not cancel the atrocities; they coexist with them as part of a complete picture.

He was a profound strategic incompetent. He launched two catastrophic wars — against Iran and against Kuwait — that achieved nothing, exhausted his country, and ultimately invited the invasion that killed him. His insistence on maintaining ambiguity about weapons he no longer possessed was perhaps the most self-destructive gamble in modern political history.

His removal from power was followed by chaos that cost more Iraqi lives than his worst individual atrocities, though not more than the sum of them. The lesson some draw is that brutal stability is preferable to liberal intervention; others draw the opposite lesson — that such stability was always a fiction built on mass graves. Both positions are worth examining rather than simply asserting.

What is not in dispute: Iraq in 2006, when Saddam was executed, was a country in the middle of a sectarian civil war. Iraq in 2026 is a functioning but fragile state, still shaped by every decision he made and every decision made after him. Understanding Saddam Hussein means understanding how personal ambition, ideology, regional conflict, and superpower interest can combine to destroy millions of lives — and how removing a tyrant, without a serious plan for what comes next, can destroy millions more.

The honest reckoning

History does not require you to choose between condemning Saddam and condemning the 2003 invasion. Both can be — and by any serious accounting, both should be — condemned. The suffering of Iraqis under Saddam was real. The suffering of Iraqis after Saddam was also real. Holding both truths at once is what honest history demands.
