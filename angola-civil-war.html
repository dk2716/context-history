The Angolan Civil War

For twenty-seven years, two liberation movements fought for a country that had barely finished freeing itself from one empire before becoming a battlefield for two more.

Background

Portugal held Angola for four hundred years, from 1575 to 1975, and left with almost nothing in order. When the April 1974 Carnation Revolution toppled Lisbon's dictatorship, the new government scrambled to exit its African colonies as fast as possible. The Alvor Agreement of January 1975 gave Angola's three main liberation movements — the MPLA, FNLA, and UNITA — just ten months to form a transitional government before independence on November 11, 1975. Within weeks, the arrangement had collapsed into open fighting in Luanda's streets.

The three movements had never been natural allies. The MPLA (Movimento Popular de Libertação de Angola) was founded in 1956 and drew its base from the capital's educated urban class — mestiços, assimilados, and workers who had been absorbed into Portuguese civic life. It was Marxist-Leninist in orientation, Soviet and Cuban in its backers, and commanded Luanda when independence arrived. The FNLA (Frente Nacional de Libertação de Angola), led by Holden Roberto, was rooted in the Bakongo people of northern Angola and had CIA backing through Zaire's Mobutu Sese Seko, Roberto's brother-in-law. The UNITA (União Nacional para a Independência Total de Angola), led by Jonas Savimbi, drew its core from the Ovimbundu — Angola's largest ethnic group — in the central highlands, and had begun as a Maoist insurgency before pivoting to South African and American support.

By October 1975, both South African forces and Cuban combat troops were on Angolan soil. Pretoria sent armoured columns north to back UNITA and the FNLA; Havana launched Operation Carlota, airlifting thousands of soldiers to shore up the MPLA. The Cold War had landed in sub-Saharan Africa with full force. When the Portuguese flag finally came down in Luanda on November 11, there was no unified Angolan government to raise its own — just an MPLA declaration of a People's Republic and the opening shots of what would become one of the longest and most destructive civil wars of the twentieth century. Pearce (2012)

Key Figures

Agostinho Neto
MPLA President, 1975–1979
A poet and physician who led the MPLA from its founding until his death from cancer in Moscow in 1979. Neto steered Angola into a Soviet alliance and survived a near-revolt within his own movement in 1977 (the "Nitistas" purge). His death handed power to Eduardo dos Santos, who would rule for 38 years.
Eduardo dos Santos
MPLA President, 1979–2017
A Soviet-trained petroleum engineer who consolidated MPLA rule through oil wealth and military might. Dos Santos presided over the end of the war and Angola's post-2002 oil boom, but his government became synonymous with kleptocracy — his daughter Isabel became one of Africa's richest people by controlling state assets.
Jonas Savimbi
UNITA Founder and Leader, 1966–2002
The most charismatic African guerrilla leader of the Cold War era — and one of the most destructive. A brilliant strategist educated in Switzerland, Savimbi cultivated alliances with Mao's China, apartheid South Africa, and Reagan's America. He rejected the 1992 election results he had agreed to, resuming a war that cost hundreds of thousands of lives. He was killed in combat on February 22, 2002, near the town of Lucusse. Pearce (2012)
Fidel Castro
Cuban Leader; MPLA patron
Cuba's intervention in Angola — up to 50,000 troops at its peak — was the largest overseas military operation in Cuban history. Castro described it as internationalist solidarity against apartheid colonialism; critics called it Soviet proxy warfare. Cuban advisors trained and fought alongside MPLA forces from 1975 until 1991.
P.W. Botha
South African Prime Minister, 1978–1984
Under Botha, South Africa conducted repeated large-scale military incursions into Angola — ostensibly to suppress SWAPO, the Namibian liberation movement using Angola as a base, but simultaneously to sustain UNITA. The 1987–88 Battle of Cuito Cuanavale became the largest armoured battle in Africa since World War II and fatally damaged South Africa's military credibility.
Chester Crocker
US Assistant Secretary of State for African Affairs, 1981–1989
The architect of "constructive engagement" — the Reagan administration's policy of pressuring South Africa through diplomatic engagement rather than sanctions, while simultaneously increasing CIA support for UNITA. Crocker eventually brokered the 1988 New York Accords linking Cuban withdrawal from Angola to Namibian independence, his most lasting achievement. Rarely appears in popular accounts of the war.

Key Events

October–November 1975
Three Armies, One Independence Day
▼

By September 1975, the Alvor Agreement was dead. MPLA, FNLA, and UNITA were fighting openly across Angola, and both the Soviet Union and the United States had begun funnelling weapons to their respective clients. South Africa launched Operation Savannah on October 14, sending armoured columns north from Namibia to support UNITA and the collapsing FNLA advance on Luanda.

Cuba's response was immediate and decisive. Operation Carlota — named for a nineteenth-century Cuban slave rebel — airlifted the first Cuban troops to Luanda on November 7. Within weeks, Cuban forces and MPLA fighters had halted the South African advance at Ebo and Quifangondo. When independence arrived on November 11, MPLA held Luanda and declared the People's Republic of Angola; UNITA and the FNLA declared a rival government in Huambo. The world had to choose which to recognise. Pearce (2012)

The Organisation of African Unity recognised the MPLA government in early 1976, largely because of the apartheid stigma attached to South Africa's intervention. The United States, blocked by the Clark Amendment (passed December 1975), could no longer fund the FNLA or UNITA openly. South Africa withdrew. The FNLA collapsed. UNITA retreated to the southeastern bush.

Pearce (2012) Birmingham (2015)
1976–1985
Two States in One Country
▼

After the FNLA's collapse, Angola's war became a straight fight between the MPLA government and UNITA. But calling it a guerrilla war understates what each side actually was. Both movements functioned as proto-states, providing services, administering territory, and building loyalties that went far beyond simple military coercion. Pearce (2012)

The MPLA governed the cities and the oil-rich Cabinda enclave, funnelling petroleum revenue into the military and selective urban development. Paradoxically, the American oil company Gulf Oil (later Chevron) continued paying royalties to the MPLA government even as the CIA funnelled weapons to UNITA — one of the Cold War's stranger ironies. The MPLA formalised its Marxist-Leninist identity at its 1977 Congress, though an internal power struggle that same year — the "Nitistas" coup attempt — killed an estimated 15,000 to 80,000 people in internal purges.

UNITA controlled vast swaths of southeastern Angola from its headquarters at Jamba, in Cuando Cubango province. Savimbi built what observers described as a remarkably complete bush society — hospitals, schools, radio stations, a justice system, farms. It was coercive and authoritarian, but it was functional. South Africa supplied weapons across the Namibian border; after 1985, the CIA resumed covert support under the Reagan Doctrine. UNITA's finances rested on artisanal diamond mining in the northeast, a revenue stream that would eventually fund one of the war's most destructive phases. Pearce (2012)

Pearce (2012) Global Witness (1998)
1987–1988
The Battle of Cuito Cuanavale
▼

In 1987, the MPLA launched a major offensive — Operation Saludando Octubre — to destroy UNITA's southeastern strongholds. SADF (South African Defence Force) troops intervened in force, and the war escalated into the largest conventional battle in sub-Saharan African history. Across the Lomba River and around the town of Cuito Cuanavale, thousands of troops, Soviet advisors, Cuban regulars, MiG fighters, and South African Olifant tanks fought for months.

The strategic result is still contested. South Africa claimed a defensive victory; Cuba and the MPLA claimed they had repelled an invasion. What is clear is that South Africa took unacceptable casualties and recognised it could not sustain the war politically at home — the white electorate was growing tired of body bags from a distant conflict. Nelson Mandela later called Cuito Cuanavale "a turning point in the liberation of our continent from the scourge of apartheid." Birmingham (2015)

The battle's aftermath drove the negotiating table. In December 1988, South Africa, Cuba, and the MPLA government signed the New York Accords at the United Nations. Cuba would withdraw its forces by 1991; South Africa would leave Angola and grant Namibia independence. UNITA was not a party to the agreement, but without South African support it was severely weakened.

Birmingham (2015) Crocker (1992)
September–November 1992
The Election That Ended Peace
▼

The Bicesse Accords of May 1991 produced a ceasefire and a UN-supervised transition to multiparty democracy. After sixteen years of war, Angola held its first free election on September 29–30, 1992. International observers declared the vote free and fair. The MPLA won 54% of the parliamentary vote; in the presidential race, dos Santos took 49% against Savimbi's 40%, not enough to avoid a runoff.

Savimbi refused to accept the results. UNITA accused the MPLA of fraud — claims the UN monitoring mission found unsupported — and began redeploying its fighters, who had never been fully demobilised as required by Bicesse. On October 31, 1992, UNITA gunmen launched the "Halloween Massacre," killing MPLA ministers, police officers, and MPLA-aligned civilians in Luanda. The MPLA responded in kind, massacring UNITA members across the capital. Pearce (2012)

The war resumed with a ferocity that dwarfed everything before it. UNITA now had UN-condemned "conflict diamonds" funding it — buying weapons through Zaire and Togo — while the MPLA had oil money and a government's international legitimacy. The next phase of the war killed more people in a shorter time than all the previous years combined.

Pearce (2012) UN Security Council (1993)
1993–2002
Diamonds, Oil, and the War's Final Phase
▼

The post-1992 war produced some of the worst humanitarian disasters in modern African history. The Battle of Huambo (January–March 1993) — a three-month urban siege of Angola's second city — killed an estimated 10,000 people, mostly civilians. The UN Security Council imposed arms and oil sanctions on UNITA in 1993, though they were routinely circumvented through neighbouring countries. The Lusaka Protocol of 1994 produced another fragile ceasefire that UNITA never fully honoured.

Global Witness's 1998 report A Rough Trade exposed how UNITA was financing its resurgence: through the sale of diamonds mined by forced labour in territory it controlled, estimated to have generated $3.7 billion between 1992 and 2001. The report triggered the Kimberley Process — the international certification scheme for conflict diamonds, launched in 2003. Meanwhile, MPLA oil revenues from Cabinda funded a sustained military buildup. Global Witness (1998)

In 1998, the MPLA launched a coordinated military offensive that systematically dismantled UNITA's conventional military capacity. The army tracked Savimbi using satellite intelligence reportedly assisted by American and British firms — a bitter irony given Washington's earlier support. On February 22, 2002, government forces ambushed Savimbi near Lucusse in Moxico province. He was shot multiple times and died at the scene. With its charismatic founder dead, UNITA's military collapsed within weeks. The Luena Memorandum, signed April 4, 2002, ended the war.

Global Witness (1998) Pearce (2012)
2002–Present
Peace Without Democracy
▼

UNITA transformed from an armed movement into an opposition political party, but the process was deeply asymmetric. The MPLA had the state, the oil revenues, and the international community's recognition. UNITA had the memory of thirty years of war and a constituency traumatised by its own leadership. The 2008 elections — the first since 1992 — produced an MPLA landslide of 82%, including an astonishing performance in Huambo, UNITA's historic heartland. Pearce (2012)

Scholars have described the post-war settlement as "illiberal peacebuilding" — a peace structured around the MPLA's political and economic dominance rather than genuine democratisation. The party and state remained effectively indistinguishable; oil revenues sustained a system of patronage that made competitive politics difficult. Eduardo dos Santos ruled until 2017, when he was succeeded by João Lourenço, who has pursued limited anti-corruption measures — including targeting dos Santos's own children — but the MPLA's structural dominance has not fundamentally changed. Péclard (2025)

Angola today is simultaneously a petro-state rich enough to have hosted the Africa Cup of Nations and a country where millions lack clean water. Landmine clearance — Angola has one of the world's worst landmine legacies — continues in rural areas. UNITA competes in elections and wins municipal seats, but remains far from power. The war is over; the wounds are not.

Péclard (2025) Pearce (2012)

Turning Points

Turning Point 1
Cuba's Decision to Intervene (October 1975)

When South Africa's armoured columns moved north toward Luanda in October 1975, Castro decided to deploy combat troops rather than just advisors. Had Cuba not intervened, the FNLA–South African force would likely have captured Luanda before independence day, installing a pro-Western government and fundamentally altering the war's trajectory. The intervention made Angola a Cuban ally for sixteen years and turned Havana into the MPLA's indispensable protector.

Turning Point 2
Savimbi Rejects the 1992 Election Results

International observers certified the September 1992 elections as free and fair. Savimbi had more democratic legitimacy to contest the result through legal means than through war — UNITA controlled significant territory and a parliamentary bloc. His decision to resume fighting instead triggered the most destructive phase of the entire conflict, killed hundreds of thousands of people, and ultimately destroyed UNITA's moral credibility with much of the international community.

Turning Point 3
Global Witness Exposes Conflict Diamonds (1998)

The 1998 A Rough Trade report by Global Witness was the first systematic documentation of how diamond revenues sustained UNITA's war machine. The report triggered a cascade of UN sanctions tightening, international diamond industry pressure, and ultimately the Kimberley Process. It also reframed the war internationally — from a Cold War proxy conflict to a resource-driven atrocity — isolating UNITA politically at a moment when its military capacity was already declining.

Turning Point 4
Savimbi's Death (February 22, 2002)

UNITA had survived thirty-six years entirely around one man's personality. When FAA forces killed Savimbi in Moxico province, there was no succession plan, no ideological framework that could outlast him, no structure that could mobilise fighters without him. UNITA's military collapsed within six weeks. The Luena Memorandum followed in April. The war that Savimbi had personally chosen to restart in 1992 ended only with his death.

"UNITA and the MPLA were not simply ethnic armies or Cold War proxies. They were state-building projects, each seeking to monopolise the legitimate use of force across the same territory, and each building the institutions — including coercive ones — that states require."
— Justin Pearce, Political Identity and Conflict in Central Angola, 1975–2002 (2012)

Why It Matters Today

The Angolan Civil War's legacies reach far beyond Angola's borders. It shaped global norms around conflict resources, produced one of history's most studied examples of Cold War proxy warfare, and left a political settlement that defines African "peace" in ways scholars still contest.

The Kimberley Process Was Born Here

UNITA's diamond revenues didn't just fund one war — they produced the global framework for certifying conflict-free diamonds. The Kimberley Process Certification Scheme, launched in 2003, emerged directly from Angola and Sierra Leone's experience. Though critics argue the scheme has significant loopholes, it represented the first major international effort to link resource sales to conflict accountability.

  • Over 80 countries now participate in the Kimberley Process
  • Angola became a certification scheme member in 2003
  • The scheme is credited with reducing conflict diamond trade from ~4% to under 1% of global supply
  • Critics note it covers only diamonds, not gold, coltan, or other conflict minerals

Angola as a Model of Illiberal Peacebuilding

Angola's post-war trajectory — where peace arrived through military victory rather than political negotiation, and the victor consolidated authoritarian dominance through oil wealth — has become a reference point in peacebuilding literature. It illustrates how "negative peace" (absence of fighting) and "positive peace" (justice, democratisation) can diverge dramatically.

  • MPLA has governed Angola continuously since 1975 without interruption
  • Angola ranked among the world's most corrupt states for decades (Transparency International)
  • João Lourenço's post-2017 anti-corruption drive has been partial and selective
  • UNITA now holds significant parliamentary seats but faces structural disadvantages

Cuba's African Legacy Remains Contested

Cuba's intervention in Angola — the largest overseas military operation in Cuban history — is remembered very differently depending on where you stand. For South Africa's liberation movement and Namibia, Cuban troops were the force that stopped apartheid's northward expansion. For Cold Warriors, it was Soviet adventurism by proxy. The intervention shaped Cuba's international identity for generations.

  • An estimated 2,000+ Cuban soldiers were killed in Angola
  • Namibia achieved independence in 1990, directly linked to the New York Accords
  • Angola and Cuba maintain strong diplomatic and economic ties today
  • Castro cited Angola as evidence of Cuba's internationalist principles

Angola's Oil Wealth and Resource Governance

Angola is one of Africa's largest oil producers, and the management of that wealth — primarily through the state oil company Sonangol — has become a case study in petro-state pathology. The dos Santos family's extraction of oil revenues into private accounts, exposed through the "Luanda Leaks" investigation (2020), showed how conflict-era resource capture patterns can persist long after the guns fall silent.

  • Angola produces approximately 1.1 million barrels of oil per day
  • Oil accounts for ~95% of export revenues
  • "Luanda Leaks" (2020) exposed Isabel dos Santos's $2+ billion extraction from state assets
  • Angola joined OPEC in 2007 (though suspended membership in 2023 over quota disputes)

Legacy & Debate

The Angolan Civil War resists simple moral frameworks. It was a genuine anti-colonial struggle that became a Cold War proxy conflict that became a resource war — and at each stage, the original liberation ideals became harder to locate. The central scholarly argument is not about whether the war was bad (it was catastrophically so) but about how to assign responsibility: between external powers who fuelled it and internal actors who chose it.

Justin Pearce
University of Cambridge — Political Identity and Conflict in Central Angola, 1975–2002 (2012)
Pearce resists reducing either the MPLA or UNITA to Cold War puppets or ethnic armies. Both were state-building projects with genuine social bases; loyalties were mutable and context-dependent rather than fixed by ethnicity. The war's violence was produced by the logic of two competing sovereignty claims in the same space.
Global Witness
— A Rough Trade (1998) / All the Presidents' Men (2002)
The most damning indictment of UNITA's post-1992 war: it was financed by forced-labour diamond mining and sustained through deliberate evasion of international sanctions. The report also implicates Western diamond buyers and African transit states. Neither UNITA's grievances nor Cold War dynamics excuse what it became after 1992.
Didier Péclard
University of Geneva — UNITA and the Legacies of Defeat (2025)
Péclard examines what happened to UNITA after the war — its difficult transformation into a legitimate opposition party and what this reveals about Angola's peace settlement. The MPLA's dominance is not just a product of military victory but of a political economy of oil that makes genuine competition structurally difficult. "Illiberal peacebuilding" captures how peace and authoritarianism coexist.

Was UNITA a Legitimate Movement or a Terrorist Organisation?

The hardest interpretive question about the war is whether UNITA ever had legitimate grievances that justified its methods, or whether Savimbi's movement was simply a vehicle for his personal ambition sustained by apartheid South Africa and American Cold Warriors. The honest answer is that both things were true at different times. In the 1970s and into the 1980s, UNITA administered territory, provided services, and represented a real constituency — the Ovimbundu — who had legitimate complaints about Luanda's remote, Creole-dominated MPLA elite. Pearce (2012)

By the 1990s, that case had become much harder to make. UNITA's rejection of the 1992 elections — which it had agreed to and which international observers certified — stripped it of democratic legitimacy. The post-1992 war targeted civilians systematically, used forced mining labour, and deliberately besieged cities to produce famine. Whatever UNITA had once been, what it became after 1992 is difficult to defend by any standard. Pearce (2012) Global Witness (1998)

The MPLA, in turn, is rarely subjected to equivalent scrutiny for its pre-1975 and early post-independence repression. The 1977 Nitistas purge — in which an estimated 15,000 to 80,000 people may have been killed in political violence — has been documented only partially, and the MPLA government has not allowed systematic historical investigation. The post-war political settlement, as Péclard notes, involves a deliberate management of this history: UNITA's crimes are acknowledged; the MPLA's early violence is not. Péclard (2025)

The Case for UNITA's Original Legitimacy
  • Represented the Ovimbundu, Angola's largest ethnic group, genuinely excluded from MPLA powerPearce (2012)
  • Built functioning social institutions at Jamba — hospitals, schools, courts — without external supportPearce (2012)
  • The MPLA's one-party state (1975–1991) was authoritarian; UNITA's demand for elections was not illegitimateBirmingham (2015)
  • South African and American support, while cynical, did not automatically invalidate UNITA's domestic social base
  • Post-war UNITA has functioned as a legal opposition, suggesting a genuine political identity beyond armed strugglePéclard (2025)
The Case Against UNITA's Post-1992 War
  • Rejected internationally monitored elections it had agreed to without credible evidence of fraudUN Security Council (1993)
  • Financed the post-1992 war through forced-labour diamond mining — a war crimeGlobal Witness (1998)
  • Deliberately besieged Kuito and Malanje, producing famine as a weapon; an estimated 1,000 people per day died at peakGlobal Witness (2002)
  • Violated the Lusaka Protocol (1994) before the ink was dry, resuming full warfare by 1998
  • Savimbi had opponents within UNITA killed, including family members, suggesting personal despotism rather than political convictionPearce (2012)

The external powers do not escape scrutiny either. The United States backed UNITA through the CIA even after it became clear that the post-1992 war was a human catastrophe. Cuba's intervention, whatever its ideological framing, extended a war that might otherwise have ended sooner. South Africa used Angola to fight its Namibia proxy war with total disregard for Angolan lives. The consensus among historians is that Angola's civil war was prolonged and intensified by external interference — but that Angolan leaders, above all Savimbi, bore the primary moral responsibility for the choices they made. Birmingham (2015)

"The peace that came to Angola in 2002 was not a negotiated settlement between equals but a victory of one belligerent over another. Understanding what kind of peace that produces — and for whom — is the central challenge of Angola's post-war history."
— Didier Péclard, UNITA and the Legacies of Defeat (2025)
